CHAPTER II.

THE MARKET-PLACE.

On a certain summer morning more than two hundred years ago, a large crowd of Boston residents gathered on the grass plot in front of the jail on Prison Lane. Everyone stared intently at the heavy oak door, which was reinforced with iron clamps.

In any other town, or at a later time in New England history, the grim and frozen look on the faces of these bearded men would have meant something terrible was about to happen. It would usually suggest the execution of a famous criminal. In those cases, the court's sentence simply confirmed what the public already wanted. However, the early Puritans were so naturally stern that you could not be sure this was the reason they had gathered. They might be waiting to see a lazy servant or a disobedient child corrected at the whipping post. Or perhaps they were waiting to see a Quaker or some other religious outsider whipped out of town. It could even be an idle Native American, made rowdy by the white man’s alcohol, being driven back into the forest with a whip. Or, they might be waiting to see a witch, like old Mistress Hibbins, the bitter-tempered widow of the magistrate, die on the gallows.

In any of these cases, the spectators behaved with the same solemn attitude. This suited a society where religion and law were almost the same thing. In their character, the two were so mixed together that the mildest punishments and the most severe executions were treated with the same deep respect and awe. A criminal could expect very little cold sympathy from the bystanders at the scaffold. On the other hand, a punishment that would only bring mockery and ridicule today was treated with almost as much serious dignity as a death sentence.

On the summer morning when our story begins, the women in the crowd seemed to take a special interest in the punishment that was about to happen. Society was not yet refined enough to keep women from stepping out into the public streets and pushing their broad bodies close to the scaffold. Both morally and physically, these wives and daughters of English birth were much tougher than their descendants today, who are separated from them by six or seven generations. Over time, each generation of mothers has passed down a fainter complexion, a more delicate beauty, and a smaller physical frame to their children, along with a less forceful character. The women standing around the prison door lived less than fifty years after the time of Queen Elizabeth, who had been a very strong and fit representative of her sex. These women were her fellow countrywomen. The beef and ale of their native land, along with a simple moral diet, made them large and sturdy. The bright morning sun shone on their broad shoulders, full figures, and round, red cheeks, which had developed in England and had not yet grown pale in the cold air of New England. Furthermore, these mothers spoke with a boldness and loud volume that would startle people today.

“My friends,” said a harsh-looking woman of fifty, “I will tell you what I think. It would be much better for the public if we women of mature age, who are church members in good standing, had the power to judge criminals like Hester Prynne. What do you think, neighbors? If that shameless woman stood for judgment before the five of us here, would she get off with such a light sentence as the magistrates have given her? I certainly think not!”

“People say,” another woman added, “that the Reverend Master Dimmesdale, her holy minister, is deeply heartbroken that such a scandal has fallen upon his church.”

“The magistrates are God-fearing gentlemen, but they are too merciful, and that is the truth,” added a third, older woman. “At the very least, they should have branded Hester Prynne’s forehead with a hot iron. That would have made her feel it, I guarantee you. But as it is, that wicked woman will not care what they put on the front of her dress. She will probably just cover it with a fancy pin or some other wild decoration, and walk the streets as proud as ever!”

“Ah, but,” whispered a young wife, holding a child by the hand, “no matter how she covers the mark, she will always feel the pain of it in her heart.”

“Why are we talking about marks and brands, whether on her dress or on her forehead?” cried another woman, who was both the ugliest and the most pitiless of the group. “This woman has brought shame upon us all, and she deserves to die. Is there not a law for it? Yes, there is, both in the Bible and in the law books. If the magistrates refuse to enforce it, they can only blame themselves if their own wives and daughters go astray!”

“Have mercy on us, ladies,” a man in the crowd called out. “Is there no goodness in women except what comes from a fear of being hanged? That is a harsh thing to say! Hush now, neighbors, because the lock is turning in the prison door, and here comes Mistress Prynne herself.”

The prison door was thrown open from the inside. First, the town officer stepped out like a black shadow emerging into the sunshine. He carried a sword by his side and held his official staff in his hand. His grim appearance represented the whole dark severity of the Puritan laws, which he was responsible for enforcing. He held out the official staff in his left hand and placed his right hand on the shoulder of a young woman to draw her forward. But as they reached the threshold, she pushed him away with natural dignity and strength. She stepped out into the open air as if by her own free will. She held a baby in her arms who was about three months old. The baby squinted and turned its little face away from the bright daylight, because it had lived only in the gray twilight of a dark prison cell.

When the young mother stood fully revealed before the crowd, her first reaction was to press the baby tightly to her chest. She did this not out of motherly love, but to hide a certain symbol sewn onto her dress. However, she quickly realized that using one symbol of shame to hide another would not work. So she held the baby on her arm, blushed deeply, and looked around at her neighbors with a proud smile and a bold look. On the breast of her gown, the letter A was displayed in fine red cloth, surrounded by elaborate embroidery and beautiful patterns of gold thread. It was done so artistically and with such creative imagination that it looked like a beautiful decoration on her dress. The dress itself was elegant and fashionable for the times, but it far exceeded the simple clothing rules allowed by the colony.

The young woman was tall, with a graceful and elegant figure. She had thick, dark hair that was so glossy it caught the sunshine, and a beautiful face with regular features, a healthy complexion, a prominent brow, and deep black eyes. She moved with a dignity and state that matched the lady-like style of that era, rather than the delicate grace we value today. Hester Prynne had never looked more noble and dignified than she did as she stepped out of the prison. People who knew her before and expected to see her looking sad and ruined were astonished. They were startled to see how her beauty shone through her misfortune, turning her shame into a kind of glowing halo. To a sensitive observer, this was a painful sight. The dress she had made for herself in prison expressed her desperate and reckless mood through its unique and artistic design. However, the thing that drew everyone's eyes and transformed her appearance was the scarlet letter embroidered on her chest. It seemed to cast a spell, separating her from the rest of humanity and placing her in a world of her own.

“She is certainly very skilled with a needle,” one woman in the crowd remarked. “But no respectable woman would ever show off her skill in such a shameless way! Neighbors, she is laughing in the faces of our holy magistrates. She is turning what they meant as a punishment into something to be proud of.”

“It would be better,” muttered the harshest of the old women, “if we stripped that rich gown off her shoulders. As for the red letter she stitched so carefully, I would gladly give her a rag from my old flannel to make a more suitable one!”

“Oh, please be quiet, neighbors!” whispered the youngest woman. “Do not let her hear you. She felt the pain of every single stitch in that embroidered letter in her heart.”

The grim officer gestured with his staff. “Make way, good people, in the King's name!” he cried. “Open a passage, and I promise you that Mistress Prynne will be placed where everyone can get a clear view of her fine clothes, from now until an hour past noon. Bless the righteous Colony of Massachusetts, where sin is dragged out into the sunshine! Come along, Hester, and show your scarlet letter in the marketplace!”

A path opened through the crowd. Led by the officer and followed by a group of stern men and harsh-looking women, Hester Prynne walked toward the place of her punishment. A crowd of curious schoolboys, who did not understand what was happening but were happy to have a half-holiday, ran ahead of her. They kept turning around to stare at her face, the squinting baby in her arms, and the shameful letter on her chest. In those days, the distance from the prison door to the marketplace was not very far. However, to the prisoner, it felt like a very long journey. Despite her proud look, she suffered with every step as the crowd stared at her. It felt as if her heart had been thrown onto the street for everyone to trample. Fortunately, human nature has a merciful way of protecting us. A person in deep pain does not feel the full intensity of their suffering in the moment, but rather in the dull ache that remains afterward. So Hester Prynne walked through this part of her trial with a calm expression, and arrived at a scaffold at the western end of the marketplace. The scaffold stood near the roof of Boston's first church, where it was built as a permanent structure.

This scaffold was part of a punishment machine. Today, this machine is only remembered in history, but in the old days, it was believed to promote good citizenship, much like the guillotine did in France. It was the platform of the pillory. Above the platform rose a wooden frame designed to lock the human head in a tight grip, holding it up for the public to stare at. This wooden and iron structure was the ultimate symbol of shame. There is no greater offense against human dignity, no matter what a person has done, than to forbid them from hiding their face in shame. Yet that was the very purpose of this punishment. In Hester's case, however, her sentence was only to stand on the platform for a certain amount of time. She did not have her neck locked in the frame. Knowing what she had to do, she climbed the wooden steps and stood before the crowd, about the height of a man's shoulders above the street.

If a Catholic had been in that crowd of Puritans, this beautiful woman, holding her baby to her chest, might have reminded him of the Virgin Mary. It would have reminded him of those classic paintings of holy motherhood, though only by contrast. Here, the most sacred quality of human life was stained by deep sin. The world seemed darker because of this woman’s beauty, and more lost because of the child she had borne.

The scene was filled with awe, which always surrounds the sight of a person in guilt and shame before society becomes cold enough to laugh at it. The people witnessing Hester Prynne’s disgrace were simple and serious. They were stern enough to watch her execution, if that had been her sentence, without complaining about its cruelty. However, they were not heartless, and they would never laugh at such a sad event. Even if anyone had wanted to mock her, they would have been stopped by the solemn presence of the town's leaders. The Governor, his counselors, a judge, a general, and the town's ministers all sat or stood on the balcony of the meeting house, looking down at the platform. Because these important leaders were part of the event, the punishment carried a serious meaning. The crowd was quiet and solemn. Hester stood as best she could under the heavy weight of a thousand eyes staring directly at her chest. It was almost too much to bear. She had prepared herself to face insults and angry words. However, the silent, serious mood of the crowd was much more terrifying. She wished they would laugh at her instead. If the crowd had roared with laughter, she could have replied with a bitter, proud smile. But under their heavy, silent stare, she felt as if she would scream or throw herself off the scaffold and go mad.

Yet there were moments when the entire scene vanished from her eyes, or became blurry like a group of ghosts. Her mind and memory became highly active. She began to remember other scenes far away from this rough street on the edge of the wilderness, and other faces than the stern ones looking at her from under pointed hats. She remembered trivial things from her childhood, school days, games, and childish arguments. She also recalled the gravest events of her adult life. Every picture was equally vivid, as if they were all part of a play. This was likely a natural defense to relieve her mind from the cruel weight of her current reality.

Standing on the scaffold, Hester saw her entire life pass before her, going back to her happy childhood. In her mind, she saw her old village in England and her family home, which was a decaying stone house with an old coat of arms over the door. She saw her father's face, with his bald head and white beard. She saw her mother's face, filled with the loving concern she always remembered. She saw her own young face, glowing with beauty in an old mirror. Then she saw the face of an elderly man. He was pale and thin, with eyes dimmed from reading many heavy books. Yet those same eyes had a strange power to read the human soul. This man was slightly deformed, with his left shoulder higher than his right. Next, she remembered the narrow streets and tall gray houses of a European city, where she had started a new life with this scholar. Finally, all these memories faded away. She was back in the rough marketplace of the Puritan settlement. The townspeople were all staring at her as she stood on the scaffold, holding her baby, with the scarlet letter A embroidered on her chest.

Could this be true? She pressed the baby so tightly to her chest that the child cried out. She looked down at the scarlet letter and touched it with her finger to make sure the baby and her shame were real. Yes, this was her reality. Everything else had vanished.

CHAPTER XI.

THE INTERIOR OF A HEART.

After the incident in the study, the relationship between the minister and the doctor changed. Although their interactions looked the same on the outside, they were very different in reality. Roger Chillingworth now had a clear path before him. He appeared calm, gentle, and passionless, but a quiet depth of malice was active within him. The unfortunate old man imagined a more intimate revenge than any mortal had ever taken on an enemy. He wanted to make himself the minister's one trusted friend. In this way, Dimmesdale would confide all his fear, remorse, and agony to Chillingworth alone. The guilty sorrow, which the world would have pitied and forgiven, was revealed only to this pitiless and unforgiving doctor. All that dark secret was shared with the very man who wanted to destroy him.

The minister's shy reserve had kept this plan from working completely. However, Chillingworth was still satisfied with the situation. He felt that a revelation had been granted to him, and it did not matter whether it came from heaven or hell. With this knowledge, he could see and understand every movement of Dimmesdale's inmost soul. He was no longer just a spectator, but a chief actor in the poor minister's inner world. He could play upon the minister's feelings as he chose. If he wanted to cause him pain, he knew exactly which nerve to touch. If he wanted to startle him with fear, he could easily raise ghosts of death and shame to point at the minister's chest.

All of this was done so subtly that the minister never knew the cause of his suffering. He only felt a vague, evil influence watching over him. Dimmesdale looked at the deformed figure of the doctor with doubt, fear, and even hatred. He hated the doctor's gestures, his walk, his gray beard, and even his clothes. This was a clear sign of a deep hatred in the minister's chest, though he was not willing to admit it to himself. Because he could not find a logical reason for his distrust, Dimmesdale believed the problem lay in his own diseased mind. He blamed himself for these bad feelings and did his best to root them out. Since he could not do so, he forced himself to remain social and friendly with the old man. This gave Chillingworth constant opportunities to carry out his torment.

Despite his physical illness and spiritual torture, Mr. Dimmesdale became incredibly popular in his work. He won this popularity largely because of his sorrows. His intellectual gifts, moral insights, and power to communicate emotion were kept active by his daily pain. His fame grew larger than the reputations of his fellow ministers, even though some of them were highly respected scholars. There were scholars in the colony who had spent more years studying theology than Dimmesdale had been alive. There were also men with sturdier minds and a firmer grasp of doctrine, who made highly respectable and effective leaders. There were saintly fathers who had spent their lives reading books and communicating with the spiritual world. All these holy men lacked only one gift, which was the ability to speak to the human heart in its native language. These apostolic fathers spoke from high peaks of faith, but their voices were distant and hard to understand.

Mr. Dimmesdale naturally belonged to this group of saintly men. He would have climbed to the highest peaks of faith if his secret sin had not kept him down. His burden kept him on a level with the lowest sinners. Yet this very burden gave him a deep sympathy with the sinful brotherhood of mankind. His heart vibrated in unison with theirs, allowing him to share their pain and speak with a sad, persuasive eloquence. The people did not understand the power that moved them. They thought the young minister was a miracle of holiness, a messenger of divine wisdom and love. In their eyes, the very ground he walked on was holy. The young women of his church admired him with a devotion that they believed was entirely religious. The elderly members of his congregation, seeing how weak he was, believed he would die before them. They told their children to bury them close to their young pastor’s grave. Meanwhile, when Dimmesdale thought of his grave, he wondered if grass would ever grow on it, because an accursed thing would be buried there.

The public adoration tortured him. He loved the truth and valued it above all else. He felt like a shadow, completely empty of value. He longed to stand in his pulpit and tell the people what he really was. He wanted to shout that the pastor they respected and trusted was actually a pollution and a lie.

More than once, Mr. Dimmesdale had gone into the pulpit intending not to leave until he had confessed. More than once, he had cleared his throat and taken a deep breath to speak his secret. He had actually spoken to them, telling his congregation that he was vile, a companion of the worst sinners, and an abomination. He told them he was surprised his body did not shrivel up before their eyes under God's wrath. But the people only respected him more. They did not guess the true meaning of his words. They called him a saint on earth, believing that if someone so holy felt so sinful, their own souls must be in a terrible state. The minister knew his confessions would be viewed this way. He had tried to ease his conscience, but he had only added the sin of hypocrisy. He had spoken the truth, but he had turned it into a falsehood. He loved truth and hated lies, so he hated himself most of all.

His guilt drove him to punish himself in secret. In his closet, under lock and key, he kept a whip. He used it on his own shoulders, laughing bitterly as he struck himself. He also fasted rigorously, not to purify his body, but as penance. He fasted until his knees shook beneath him. He stayed awake night after night, sometimes in total darkness, and sometimes looking at his face in a mirror by a bright light. He tortured himself with constant self-examination, but he could not find peace. During these long nights, his brain reeled and he saw visions. He saw demons grinning at him, and angels flying upward. He saw the dead friends of his youth, his stern father, and his mother turning her face away as she passed. Finally, he saw Hester Prynne leading little Pearl, who pointed first at the scarlet letter on Hester's chest and then at the minister's own breast.

None of these visions ever completely fooled him. He could always force himself to realize they were not solid, unlike his oak table or his heavy books. Yet they felt like the only real things in his life. Guilt steals the substance out of reality, making the universe feel false. To the untruthful man, everything is empty. The only truth that gave Dimmesdale a real existence was his inner pain, and the expression of it on his face. If he had ever smiled and looked happy, he would have ceased to exist.

One night, the minister started from his chair. A new thought had struck him, offering a moment of peace. He dressed carefully in his clerical robes, walked softly down the stairs, and went out into the night.

CHAPTER XXII.

THE PROCESSION.

Before Hester Prynne could gather her thoughts and decide what to do about this alarming news, she heard military music coming from a nearby street. The music announced the arrival of the parade. The judges, officers, and citizens were marching toward the church, where Mr. Dimmesdale was scheduled to deliver the Election Sermon.

The front of the parade soon appeared. The marchers walked slowly and solemnly around the corner and into the marketplace. The musicians led the way, playing drums and horns. Although they were not highly skilled, the music gave the scene a grand and heroic feeling. Little Pearl clapped her hands at first, and then she became quiet, listening to the swelling sounds. Soon, the bright sunshine reflecting off the soldiers' armor drew her attention back to the parade. This military escort was made up of local gentlemen who wanted to learn the skills of war. They walked proudly, showing how much respect the colony had for soldiers. Some of these men had fought in Europe and were experienced veterans. Clad in polished steel helmets with nodding feathers, the soldiers looked incredibly bright and impressive.

The political leaders walking behind the soldiers were even more impressive. Their dignified presence made the soldiers' proud walks look simple by comparison. In those days, people valued stable, dignified character much more than clever talent. The early settlers respected old age, honesty, and wisdom. The statesmen they elected, such as Bradstreet, Endicott, Dudley, and Bellingham, were not necessarily brilliant speakers, but they were serious and dependable men. They had great courage and stood firm in times of danger, defending the colony like cliffs against a stormy sea. These leaders looked strong and carried themselves with a natural authority that would have fit in well among the highest councils in England.

Behind the judges walked the young and famous minister who was to preach the sermon. In that era, the priesthood was the most respected career in the colony, attracting the most ambitious and intelligent men. Successful ministers held immense influence, sometimes even gaining political control.

The crowd noticed that Mr. Dimmesdale walked with more energy than he ever had since arriving in New England. His step was firm, his back was straight, and he did not keep his hand over his heart. However, his strength did not seem to be physical. It looked like a spiritual energy, or perhaps the excitement of his deep thoughts. He was so focused that he did not seem to hear the music or see the crowd around him. His mind was entirely occupied with organizing the thoughts for his sermon. His spirit carried his weak body along, making him forget his illness. Brilliant but troubled men often possess this ability to summon a massive burst of energy for a short time, only to feel completely exhausted afterward.

Hester Prynne watched the minister and felt a cold weight settle on her heart. He seemed so distant and completely out of reach. She had hoped for at least one look of recognition. She remembered their meeting in the forest clearing, where they had held hands and shared their deep love and pain by the murmuring brook. They had known each other so deeply then. Yet now she hardly recognized him as he walked proudly past, surrounded by the town's leaders and the grand music. He looked so far above her, and his thoughts seemed to be in a world where she did not exist. Hester felt a sinking fear that their connection was just a dream. She found it hard to forgive him for being able to detach himself so completely from their shared life, especially now when their fate was closing in on them. She felt as if she were reaching out in the dark and finding nothing.

Pearl sensed her mother's feelings or noticed the minister's distant look himself. She was restless while the parade passed, jumping up and down like a bird ready to fly. Once the crowd had gone by, she looked up at Hester.

"Mother," she said, "was that the same minister who kissed me by the brook?"

"Shh, quiet, dear Pearl," her mother whispered. "We must not talk in public about what happens in the forest."

"I was not sure it was him because he looked so different," the child said. "Otherwise, I would have run to him and asked him to kiss me in front of everyone, just like he did under the trees. What would he have done, mother? Would he have covered his heart, frowned, and told me to go away?"

"He would have told you that this is not the time or place for kisses," Hester answered. "It is a good thing you did not speak to him."

Mistress Hibbins was the only person bold enough to speak to Hester in public. She arrived in a magnificent velvet gown and a gold-headed cane. Because she was rumored to be a witch, the crowd stepped back to avoid touching her clothes. Seeing her stand next to Hester made the crowd move away even faster, leaving the two women alone.

"Who would believe it!" the old woman whispered to Hester. "That saintly man! Everyone thinks he is a saint, and he certainly looks like one. But who would guess that he was just in the forest, pretending to study scriptures while actually taking a walk in the woods? We know what that means, Hester Prynne. I see many church members in the parade who have danced with me in the forest at night. But this minister is a mystery. Tell me, Hester, was he the same man you met on the forest path?"

"Madam, I do not know what you are talking about," Hester answered. She believed Mistress Hibbins was losing her mind, but she was still startled by the old woman's claims. "I should not speak lightly of a respected minister like Mr. Dimmesdale."

"Nonsense!" the old lady cried, shaking her finger at Hester. "Do you think I have visited the forest so often without learning to recognize who else has been there? Even if they wash their hair and wear no leaves, I can tell. I know you, Hester, because I see your badge. It shines openly in the daylight. But this minister is trying to hide his connection. Let me tell you a secret. When the devil sees one of his servants hiding their bond, he has a way of revealing their mark in the broad daylight. What is the minister hiding under his hand?"

"What is it, good Mistress Hibbins?" little Pearl asked eagerly. "Have you seen it?"

"It does not matter, darling," Mistress Hibbins replied, bowing to the child. "You will see it yourself eventually. They say you are related to the prince of the air. Would you like to ride with me tonight to see him? Then you will understand why the minister keeps his hand over his heart."

The old woman walked away, laughing loudly enough for the whole marketplace to hear.

By this time, the opening prayer had ended inside the church, and Mr. Dimmesdale began to speak. A strong feeling kept Hester near the building. Since the church was too crowded for her to enter, she stood next to the pillory scaffold. This spot was close enough for her to hear the sound and rhythm of the minister's voice, even if she could not understand every word.

His voice was a remarkable instrument. Even a listener who did not speak the language could have been moved by its tone and rhythm. The sound carried deep emotion, passion, and tenderness. Although the church walls muffled the words, Hester listened intently and felt the meaning of the speech. The muffled tone seemed to make it more spiritual than if she had heard the words clearly. She heard the low undertone sink down like a dying wind, and then rise in sweetness and power until it filled the air with solemn grandeur. Yet, no matter how majestic the voice became, it always kept a sad, painful tone. It was a cry of human suffering that touched every heart. Even when his voice rose to a powerful command and filled the square outside, a listener could still detect the same cry of pain. It was the sound of a guilty, sorrowful heart sharing its secret with humanity, begging for sympathy and forgiveness. This constant undertone of pain gave the minister his greatest power.

Hester stood like a statue at the base of the scaffold. Even without the minister's voice, the spot held a powerful attraction for her. It was the place where her life of public shame had begun. She felt a heavy, wordless sense that her entire life was connected to this scaffold, which served as the center of her existence.

Meanwhile, Pearl left her mother's side and played around the marketplace. Her bright presence made the serious crowd look more cheerful, like a colorful bird darting through dark trees. She moved with a restless energy that was increased by her mother's tension. She ran toward anything that caught her curiosity, acting as if she owned whatever she looked at. The Puritans watched her, smiling but still believing she was a demon child because of her wild beauty and behavior. She looked directly into the faces of the Indians, who felt she possessed a nature even wilder than their own. She then ran into the middle of a group of sailors. These men watched her with wonder, as if a piece of sea foam had turned into a little girl.

The ship's captain, who had spoken to Hester, was so charmed by Pearl that he tried to kiss her. Since she was as quick and hard to catch as a hummingbird, he took a gold chain from his hat and threw it to her instead. Pearl wrapped it around her neck and waist. It looked so natural on her that it seemed like a part of her outfit.

"Your mother is the woman with the scarlet letter," the sailor said. "Will you carry a message to her?"

"I will if I like the message," Pearl answered.

"Tell her that I spoke again with the dark, hump-shouldered old doctor," he said. "He has promised to bring his friend, the gentleman she knows, on board with him. Tell your mother to only worry about herself and you. Will you tell her that, you little witch?"

"Mistress Hibbins says my father is the prince of the air!" Pearl cried with a mischievous smile. "If you call me names, I will tell him, and he will send a storm after your ship!"

The child ran back to her mother and told her what the sailor had said. Hester's strong spirit almost gave way. Just when a path of escape seemed to open for them, this dark sign of unavoidable doom appeared directly in their way.

While Hester was worrying about the sailor's news, she faced another trial. Many visitors from the countryside who had heard rumors about the scarlet letter but had never seen it now gathered around her out of curiosity. Although they were rude, they stayed a few yards away because of the letter's reputation. The sailors also noticed the crowd and came over to stare at her. Even the Indians watched her, believing the beautifully embroidered letter was a mark of high status. Finally, the townspeople joined in, staring at the familiar symbol of her shame. Hester saw the same group of women who had stood outside the prison seven years ago, except for the youngest, kindest one who had since died. At this final moment, when Hester was so close to throwing the letter away, it had become the center of attention once again, causing her more pain than it had in years.

While Hester stood in this circle of shame in the marketplace, the minister was looking down from the pulpit at the congregation he had deeply moved. The holy minister stood inside the church, while the woman with the scarlet letter stood outside in the square. No one in the crowd would have guessed that both of them carried the very same mark of guilt.

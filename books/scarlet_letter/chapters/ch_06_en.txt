CHAPTER VI.

PEARL.

We have barely spoken of the baby yet. She was a little creature whose innocent life had sprung, by the mysterious will of God, like a lovely and immortal flower out of a guilty passion. Hester watched the child’s growth with wonder. Every day, the baby became more beautiful, and her intelligence began to shine over her tiny features. Hester named her Pearl. She did not choose the name because the child possessed a calm, white, or quiet beauty. Instead, she named the infant Pearl because she was of great price. Hester had purchased her with everything she owned, and the child was her mother’s only treasure. It was a strange situation. Society had marked the mother’s sin with a scarlet letter, which isolated her completely from human sympathy. Yet God, as a direct consequence of that same sin, had given her a lovely child to hold against her chest. This child connected Hester to the rest of humanity, and was destined to be a blessed soul in heaven. However, these thoughts filled Hester with worry rather than hope. She knew her action had been sinful, so she had no faith that the result would be good. Day after day, she watched the child’s developing personality with fear. She dreaded finding some dark and wild trait that would reflect the guilt that had brought the child into the world.

Physically, the baby was absolutely perfect. With her healthy shape, her energy, and her natural coordination, she was worthy to have been born in the Garden of Eden. She looked like she belonged there, to be a plaything for angels after the first parents were driven out. Pearl possessed a natural grace that made whatever she wore look beautiful. Her mother did not dress her in simple, plain clothes. Instead, Hester bought the richest fabrics available, and used all her creativity to design gorgeous dresses for the child to wear in public. When dressed in these fine clothes, Pearl looked magnificent. Her own natural beauty shone so brightly through the rich fabric that she seemed to light up the dark cottage floor. Yet, she looked just as perfect in a simple, torn dress after playing outside. Pearl's appearance seemed to change constantly. In this one child, you could see many different personalities. She could look like a sweet wild flower or a tiny royal princess. Through all her changes, however, she kept a passionate nature and a deep, bright energy. If she had ever grown pale or quiet, she would have ceased to be herself. She would no longer be Pearl.

This changing appearance reflected her complex inner life. Her nature possessed both depth and variety. However, Hester feared that the child was completely unsuited for the world she was born into. Pearl could not be made to follow rules. Because a great law had been broken in bringing her to life, the child's mind was beautiful but disorganized. Her thoughts followed a unique order of their own that was impossible to understand. Hester could only explain Pearl’s character by remembering her own state of mind during her pregnancy. The mother's intense emotions had shaped the unborn child. Those feelings had stained the baby's spirit with dark shadows, fiery passions, and intense highlights. Above all, the conflict in Hester's soul during that time lived on in Pearl. Hester recognized her own wild, defiant mood and her flighty temper in the child. She even saw the same dark clouds of sadness that had hung over her own heart. In the young child, these traits were currently brightened by childhood energy. But Hester feared they could grow into storms later in the girl's life.

Family discipline in those days was far more rigid than it is now. Strict frowns, harsh lectures, and physical punishment were common. Parents used them not just to punish bad behavior, but to build good character. However, Hester was a lonely mother with only one child, and she was unlikely to be too harsh. Still, remembering her own mistakes, she tried to establish a gentle but firm control over her daughter. But the task was too difficult. She tried both smiles and frowns, but neither had any effect. Eventually, Hester had to step aside and let the child follow her own impulses. Physical restraint worked only while it lasted. As for any other kind of discipline, Pearl only listened when she felt like it. While Pearl was still a baby, Hester noticed a peculiar look that warned her when it was useless to argue, persuade, or plead. It was a highly intelligent, mysterious look. Sometimes it seemed stubborn or even mischievous, but it was usually accompanied by a wild burst of energy. At these moments, Hester wondered if Pearl was a human child. She looked more like a woodland sprite that would play on the floor and then vanish with a mocking smile. When that look appeared in Pearl’s bright, dark eyes, she seemed distant and unreal. It felt as if she might vanish like a flickering light. Seeing this, Hester would rush toward the child, chasing the little girl as she ran away. She would hold her tight and kiss her, not just out of love, but to prove to herself that Pearl was real and made of flesh and blood. But Pearl's laugh, though musical and happy, only made her mother more doubtful.

Saddened by this baffling distance between herself and her only treasure, Hester would sometimes burst into tears. When this happened, Pearl might react in unpredictable ways. Sometimes she would frown, clench her little fist, and stare coldly at her mother. Often, she would simply laugh louder than before, as if she could not understand human sadness. Other times, she would cry with a sudden rage of grief, sobbing out her love for her mother in broken words. She seemed determined to prove she had a heart by breaking it. But Hester could not rely on these sudden bursts of affection, because they passed as quickly as they arrived. Hester felt like a wizard who had summoned a spirit, but did not know the magic word to control it. Her only comfort was when Pearl was asleep. Then, she was sure of her daughter, and enjoyed hours of quiet, sad happiness, until Pearl woke up, often with that mischievous look returning to her eyes.

Pearl opened up very quickly. She was soon old enough to talk and play with others, rather than just playing with her mother. It would have made Hester so happy to hear Pearl’s clear, bird-like voice playing with other children. But this could never happen. Pearl was born as an outcast in the children’s world. Because she was the product of sin, she was excluded from playing with other baptized children. Pearl seemed to understand her loneliness instinctively. She recognized the circle that isolated her from other children. Ever since leaving the prison, Hester never went out in public without Pearl. The child walked beside her, holding her mother's finger and taking several quick steps for every one of Hester's. Pearl saw the local children playing their serious games on the streets. They played at going to church, whipping Quakers, fighting native tribes, or pretending to be witches. Pearl watched them closely, but she never tried to join them. If they spoke to her, she did not answer. If they gathered around her, she would grow furious. She would throw stones at them and scream in a high, wild voice, making Hester tremble because the cries sounded like witch curses in a foreign language.

The truth was that the Puritan children had learned to look down on Hester and Pearl. They sensed that there was something strange and unearthly about them, so they often called them names. Pearl felt their scorn and returned it with deep hatred. These bursts of anger actually brought some comfort to Hester, because they showed a clear and genuine emotion instead of Pearl's usual unpredictable moods. Still, it terrified Hester to see her own anger reflected in the child. Pearl had inherited this passion directly from Hester’s heart. Mother and daughter stood together in their isolation, and Pearl seemed to carry the same troubled spirit that had tormented Hester before the child was born.

At home, in and around the cottage, Pearl had plenty of playmates. Her creative imagination brought a thousand objects to life, like a torch lighting a fire. The simplest materials, like a stick, a bunch of rags, or a flower, became characters in her games. Her single voice spoke for many imaginary characters, both old and young. The black, solemn pine trees, groaning in the wind, became the Puritan elders. The ugly weeds in the garden became their children, whom Pearl chopped down and uprooted without mercy. It was amazing to see how quickly she changed her games. She danced with endless energy, and then collapsed when she was tired, before starting a new, wild game. In this way, she was like other highly creative children, except that she had no real playmates. What was truly strange was that she was always hostile toward the characters she created. She never imagined a friend. Instead, she seemed to create enemies to fight against. It was deeply sad for Hester to watch this. She knew her own conflict had caused Pearl to see the world as a hostile place at such a young age.

Watching Pearl, Hester would often drop her sewing onto her knees and cry out in pain. “Father in Heaven,” she would whisper, “if you are still my Father, what is this child I have brought into the world?” Pearl, hearing her mother's words, would turn her beautiful face, smile with a mysterious look, and go back to her play.

There was one other strange thing about Pearl’s behavior. The very first thing she noticed in her life was not her mother’s smile. Instead, the first object she became aware of was the scarlet letter on Hester’s chest. One day, as Hester leaned over the cradle, the baby noticed the gold embroidery around the letter. She reached up and grabbed it, smiling with a sharp look that made her face look much older. Hester gasped and instinctively clutched the letter, trying to pull it away from the child's touch. But Pearl looked into her eyes and smiled, as if her mother's pain was a game. From that moment on, Hester never felt safe or at ease with her child, except when Pearl was asleep. Weeks might pass without Pearl looking at the letter, but then she would suddenly stare at it again with that same mysterious smile.

Once, that mysterious look came into Pearl’s eyes while Hester was looking at her own reflection in them. Suddenly, Hester fancied she saw another face in the dark reflection of Pearl’s eye. It was not her own face, but a mischievous, malicious face that looked like someone she knew well, though she had never seen them smile with malice before. It felt as if an evil spirit was peeping out of the child in mockery. Hester was often troubled by this same illusion afterward.

One summer afternoon, after Pearl was old enough to run around, she gathered wild flowers and threw them one by one at her mother’s chest. She danced up and down like an elf whenever she hit the scarlet letter. Hester's first reaction was to cover the letter with her hands. But she stopped herself, choosing to sit pale and still, looking sadly into Pearl's eyes. The flowers kept hitting the mark, causing Hester deep pain. Finally, out of flowers, the child stood and stared at her mother with a mischievous look in her dark eyes. “Child, what are you?” Hester cried. “I am your little Pearl!” the child answered. But as she said it, she laughed and danced around like a little imp.

“Are you really my child?” Hester asked. She did not ask the question completely in jest. Pearl was so intelligent that Hester sometimes wondered if the child knew the secret of her birth. “Yes, I am little Pearl!” the child repeated, continuing to dance. “You are not my child! You are no Pearl of mine,” Hester said half-playfully, as she often tried to joke even in her deepest pain. “Tell me, then, what you are, and who sent you here.”

“Tell me, mother!” the child said seriously, coming close to Hester's knees. “You tell me!” “Your Heavenly Father sent you,” Hester answered. But she hesitated slightly, and the clever child noticed it immediately. Pearl put up her finger and touched the scarlet letter. “He did not send me!” she cried. “I have no Heavenly Father!”

“Hush, Pearl, hush! You must not talk like that!” Hester answered, holding back a groan. “He sent us all into this world. He sent me, and he certainly sent you. If not, you strange child, where did you come from?” “Tell me! Tell me!” Pearl laughed, dancing around the floor. “You must tell me!” But Hester could not answer. She was lost in her own doubts. She remembered how the neighbors rumored that Pearl was a demon child born from sin to serve some wicked purpose. The Puritans often whispered such stories about children born under unusual circumstances, just as old stories claimed that Martin Luther was born from a hellish breed.

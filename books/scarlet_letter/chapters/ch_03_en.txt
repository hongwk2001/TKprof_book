CHAPTER III.

THE RECOGNITION.

Hester Prynne was finally relieved of the intense feeling of being stared at by everyone. On the outer edge of the crowd, she spotted a figure that immediately captured her attention. A Native American man in his traditional clothes was standing there. However, native visitors were common in the English settlements. Under normal circumstances, Hester would not have paid attention to him, let alone let him occupy her entire mind. But next to the Native American man stood a white man, who was clearly traveling with him. The white man wore a strange mix of civilized clothing and native garments.

The white man was short, with a lined face that did not look old yet. His features showed remarkable intelligence. It was clear that he had trained his mind so deeply that it shaped his physical appearance. Although he had tried to hide his shape under his mixed clothing, Hester Prynne could see clearly that one of his shoulders was higher than the other. As soon as she saw his thin face and slight deformity, she squeezed her baby to her chest so hard that the poor child cried out in pain. But the mother did not seem to hear the cry.

When he first arrived in the marketplace, before she even noticed him, the stranger stared at Hester Prynne. At first, his look was casual, like a man who spent most of his time thinking and cared little about the outside world. Very soon, however, his gaze became sharp and focused. A wave of horror twisted across his features, like a snake sliding quickly over his face. His face darkened with a powerful emotion. But he controlled it so instantly by an effort of will that his expression quickly returned to calmness. Soon, the look of shock faded completely into the depths of his nature. When he saw Hester Prynne staring at him and realized she recognized him, he slowly and calmly raised his finger, made a quiet gesture in the air, and placed his finger on his lips.

Then, he touched the shoulder of a town resident standing next to him and spoke in a polite, formal tone. “Excuse me, sir,” he said. “Who is this woman, and why is she standing here on display for public shame?”

“You must be new to this region, friend,” the townsman replied, looking curiously at the stranger and his native companion. “Otherwise, you would surely have heard of Mistress Hester Prynne and her wicked actions. She has caused a massive scandal in holy Master Dimmesdale’s church.”

“You are right,” replied the stranger. “I am indeed a stranger, and I have been a wanderer against my will. I have suffered terrible accidents on sea and land, and I was held captive for a long time by the native tribes to the south. Now this Native American man has brought me here to be freed from my captivity. Please tell me about Hester Prynne—did I say her name correctly? What are her offenses, and what has brought her to that scaffold?”

“Well, friend, after all your troubles in the wilderness, it must make you happy to find yourself in a land where sin is searched out and punished before the rulers and the people, just as it is here in New England. You see, that woman was the wife of a learned Englishman. They lived for a long time in Amsterdam, but he decided to cross the ocean and live with us here in Massachusetts. He sent his wife ahead of him while he stayed behind to take care of some business. But in the two years that she has lived here in Boston, we have heard no news from this learned gentleman, Master Prynne. His young wife, left alone and guided only by herself...”

“Ah, I see,” said the stranger with a bitter smile. “A learned man should have learned to expect this from his books. And tell me, sir, who is the father of the baby she is holding in her arms? The child looks to be about three or four months old.”

“To tell you the truth, friend, that matter remains a mystery. We are still waiting for someone who can solve it. Hester absolutely refuses to speak, and the magistrates have met in vain. Perhaps the guilty man is standing in the crowd right now, looking at this sad sight, unknown to anyone, and forgetting that God sees him.”

“The learned husband,” the stranger remarked with another smile, “should come here himself to look into the mystery.”

“He certainly should, if he is still alive,” the townsman agreed. “Our Massachusetts magistrates took into account that this woman is young and beautiful, and was likely strongly tempted to commit this sin. Since it is highly probable that her husband is at the bottom of the sea, they decided not to enforce the full severity of our law against her. The normal penalty for this crime is death. But in their great mercy, they sentenced Hester Prynne to stand for only three hours on the scaffold, and then wear a mark of shame on her chest for the rest of her life.”

“A wise sentence!” the stranger remarked, bowing his head seriously. “In this way, she will be a living sermon against sin until that shameful letter is carved onto her tombstone. Still, it bothers me that the partner of her sin does not stand on the scaffold beside her. But he will be known! He will be known! He will be known!”

He bowed politely to the talkative townsman, whispered a few words to his Native American companion, and the two walked away through the crowd. Meanwhile, Hester Prynne stood on her platform, staring fixedly at the stranger. She was so absorbed in looking at him that everything else in the world seemed to vanish, leaving only the two of them. Meeting him face to face in private would have been even more terrifying than this public encounter. Here, she stood in the hot midday sun with her shame exposed. The scarlet letter was on her chest, her baby was in her arms, and a crowd of people stared at her. These features should only have been seen by a quiet fireside at home, or behind a veil at church. Yet, as dreadful as this public exposure was, Hester felt protected by the presence of a thousand witnesses. It was better to stand with a crowd between them than to face him alone. She felt that the public display was a shield, and she dreaded the moment she would have to step down. Lost in these thoughts, she barely heard a voice behind her until it repeated her name several times in a loud, solemn tone that the whole crowd could hear.

“Listen to me, Hester Prynne!” the voice said.

Directly above the platform where Hester stood was a balcony attached to the meeting house. This was where official announcements were made before the town officials, with all the ceremony of those days. Governor Bellingham himself sat there, surrounded by four guards carrying weapons. The elderly governor wore a hat with a dark feather, an embroidered cloak, and a black velvet tunic. His wrinkles showed his long, hard experience. He was a suitable leader for a colony that was built on the serious energy and quiet wisdom of older men, rather than the impulses of youth. The other leaders around him carried themselves with great dignity. They believed their authority came directly from God. They were good, just, and wise men. However, out of all the people in the world, it would have been hard to find a group of men less capable of judging a woman's heart. They could not understand the mix of good and evil within her. Hester seemed to know that any sympathy she might find would come from the warm hearts of the crowd, not these strict leaders. As she looked up at the balcony, she grew pale and trembled.

The voice that called out to her belonged to the famous Reverend John Wilson, the oldest minister in Boston. He was a great scholar and a man with a naturally kind and friendly spirit. However, he had focused so much on his studies that he often hid his kind nature, feeling it was a weakness. He stood there with gray hair showing beneath his cap. His gray eyes, used to the dim light of his study, squinted in the bright sunshine just like Hester's baby did. He looked like one of the dark portraits printed at the beginning of old sermon books. He had no more business meddling with a matter of human guilt, passion, and pain than a portrait would.

“Hester Prynne,” John Wilson said, placing his hand on the shoulder of a pale young man beside him. “I have tried to persuade my young brother here, whose sermons you have listened to. I argued that he should speak with you before God, these wise rulers, and all the townspeople about the gravity of your sin. Since he knows your character better than I do, he is better suited to choose the right arguments. He could use tenderness or fear to overcome your stubborn silence, so you will no longer hide the name of the man who led you into this sin. But he argues that it would violate a woman's nature to force her to reveal her heart's secrets in public. I tried to convince him that the real shame lies in committing the sin, not in confessing it. What do you say, Brother Dimmesdale? Must it be you or I who speaks to this poor sinner's soul?”

A murmur passed among the leaders on the balcony. Governor Bellingham spoke with authority, but also with respect toward the young minister. “Master Dimmesdale,” the Governor said, “the responsibility for this woman’s soul lies largely with you. It is your duty to urge her to repent and confess her sin as proof of her repentance.”

This direct appeal drew the eyes of the entire crowd toward the Reverend Arthur Dimmesdale. He was a young minister who had graduated from a great English university, bringing high scholarship to our wild colony. His eloquence and religious passion already made him highly respected. He had a striking appearance, with a high white forehead, large and sad brown eyes, and a mouth that trembled unless he held it tight. This showed both his nervous sensitivity and his great self-control. Despite his talents and education, he had a worried, startled, and half-frightened look. He seemed like someone who felt lost in the world and could only find peace in private. Therefore, he stayed out of the public eye as much as possible, keeping himself simple and childlike. When he did speak, his thoughts were so fresh and pure that people said he sounded like an angel.

This was the young man the Governor and John Wilson called upon to speak. They commanded him to address the mystery of this woman's soul. The pressure of this situation turned Dimmesdale's face pale and made his lips tremble. “Speak to the woman, my brother,” Mr. Wilson urged. “This is important for her soul, and as the Governor said, it is equally important for your own soul, since you are responsible for her. Urge her to confess the truth!”

Arthur Dimmesdale bowed his head, seemingly in silent prayer, and then stepped forward. He leaned over the balcony, looked directly into Hester's eyes, and spoke. “Hester Prynne, you hear what this good man says, and you see the heavy responsibility I bear. If you feel it will bring peace to your soul, and make your earthly punishment help save you, I charge you to speak the name of the man who sinned and suffered with you. Do not remain silent out of a mistaken pity for him. Believe me, Hester, even if he must step down from a high position to stand beside you in shame, it is far better to do that than to hide a guilty heart for the rest of his life. Your silence only tempts or even forces him to add hypocrisy to his sin. Heaven has given you a public shame so that you can win a victory over the evil inside you and the sorrow outside. Be careful not to deny him this bitter but healing cup of confession, because he may not have the courage to choose it for himself.”

The young pastor’s voice was sweet, rich, deep, and filled with emotion. The feeling in his voice, even more than his words, touched everyone in the crowd and united them in sympathy. Even the baby at Hester’s chest was affected. The child looked at Arthur Dimmesdale and held up its little arms with a soft murmur. The minister's appeal was so powerful that the crowd was sure Hester would reveal the father's name. They also expected that the guilty man himself, wherever he stood in the crowd, would feel forced by his own conscience to step forward and climb the scaffold. But Hester shook her head.

“Woman, do not go beyond the limits of Heaven’s mercy!” Reverend John Wilson called out, more harshly than before. “That little baby has been given a voice to support the advice you have just heard. Speak the name! Confessing the name and showing repentance may help remove the scarlet letter from your chest.”

“Never!” Hester replied, looking not at Mr. Wilson, but directly into the deep, troubled eyes of the younger minister. “It is branded too deeply. You cannot take it off. I only wish I could bear his pain along with my own.”

“Speak, woman!” a cold, harsh voice called out from the crowd near the scaffold. “Speak, and give your child a father!”

“I will not speak!” Hester answered. She turned as pale as death but responded clearly to the voice, which she recognized all too well. “My child must seek a Father in heaven. She will never know an earthly father!”

“She will not speak!” Arthur Dimmesdale murmured. He had been leaning over the balcony with his hand over his heart, waiting for her answer. He now stepped back with a deep sigh. “What wonderful strength and generosity in a woman’s heart! She will not speak!”

Seeing that Hester would not change her mind, the older minister delivered a long sermon about sin to the crowd. He spoke for more than an hour, referencing the shameful letter on her chest. He described the symbol so vividly that the crowd grew terrified, imagining that the red letter was colored by the fires of hell. Meanwhile, Hester Prynne stood on the scaffold with blank eyes, looking tired and indifferent. She had endured all she possibly could that morning. Since she was not the type of person who escapes pain by fainting, her mind went numb to protect itself. The preacher's voice thundered in her ears, but it had no effect on her. During the end of the trial, the baby began to cry and scream. Hester tried to quiet the child mechanically, but she was too numb to feel its distress. With the same cold expression, she was led back to the prison and vanished behind the heavy iron-clamped door. The people who watched her go whispered that the scarlet letter cast a glowing red light along the dark hallway inside.

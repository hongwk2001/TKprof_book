CHAPTER XII.

THE MINISTER’S VIGIL.

Mr. Dimmesdale walked like a sleepwalker, caught in the shadow of a dream. He eventually reached the spot where Hester Prynne had stood in her first hours of public disgrace seven years ago. The platform was black, weather-stained, and worn. The minister went up the steps.

It was a dark night in early May. The sky was covered in thick clouds. If the crowd that had watched Hester's punishment stood there now, they would not have seen him. The town was asleep, so there was no danger of being discovered. The minister could stand there until morning if he wanted. The only risk was that the cold night air might make him sick and ruin his sermon the next day.

Only the eye of God, which had seen him whip himself in his closet, could see him now. He had been driven there by remorse, but his cowardice always held him back from a public confession. A weak man should not carry such guilt. Guilt is for the strong, who can handle it or throw it off. The minister could do neither, and his guilt only led to vain repentance.

Standing on the scaffold, Dimmesdale felt a great horror. He felt as if the universe were staring at a scarlet letter on his naked chest. He felt a sharp physical pain in his heart, and he shrieked aloud. The sound echoed through the hills.

He covered his face, fearing the town would wake and find him there.

But the town did not wake. Some who heard the cry thought it was a bad dream or the sound of witches riding through the air.

Looking out, Dimmesdale saw Governor Bellingham at a window with a lamp. The Governor looked like a ghost in his nightcap and gown. The cry had startled him. At another window, Mistress Hibbins appeared with a lamp, looking sour. She probably thought the noise was made by demons. When she saw the Governor's lamp, she turned hers off and went inside. The Governor also left the window.

Soon, the minister saw a small light approaching. It was Father Wilson carrying a lantern. He was returning from the deathbed of Governor Winthrop, who had died that hour. Dimmesdale imagined calling out to Father Wilson to join him on the scaffold. But he only said it in his mind. Father Wilson walked past without looking up, focused on the muddy path. Once the lantern light faded, Dimmesdale felt the exhaustion of his anxiety.

He began to worry about what would happen in the morning. He imagined the first riser seeing him and bringing a crowd of shocked townspeople to the scaffold. He pictured Governor Bellingham, Mistress Hibbins, Father Wilson, the deacons, and the young women all staring at him in his shame under the morning sun. Overcome by the horror of this image, the minister laughed aloud.

A child's laugh answered him. He recognized Pearl's voice.

“Pearl! Little Pearl!” he called out. Then he whispered, “Hester! Hester Prynne! Are you there?”

“Yes, it is Hester Prynne,” she replied in surprise, walking toward him with Pearl.

Hester explained that she had been at Governor Winthrop's deathbed, measuring him for a shroud, and was walking home.

“Come up here, Hester, you and little Pearl,” the minister said. “You have both stood here before, but I was not with you. Come up, and we will stand together.”

Hester and Pearl went up the steps. The three held hands. As the minister took Pearl's hand, he felt a rush of warmth and energy flow from the mother and child into his cold body.

“Minister!” Pearl whispered.

“What is it, child?” Dimmesdale asked.

“Will you stand here with mother and me tomorrow at noon?” Pearl asked.

“No, my little Pearl,” the minister answered. All his fear of public exposure had returned, and he trembled even as he felt a strange joy. “I will stand with you another day, but not tomorrow.”

Pearl tried to pull away, but the minister held her hand. She asked again if he would promise to hold their hands tomorrow at noon.

“Not then, Pearl,” the minister said, “but another time.”

“What other time?” the child asked.

“At the great judgment day,” the minister whispered. “Then we must stand together before God. But we will not do it in the daylight of this world.”

Pearl laughed.

Suddenly, a bright meteor lit up the sky, illuminating the clouds like a giant lamp. It showed the houses, doorsteps, gardens, and street with the clarity of midday, but with a strange and awful quality. The three stood in this bright light: the minister with his hand over his heart, Hester with her scarlet letter, and Pearl connecting them. Pearl looked at the minister with her usual elvish smile. She pulled her hand away and pointed across the street.

In those days, people saw meteors as messages from God, warning of wars or disease. But the minister, driven by his guilty conscience, saw the meteor as a personal message. He looked up and saw a giant letter “A” written in red light in the sky.

While looking up, the minister realized Pearl was pointing at Roger Chillingworth, who stood near the scaffold. The meteor's light made the doctor look like the devil, standing there with a smile and a scowl. The image was so vivid that it remained in the minister's mind even after the light faded.

“Who is that man, Hester?” Dimmesdale gasped in terror. “I shiver when I look at him. Do you know him? I hate him, Hester!”

Hester remembered her promise to Chillingworth and remained silent.

“I tell you, my soul shivers at him!” the minister muttered. “Who is he? Can you do nothing for me? I have a nameless horror of that man!”

“Minister,” Pearl said, “I can tell you who he is!”

“Quickly, child!” the minister said, leaning down to listen.

Pearl whispered gibberish into his ear.

“Are you mocking me?” the minister asked.

“You were not brave, and you were not true!” the child answered. “You would not promise to hold our hands tomorrow at noon!”

“Pious Master Dimmesdale, can this be you?” Roger Chillingworth called out, walking up to the scaffold. “Scholars often walk in their sleep and need to be looked after. Come, my dear friend, let me lead you home.”

“How did you know I was here?” the minister asked fearfully.

“I knew nothing about it,” Chillingworth replied. “I was caring for Governor Winthrop and was walking home when the light appeared. Come with me, or you will not be able to preach tomorrow. Books trouble the brain, and you should study less to avoid these night fantasies.”

“I will go home with you,” Dimmesdale said. He felt cold and depressed, like someone waking from a nightmare.

The next day was Sunday, and the minister preached a very powerful sermon that brought many souls to God. As he came down the pulpit steps, the sexton handed him a black glove that had been found on the scaffold. The sexton joked that Satan must have dropped it there as a trick, but said a pure hand needs no glove.

“Thank you, my good friend,” the minister said, startled at heart. “Yes, it seems to be my glove.”

“And since Satan stole it, you must handle him without gloves from now on,” the sexton joked. “But did you hear of the sign in the sky last night? A great red letter 'A' was seen, which we interpret to mean 'Angel.' Since Governor Winthrop died last night, it was fitting that a sign should appear.”

“No,” the minister answered, “I had not heard of it.”

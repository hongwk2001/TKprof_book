CHAPTER IV.

THE INTERVIEW.

When Hester Prynne returned to the prison, she was in a state of nervous excitement. The guards had to watch her constantly to prevent her from harming herself or her baby. As night approached, her anger could not be quieted by warnings or threats. So Master Brackett, the jailer, decided to bring in a physician. He described the doctor as a man skilled in both European medicine and the forest herbs and roots used by the native tribes. In truth, they desperately needed medical help. While Hester needed attention, the baby was in even greater danger. The child, drinking its mother’s milk, seemed to have absorbed all the distress, pain, and despair that Hester had felt. The baby writhed in convulsions of pain, reflecting in its tiny body the mental suffering that Hester had endured all day.

The stranger with the unusual appearance entered the dark cell right behind the jailer. His presence in the crowd earlier had deeply startled Hester. The authorities kept him in the prison, not because he was suspected of any crime, but as a convenient place to stay while the magistrates negotiated his ransom with the native chiefs. His name was announced as Roger Chillingworth. After showing him into the room, the jailer stood there for a moment, surprised by the quiet that followed. Hester Prynne had immediately become as still as death, though the baby continued to moan.

“Please, friend, leave me alone with my patient,” the doctor said. “Trust me, jailer, you will soon have peace in this prison. I promise you that Hester Prynne will be much more cooperative than she has been so far.”

“Well, if you can do that, sir,” Master Brackett replied, “I will say you are a man of true skill! The woman has been acting like she is possessed. I was almost ready to whip the devil out of her myself.”

The stranger entered the room with the quiet manner of a doctor. His behavior did not change when the jailer left, leaving him face to face with Hester. Her intense reaction in the crowd had shown how close their relationship was. He focused first on the child. The baby lay crying and twisting in pain on the small bed. It was absolutely necessary to soothe the child before doing anything else. He examined the infant carefully, and then opened a leather case from under his clothes. The case contained medical mixtures, and he mixed one of them into a cup of water.

“My old studies in science,” he remarked, “along with my time living with native people who know the healing properties of herbs, have made me a better doctor than many who hold medical degrees. Here, woman. The child is yours. She is not mine, and she will never recognize my voice or face as her father's. Give her this medicine yourself.”

Hester pushed the medicine away, looking at him with deep fear. “Would you take revenge on an innocent baby?” she whispered.

“Foolish woman!” the physician replied, speaking half coldly and half gently. “Why would I want to harm this poor, miserable child? The medicine is very good. Even if this were my own child, I could do no better for it.”

Since Hester still hesitated in her state of panic, he took the baby in his arms and gave her the medicine himself. The medicine worked quickly, proving the doctor's promise. The baby's moaning subsided and she stopped twisting in pain. In a few moments, she fell into a deep, peaceful sleep. The physician next turned his attention to the mother. He calmly felt her pulse and looked into her eyes. His stare made Hester's heart shrink and shudder, because it was so familiar, yet so cold and strange. Finally, satisfied with his examination, he mixed another medicine.

“I do not have magical potions to make you forget,” he remarked. “But I have learned many secrets in the wilderness. Here is a recipe that a native teacher taught me in exchange for some lessons of my own. Drink it. It may be less soothing than a clear conscience, which I cannot give you. But it will calm your wild passion, like oil poured onto a stormy sea.”

He handed the cup to Hester. She took it, looking slowly and earnestly at his face. Her look was not quite one of fear, but it was filled with doubt and questions about his motives. She also looked at her sleeping baby. “I have thought of death,” she said. “I have wished for it. I would have prayed for it, if someone like me could pray for anything. But if death is in this cup, I ask you to think again before you watch me drink it. See, it is at my lips right now.”

“Drink, then,” he replied with the same cold calmness. “Do you know me so little, Hester Prynne? Are my plans ever that simple? Even if I wanted revenge, the best thing I could do is let you live. I want to give you medicine to protect your life so that this burning shame can continue to blaze on your chest.” As he spoke, he placed his long finger on the scarlet letter. It felt as if it burned into Hester’s chest like a red-hot iron. He noticed her flinch and smiled. “So, live. Carry your doom with you in the eyes of men and women, in the eyes of the man you called your husband, and in the eyes of that child. Drink this so that you can live.”

Without any more arguments or delay, Hester Prynne drank the medicine. The doctor gestured for her to sit on the bed where the child was sleeping, while he took the only chair in the room and sat beside her. She trembled at these preparations. She felt that he had done all he could to ease her physical pain, whether out of kindness, duty, or a refined cruelty. Now he was ready to speak to her as the husband she had deeply and permanently injured.

“Hester,” he said, “I will not ask why or how you fell into this trap of shame. The reason is easy to see. It was my foolishness and your weakness. I was a man of thought, a bookworm in great libraries. I was already growing old, having given my best years to the pursuit of knowledge. I had no business marrying someone as young and beautiful as you. I was deformed from birth. How could I trick myself into thinking that my intelligence could hide my physical shape in a young girl's eyes? People call me wise. If wise men were actually wise in their own lives, I would have foreseen all of this. I would have known that the moment I walked out of the dark forest and entered this Christian settlement, the very first thing I would see would be you, Hester Prynne, standing in shame before the crowd. Indeed, from the moment we walked down the church steps as a married couple, I should have seen this scarlet letter burning at the end of our path.”

“You know,” Hester said, unable to bear this quiet reminder of her shame, “you know that I was honest with you. I did not feel any love for you, and I never pretended to.”

“True,” he replied. “It was my foolishness, just as I said. But up to that point, I had lived in vain. The world had been so cheerless. My heart was like a large, lonely house, cold and without a fireplace. I longed to build a fire there. It did not seem like a wild dream, even though I was old, serious, and deformed, that the simple happiness shared by all mankind could be mine. So, Hester, I welcomed you into the deepest part of my heart. I hoped to find warmth in your presence.”

“I have wronged you deeply,” Hester murmured.

“We have wronged each other,” he answered. “My wrong was the first, when I tied your young youth to my old age. Therefore, since I have studied wisdom, I seek no revenge and plot no evil against you. The scale between us is balanced. But Hester, the man who wronged us both is still alive. Who is he?”

“Do not ask me!” Hester replied, looking firmly into his face. “You will never know!”

“Never, you say?” he replied with a dark, confident smile. “Never know him? Believe me, Hester, there are very few secrets in this world, or in the deep thoughts of men, that remain hidden from a person who devotes himself to solving a mystery. You can hide your secret from the curious crowd. You can also hide it from the ministers and magistrates, just as you did today when they tried to force the name from your heart to place a partner beside you. But I come to this search with different methods than they have. I will seek this man just as I have sought truth in books and science. A deep connection will make me recognize him. I will see him tremble, and I will feel myself shudder suddenly without warning. Sooner or later, he will be mine.”

The wrinkled scholar's eyes glowed so intensely that Hester Prynne pressed her hands over her heart, fearing he might read the secret right there.

“You will not reveal his name? He is still mine,” he continued confidently, as if destiny were on his side. “He does not wear a letter of shame on his clothes as you do, but I will read it on his heart. Yet, do not fear for him. Do not think I will interfere with Heaven’s own punishment, or betray him to the law. Do not imagine that I will plot against his life, or against his reputation if he is a respected man. Let him live. Let him hide behind his public honor if he can. He will still be mine.”

“Your actions are merciful,” Hester said, confused and terrified. “But your words sound like a threat.”

“I must ask one thing of you, my former wife,” the scholar continued. “You have kept the secret of your lover. Keep my secret as well. No one in this land knows me. Do not whisper to any living soul that I was once your husband. I will settle here on this wild edge of the earth. Though I am a wanderer elsewhere, cut off from human interests, I find here a woman, a man, and a child who are closely tied to me. It does not matter whether those ties are of love or hate, right or wrong. You and yours belong to me, Hester Prynne. My home is where you are, and where he is. Do not betray me.”

“Why do you want this?” Hester asked, shrinking from this secret agreement. “Why not announce yourself openly and divorce me at once?”

“Perhaps,” he replied, “because I do not want to face the dishonor of being known as the husband of an unfaithful woman. Or it may be for other reasons. It is enough that I plan to live and die unknown. Let your husband be dead to the world, a man of whom no news will ever come. Do not recognize me by word, sign, or look. Above all, do not whisper this secret to the man you know. If you fail me in this, beware. His reputation, his position, and his life will be in my hands. Beware.”

“I will keep your secret, just as I have kept his,” Hester said.

“Swear it!” he demanded.

And she took the oath.

“And now, Mistress Prynne,” said old Roger Chillingworth, which was the name he would use from then on, “I leave you alone. I leave you alone with your baby and the scarlet letter. How is it, Hester? Does your sentence force you to wear that mark in your sleep? Are you not afraid of nightmares and terrible dreams?”

“Why do you smile at me like that?” Hester asked, troubled by his look. “Are you like the devil that haunts the forest around us? Have you tricked me into an agreement that will ruin my soul?”

“Not your soul,” he answered with another smile. “No, not yours.”

CHAPTER VIII.

THE ELF-CHILD AND THE MINISTER.

Governor Bellingham walked first. He wore a loose gown and a comfortable cap, the kind of clothing elderly gentlemen loved to wear in the privacy of their own homes. He seemed to be showing off his estate and explaining his planned improvements in detail. He wore an elaborate, wide collar beneath his gray beard. It was in the old-fashioned style of King James’s reign, and it made his head look a bit like John the Baptist's head on a platter. His appearance was rigid, severe, and worn with old age. This harsh look did not match the comfortable, luxurious things he had gathered around himself. However, it is a mistake to think that our serious ancestors rejected comfort or luxury when they could get it. They did speak and think of human life as a time of trial and struggle, and they were truly ready to sacrifice their property and lives for duty. Still, they did not feel it was against their conscience to enjoy good things when they were available.

For example, the respected pastor John Wilson never taught such a strict rule. His beard, which was as white as a snowdrift, could be seen over Governor Bellingham's shoulder. Mr. Wilson was suggesting that pears and peaches might eventually grow well in the New England climate. He also thought they might train purple grapes to grow against the sunny garden wall. The old clergyman had been raised in the wealthy Church of England, and he had a long-standing, natural appreciation for good and comfortable things. He could be stern in the pulpit, especially when publicly correcting sins like Hester Prynne's. Even so, his kind and warm private life made people love him far more than any other minister of his time.

Two other guests walked behind the Governor and Mr. Wilson. One was the Reverend Arthur Dimmesdale, who had taken a brief and reluctant part in Hester Prynne’s public disgrace. Walking closely beside him was old Roger Chillingworth, a highly skilled doctor who had settled in the town two or three years earlier. Everyone knew that this learned doctor was also a close friend to the young minister. Dimmesdale's health had suffered severely recently because he worked too hard and sacrificed himself for his church duties.

The Governor walked ahead of his guests and went up a couple of steps. He opened the large hall window and found himself standing right next to little Pearl. The shadow of the curtain fell over Hester Prynne, keeping her partly hidden from view.

“What do we have here?” Governor Bellingham said, looking with surprise at the little red figure in front of him. “I must say, I haven't seen anything like this since my younger days in old King James’s time, when I thought it was a great privilege to attend a court masquerade. A crowd of these little figures used to appear during holidays, and we called them children of the Lord of Misrule. But how did such a guest get into my hall?”

“Yes, indeed!” good old Mr. Wilson cried. “What is this little bird with bright red feathers? I think I have seen figures like this when the sun shines through a stained-glass window, casting golden and red patterns across the floor. But that was back in England. Tell me, little one, who are you? And why did your mother dress you up in this strange way? Are you a Christian child? Do you know your catechism? Or are you one of those mischievous elves or fairies that we thought we left behind in old England, along with other Catholic traditions?”

“I am my mother’s child,” the red figure answered, “and my name is Pearl!”

“Pearl? You look more like a Ruby, or Coral, or a Red Rose at the very least, judging by your color!” the old minister replied. He reached out to pat little Pearl on the cheek, but she pulled away. “But where is your mother? Ah, I see,” he added. He turned to Governor Bellingham and whispered, “This is the same child we were discussing earlier. And look, there is the unhappy mother, Hester Prynne!”

“Is that so?” the Governor cried. “Indeed, we might have guessed that such a child's mother would be a scarlet woman, like the notorious woman of Babylon. But she has arrived at a good time. We will investigate this matter immediately.”

Governor Bellingham stepped through the window into the hall, followed by his three guests.

“Hester Prynne,” the Governor said, looking sternly at the woman wearing the scarlet letter. “There has been a lot of discussion about you lately. We leaders have been debating whether we are doing the right thing by leaving an immortal soul, like the one in your child, under the guidance of someone who has fallen into sin. Speak up, as the child's mother. Do you not think it would be better for your little girl's physical and spiritual well-being if she were taken from you? She could be dressed in plain clothes, disciplined strictly, and taught the truths of heaven and earth. What can you do for the child in this regard?”

“I can teach my little Pearl what I have learned from this!” Hester Prynne answered, pointing her finger at the red symbol on her chest.

“Woman, that is your badge of shame!” the stern magistrate replied. “It is because of the sin that the letter represents that we want to place your child in other hands.”

“Even so,” the mother said calmly, though she grew paler. “This badge has taught me, and continues to teach me every day, lessons that can make my child wiser and better, even if they cannot help me.”

“We will judge carefully,” Bellingham said, “and be sure of what we are doing. Good Mr. Wilson, please examine this Pearl, since that is her name, and see if she has received the Christian upbringing that is proper for her age.”

The old minister sat down in an armchair and tried to pull Pearl between his knees. But the child was not used to being touched by anyone except her mother. She escaped through the open window and stood on the top step outside. She looked like a wild tropical bird with bright feathers, ready to fly away into the sky. Mr. Wilson was quite surprised by this behavior, because he was a grandfatherly man who was usually very popular with children. Still, he tried to go on with his questions.

“Pearl,” he said very solemnly, “you must pay attention to lessons so that, in time, you may have a pure soul. Can you tell me, my child, who made you?”

Pearl knew very well who made her. Hester Prynne had grown up in a religious home, and she had taught her daughter about God soon after Pearl was old enough to talk. Children usually absorb these spiritual truths with great interest, and Pearl had learned a lot in her three years of life. She could have easily passed a test on the New England Primer or the Westminster Catechism, even if she had never seen the actual books. However, a stubborn streak, which all children have, took hold of Pearl at that very moment. She had a double share of this stubbornness, and it made her refuse to speak or say the wrong thing on purpose. She put her finger in her mouth and refused to answer good Mr. Wilson's questions. Finally, the child announced that she had not been made at all, but had been plucked by her mother from the wild rosebush that grew by the prison door.

She probably made up this story because she was standing near the Governor's red roses outside the window, and she remembered the rosebush by the prison door that she had passed on her way here.

Old Roger Chillingworth smiled and whispered something into the young minister's ear. Hester Prynne looked at the doctor. Even though her fate was hanging in the balance, she was shocked to see how much his features had changed since she last knew him. He looked much uglier, his dark skin seemed even darker, and his figure was more deformed. She met his eyes for a second, but she had to turn her attention back to what was happening.

“This is terrible!” the Governor cried, slowly recovering from his shock. “Here is a three-year-old child who cannot even tell us who made her. She must be completely ignorant about her soul, its sinful nature, and her eternal destiny. Gentlemen, I do not think we need to ask any more questions.”

Hester grabbed Pearl and pulled her tightly into her arms. She stared at the old Puritan magistrate with a fierce expression. She was alone in the world and cast out by society, and this child was the only treasure she had to keep her heart alive. She felt she had a natural right to her child that the world could not take away, and she was ready to defend that right to the death.

“God gave me the child!” she cried. “He gave her to replace everything else that you took from me. She is my happiness, but she is my torture too. Pearl keeps me alive, but she also punishes me. Don't you see that she is the living version of the scarlet letter? I can love her, and that gives her a million times more power to punish me for my sin. You shall not take her from me. I will die first!”

“My poor woman,” the old minister said, not unkindly, “the child will be well cared for, far better than you can do it yourself.”

“God gave her to me to take care of,” Hester Prynne repeated, raising her voice to a shriek. “I will not give her up!” By a sudden impulse, she turned to the young minister, Mr. Dimmesdale. Up to this point, she had barely looked at him. “Speak for me!” she cried. “You were my pastor, and you had charge of my soul. You know me better than these men do. I will not lose my child. Speak for me. You understand the feelings that these other men lack. You know what is in my heart, and you know a mother's rights. You know how much stronger those rights are when she has nothing but her child and the scarlet letter. Do something. I will not lose my child. Take care of this!”

The young minister came forward at this wild and unusual appeal, which showed that Hester was driven almost to madness by her situation. He was pale and held his hand over his heart, which was his habit whenever he felt anxious or agitated. He looked more worn and thin now than he did during Hester's public disgrace. Whether it was due to failing health or some other cause, his large, dark eyes showed a deep world of pain.

“There is truth in what she says,” the minister began. His voice was sweet, shaking, yet powerful. It echoed through the hall, making the hollow armor ring. “There is truth in what Hester says and in the feelings that drive her. God gave her the child. He also gave her an instinctive understanding of the child's unique nature and needs, which no other person could have. Furthermore, is there not a sacred quality to the bond between this mother and her child?”

“Yes, how is that, good Mr. Dimmesdale?” the Governor interrupted. “Please explain that clearly.”

“It must be so,” the minister continued. “If we think otherwise, we would be saying that God, the Creator of everyone, does not care about sin. We would be saying that He ignores the difference between sinful lust and holy love. This child, born from her father's guilt and her mother's shame, came from the hand of God. She was sent to work on the heart of the mother who pleads so bitterly to keep her. She was meant to be a blessing, the only blessing in Hester's life. But she was also meant to be a punishment, just as the mother told us. She is a source of torture to be felt at unexpected moments, a sting and a constant agony in the middle of a troubled joy. Hester has even expressed this idea in the child's clothing, which reminds us so strongly of the red symbol on her chest.”

“Well said!” good Mr. Wilson cried. “I was afraid the woman only wanted to turn her child into a showpiece!”

“Oh, no, that is not the case,” Mr. Dimmesdale continued. “Believe me, she recognizes the solemn miracle that God has performed in this child's existence. I believe this gift was given above all to keep the mother's soul alive. It protects her from falling into even darker sins that Satan might tempt her with. Therefore, it is good for this poor, sinful woman to have an immortal soul trusted to her care. This child can experience eternal joy or sorrow. Hester can train her to do right. The child will remind her of her fall at every moment, but she is also a promise from the Creator. If Hester can guide her child to heaven, the child will bring her there too. In this way, the sinful mother has a better chance at salvation than the sinful father. For Hester's sake, and for the child's sake, let us leave them together as God has intended.”

“You speak with unusual earnestness, my friend,” old Roger Chillingworth said, smiling at him.

“And there is great meaning in what our young brother has said,” the Reverend Mr. Wilson added. “What do you say, honored Governor Bellingham? Has he not argued well for the poor woman?”

“Yes, he has,” the magistrate answered. “His arguments are strong enough that we will leave the matter as it is, at least as long as the woman causes no further scandal. Still, we must make sure the child is regularly tested on her catechism, either by you or Mr. Dimmesdale. Also, when she is old enough, the local officials must make sure she goes to both school and church services.”

When the young minister finished speaking, he stepped back a few paces and stood with his face partly hidden in the heavy curtain of the window. The shadow of his body on the floor trembled from the intensity of his speech. Pearl, the wild and unpredictable child, walked softly toward him. She took his hand in both of her own and laid her cheek against it. The gesture was so quiet and tender that Hester, who was watching, asked herself if that was really her Pearl. Yet Hester knew there was love in the child's heart, even if it usually showed as passion. Rarely had Pearl been so gentle in her life. The minister looked down. He laid his hand on the child's head, hesitated for a moment, and kissed her forehead. A child's spontaneous affection is sweet, as if it shows we are truly worthy of love. Pearl’s unusual moment of tenderness did not last long. She laughed and skipped down the hall so lightly that Mr. Wilson wondered if her feet even touched the floor.

“The little thing has witchcraft in her, I declare,” Mr. Wilson said to Mr. Dimmesdale. “She doesn't need an old witch's broomstick to fly!”

“A strange child!” Roger Chillingworth remarked. “It is easy to see the mother's influence in her. Do you think, gentlemen, that a wise man could analyze the child's nature and make a good guess at who her father is?”

“No, it would be sinful to use human science for such a question,” Mr. Wilson said. “It is better to fast and pray about it. Perhaps it is best to leave the mystery alone unless God chooses to reveal it himself. That way, every good Christian man has a right to show a father’s kindness to the poor, abandoned child.”

With the matter settled, Hester Prynne and Pearl left the house. As they walked down the steps, a window was opened. Mistress Hibbins, Governor Bellingham’s bitter sister, looked out into the sunny day. A few years later, she would be executed as a witch.

“Hey there!” she called out. Her dark look seemed to cast a shadow over the bright house. “Will you come with us tonight? There will be a lively gathering in the forest, and I almost promised the Devil that the pretty Hester Prynne would join us.”

“Please give him my excuses!” Hester answered with a triumphant smile. “I must stay home and watch over my little Pearl. If they had taken her from me, I would have gladly gone with you into the forest and signed my name in the Devil's book, using my own blood!”

“We will have you there soon enough!” the witch-lady said, frowning as she pulled her head back inside.

If this conversation actually happened, it showed the truth of the young minister's argument. Separating a mother from the child of her sin would only drive her to evil. Even this early, the child had saved her mother from the Devil's trap.

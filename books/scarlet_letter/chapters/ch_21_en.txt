CHAPTER XXI.

THE NEW ENGLAND HOLIDAY.

Early in the morning of the day when the new governor was to take office, Hester Prynne and little Pearl entered the marketplace. The square was already crowded with townspeople and craftsmen. There were also many rough-looking men dressed in deerskins, showing they had traveled from the nearby forest settlements.

On this holiday, as she had for the last seven years, Hester wore a coarse gray dress. The color and simple design of the dress made her blend into the background. However, the bright scarlet letter on her chest drew everyone's eyes back to her, highlighting her shame. Her face showed a quiet, marble-like calmness. It looked like a mask, or even the frozen face of a dead woman. This cold appearance matched her actual state, since she was dead to the sympathy of the community and lived apart from the society around her.

A very close observer might have noticed a tiny change in her expression on this day. Anyone who could read her heart would see that after enduring the town's stares for seven years as a duty and penance, she was facing them voluntarily for one last time. She was turning her long suffering into a quiet victory. She was silently telling the crowd to take their last look at the scarlet letter and its wearer. Soon, she would be out of their reach. In a few hours, the ocean would hide the burning symbol forever. It would also be natural if Hester felt a touch of regret as she prepared to leave the pain that had defined her life for so long. She had lived with bitterness for so many years that she was used to its taste. Her new life would have to be very rich and happy to make her forget the bitter years she had left behind.

Pearl was dressed in bright, airy clothing. It was hard to believe that this cheerful child had come from the same sad, gray mother. The beautiful dress suited Pearl perfectly, looking like a natural part of her character, just like the colors on a butterfly's wing or the petals of a flower. Her outfit perfectly matched her wild nature. On this important day, Pearl was also feeling very excited and restless. She was like a sparkling diamond that flashed with every movement. Children always sense the tension and changes in their parents' lives. Pearl, who was the jewel on her mother's troubled chest, showed her excitement through her dancing movements, revealing the feelings that Hester hid behind her calm face.

This energy made her flit about like a bird instead of walking quietly next to Hester. She kept making wild, happy, and sometimes loud cries. When they reached the marketplace, she became even more active. She noticed all the bustle around the square, which was usually very quiet.

"What is this, mother?" she cried. "Why has everyone stopped working today? Is it a holiday for the whole world? Look, the blacksmith has washed his face and put on his Sunday clothes. He looks like he wants to be happy, if someone would only show him how. And there is Master Brackett, the jailer, nodding and smiling at me. Why is he doing that, mother?"

"He remembers you when you were a baby, my child," Hester answered.

"He should not nod and smile at me, that ugly old man," Pearl said. "He can nod at you if he wants, because you are dressed in gray and wear the scarlet letter. But look, mother, there are so many strangers, Indians, and sailors. What are they all doing in the marketplace?"

"They are waiting to see the parade pass by," Hester said. "The governor, the judges, and the ministers will walk past. All the important and good people will march behind the musicians and the soldiers."

"Will the minister be there?" Pearl asked. "And will he hold out both his hands to me, like when you led me to him by the brook?"

"He will be there, child," her mother answered. "But he will not say hello to you today, and you must not say hello to him."

"What a strange, sad man he is," the child said, almost to herself. "At night, he calls us to him and holds our hands on the scaffold. In the deep forest, where only the trees can hear and the sky can see, he sits on the moss and talks with you. He even kisses my forehead so hard that the brook could barely wash it off. But here, in the daylight and among all the people, he does not know us, and we must not know him. He is a strange, sad man, with his hand always over his heart."

"Be quiet, Pearl, you do not understand these things," her mother said. "Do not think about the minister now. Look around and see how happy everyone is today. The children have left their schools, and the adults have left their work to celebrate. A new governor is starting to rule today, and it is the custom to rejoice as if a wonderful new year is beginning."

It was true that the people looked unusually cheerful. During this annual holiday, the Puritans allowed themselves to enjoy whatever public joy and celebration they could tolerate. They managed to clear away their usual gloomy attitude for one day, so they looked no more serious than other communities might look during a time of national sadness.

We might exaggerate the gloom of that era. The people in the Boston marketplace had not been born into this strict lifestyle. They were native Englishmen whose fathers had lived during the bright and joyful Elizabethan era. If they had followed their natural tastes, the New England settlers would have celebrated with bonfires, feasts, and grand parades. They would have mixed fun with solemn ceremonies. The colony tried to recreate some of this old London splendor during the election day celebrations. The founders of the colony, including the statesmen, priests, and soldiers, believed it was their duty to dress in their finest clothes to show the majesty of their office. They all marched in a procession to give dignity to their new government.

On this day, the people were allowed to rest from their hard work, which was usually just as strict as their religion. There were no plays, singers, dancers, or magicians here, as they would have found in England. Such performers would have been banned by the law. Even so, the crowd was smiling and happy. They held sports that they remembered from England to show their strength and courage. There were wrestling matches in different styles and friendly fights with wooden staffs. Two men even started a sword fight on the pillory platform. However, the town officer stopped the fight to the disappointment of the crowd. He did not want a place of punishment used for entertainment.

Still, these early Puritans knew how to celebrate better than their children would. The next generation became even more strict, casting a dark shadow over the country's culture that lasted for centuries. We are still learning how to enjoy ourselves again.

The crowd in the marketplace was mostly dressed in gray, brown, or black, but there were some colorful groups. A group of Indians stood apart in their decorated deerskins, feathers, and paint. They carried bows, arrows, and spears, and their faces were even more serious than the Puritans' faces. But the wildest people in the square were the sailors from the ship in the harbor. They were rough, bearded men with sun-bronzed skin. They wore wide trousers with belts clasped in gold, and they carried long knives and swords. Their eyes had a wild look even when they laughed. They broke the town rules without fear, smoking tobacco right in front of the town officer and drinking wine from their flasks, which they offered to the crowd. During this era, sailors were allowed a lot of freedom, even though many of their actions at sea would be considered piracy today. This crew had likely attacked Spanish ships, a crime that would have been punished by hanging in later times.

The sea was wild and unregulated by laws back then. A pirate could easily retire and become a respected, religious citizen on land. Even active sailors were not avoided by honest merchants. The Puritan elders in their black cloaks and tall hats simply smiled at the sailors' noisy behavior. Nobody was surprised to see Roger Chillingworth enter the marketplace in close conversation with the ship's captain.

The commander of the Bristol ship was the most colorful figure in the crowd. He wore ribbons on his clothes, gold lace on his hat, and a feather in his cap. He had a sword at his side and a scar on his forehead, which he showed off proudly. A local citizen would have been fined or arrested for dressing and acting this way, but it was accepted as part of a sailor's character.

After speaking with Chillingworth, the captain walked through the marketplace. He approached Hester Prynne, who was standing in her usual isolated spot. A small, empty space always formed around Hester in crowds. The townspeople stayed back, partly because of her quiet dignity and partly out of habit. This isolation allowed Hester and the sailor to talk without being overheard.

"So, mistress," the sailor said, "I have to tell the steward to prepare one more cabin than you requested. We will not have to worry about sickness on this voyage. With our own surgeon and this other doctor on board, our only danger will be taking too many pills. We have plenty of medical supplies that I traded from a Spanish ship."

"What do you mean?" Hester asked, hiding her alarm. "Do you have another passenger?"

"Don't you know?" the captain cried. "This doctor, Chillingworth, wants to travel with you. He told me he is part of your group and a close friend of the gentleman you spoke of, the one who is in trouble with the Puritan rulers."

"They know each other well," Hester replied calmly, though she was terrified. "They have lived together for a long time."

The sailor walked away, but Hester looked across the busy marketplace and saw Roger Chillingworth. He was standing in a far corner, smiling at her with a dark, threatening look.

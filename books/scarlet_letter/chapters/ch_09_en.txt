CHAPTER IX.

THE LEECH.

Under the name of Roger Chillingworth, the reader will remember, was hidden another name. The man who wore it had decided that his real name would never be spoken again. He had stood in the crowd and watched Hester Prynne’s public exposure. He was an elderly, travel-worn man who had just come out of the dangerous wilderness. He had hoped to find a warm, cheerful home with Hester, but instead he saw her displayed as a symbol of sin. Her reputation was ruined, and people in the marketplace whispered about her shame. If the news reached her family and old friends, they would only feel the stain of her dishonor. The man whose relationship with Hester had been the most intimate and sacred decided not to claim his place beside her on the platform of shame. Nobody knew his secret except Hester, and he had the power to keep her quiet. He decided to withdraw his name from the world. He vanished from his old life as completely as if he had drowned in the ocean, which is what the rumors claimed. He would find new interests and a new purpose. This purpose might be dark and even sinful, but it would use all his mental strength.

With this plan in mind, he settled in the Puritan town as Roger Chillingworth. He did not need any introduction other than his own intelligence and learning, which were far greater than average. He had studied medicine earlier in his life, so he chose to practice as a physician. The colony welcomed him warmly, because skilled doctors and surgeons were very rare in the settlement. Such professional men rarely shared the religious enthusiasm that drove other emigrants across the Atlantic. They studied the human body so closely that they often lost their spiritual view of life, focusing instead on the complex physical machine. Before Chillingworth arrived, the health of Boston had been in the hands of an elderly deacon who was also an apothecary. This man's piety and holy behavior were his main qualifications, rather than any medical degree. The only other surgeon in town was a barber who occasionally practiced surgery between shaves. Roger Chillingworth was a wonderful addition to this group. He quickly showed his deep knowledge of old-fashioned medical science. In those days, remedies contained many rare and unusual ingredients, mixed together as if they were trying to create the Elixir of Life. During his time as a captive of the Native Americans, Chillingworth had also learned a lot about local herbs and roots. He openly told his patients that these simple natural medicines, taught to him by the native priests, were just as reliable as the European drugs that doctors had spent centuries developing.

This learned stranger lived a very respectable religious life, at least outwardly. Soon after arriving, he chose the Reverend Mr. Dimmesdale as his spiritual guide. The young minister was famous for his scholarship at Oxford. His devoted followers looked at him as a heaven-sent apostle. They believed that if he lived a normal lifespan, he would do great things for the young New England Church, just as the early Church fathers had done for the Christian faith. Around this time, however, Mr. Dimmesdale's health began to fail. People who knew his habits thought his pale cheeks were caused by too much study, his tireless work for his parish, and his frequent fasting. He often stayed awake all night to pray, trying to keep his spiritual mind free from the grossness of the physical body. Some people said that if Mr. Dimmesdale was going to die, it was simply because the world was not worthy of him. The minister himself, with characteristic humility, said that if God chose to take him, it was because he was too unworthy to perform his humble mission on earth. Although everyone had different ideas about the cause of his decline, nobody could deny that he was growing weaker. He became very thin, and his rich, sweet voice had a sad quality that suggested an early death. Whenever he was startled or surprised, he would put his hand over his heart. First his face would flush, and then it would turn pale with pain.

This was the young minister's condition, and it seemed likely that his life would end early when Roger Chillingworth arrived in town. His first appearance in the settlement had a touch of mystery that people easily turned into something miraculous, since nobody knew where he came from. He seemed to drop from the sky or rise up from the earth. Once people knew he was a skilled doctor, they noticed him gathering herbs, wild flowers, roots, and twigs in the forest. He seemed to know the hidden qualities of plants that looked useless to ordinary people. He also mentioned that he had worked with Sir Kenelm Digby and other famous scientists whose discoveries seemed almost supernatural. People wondered why a man of such high standing in the scientific world had come to this wilderness instead of living in a great city. A rumor spread, which some very sensible people actually believed, that God had performed a miracle by flying a famous doctor from a German university through the air and dropping him right at Mr. Dimmesdale’s study door. Wiser people, who knew that God works through natural events rather than dramatic miracles, simply saw the doctor's timely arrival as a sign of divine providence.

This belief grew stronger because the doctor showed a deep interest in the young minister. He joined Dimmesdale's church and tried to win his friendship and trust, respecting the young man's naturally quiet and sensitive nature. Chillingworth expressed great concern about the pastor's health, but he was eager to try to cure him. He felt hopeful about a recovery if they started the treatment early. The church elders, deacons, motherly women, and young ladies of the congregation all urged Dimmesdale to try the doctor's offered skill. But Mr. Dimmesdale gently refused their pleas.

“I do not need medicine,” he said.

Yet the young minister could not deny his illness. Every Sunday, his cheeks looked paler and thinner, and his voice trembled more. It had become a constant habit for him to press his hand against his heart. People wondered if he was tired of his work or if he wanted to die. The older ministers of Boston and the church deacons spoke to Mr. Dimmesdale solemnly about this. They warned him that it was a sin to reject the medical help that God had clearly provided for him. The minister listened in silence, and he finally promised to speak with the doctor.

“If it were God’s will,” the Reverend Mr. Dimmesdale said when he finally asked for Roger Chillingworth’s advice, “I would be happy to let my labors, my sorrows, my sins, and my pains end with me. I would rather let my earthly troubles be buried in my grave, and send my spirit to its eternal home, than have you use your skill to save my life.”

“Ah,” Roger Chillingworth replied with his usual quietness, “that is how a young minister is likely to speak. Young men have not yet taken deep root in the world, so they give up their hold on life very easily. And holy men who walk with God on earth naturally want to leave and walk on the golden streets of heaven.”

“No,” the young minister replied, putting his hand to his heart as a flash of pain crossed his face. “If I were worthier of heaven, I would be more content to stay and work here on earth.”

“Good men always judge themselves too harshly,” the doctor said.

In this way, the mysterious old Roger Chillingworth became the medical adviser to the Reverend Mr. Dimmesdale. The doctor was interested in the disease, but he was also fascinated by the minister's character. These two men of different ages began to spend a great deal of time together. To help the minister's health and let the doctor gather healing plants, they took long walks along the seashore or in the forest. They talked about many things to the sound of the whispering waves and the wind in the treetops. They also visited each other's studies. The minister enjoyed the company of the scientist, recognizing that Chillingworth had a deep intellect and a freedom of thought that could not be found among other ministers. In fact, Dimmesdale was surprised, and almost shocked, to find such broad views in the doctor. Dimmesdale himself was a true priest, deeply religious and respectful of authority. His mind naturally followed the path of his church's creed, and his beliefs grew deeper over time. Under no circumstances would he have been considered a man of liberal views. He needed the structure of his faith to support him, even if it limited his thinking. Still, he felt a quiet pleasure in looking at the universe through the eyes of a different kind of thinker. It was like opening a window to let fresh air into a stuffy study where his life was wasting away among books and dim lamplight. But this intellectual air was too cold to breathe for long. The minister and the doctor would always return to the orthodox beliefs defined by their church.

Roger Chillingworth watched his patient very closely. He observed him in his daily life, following his usual path of familiar thoughts, and also saw how he reacted to new moral ideas that might reveal hidden parts of his character. The doctor felt it was essential to know the man completely before trying to heal his body. Whenever a person has a strong mind and heart, physical diseases are deeply connected to their emotions. In Arthur Dimmesdale, the mind and imagination were very active, and his feelings were intense. His physical illness was likely rooted in his soul. So Roger Chillingworth, acting as a kind and friendly doctor, tried to go deep into his patient’s inner life. He examined his principles, pried into his memories, and tested everything with a cautious touch, like a treasure hunter in a dark cave. Few secrets can escape a skilled investigator who has the opportunity and permission to look for them. A man carrying a secret should especially avoid getting close to his doctor. If the doctor has natural intelligence and intuition, and if he is not too self-centered, he can easily connect his mind with the patient's mind. Before long, the patient will speak aloud things he thought he only kept in his mind. If the doctor receives these confessions quietly, showing understanding with silence or a simple word rather than dramatic sympathy, the patient's soul will eventually open up. It will flow out like a dark but clear stream, bringing all its mysteries into the light of day.

Roger Chillingworth had all of these qualities. As time passed, the two men developed a close relationship. They discussed ethics, religion, public affairs, and human character. They talked about things that seemed personal to them, yet the minister never revealed any secret. The doctor suspected that Dimmesdale had not even told him the true nature of his physical illness. It was a strange and stubborn silence.

After a while, Roger Chillingworth suggested that they move in together, and the minister's friends arranged it. This allowed the doctor to watch the minister's daily life closely. The townspeople were very happy about this arrangement. They believed it was the best thing for the young minister's health, unless he chose to marry one of the many young women in the congregation who admired him. However, there was no sign that Arthur Dimmesdale would ever marry. He rejected all suggestions of marriage, acting as if ministers should remain single. Since he chose to live alone, eating at other people's tables and missing the warmth of his own family, it seemed perfect that this wise, experienced, and kind doctor was always nearby to help him.

The two friends moved into the house of a religious widow. Her house stood near the spot where King’s Chapel was later built. The house sat next to the cemetery, which had originally been Isaac Johnson's land. This location was well suited to bring serious thoughts to both the minister and the doctor. The kind widow gave Mr. Dimmesdale a front room with plenty of sunshine and heavy curtains to block the light when he wanted to rest. The walls were decorated with tapestries showing the biblical story of David, Bathsheba, and Nathan the Prophet. The colors were still bright, making the characters look vivid and dramatic. In this room, the pale minister stacked his library of heavy books, written by church fathers and rabbis, which even Protestant ministers read. On the other side of the house, Roger Chillingworth set up his study and laboratory. It was not a modern laboratory, but it had a distilling apparatus and tools for mixing chemicals and drugs, which the doctor knew how to use well. The two learned men lived in their own spaces but visited each other often, keeping a close eye on each other's work.

The minister's closest friends believed that God had arranged all of this in response to their prayers, hoping to restore the young man's health. But other people in the community began to take a different view of the relationship between Dimmesdale and the mysterious doctor. When ordinary people try to judge things, they are often deceived by appearances. However, when they judge using their instincts and hearts, their conclusions are often very accurate and true. The people in the town could not prove their suspicions about Roger Chillingworth with facts, but they felt uneasy anyway. An old craftsman who had lived in London during a famous murder case thirty years earlier claimed he had seen the doctor there under a different name. He said the doctor had been associated with a famous old magician involved in that murder. Other people whispered that during his captivity, the doctor had learned black magic from Native American priests, who were known for performing strange cures using the dark arts. Many sensible people also noticed that Chillingworth's appearance had changed since he moved in with the minister. At first, his face was calm, thoughtful, and scholarly. Now, there was something ugly and evil in his look, which grew more obvious the more they looked at him. The common people believed that the fire in his laboratory came from hell, and that the smoke was making his face look dark and dirty.

In short, a popular opinion grew that Arthur Dimmesdale was being haunted by the Devil or his messenger in the form of Roger Chillingworth. They believed this evil agent had permission from God to get close to the minister and try to destroy his soul. No sensible person doubted that the minister would win this spiritual battle in the end. The people hoped to see him emerge from the conflict even holier and more glorious than before. Still, it was sad to think of the terrible suffering he had to endure during this struggle.

Unfortunately, the look of gloom and terror in the young minister's eyes showed that the battle was very difficult, and the victory was far from certain.

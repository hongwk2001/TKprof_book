CHAPTER X.

THE LEECH AND HIS PATIENT.

Throughout his life, Roger Chillingworth had been a calm and kind man. Although he did not have warm feelings, he had always been upright and honest. When he began to study the young minister, he believed he was acting with the fairness of a judge who only wanted the truth. He thought he was solving a simple geometry problem, rather than dealing with human emotions and the wrongs done to himself. But as he went on, a terrible fascination took hold of the old man. A fierce necessity grabbed him and never let him go. He dug into the minister's heart like a miner searching for gold, or like a grave digger searching for a buried jewel, though he was only likely to find decay and corruption. It was a dark search, and it threatened his own soul.

Sometimes a blue, threatening light burned in the doctor's eyes, like the reflection of a furnace. The soil he was digging in had encouraged him.

“This man,” Chillingworth said to himself, “looks pure and holy to everyone. But he has inherited a strong animal nature from his parents. Let us dig a little further in this direction.”

The doctor searched deep into the minister's mind. He found many precious qualities, like high goals for his people, a warm love for others, pure thoughts, and a deep religious devotion strengthened by study. But these valuable things were like rubbish to the doctor, who was looking for something else. He would turn back discouraged and start his search in a different direction. He moved as quietly and cautiously as a thief entering a room where a man lies half-awake, planning to steal his most guarded treasure. In spite of his caution, the floor would sometimes creak or his clothes would rustle. The shadow of his presence would fall across his victim. Arthur Dimmesdale, whose sensitive nerves gave him a kind of spiritual intuition, would feel a vague sense that something dangerous to his peace was near him. But Roger Chillingworth also had quick instincts. Whenever the minister looked up startled, the doctor sat there as a kind, watchful, and sympathetic friend.

Dimmesdale might have seen the doctor's true character if his illness had not made him suspicious of everyone. Because he trusted no one, he could not recognize his actual enemy. He kept up their daily meetings, receiving the doctor in his study or visiting the laboratory to watch weeds being turned into powerful medicines.

One day, Dimmesdale sat with his head in his hand, leaning on the window sill. He looked out at the graveyard while Chillingworth examined a bundle of plants.

“Where did you gather those herbs with the dark, flabby leaves, my kind doctor?” the minister asked. He looked at them sideways, because he rarely looked directly at anything these days.

“Right here in the graveyard,” the doctor answered, without stopping his work. “They are new to me. I found them growing on a grave that had no tombstone. These ugly weeds are the only things left to remember the dead man. They grew out of his heart. Perhaps they represent some dark secret that he buried with him, which he should have confessed while he was alive.”

“Perhaps,” Dimmesdale said, “he wanted to confess but could not.”

“Why not?” the doctor asked. “All the powers of nature call for the confession of sin. These black weeds have grown out of a buried heart to show an unspoken crime.”

“That is just a fantasy of yours, my good doctor,” the minister replied. “Only God’s mercy can reveal the secrets buried in a human heart. The guilty heart must hold its secrets until the day when all hidden things are revealed. I do not believe the Bible teaches that this final revelation is meant as a punishment. That would be a shallow view. I believe these secrets are revealed at the end of time to satisfy the minds of all intelligent beings, who will stand waiting to see the problems of life made clear. A knowledge of men’s hearts is necessary to solve those problems. I believe that those who hold these miserable secrets will yield them up at the last day, not with reluctance, but with unspeakable joy.”

“Then why not reveal them now?” Roger Chillingworth asked, looking sideways at the minister. “Why don't the guilty find that relief today?”

“Most of them do,” the minister said, clutching his chest as if he felt a sudden throb of pain. “Many souls have confessed their sins to me, not only on their deathbed, but while they were still healthy and respected. I have seen the immense relief they feel after pouring out their hearts. It is like drawing fresh air after choking on one's own breath. Why should a guilty man keep a secret buried like a corpse in his heart, rather than throwing it out and letting God take care of it?”

“Yet some men still bury their secrets,” the doctor observed.

“True, there are such men,” Dimmesdale answered. “Some are kept silent by their very nature. Others, even though they are guilty, still want to serve God and help their fellow men. They shrink from showing their sins because they know that once they are exposed, they can do no more good in the world. So, they live with unspeakable torment. They walk among us looking as pure as new snow, while their hearts are spotted with sins they cannot escape.”

“These men are only deceiving themselves,” Chillingworth said, speaking with more energy than usual and gesturing with his finger. “They are afraid to face the shame they deserve. They may still have holy impulses, but if they want to glorify God, they should not lift unclean hands to heaven. If they want to serve their fellow men, they should show the power of conscience by humbling themselves. Do you believe that a false appearance can serve God better than the truth? Trust me, these men are fooling themselves.”

“It may be so,” the young minister said, waving the discussion away as if it were not important. He was skilled at escaping topics that made him anxious. “But now, I want to ask my doctor if his care has helped my weak body.”

Before Roger Chillingworth could answer, they heard a child's wild laughter from the graveyard. They looked out the window and saw Hester Prynne and little Pearl walking along the path. Pearl looked beautiful, but she was in a mischievous mood. She skipped from one grave to another. When she reached a flat, stone tomb, she began to dance on it. Her mother told her to behave, but Pearl ignored her. Instead, she gathered prickly burrs from a tall weed growing near the tomb. She arranged these burrs along the lines of the scarlet letter on Hester’s breast, and the burrs stuck to the fabric. Hester did not pull them off.

Roger Chillingworth came to the window and smiled down grimly.

“That child has no respect for authority or human opinion,” he remarked, speaking as much to himself as to the minister. “The other day, I saw her splash the Governor with water at the trough. What is she? Is she entirely evil? Does she feel affection? Does she have any guiding principles?”

“She only has the freedom of a broken law,” Dimmesdale answered quietly, as if speaking to himself. “I do not know if she is capable of good.”

The child must have heard them, because she looked up at the window with a bright, naughty smile. She threw one of the prickly burrs at the minister. The sensitive clergyman shrank back in fear. Seeing his reaction, Pearl clapped her hands in delight. Hester also looked up. All four of them looked at each other in silence until the child laughed and shouted, “Come away, mother! Come away, or the old Black Man will catch you! He has already caught the minister. Come away, or he will catch you too! But he cannot catch little Pearl!”

She pulled her mother away, dancing and skipping among the graves like a creature that had nothing to do with the dead. It was as if she were a new kind of being who had to live by her own laws.

“There goes a woman,” Chillingworth said after a pause, “who has no mystery of hidden sin. Do you think Hester Prynne is less miserable because the scarlet letter is out in the open?”

“I believe so,” the minister answered. “Yet I saw the pain in her face, and I wish I had not. Still, it must be better for the sufferer to show his pain, as Hester does, than to cover it up in his heart.”

There was another pause, and the doctor began arranging his plants again.

“You asked me earlier about your health,” he said.

“I did,” the minister replied, “and I want to hear your honest opinion, whether it means life or death.”

“To speak plainly,” the doctor said, keeping a close eye on Dimmesdale, “your illness is strange. The physical symptoms are clear enough, and I believe a careful doctor could cure you. But the disease itself is something I seem to know, yet cannot fully understand.”

“You speak in riddles, doctor,” the minister said, looking out the window.

“Let me speak more plainly, and I ask your pardon if I offend you,” the doctor continued. “As your friend and your doctor, let me ask: have you told me everything about this illness?”

“How can you doubt it?” the minister asked. “It would be foolish to call a doctor and then hide the pain.”

“Do I know everything then?” Chillingworth asked, staring intensely at the minister's face. “A doctor who only knows the physical symptoms often knows only half the disease. A physical illness is sometimes just a symptom of a spiritual wound. Forgive me, but you are a man whose body is deeply connected to his spirit.”

“Then I do not need to ask any more,” the minister said, rising quickly from his chair. “I assume you do not practice medicine for the soul.”

“A sickness in your spirit immediately shows itself in your body,” Chillingworth continued, standing up to face the pale minister. “How can I heal your body unless you first show me the wound in your soul?”

“No! Not to you! Not to an earthly doctor!” Dimmesdale cried passionately, staring fiercely at Chillingworth. “If my soul is sick, I will commit myself to God. He can cure or he can kill. Let Him do what is right. But who are you to interfere? How dare you stand between a sufferer and God?”

With a wild gesture, the minister rushed out of the room.

“It was good to take this step,” Chillingworth said to himself, watching the minister leave with a grave smile. “I have lost nothing, and we will be friends again soon. But look how passion drives him. He has done a wild thing in the past, in the hot passion of his heart.”

It was not difficult to restore their friendship. After a few hours alone, the minister realized he had let his temper get the better of him. The doctor had done nothing to deserve such anger. Dimmesdale felt remorseful and apologized to his friend, asking him to continue his care. Chillingworth agreed and went on with his treatment. But every time the doctor left the minister's room, a puzzled smile appeared on his face.

“A rare case,” he muttered. “I must look deeper. There is a strange connection between his body and soul. For the sake of science, I must search this matter to the bottom.”

Not long after this, the minister fell into a very deep sleep while sitting in his chair with a large book open before him. His sleep was usually very light, like a bird on a branch. But his mind had withdrawn so deeply into itself that he did not move when Roger Chillingworth entered the room. The doctor walked up to him, laid his hand on his chest, and pushed aside his clothing.

Mr. Dimmesdale shuddered and moved slightly, but did not wake.

After a moment, the doctor turned away.

His face showed a wild mix of wonder, joy, and horror. He felt a ghastly rapture that showed in his ugly figure. He threw his arms toward the ceiling and stamped his foot in triumph. If anyone had seen him at that moment, they would have known how the Devil looks when he wins a precious soul.

But what made the doctor's joy different from the Devil's was the look of pure wonder on his face.

CHAPTER XX.

THE MINISTER IN A MAZE.

The minister walked ahead of Hester Prynne and little Pearl. He looked back, half expecting to see only a faint outline of the mother and child fading into the forest twilight. Such a massive change in his life did not feel real yet. But Hester was still standing there in her gray robe. She stood next to the fallen tree trunk, which had been blown down long ago and covered in moss. That spot had allowed these two fated people, carrying the world's heaviest burden, to sit and find an hour of peace. Pearl was there too, dancing away from the edge of the brook now that the minister had stepped away. She took her usual place by her mother's side. The minister realized he had not simply fallen asleep and dreamed.

To clear his mind of these confusing thoughts, he reviewed the plans that he and Hester had made for their escape. They had decided that Europe, with its crowded cities, offered a much better hiding place than the wilds of America. In America, the only choices were living in an Indian camp or staying in the few tiny European settlements along the coast. Furthermore, the minister's poor health was not strong enough to survive the hardships of the wilderness. His education, talents, and refined personality meant he could only thrive in a civilized society. Fortunately, a ship was currently anchored in the harbor. It was one of those private vessels common at the time, which were not quite pirate ships but sailed with a lot of freedom. The ship had recently arrived from the Spanish Main and would sail for Bristol in three days. Hester Prynne had met the captain and crew through her charitable work. She took it upon herself to secure passage for two adults and a child, keeping their plans completely secret.

The minister had asked Hester exactly when the ship would leave. She told him it would depart on the fourth day. He had thought to himself that this was very fortunate. His reason for thinking so was that he was scheduled to preach the Election Sermon on the third day. This sermon was a major event in the career of a New England minister, and it would be the perfect way to end his public life. He thought that people would at least say he left no duty unfinished or poorly done. It was sad that such a deeply reflective man could deceive himself so completely. This was a pitiable weakness in his character, showing how a subtle moral disease had eaten away at him over the years. A man cannot wear one face in public and another in private for very long without finally forgetting who he really is.

The excitement from his meeting with Hester gave Mr. Dimmesdale unexpected energy, and he walked quickly toward the town. The forest path seemed wilder, rougher, and less traveled than he remembered it. Yet he leaped over muddy spots, pushed through thick bushes, climbed hills, and crossed valleys with an active strength that amazed him. He remembered how slowly he had walked and how often he had stopped to breathe on the same path just two days ago. As he approached the town, the familiar sights looked completely different to him. It felt as if years, rather than days, had passed since he left. The streets and houses were exactly the same, but he felt a strong sense of change. The same was true of the people he met. They were not actually older or younger, but they felt different to him. He felt this strangeness most strongly when he passed his own church. The building looked both so strange and so familiar that he wondered if his whole life had been a dream, or if he was dreaming now.

This feeling of change was entirely within himself. A single day had changed his perspective so deeply that it felt like years had passed. His own will, Hester's will, and their shared fate had transformed him. The town was the same, but he was not the same man who had walked into the forest. He wanted to tell the friends who greeted him that he was not the minister they thought he was. He felt as if he had left his old self in the woods, lying by the mossy tree trunk near the sad brook like a discarded suit of clothes. His friends would have insisted that he was the same person, but they would have been wrong.

Before he reached home, his inner thoughts showed further signs of a revolution. His entire moral code seemed to have changed, giving the minister strange, wild impulses at every step. He felt tempted to do wicked things, acting against his usual habits yet coming from a deep, hidden part of himself. For instance, he met one of his church deacons. The good old man spoke to him with fatherly affection and deep respect, as was fitting for his age and position. During their short conversation, Mr. Dimmesdale had to use extreme self-control to keep from saying blasphemous things about the communion supper. He turned pale and trembled, fearing his tongue would speak these terrible thoughts on its own. Even in his terror, he wanted to laugh at the thought of how shocked the holy deacon would be by his minister's words.

A second incident occurred shortly after. Walking along the street, Mr. Dimmesdale met the oldest woman in his church. She was a pious, poor, and lonely widow whose heart was full of memories of her dead family. Her faith and reading of the Scriptures had turned her heavy sorrow into a quiet joy for over thirty years. Since Mr. Dimmesdale had become her pastor, her greatest comfort was to hear words of Gospel truth from him. But on this day, as he leaned in to speak to her, the minister could not remember a single comforting verse. Instead, he could only think of a short, logical argument against the immortality of the human soul. Whispering this to her would have devastated the old woman, perhaps even killing her from shock. He could never remember what he actually said. There was probably some confusion in his speech that she did not understand, or perhaps God guided her to hear something else. When the minister looked back, he saw an expression of deep gratitude and joy on her wrinkled face.

Then there was a third encounter. He met the youngest female member of his church. She was a young woman who had recently turned to religion after hearing one of his sermons. She was as pure and beautiful as a lily. The minister knew that she held a deep, innocent respect for him in her heart, combining religious devotion with pure affection. It felt as if Satan had led her to him on this afternoon, placing her in the path of this desperate, tempted man. As she approached, a wicked impulse whispered to him to say something that would ruin her innocence and plant a seed of doubt in her mind. He felt a dark sense of power over her trusting soul, feeling that a single bad look or word could destroy her innocence. With a massive struggle, he covered his face with his cloak and hurried past her without a word. The poor girl was left to wonder why he was so rude. She searched her conscience for any tiny mistake she might have made, blaming herself for imaginary faults and crying herself to sleep that night.

Before he could celebrate his victory over that temptation, he felt another impulse that was both ridiculous and horrible. He wanted to stop and teach swear words to a group of young Puritan children playing nearby. Resisting this urge as inappropriate for a minister, he next saw a drunken sailor from the ship in the harbor. Because he had resisted his other urges, Mr. Dimmesdale now wanted to shake hands with the sailor. He longed to trade dirty jokes and shout loud, angry swear words with him. It was his good taste and long habit of acting like a proper minister, rather than his moral principles, that saved him this time.

"What is haunting and tempting me like this?" the minister cried to himself, stopping in the street and hitting his forehead. "Am I losing my mind? Have I given myself over entirely to the devil? Did I sign a contract with him in the forest using my blood, and is he now demanding I fulfill it by suggesting every evil deed possible?"

As Mr. Dimmesdale stood there hitting his forehead, old Mistress Hibbins, the town's reputed witch, passed by. She made a grand appearance in a high headdress and a rich velvet gown. Her collar was styled with the famous yellow starch she had learned to make from her friend Ann Turner, who was later hanged for murder. She stopped and looked closely at the minister, smiling knowingly. Although she rarely spoke to clergymen, she began a conversation.

"So, reverend sir, you have been visiting the forest," the old woman said, nodding her tall headpiece at him. "Next time, please give me some warning, and I will gladly join you. I can promise you that my recommendation will go a long way with the powerful master you met out there."

"I assure you, madam," the minister answered with a formal bow, "I have absolutely no idea what you mean. I did not go into the forest to find any master, and I have no plans to go back there to seek anyone's favor. My only purpose was to visit my holy friend, the Apostle Eliot, and share his joy over the souls he has converted."

"Ha, ha, ha!" the old witch cackled, nodding her headdress. "Well, we must speak this way during the day. You play your part very well. But at midnight in the forest, we will have a different conversation."

She walked away with a dignified air, but she kept turning back to smile at him as if they shared a secret bond.

"Have I really sold myself to the devil?" the minister thought. "Is that the same master this old witch has chosen to serve?"

The miserable minister had indeed made a similar bargain. Tempted by a dream of happiness, he had chosen to yield to what he knew was a deadly sin. The poison of that choice had spread quickly through his soul. It silenced his good impulses and woke up all his bad ones. Bitterness, scorn, a desire to do harm, and a urge to mock holy things all tempted and frightened him. His meeting with Mistress Hibbins, if it really happened, showed his new connection to wicked people and evil spirits.

By this time, he had reached his house next to the graveyard. He hurried upstairs and took shelter in his study. He was relieved to be safe inside without having given away his strange state to the townspeople. He looked around the familiar room at his books, windows, fireplace, and tapestries. Everything looked strange to him now, just as the town had during his walk. In this room, he had studied and written. He had fasted, prayed, and suffered a thousand torments. His Hebrew Bible lay there, representing the word of God. On the table, next to his pen, lay his unfinished Election Sermon, which he had stopped writing two days ago. He knew that he was the same pale minister who had written those pages, but he felt disconnected from that person. That old self was gone. A different, wiser man had returned from the forest, carrying a dark knowledge that his former simple self could never have understood.

While he was thinking about this, someone knocked on the door. The minister said, "Come in," half expecting to see an evil spirit. In a way, he did. Roger Chillingworth entered the room. The minister stood pale and silent, with one hand on his Bible and the other on his chest.

"Welcome home, reverend sir," the physician said. "How was your visit with the Apostle Eliot? You look very pale. Perhaps the long walk through the wilderness was too much for you. Do you need my help to build your strength for the Election Sermon?"

"No, I do not think so," Mr. Dimmesdale replied. "My journey, the visit with the Apostle, and the fresh air have done me a lot of good after being cooped up in my study. I do not think I need any more of your medicine, my good physician, even though you have always offered it with a friendly hand."

Roger Chillingworth watched the minister with the serious gaze of a doctor studying a patient. Despite his calm expression, the minister felt sure that the old man knew or strongly suspected his meeting with Hester. The physician realized that the minister no longer trusted him and saw him as an enemy. Even though they both knew this, they did not say it out loud. People who want to avoid a topic can often talk right around it without ever mentioning it directly. The minister knew Chillingworth would not speak of the secret openly, yet the physician managed to hint at it in his dark way.

"Wouldn't it be better if you let me treat you tonight?" the physician asked. "We must make sure you are strong and healthy for the sermon. The people expect great things from you, especially since they worry that you might not be with them next year."

"Yes, I may be in another world," the minister replied with resignation. "I hope it is a better one, because I do not expect to stay with my congregation for another year. But as for your medicine, I do not need it in my current condition."

"I am glad to hear that," the physician said. "It may be that my remedies are finally working after all this time. I would be very happy, and the people of New England would be grateful, if I could cure you."

"I thank you from my heart, my watchful friend," Mr. Dimmesdale said with a solemn smile. "I can only pay you back with my prayers."

"A good man's prayers are a golden reward," Roger Chillingworth replied as he left. "They are like the gold coins of heaven itself."

Once he was alone, the minister called a servant and asked for food. He ate hungrily when it was brought to him. Then, he threw his unfinished sermon into the fire and immediately began writing a new one. He wrote with such a rush of ideas and emotions that he felt inspired. He could only wonder why heaven would choose to speak through someone as sinful as him. Leaving that mystery aside, he worked through the night with intense focus. The hours flew by quickly. Morning arrived, and the sunrise shone a golden beam of light across his desk. He sat there with his pen in hand, having written a massive amount of pages.

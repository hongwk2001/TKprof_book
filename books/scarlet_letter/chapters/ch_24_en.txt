CHAPTER XXIV.

CONCLUSION.

After some time had passed and the townspeople could process what they had seen on the scaffold, several different explanations arose.

Most witnesses swore they saw a scarlet letter, identical to the one worn by Hester Prynne, marked directly into the minister's flesh. People had various theories about its origin. Some believed that Mr. Dimmesdale had carved it into himself as a form of secret penance starting the day Hester first wore her badge. Others claimed that Roger Chillingworth had caused the mark to appear using magic and poisonous drugs. A third group, who understood the minister's sensitive nature, believed that his intense remorse had caused the symbol to grow outward from his heart. The reader may choose which theory to believe. We have shared all the details we could find, and we are glad to finally lay the story to rest.

Surprisingly, some witnesses who watched the entire event claimed that there was no mark at all on his chest. They also reported that his final words did not confess any connection to Hester's guilt. According to these people, the minister had chosen to die in Hester's arms simply to show that even the most righteous men are weak without God. They believed he wanted to teach his followers that all humans are equally sinful in the eyes of God. While this is an important lesson, this version of the story is likely just a sign of how stubbornly a minister's friends will defend his character, even when the truth is as clear as day.

The old manuscript we have followed, which was written based on the accounts of people who knew Hester, supports the version of events we have told. Among the many lessons from the minister's sad life, we offer this simple rule. Be true. Show your real self to the world, or at least show enough that others can guess your weaknesses.

Immediately after the minister's death, Roger Chillingworth changed completely. All his strength and energy left him. He withered away and shriveled like a weed pulled from the ground. He had built his entire life around revenge. Once his victim was gone, he had no purpose left. Within a year, he died and went to his final rest. It is interesting to consider whether hatred and love are not actually very similar. Both passions require a deep understanding of another person, and both make us dependent on them. In the spiritual world, the old doctor and the minister, who were both victims of each other, may have found their hatred turned into love.

Roger Chillingworth left a large fortune in his will to little Pearl, Hester's daughter. Governor Bellingham and John Wilson managed the estate.

Pearl became the wealthiest heiress in the colony. This fortune changed how people viewed her. If she had stayed, she might have married into one of the most respected Puritan families. However, shortly after Chillingworth's death, Hester and Pearl disappeared. For many years, no one heard any real news of them, and the story of the scarlet letter became a legend. The scaffold and Hester's empty cottage remained landmarks. One afternoon years later, children playing nearby saw a tall woman in a gray robe approach the cottage. She opened the door and went inside.

She paused on the threshold, turning back for a moment as if the memories were too heavy to bear. But she hesitated for only an instant, revealing the scarlet letter on her chest.

Hester Prynne had returned to her old home and taken up her shame once again. Nobody knew for sure what had happened to Pearl. She might have died young, or she might have found happiness as a married woman in Europe. Throughout the rest of Hester's life, she received letters with official seals from another country. She also received comfortable and expensive goods that only wealth could buy. Visitors noticed beautiful ornaments in her cottage, showing that someone loved and remembered her. Once, Hester was even seen embroidering a rich baby blanket with gold thread.

Most people believed that Pearl was alive, married, and happy, and that she had invited her mother to live with her.

But Hester chose to stay in New England. This was where she had sinned and suffered, and this was where she wanted to spend her remaining years. She wore the scarlet letter for the rest of her life. Over time, the symbol ceased to be a source of scorn. Instead, it became a symbol of wisdom and sorrow. People brought their troubles to her, asking for advice. Women especially visited her cottage to share their pain over broken relationships or unrequited love. Hester comforted them and assured them that in the future, a new truth would make relationships happier. She had once hoped to bring this new truth herself, but she realized that such a holy mission must be carried by a pure, happy woman who showed the joy of sacred love.

Hester lived out her days in this manner. When she died, she was buried near an old, sunken grave in the churchyard. The two graves had a small space between them, as if their dust could not mix, yet they shared a single tombstone. It was a simple slate headstone marked with a coat of arms. The motto on the stone served as a description of their sad story: "On a field of black, the red letter A."

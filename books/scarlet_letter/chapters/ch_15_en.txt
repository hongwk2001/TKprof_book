CHAPTER XV.

HESTER AND PEARL.

Roger Chillingworth, a deformed old figure whose face haunted people's memories, said goodbye to Hester Prynne. He walked away, stooping toward the ground to gather herbs and dig up roots for his basket. His gray beard almost touched the earth as he crawled along. Hester watched him go, wondering with a strange curiosity if the new spring grass would wither under his feet, leaving a brown trail across the green field. She wondered what kind of herbs he was gathering. She wondered if the earth would produce new poisonous plants just for him, or if his touch would turn healthy plants into poison. She wondered if the sun really shone on him, or if a shadow followed his deformed body wherever he went. Where was he going now? Would he sink into the ground, leaving a dead spot where only poisonous weeds would grow? Or would he sprout bat wings and fly away, looking uglier the higher he flew?

“Whether it is a sin or not,” Hester muttered, watching him go, “I hate the man!”

She felt guilty for feeling this way, but she could not help it. She remembered the old days in England when he would leave his study at night to sit with her by the fire. He used to say he needed her smile to warm his cold heart after studying for so long. Those memories used to seem happy, but now they felt ugly. She wondered how she could have married him. She felt that her greatest mistake was allowing him to hold her hand and letting herself smile at him. It seemed a worse crime for Chillingworth to persuade her that she could be happy with him when she was too young to know better.

“Yes, I hate him,” Hester repeated with more anger. “He betrayed me. He did a worse wrong to me than I did to him.”

Men should be careful when they marry a woman, and they should make sure they win her whole heart. Otherwise, they might share Chillingworth's fate. When a stronger love finally awakens the woman's feelings, she will resent the cold imitation of happiness she was forced to accept. But Hester should have let go of this anger long ago. Her seven years of suffering with the scarlet letter had not yet cured her.

Her thoughts as she watched Chillingworth showed how troubled her mind still was.

Once the doctor was gone, she called her child back.

“Pearl! Little Pearl! Where are you?”

Pearl had kept herself busy while her mother talked to the old man. First, she played with her reflection in a pool, trying to coax the image out. When that did not work, she looked for a way to enter the water. Soon, she realized the image was not real, so she looked for other games. She built little boats out of birch bark and loaded them with snail shells. She launched them into the ocean, though most sank near the shore. She caught a horseshoe crab by its tail, found some starfish, and left a jellyfish to melt in the sun. Then, she threw sea foam into the air, chasing it like snowflakes. She saw a flock of shorebirds feeding near the water, filled her apron with pebbles, and chased them from rock to rock. She was very good at throwing. She thought she hit one gray bird, which flew away with a hurt wing. The little girl felt sad and stopped her game because she did not want to hurt a creature as wild as herself.

Finally, she gathered seaweed of different colors to make a scarf, a cape, and a headdress. She looked like a little mermaid. She had her mother's talent for designing clothes. To finish her outfit, Pearl used green eelgrass to make a letter "A" on her own chest, imitating the scarlet letter she saw on her mother. She looked down at it with great interest, as if she were born to understand what the symbol meant.

“I wonder if mother will ask me what it means,” Pearl thought.

Just then, she heard her mother call. She ran back like a seabird, dancing and laughing as she pointed to the green letter on her chest.

“My little Pearl,” Hester said after a moment, “that green letter on your chest has no meaning. But do you know what the letter on my chest means?”

“Yes, mother,” the child said. “It is the letter A. You taught it to me from my spelling book.”

Hester looked at her closely. Pearl had a strange look in her dark eyes, and Hester could not tell if the child understood the symbol's true meaning. She felt a strong desire to find out.

“Do you know why I wear this letter, Pearl?”

“Yes, I do,” Pearl answered, looking up. “It is the same reason the minister keeps his hand over his heart.”

“And what is that reason?” Hester asked. She tried to smile at the strange answer, but she turned pale. “What does my letter have to do with the minister's heart?”

“That is all I know,” Pearl said seriously. “Ask the old man you were talking to. He might know. But tell me, mother, what does the scarlet letter really mean? Why do you wear it? And why does the minister always keep his hand over his heart?”

She took her mother's hand and stared into her eyes with unusual seriousness. Hester thought the child might be trying to connect with her in a loving, trusting way. Pearl had always been wild and unpredictable, like a spring breeze. Hester loved her deeply but never expected much affection in return. The child would usually play and shout, occasionally kissing her mother's cheek before running off again. But now, Hester thought Pearl was old enough to be a friend and confidant. She saw that Pearl possessed courage, a strong will, pride that could become self-respect, and a hatred of lies. She also had deep feelings, even if they were still undeveloped. Hester believed that Pearl could grow into a noble woman despite her wild nature.

Pearl's interest in the scarlet letter seemed to be a natural part of her character. She had been focused on it since she was a baby. Hester had always thought God designed this to punish her, but now she wondered if it was meant to help her instead. Perhaps Pearl was meant to comfort her mother's cold heart and help her overcome her past passion.

These thoughts ran through Hester's mind. Meanwhile, Pearl held her hand, looked up, and asked the same questions again.

“What does the letter mean, mother? Why do you wear it? And why does the minister keep his hand over his heart?”

Hester wondered what she should say. She decided she could not tell the truth, even if it meant losing her daughter's sympathy.

“Silly Pearl,” Hester said aloud, “what kind of questions are these? Children should not ask about such things. I know nothing about the minister's heart. And I wear the scarlet letter simply for its gold thread.”

In seven years, Hester had never lied about the symbol on her chest. It was as if her guardian spirit left her in that moment, realizing that a new lie had entered her heart. Pearl's serious expression quickly vanished.

But the child did not let the subject go. On the walk home, at supper, and while she was going to bed, Pearl kept asking with a mischievous look in her eyes.

“Mother, what does the scarlet letter mean?”

The next morning, as soon as she woke up, Pearl popped her head up from the pillow and asked the other question.

“Mother, why does the minister keep his hand over his heart?”

“Be quiet, you naughty child,” Hester answered with an anger she had never shown before. “Do not tease me, or I will lock you in the dark closet.”

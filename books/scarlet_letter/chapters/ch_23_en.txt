CHAPTER XXIII.

THE REVELATION OF THE SCARLET LETTER.

The minister's eloquent voice finally fell silent, ending the sermon that had deeply moved the crowd. A brief, profound silence followed his words. Then a quiet murmur spread through the audience. The people felt as if they were waking up from a spell. Soon, the crowd began to pour out of the church doors, returning to their everyday lives.

Once outside, the crowd began praising the minister. The streets and marketplace echoed with their applause. Everyone agreed that no one had ever spoken with such wisdom, holiness, and inspiration. The speaker seemed to be possessed by a higher power that led him beyond his written notes, filling him with wonderful ideas. His sermon focused on the relationship between God and humanity, specifically describing the future of New England. Near the end, he spoke like a prophet, predicting a bright and glorious future for the colony. Yet the entire speech carried a sad, painful undertone. The listeners felt it was the natural sadness of a man who knew he was dying. They believed their beloved minister had a premonition of his death and would soon leave them. This thought made the sermon even more powerful.

Mr. Dimmesdale had reached the most successful moment of his career. He stood at the peak of respect, admired for his intelligence, learning, eloquence, and holy reputation. As the sermon ended, he bowed his head on the pulpit cushions. Meanwhile, Hester Prynne stood by the scaffold, the scarlet letter still burning on her chest.

The music began again, and the soldiers marched out of the church doors. The parade was to walk to the town hall for a banquet. The leaders of the colony walked through the crowd, and the people stepped back respectfully to let them pass. When they reached the marketplace, the crowd erupted into a massive shout of enthusiasm. The shout was fueled by the powerful emotions from the sermon. The sound rose into the sky, louder than thunder or the roar of the sea. It was the loudest shout of joy ever heard in New England, and the minister was the most honored man in the colony.

But how was the minister doing? Although the crowd admired him like a saint, he was in a terrible state. As the procession moved forward, all eyes turned to him. The cheers quickly turned to worried whispers as the people saw him. He looked pale and incredibly weak. The energy that had supported him during his sermon was gone now that his duty was done. His face had a deathly color, and he tottered weakly along the path, barely able to stand.

The old minister, John Wilson, stepped forward to offer his arm. Mr. Dimmesdale trembled but firmly pushed the old man's support away. He kept walking slowly, looking like a baby trying to reach its mother's arms. Finally, he reached the dark wooden scaffold where Hester Prynne had stood years ago. Hester was standing there now, holding Pearl's hand. The minister stopped, even though the festival music urged him to keep walking.

Governor Bellingham noticed his weakness and stepped forward to help him. However, something in the minister's look made the governor stop. The crowd watched with wonder, believing his weakness was a sign of his holy devotion. Some half expected him to ascend directly into heaven.

He turned toward the scaffold and held out his arms.

"Hester," he said, "come here. Come, my little Pearl."

His expression was pale and terrible, yet it looked tender and triumphant. Pearl ran to him and wrapped her arms around his knees. Hester slowly approached, as if pulled by fate, but stopped in front of him. At that moment, Roger Chillingworth pushed through the crowd. He looked dark and angry, trying to stop the minister. He rushed forward and grabbed Arthur's arm.

"Stop, madman, what are you doing?" Chillingworth whispered. "Send the woman away. Cast off this child, and all will be well. Do not destroy your reputation and die in dishonor. I can still save you. Would you bring shame on your office?"

"Tempter, you are too late," the minister answered, looking at him with fear but determination. "Your power over me is gone. With God's help, I will escape you now."

He held out his hand to Hester again.

"Hester Prynne," he cried out, "in the name of God, who gives me the strength at this last moment to do what I avoided doing seven years ago, come here. Help me. Let your strength support me, guided by God's will. This wronged old man is trying to stop me with all his power. Come, Hester, support me as I climb the scaffold."

The crowd was confused. The leaders stood in silence, watching what was happening. They saw the minister lean on Hester's shoulder and climb the scaffold steps, holding little Pearl's hand. Roger Chillingworth followed them closely.

"If you had searched the whole earth," Chillingworth said darkly, "there was no place where you could have escaped me, except on this scaffold."

"Thanks be to God who led me here," the minister answered.

He trembled and looked at Hester with a worried smile. "Is this not better than our plans in the forest?" he murmured.

"I do not know," she replied quickly. "Is it better to die together like this?"

"For you and Pearl, let God decide," the minister said. "God is merciful. Let me do what he has commanded. I am dying, Hester, so let me confess my shame."

Supported by Hester and holding Pearl's hand, Mr. Dimmesdale turned to the judges, the ministers, and the crowd. The people watched in shocked silence, filled with sympathy as they realized a major secret was about to be revealed. The sun shone directly on the minister as he prepared to plead guilty.

"People of New England!" he cried, his voice rising solemn and powerful, yet trembling with remorse. "You who have loved me and thought I was holy, look at me now. I am the greatest sinner in the world. At last, I stand on the spot where I should have stood seven years ago. I stand here with this woman, whose arm keeps me from falling on my face. Look at Hester's scarlet letter. You have all shuddered at it. But there was someone else among you who carried a mark of sin, and you did not suspect him."

He seemed about to collapse, but he fought back his weakness. He stepped away from Hester and Pearl and walked forward a pace.

"His mark was hidden from men!" he shouted with determination. "God saw it, the angels knew it, and the devil touched it with his burning finger. But he hid it from you and walked among you like a pure spirit. Now, in his final hour, he stands before you. Look at Hester's letter. He tells you it is only a shadow of what he bears on his own chest. His own red mark is a symbol of what has burned his heart. Does anyone here doubt God's judgment? Look at this witness!"

With a sudden movement, he tore open his shirt to reveal his chest. The crowd stared in horror at what they saw. The minister stood there with a look of triumph, like a man who had won a great victory through extreme pain. Then he collapsed onto the scaffold. Hester held him, resting his head on her chest. Roger Chillingworth knelt next to him, looking defeated.

"You have escaped me," the old man repeated. "You have escaped me."

"May God forgive you," the minister said. "You have also sinned deeply."

He looked away from Chillingworth and turned to Hester and Pearl.

"My little Pearl," he said weakly, a gentle smile appearing on his face. "Will you kiss me now? You refused to kiss me in the forest, but will you now?"

Pearl kissed his lips, and a spell seemed to break. The shared sorrow finally awakened the child's sympathy. Her tears fell on her father's cheek, showing that she would grow up to experience normal human joy and sadness, and live a peaceful life as a woman. Her role as a source of pain for her mother was finished.

"Hester," the minister said, "goodbye."

"Won't we meet again?" she whispered, leaning close to him. "Won't we spend our eternal life together? Surely we have paid for our happiness with all this suffering. You are looking into eternity with your dying eyes. Tell me what you see."

"Shh, Hester, quiet," he said solemnly. "Think only of the law we broke and the sin we have confessed. I fear that when we forgot God and violated the purity of each other's souls, we lost the hope of meeting in a pure reunion. God knows, and he is merciful. He has shown his mercy through my suffering, by giving me this burning pain on my chest, by letting Chillingworth keep the torture alive, and by bringing me here to confess my sin before I die. If I had not suffered these things, I would have been lost forever. Praised be his name. Goodbye."

With that final word, the minister breathed his last. The silent crowd let out a deep, heavy murmur of awe that echoed through the square.

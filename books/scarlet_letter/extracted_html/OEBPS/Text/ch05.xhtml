Chapter V: Hester at Her Needle

Hester Prynne’s time in prison was finally over. The prison door was thrown open, and she stepped out into the sunshine. The sun shone on everyone alike, but to Hester’s sad heart, the light seemed to exist only to reveal the scarlet letter on her chest. Walking away from the prison threshold alone for the first time was perhaps even more painful than the public display she had just endured. During that public trial, she had been supported by a state of nervous tension and her own combative energy. This had allowed her to turn the shameful scene into a sort of dark triumph. That display was also a single, unique event in her life. She could call upon all her inner strength to face it, knowing it would happen only once. Even the law that condemned her, like a stern giant with an iron arm, had supported her through the ordeal. But now, as she walked away from the prison door alone, her daily life began. She had to survive it using her ordinary daily strength, or sink under the weight. She could no longer rely on temporary energy to get through her current grief. Tomorrow would bring its own trials, and so would the next day, and the next. Each day would bring the same heavy burden. The years ahead would pass slowly, bringing the same weight for her to carry and never lay down. As the days accumulated, her misery would pile up. She would lose her individuality and become a symbol for preachers and moralists to point at, representing human weakness and sinful passion. Young and pure people would be taught to look at her, with the scarlet letter burning on her chest. They would see a daughter of honorable parents, the mother of a child, a woman who was once innocent, as the very symbol and reality of sin. And over her grave, the shame she carried would be her only monument.

It may seem strange that Hester chose to stay in this remote Puritan settlement. No rule in her sentence forced her to remain. She was free to return to her birthplace in England, or to any other European land. There, she could have hidden her identity under a new name and started a completely new life. She also could have walked into the dark forest, where she could live among native tribes whose customs knew nothing of the law that had condemned her. Yet, she chose to call this place her home, even though it was the only place where she was marked with shame. But there is a powerful feeling, like a sense of doom, that compels human beings to linger around the place where a major event has shaped their lives. This feeling is even stronger when the event is dark and sad. Hester’s sin and shame were the roots she had planted in this soil. It was as if a new birth had tied her to this wild land, making it her dreary, lifelong home. All other places on earth, even the English village of her happy childhood, felt foreign to her now. The chain that bound her to Boston was made of iron, and although it hurt her soul, it could never be broken.

There was another feeling that kept her here, though she hid the secret from herself and grew pale whenever she thought of it. In this town lived the man she felt connected to in a secret union. Although this union was unrecognized on earth, she believed it would bring them together before God's final judgment. She imagined that judgment day would be their marriage altar, bringing them together for eternity. The devil often tempted her with this thought, laughing at the desperate joy she felt before she tried to push the idea away. She barely let herself look at the thought, and quickly locked it away in her mind. Instead, she convinced herself of a different reason for staying in New England. This reason was half-truth and half-delusion. She told herself that since Boston was the scene of her guilt, it should also be the place of her earthly punishment. Perhaps the daily torture of her shame would eventually cleanse her soul and help her achieve a new, saint-like purity born from her suffering.

So Hester Prynne did not flee. On the edge of town, near the shore but far from any neighbors, stood a small thatched cottage. An earlier settler had built it and then abandoned it because the soil was too poor for farming. Its remote location kept it away from the social life of the town. The cottage stood on the shore, looking across the water toward the forest-covered hills to the west. A clump of scrubby trees stood nearby. These trees did not hide the cottage, but they made it look like a place that was meant to be concealed. Hester settled in this lonely cottage with her baby, using the little money she had and with the permission of the magistrates, who still watched her closely. A shadow of suspicion immediately hung over the place. Children who were too young to understand why this woman was isolated would creep close to watch her. They saw her sewing by the cottage window, standing in the doorway, working in her garden, or walking along the path to town. But as soon as they spotted the scarlet letter on her chest, they would run away in sudden fear.

Although Hester was completely lonely and had no friends who dared to support her, she was in no danger of starving. She possessed a skill that allowed her to earn a living and feed herself and her baby, even in a colony with few luxuries. This was the art of needlework, which was then one of the few ways a woman could earn money. The beautifully embroidered letter on her chest was a prime example of her delicate skill. Even wealthy court ladies in Europe would have gladly used her talent to add gold and silk designs to their clothes. In Boston, the Puritans generally dressed in simple, dark clothes, so there was rarely a need for fine fashion. However, the style of that era demanded elaborate designs for special occasions, and this taste influenced even the stern Puritans. Public ceremonies like ordinations and the installations of magistrates were marked by stately events and serious magnificence. Deep ruffs, carefully detailed collars, and embroidered gloves were considered necessary for the official uniforms of men in power. These luxuries were allowed for people of high rank or wealth, even though laws forbade ordinary citizens from wearing them. Hester also found regular work preparing clothes for funerals, where black cloth and white linen represented the sorrow of the family. Finally, baby clothing, which was very formal in those days, provided another source of work and income.

Gradually, and quite quickly, her needlework became highly fashionable. This happened for several reasons. Some people felt pity for her sad fate, while others had a curious interest in things associated with a scandal. Some simply wanted her work because it was unique, or because she was the only skilled embroiderer in the area. In any case, Hester had plenty of well-paying work for as many hours as she chose to sew. People of high standing wore garments made by her sinful hands at formal events. Her needlework was seen on the Governor's collar, on the sashes of military men, and on the collars of ministers. It decorated baby caps, and was buried with the dead in their coffins. But her skill was never used to embroider a white wedding veil for a bride. This single exception showed the relentless severity with which the community viewed her sin.

Hester did not try to earn anything beyond a simple living for herself, though she provided well for her child. Her own dress was made of coarse, dark fabric, with only one ornament: the scarlet letter she was forced to wear. In contrast, her child’s clothes were made with creative and fantastic detail. This heightened the charming, lively nature of the little girl, but the clothing also seemed to have a deeper meaning that we will discuss later. Except for the money she spent on decorating her baby's clothes, Hester gave all her extra income to charity, helping poor people who were less miserable than herself and who often insulted her when she gave them aid. She spent much of her time making simple, rough clothes for the poor. She likely did this as a way to repent, sacrificing her own enjoyment by spending hours on rough handiwork. Hester naturally possessed a love for beautiful, rich things, which she could only express through her fine needlework. Designing clothes was her only creative outlet. This delicate work gave her a way to express and soothe her inner passion. Yet, like all other joys in her life, she felt this pleasure was a sin. This constant guilt over a harmless activity showed that her mind had not found true peace, and that she was still deeply troubled within.

In this way, Hester Prynne carved out a place for herself in the world. Because of her strong character and talent, the community could not completely cast her off, even though they had marked her with a symbol more painful than the mark of Cain. Yet in all her interactions with society, she never felt like she belonged. Every gesture, word, and silence from others reminded her that she was banished. She felt as isolated as if she lived in another world. She stood apart from the community like a ghost visiting a familiar fireplace, unable to make itself seen or felt. The ghost could no longer share in the household joy or mourn with the family. If the ghost tried to show sympathy, it would only awaken terror and disgust. These feelings of disgust and bitter scorn were the only things the community felt toward Hester. It was a harsh era, and she was often reminded of her shame by the rude behavior of others. The poor people she helped often insulted her. Wealthy women whose houses she visited to deliver needlework would drop bitter words into her heart. Sometimes they did this with a quiet malice, turning ordinary details into subtle insults. Other times, they spoke with a coarse cruelty that felt like a rough blow to a wound. Hester had trained herself to endure these attacks. She never responded to these attacks, except for a sudden blush that rose on her pale cheeks and then faded away. She was patient and suffered like a martyr. But she refused to pray for her enemies, fearing that her angry thoughts might turn the words of a blessing into a curse.

She continually felt the painful consequences of her sentence in a thousand ways. Ministers would stop her in the street to lecture her, drawing a crowd of staring people around her. If she entered a church, hoping to share in the worship, she would often find herself used as the subject of the sermon. She grew to fear children. They had learned from their parents that there was something horrible about this quiet woman who walked silently through town with her only child. The children would let her pass and then follow her at a distance, yelling a word that they did not fully understand. Hearing this word from the lips of children was terrible to Hester. It made her feel that her shame was so widespread that all of nature knew about it. It would have hurt no less if the leaves of the trees had whispered her story, or if the winter wind had shrieked it aloud. She also felt a unique pain whenever a stranger stared at her. Strangers always looked curiously at the scarlet letter, branding the shame afresh into her soul. Many times, she wanted to cover the letter with her hand, but she always stopped herself. Even the familiar stares of neighbors had their own pain. Their cool, casual looks were intolerable. From first to last, Hester suffered whenever someone looked at the token. Her heart never grew numb to the pain. Instead, it became more sensitive with each passing day.

But sometimes, once in many days or months, she would feel someone look at the scarlet letter in a way that gave her a moment of relief. It felt as if half of her pain was shared. The next instant, the pain rushed back even stronger, because in that brief moment, she felt she had sinned again. Had Hester sinned alone?

Her lonely and painful life began to affect her imagination. Walking back and forth in her small world, Hester began to feel that the scarlet letter had given her a new sense. She shuddered to think of it, but she could not help believing that the letter allowed her to sense the hidden sins in other people's hearts. She was terrified by these feelings. She wondered if they were simply the whispers of the devil, trying to convince her that everyone's purity was a lie. The devil wanted her to believe that if the truth were known, a scarlet letter would burn on many chests besides her own. She did not know if these sudden feelings were the truth or a delusion. In all her sad experience, nothing was more awful than this new sense. It shocked her because it happened at the most unexpected times. Sometimes the red letter on her chest would seem to throb when she walked near a respected minister or magistrate, men whom the community looked up to as saints. Hester would ask herself what evil was near, only to look up and see no one but this respected leader. Other times, she felt a secret connection when she met the strict frown of a highly respected woman who was known for her lifelong purity. What could Hester's burning shame have in common with that matron's cold heart? Or again, she would feel a sudden thrill of warning, and look up to see a young girl glancing shyly at the scarlet letter. The girl would quickly look away, her cheeks turning red, as if her own purity had been stained by the glance. It seemed as if the devil, using the letter as his symbol, wanted to destroy her faith in everyone, both young and old. Losing her faith was one of the saddest results of her sin. Yet, it was proof that Hester was not completely corrupt that she struggled to believe that other people were not guilty like herself.

Ordinary people in those dark times loved to create scary stories. They started a rumor about the scarlet letter that could easily be turned into a terrifying legend. They claimed that the symbol was not made of simple red cloth, but was red-hot with hellfire. They said it could be seen glowing in the dark whenever Hester walked out at night. And we must say, the letter burned so deeply into Hester’s chest that perhaps there was more truth in that story than we would like to believe today.

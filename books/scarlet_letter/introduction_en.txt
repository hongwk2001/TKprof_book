A Note to the Reader


The Scarlet Letter was written by Nathaniel Hawthorne and first published in 1850. It is one of the most celebrated novels in American literature.

The story is set in the 1640s in the Puritan settlement of Boston, Massachusetts. At that time, the Puritan community lived by a strict religious code. People were expected to follow firm rules of behavior, and those who broke the rules faced public punishment and lasting shame.

The story centers on a woman named Hester Prynne. Hester has committed adultery, which means she had a child with a man who was not her husband. As punishment, the community forces her to wear a large red letter A on her chest for the rest of her life. The letter stands for the word "adultery." It is meant to mark her as a sinner in the eyes of the world.

But Hester refuses to be destroyed by her punishment. She raises her daughter, Pearl, with love and dignity. She quietly performs acts of kindness for those around her, earning a measure of respect over many years. At the same time, the secret of who fathered her child weighs heavily on two other lives: the man himself, and Hester's husband, who has arrived in secret and seeks revenge.

The novel explores guilt, punishment, forgiveness, and the cost of hidden sin. It asks a powerful question: who suffers more, the person who is punished openly, or the person who hides their shame in secret?


About This Edition

This edition was adapted into modern English for listeners and casual readers. Every sentence was structured for clear, natural flow when read aloud. Complex Victorian prose has been simplified without losing the drama and emotion of Hawthorne's original story.

To make the narrative pacing more natural and accessible, this modernization simplifies the language structure, which reduces the text size from the original 83,826 words to 57,450 words in the modernized version.

The original 58 black-and-white illustrations from the 1878 edition have been preserved and digitally colorized for this edition.


About "The Custom-House"

Hawthorne originally placed a long autobiographical essay at the front of this novel. The essay is titled "The Custom-House." In it, Hawthorne describes his years working as a government customs officer in Salem, Massachusetts, and explains how he came to discover the story of Hester Prynne.

In this edition, "The Custom-House" has been moved to the back of the book. This allows new readers to step directly into Hester's story. If you are already familiar with the novel, or if you would like to understand more about Hawthorne's personal life and the history behind the story, you will find the full essay at the end.

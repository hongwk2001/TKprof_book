A Note to the Reader


The Scarlet Letter was written by Nathaniel Hawthorne and first published in 1850. It is one of the most celebrated novels in American literature.

The story is set in the 1640s in the Puritan settlement of Boston, Massachusetts. At that time, the Puritan community lived by a strict religious code. People were expected to follow firm rules of behavior, and those who broke the rules faced public punishment and lasting shame.

The story centers on a woman named Hester Prynne. Hester has committed adultery, which means she had a child with a man who was not her husband. As punishment, the community forces her to wear a large red letter A on her chest for the rest of her life. The letter stands for the word "adultery." It is meant to mark her as a sinner in the eyes of the world.

But Hester refuses to be destroyed by her punishment. She raises her daughter, Pearl, with love and dignity. She quietly performs acts of kindness for those around her, earning a measure of respect over many years. At the same time, the secret of who fathered her child weighs heavily on two other lives: the man himself, and Hester's husband, who has arrived in secret and seeks revenge.

The novel explores guilt, punishment, forgiveness, and the cost of hidden sin. It asks a powerful question: who suffers more, the person who is punished openly, or the person who hides their shame in secret?


About This Edition

This edition was adapted into modern English for listeners and casual readers. Every sentence was structured for clear, natural flow when read aloud. Complex Victorian prose has been simplified without losing the drama and emotion of Hawthorne's original story.

The original 58 black-and-white illustrations from the 1878 edition have been preserved and digitally colorized for this edition.


About "The Custom-House"

Hawthorne originally placed a long autobiographical essay at the front of this novel. The essay is titled "The Custom-House." In it, Hawthorne describes his years working as a government customs officer in Salem, Massachusetts, and explains how he came to discover the story of Hester Prynne.

In this edition, "The Custom-House" has been moved to the back of the book. This allows new readers to step directly into Hester's story. If you are already familiar with the novel, or if you would like to understand more about Hawthorne's personal life and the history behind the story, you will find the full essay at the end.

CHAPTER I.

THE PRISON-DOOR.

A crowd of bearded men in dark, gloomy clothes and gray, pointed hats, mixed with women, some wearing hoods and others bareheaded, gathered in front of a wooden building. The building's door was made of heavy oak timber and covered with iron spikes.

Whenever founders start a new colony, even if they plan a perfect society, they always have to set aside land for two things: a cemetery and a prison. Following this rule, the first settlers of Boston built their very first prison near Cornhill at about the same time that they created the first burial ground on Isaac Johnson's land. That grave later became the center of all the subsequent graves in the old churchyard of King's Chapel. It is certain that only fifteen or twenty years after the town was settled, the wooden jail was already stained by the weather. These stains and other signs of age made its dark, low-roofed front look even gloomier. The rust on the heavy ironwork of its oak door looked older than anything else in the New World. Like everything associated with crime, the prison seemed as if it had never been new.

There was a patch of grass in front of this ugly building, between it and the carriage tracks on the street. The grass was overgrown with burdock, pigweed, and other ugly weeds. These weeds seemed to thrive in the soil around the prison, which was like a dark flower in the middle of their new society. But a wild rosebush grew on one side of the entrance, rooted right at the threshold. In this month of June, it was covered with delicate blossoms. It seemed as if these flowers offered their sweet scent and fragile beauty to the prisoner going inside, or to the condemned criminal walking out to meet his doom. It was a sign that the deep heart of Nature could still feel pity and be kind to them.

By a strange chance, this rosebush has been preserved in history. We will not try to decide whether it had simply survived from the ancient forest long after the giant pines and oaks were chopped down. Or, as some stories say, perhaps it had sprouted from the ground beneath the feet of the sainted Anne Hutchinson as she walked through the prison door. Since we find it right at the threshold of our story, which is now about to begin from that gloomy door, we must pluck one of its flowers and present it to you. Let us hope it serves to represent some sweet moral lesson to be found along the way, or to bring some comfort to the dark end of this story of human weakness and sorrow.

CHAPTER II.

THE MARKET-PLACE.

On a certain summer morning more than two hundred years ago, a large crowd of Boston residents gathered on the grass plot in front of the jail on Prison Lane. Everyone stared intently at the heavy oak door, which was reinforced with iron clamps.

In any other town, or at a later time in New England history, the grim and frozen look on the faces of these bearded men would have meant something terrible was about to happen. It would usually suggest the execution of a famous criminal. In those cases, the court's sentence simply confirmed what the public already wanted. However, the early Puritans were so naturally stern that you could not be sure this was the reason they had gathered. They might be waiting to see a lazy servant or a disobedient child corrected at the whipping post. Or perhaps they were waiting to see a Quaker or some other religious outsider whipped out of town. It could even be an idle Native American, made rowdy by the white man’s alcohol, being driven back into the forest with a whip. Or, they might be waiting to see a witch, like old Mistress Hibbins, the bitter-tempered widow of the magistrate, die on the gallows.

In any of these cases, the spectators behaved with the same solemn attitude. This suited a society where religion and law were almost the same thing. In their character, the two were so mixed together that the mildest punishments and the most severe executions were treated with the same deep respect and awe. A criminal could expect very little cold sympathy from the bystanders at the scaffold. On the other hand, a punishment that would only bring mockery and ridicule today was treated with almost as much serious dignity as a death sentence.

On the summer morning when our story begins, the women in the crowd seemed to take a special interest in the punishment that was about to happen. Society was not yet refined enough to keep women from stepping out into the public streets and pushing their broad bodies close to the scaffold. Both morally and physically, these wives and daughters of English birth were much tougher than their descendants today, who are separated from them by six or seven generations. Over time, each generation of mothers has passed down a fainter complexion, a more delicate beauty, and a smaller physical frame to their children, along with a less forceful character. The women standing around the prison door lived less than fifty years after the time of Queen Elizabeth, who had been a very strong and fit representative of her sex. These women were her fellow countrywomen. The beef and ale of their native land, along with a simple moral diet, made them large and sturdy. The bright morning sun shone on their broad shoulders, full figures, and round, red cheeks, which had developed in England and had not yet grown pale in the cold air of New England. Furthermore, these mothers spoke with a boldness and loud volume that would startle people today.

“My friends,” said a harsh-looking woman of fifty, “I will tell you what I think. It would be much better for the public if we women of mature age, who are church members in good standing, had the power to judge criminals like Hester Prynne. What do you think, neighbors? If that shameless woman stood for judgment before the five of us here, would she get off with such a light sentence as the magistrates have given her? I certainly think not!”

“People say,” another woman added, “that the Reverend Master Dimmesdale, her holy minister, is deeply heartbroken that such a scandal has fallen upon his church.”

“The magistrates are God-fearing gentlemen, but they are too merciful, and that is the truth,” added a third, older woman. “At the very least, they should have branded Hester Prynne’s forehead with a hot iron. That would have made her feel it, I guarantee you. But as it is, that wicked woman will not care what they put on the front of her dress. She will probably just cover it with a fancy pin or some other wild decoration, and walk the streets as proud as ever!”

“Ah, but,” whispered a young wife, holding a child by the hand, “no matter how she covers the mark, she will always feel the pain of it in her heart.”

“Why are we talking about marks and brands, whether on her dress or on her forehead?” cried another woman, who was both the ugliest and the most pitiless of the group. “This woman has brought shame upon us all, and she deserves to die. Is there not a law for it? Yes, there is, both in the Bible and in the law books. If the magistrates refuse to enforce it, they can only blame themselves if their own wives and daughters go astray!”

“Have mercy on us, ladies,” a man in the crowd called out. “Is there no goodness in women except what comes from a fear of being hanged? That is a harsh thing to say! Hush now, neighbors, because the lock is turning in the prison door, and here comes Mistress Prynne herself.”

The prison door was thrown open from the inside. First, the town officer stepped out like a black shadow emerging into the sunshine. He carried a sword by his side and held his official staff in his hand. His grim appearance represented the whole dark severity of the Puritan laws, which he was responsible for enforcing. He held out the official staff in his left hand and placed his right hand on the shoulder of a young woman to draw her forward. But as they reached the threshold, she pushed him away with natural dignity and strength. She stepped out into the open air as if by her own free will. She held a baby in her arms who was about three months old. The baby squinted and turned its little face away from the bright daylight, because it had lived only in the gray twilight of a dark prison cell.

When the young mother stood fully revealed before the crowd, her first reaction was to press the baby tightly to her chest. She did this not out of motherly love, but to hide a certain symbol sewn onto her dress. However, she quickly realized that using one symbol of shame to hide another would not work. So she held the baby on her arm, blushed deeply, and looked around at her neighbors with a proud smile and a bold look. On the breast of her gown, the letter A was displayed in fine red cloth, surrounded by elaborate embroidery and beautiful patterns of gold thread. It was done so artistically and with such creative imagination that it looked like a beautiful decoration on her dress. The dress itself was elegant and fashionable for the times, but it far exceeded the simple clothing rules allowed by the colony.

The young woman was tall, with a graceful and elegant figure. She had thick, dark hair that was so glossy it caught the sunshine, and a beautiful face with regular features, a healthy complexion, a prominent brow, and deep black eyes. She moved with a dignity and state that matched the lady-like style of that era, rather than the delicate grace we value today. Hester Prynne had never looked more noble and dignified than she did as she stepped out of the prison. People who knew her before and expected to see her looking sad and ruined were astonished. They were startled to see how her beauty shone through her misfortune, turning her shame into a kind of glowing halo. To a sensitive observer, this was a painful sight. The dress she had made for herself in prison expressed her desperate and reckless mood through its unique and artistic design. However, the thing that drew everyone's eyes and transformed her appearance was the scarlet letter embroidered on her chest. It seemed to cast a spell, separating her from the rest of humanity and placing her in a world of her own.

“She is certainly very skilled with a needle,” one woman in the crowd remarked. “But no respectable woman would ever show off her skill in such a shameless way! Neighbors, she is laughing in the faces of our holy magistrates. She is turning what they meant as a punishment into something to be proud of.”

“It would be better,” muttered the harshest of the old women, “if we stripped that rich gown off her shoulders. As for the red letter she stitched so carefully, I would gladly give her a rag from my old flannel to make a more suitable one!”

“Oh, please be quiet, neighbors!” whispered the youngest woman. “Do not let her hear you. She felt the pain of every single stitch in that embroidered letter in her heart.”

The grim officer gestured with his staff. “Make way, good people, in the King's name!” he cried. “Open a passage, and I promise you that Mistress Prynne will be placed where everyone can get a clear view of her fine clothes, from now until an hour past noon. Bless the righteous Colony of Massachusetts, where sin is dragged out into the sunshine! Come along, Hester, and show your scarlet letter in the marketplace!”

A path opened through the crowd. Led by the officer and followed by a group of stern men and harsh-looking women, Hester Prynne walked toward the place of her punishment. A crowd of curious schoolboys, who did not understand what was happening but were happy to have a half-holiday, ran ahead of her. They kept turning around to stare at her face, the squinting baby in her arms, and the shameful letter on her chest. In those days, the distance from the prison door to the marketplace was not very far. However, to the prisoner, it felt like a very long journey. Despite her proud look, she suffered with every step as the crowd stared at her. It felt as if her heart had been thrown onto the street for everyone to trample. Fortunately, human nature has a merciful way of protecting us. A person in deep pain does not feel the full intensity of their suffering in the moment, but rather in the dull ache that remains afterward. So Hester Prynne walked through this part of her trial with a calm expression, and arrived at a scaffold at the western end of the marketplace. The scaffold stood near the roof of Boston's first church, where it was built as a permanent structure.

This scaffold was part of a punishment machine. Today, this machine is only remembered in history, but in the old days, it was believed to promote good citizenship, much like the guillotine did in France. It was the platform of the pillory. Above the platform rose a wooden frame designed to lock the human head in a tight grip, holding it up for the public to stare at. This wooden and iron structure was the ultimate symbol of shame. There is no greater offense against human dignity, no matter what a person has done, than to forbid them from hiding their face in shame. Yet that was the very purpose of this punishment. In Hester's case, however, her sentence was only to stand on the platform for a certain amount of time. She did not have her neck locked in the frame. Knowing what she had to do, she climbed the wooden steps and stood before the crowd, about the height of a man's shoulders above the street.

If a Catholic had been in that crowd of Puritans, this beautiful woman, holding her baby to her chest, might have reminded him of the Virgin Mary. It would have reminded him of those classic paintings of holy motherhood, though only by contrast. Here, the most sacred quality of human life was stained by deep sin. The world seemed darker because of this woman’s beauty, and more lost because of the child she had borne.

The scene was filled with awe, which always surrounds the sight of a person in guilt and shame before society becomes cold enough to laugh at it. The people witnessing Hester Prynne’s disgrace were simple and serious. They were stern enough to watch her execution, if that had been her sentence, without complaining about its cruelty. However, they were not heartless, and they would never laugh at such a sad event. Even if anyone had wanted to mock her, they would have been stopped by the solemn presence of the town's leaders. The Governor, his counselors, a judge, a general, and the town's ministers all sat or stood on the balcony of the meeting house, looking down at the platform. Because these important leaders were part of the event, the punishment carried a serious meaning. The crowd was quiet and solemn. Hester stood as best she could under the heavy weight of a thousand eyes staring directly at her chest. It was almost too much to bear. She had prepared herself to face insults and angry words. However, the silent, serious mood of the crowd was much more terrifying. She wished they would laugh at her instead. If the crowd had roared with laughter, she could have replied with a bitter, proud smile. But under their heavy, silent stare, she felt as if she would scream or throw herself off the scaffold and go mad.

Yet there were moments when the entire scene vanished from her eyes, or became blurry like a group of ghosts. Her mind and memory became highly active. She began to remember other scenes far away from this rough street on the edge of the wilderness, and other faces than the stern ones looking at her from under pointed hats. She remembered trivial things from her childhood, school days, games, and childish arguments. She also recalled the gravest events of her adult life. Every picture was equally vivid, as if they were all part of a play. This was likely a natural defense to relieve her mind from the cruel weight of her current reality.

Standing on the scaffold, Hester saw her entire life pass before her, going back to her happy childhood. In her mind, she saw her old village in England and her family home, which was a decaying stone house with an old coat of arms over the door. She saw her father's face, with his bald head and white beard. She saw her mother's face, filled with the loving concern she always remembered. She saw her own young face, glowing with beauty in an old mirror. Then she saw the face of an elderly man. He was pale and thin, with eyes dimmed from reading many heavy books. Yet those same eyes had a strange power to read the human soul. This man was slightly deformed, with his left shoulder higher than his right. Next, she remembered the narrow streets and tall gray houses of a European city, where she had started a new life with this scholar. Finally, all these memories faded away. She was back in the rough marketplace of the Puritan settlement. The townspeople were all staring at her as she stood on the scaffold, holding her baby, with the scarlet letter A embroidered on her chest.

Could this be true? She pressed the baby so tightly to her chest that the child cried out. She looked down at the scarlet letter and touched it with her finger to make sure the baby and her shame were real. Yes, this was her reality. Everything else had vanished.

CHAPTER III.

THE RECOGNITION.

Hester Prynne was finally relieved of the intense feeling of being stared at by everyone. On the outer edge of the crowd, she spotted a figure that immediately captured her attention. A Native American man in his traditional clothes was standing there. However, native visitors were common in the English settlements. Under normal circumstances, Hester would not have paid attention to him, let alone let him occupy her entire mind. But next to the Native American man stood a white man, who was clearly traveling with him. The white man wore a strange mix of civilized clothing and native garments.

The white man was short, with a lined face that did not look old yet. His features showed remarkable intelligence. It was clear that he had trained his mind so deeply that it shaped his physical appearance. Although he had tried to hide his shape under his mixed clothing, Hester Prynne could see clearly that one of his shoulders was higher than the other. As soon as she saw his thin face and slight deformity, she squeezed her baby to her chest so hard that the poor child cried out in pain. But the mother did not seem to hear the cry.

When he first arrived in the marketplace, before she even noticed him, the stranger stared at Hester Prynne. At first, his look was casual, like a man who spent most of his time thinking and cared little about the outside world. Very soon, however, his gaze became sharp and focused. A wave of horror twisted across his features, like a snake sliding quickly over his face. His face darkened with a powerful emotion. But he controlled it so instantly by an effort of will that his expression quickly returned to calmness. Soon, the look of shock faded completely into the depths of his nature. When he saw Hester Prynne staring at him and realized she recognized him, he slowly and calmly raised his finger, made a quiet gesture in the air, and placed his finger on his lips.

Then, he touched the shoulder of a town resident standing next to him and spoke in a polite, formal tone. “Excuse me, sir,” he said. “Who is this woman, and why is she standing here on display for public shame?”

“You must be new to this region, friend,” the townsman replied, looking curiously at the stranger and his native companion. “Otherwise, you would surely have heard of Mistress Hester Prynne and her wicked actions. She has caused a massive scandal in holy Master Dimmesdale’s church.”

“You are right,” replied the stranger. “I am indeed a stranger, and I have been a wanderer against my will. I have suffered terrible accidents on sea and land, and I was held captive for a long time by the native tribes to the south. Now this Native American man has brought me here to be freed from my captivity. Please tell me about Hester Prynne—did I say her name correctly? What are her offenses, and what has brought her to that scaffold?”

“Well, friend, after all your troubles in the wilderness, it must make you happy to find yourself in a land where sin is searched out and punished before the rulers and the people, just as it is here in New England. You see, that woman was the wife of a learned Englishman. They lived for a long time in Amsterdam, but he decided to cross the ocean and live with us here in Massachusetts. He sent his wife ahead of him while he stayed behind to take care of some business. But in the two years that she has lived here in Boston, we have heard no news from this learned gentleman, Master Prynne. His young wife, left alone and guided only by herself...”

“Ah, I see,” said the stranger with a bitter smile. “A learned man should have learned to expect this from his books. And tell me, sir, who is the father of the baby she is holding in her arms? The child looks to be about three or four months old.”

“To tell you the truth, friend, that matter remains a mystery. We are still waiting for someone who can solve it. Hester absolutely refuses to speak, and the magistrates have met in vain. Perhaps the guilty man is standing in the crowd right now, looking at this sad sight, unknown to anyone, and forgetting that God sees him.”

“The learned husband,” the stranger remarked with another smile, “should come here himself to look into the mystery.”

“He certainly should, if he is still alive,” the townsman agreed. “Our Massachusetts magistrates took into account that this woman is young and beautiful, and was likely strongly tempted to commit this sin. Since it is highly probable that her husband is at the bottom of the sea, they decided not to enforce the full severity of our law against her. The normal penalty for this crime is death. But in their great mercy, they sentenced Hester Prynne to stand for only three hours on the scaffold, and then wear a mark of shame on her chest for the rest of her life.”

“A wise sentence!” the stranger remarked, bowing his head seriously. “In this way, she will be a living sermon against sin until that shameful letter is carved onto her tombstone. Still, it bothers me that the partner of her sin does not stand on the scaffold beside her. But he will be known! He will be known! He will be known!”

He bowed politely to the talkative townsman, whispered a few words to his Native American companion, and the two walked away through the crowd. Meanwhile, Hester Prynne stood on her platform, staring fixedly at the stranger. She was so absorbed in looking at him that everything else in the world seemed to vanish, leaving only the two of them. Meeting him face to face in private would have been even more terrifying than this public encounter. Here, she stood in the hot midday sun with her shame exposed. The scarlet letter was on her chest, her baby was in her arms, and a crowd of people stared at her. These features should only have been seen by a quiet fireside at home, or behind a veil at church. Yet, as dreadful as this public exposure was, Hester felt protected by the presence of a thousand witnesses. It was better to stand with a crowd between them than to face him alone. She felt that the public display was a shield, and she dreaded the moment she would have to step down. Lost in these thoughts, she barely heard a voice behind her until it repeated her name several times in a loud, solemn tone that the whole crowd could hear.

“Listen to me, Hester Prynne!” the voice said.

Directly above the platform where Hester stood was a balcony attached to the meeting house. This was where official announcements were made before the town officials, with all the ceremony of those days. Governor Bellingham himself sat there, surrounded by four guards carrying weapons. The elderly governor wore a hat with a dark feather, an embroidered cloak, and a black velvet tunic. His wrinkles showed his long, hard experience. He was a suitable leader for a colony that was built on the serious energy and quiet wisdom of older men, rather than the impulses of youth. The other leaders around him carried themselves with great dignity. They believed their authority came directly from God. They were good, just, and wise men. However, out of all the people in the world, it would have been hard to find a group of men less capable of judging a woman's heart. They could not understand the mix of good and evil within her. Hester seemed to know that any sympathy she might find would come from the warm hearts of the crowd, not these strict leaders. As she looked up at the balcony, she grew pale and trembled.

The voice that called out to her belonged to the famous Reverend John Wilson, the oldest minister in Boston. He was a great scholar and a man with a naturally kind and friendly spirit. However, he had focused so much on his studies that he often hid his kind nature, feeling it was a weakness. He stood there with gray hair showing beneath his cap. His gray eyes, used to the dim light of his study, squinted in the bright sunshine just like Hester's baby did. He looked like one of the dark portraits printed at the beginning of old sermon books. He had no more business meddling with a matter of human guilt, passion, and pain than a portrait would.

“Hester Prynne,” John Wilson said, placing his hand on the shoulder of a pale young man beside him. “I have tried to persuade my young brother here, whose sermons you have listened to. I argued that he should speak with you before God, these wise rulers, and all the townspeople about the gravity of your sin. Since he knows your character better than I do, he is better suited to choose the right arguments. He could use tenderness or fear to overcome your stubborn silence, so you will no longer hide the name of the man who led you into this sin. But he argues that it would violate a woman's nature to force her to reveal her heart's secrets in public. I tried to convince him that the real shame lies in committing the sin, not in confessing it. What do you say, Brother Dimmesdale? Must it be you or I who speaks to this poor sinner's soul?”

A murmur passed among the leaders on the balcony. Governor Bellingham spoke with authority, but also with respect toward the young minister. “Master Dimmesdale,” the Governor said, “the responsibility for this woman’s soul lies largely with you. It is your duty to urge her to repent and confess her sin as proof of her repentance.”

This direct appeal drew the eyes of the entire crowd toward the Reverend Arthur Dimmesdale. He was a young minister who had graduated from a great English university, bringing high scholarship to our wild colony. His eloquence and religious passion already made him highly respected. He had a striking appearance, with a high white forehead, large and sad brown eyes, and a mouth that trembled unless he held it tight. This showed both his nervous sensitivity and his great self-control. Despite his talents and education, he had a worried, startled, and half-frightened look. He seemed like someone who felt lost in the world and could only find peace in private. Therefore, he stayed out of the public eye as much as possible, keeping himself simple and childlike. When he did speak, his thoughts were so fresh and pure that people said he sounded like an angel.

This was the young man the Governor and John Wilson called upon to speak. They commanded him to address the mystery of this woman's soul. The pressure of this situation turned Dimmesdale's face pale and made his lips tremble. “Speak to the woman, my brother,” Mr. Wilson urged. “This is important for her soul, and as the Governor said, it is equally important for your own soul, since you are responsible for her. Urge her to confess the truth!”

Arthur Dimmesdale bowed his head, seemingly in silent prayer, and then stepped forward. He leaned over the balcony, looked directly into Hester's eyes, and spoke. “Hester Prynne, you hear what this good man says, and you see the heavy responsibility I bear. If you feel it will bring peace to your soul, and make your earthly punishment help save you, I charge you to speak the name of the man who sinned and suffered with you. Do not remain silent out of a mistaken pity for him. Believe me, Hester, even if he must step down from a high position to stand beside you in shame, it is far better to do that than to hide a guilty heart for the rest of his life. Your silence only tempts or even forces him to add hypocrisy to his sin. Heaven has given you a public shame so that you can win a victory over the evil inside you and the sorrow outside. Be careful not to deny him this bitter but healing cup of confession, because he may not have the courage to choose it for himself.”

The young pastor’s voice was sweet, rich, deep, and filled with emotion. The feeling in his voice, even more than his words, touched everyone in the crowd and united them in sympathy. Even the baby at Hester’s chest was affected. The child looked at Arthur Dimmesdale and held up its little arms with a soft murmur. The minister's appeal was so powerful that the crowd was sure Hester would reveal the father's name. They also expected that the guilty man himself, wherever he stood in the crowd, would feel forced by his own conscience to step forward and climb the scaffold. But Hester shook her head.

“Woman, do not go beyond the limits of Heaven’s mercy!” Reverend John Wilson called out, more harshly than before. “That little baby has been given a voice to support the advice you have just heard. Speak the name! Confessing the name and showing repentance may help remove the scarlet letter from your chest.”

“Never!” Hester replied, looking not at Mr. Wilson, but directly into the deep, troubled eyes of the younger minister. “It is branded too deeply. You cannot take it off. I only wish I could bear his pain along with my own.”

“Speak, woman!” a cold, harsh voice called out from the crowd near the scaffold. “Speak, and give your child a father!”

“I will not speak!” Hester answered. She turned as pale as death but responded clearly to the voice, which she recognized all too well. “My child must seek a Father in heaven. She will never know an earthly father!”

“She will not speak!” Arthur Dimmesdale murmured. He had been leaning over the balcony with his hand over his heart, waiting for her answer. He now stepped back with a deep sigh. “What wonderful strength and generosity in a woman’s heart! She will not speak!”

Seeing that Hester would not change her mind, the older minister delivered a long sermon about sin to the crowd. He spoke for more than an hour, referencing the shameful letter on her chest. He described the symbol so vividly that the crowd grew terrified, imagining that the red letter was colored by the fires of hell. Meanwhile, Hester Prynne stood on the scaffold with blank eyes, looking tired and indifferent. She had endured all she possibly could that morning. Since she was not the type of person who escapes pain by fainting, her mind went numb to protect itself. The preacher's voice thundered in her ears, but it had no effect on her. During the end of the trial, the baby began to cry and scream. Hester tried to quiet the child mechanically, but she was too numb to feel its distress. With the same cold expression, she was led back to the prison and vanished behind the heavy iron-clamped door. The people who watched her go whispered that the scarlet letter cast a glowing red light along the dark hallway inside.

CHAPTER IV.

THE INTERVIEW.

When Hester Prynne returned to the prison, she was in a state of nervous excitement. The guards had to watch her constantly to prevent her from harming herself or her baby. As night approached, her anger could not be quieted by warnings or threats. So Master Brackett, the jailer, decided to bring in a physician. He described the doctor as a man skilled in both European medicine and the forest herbs and roots used by the native tribes. In truth, they desperately needed medical help. While Hester needed attention, the baby was in even greater danger. The child, drinking its mother’s milk, seemed to have absorbed all the distress, pain, and despair that Hester had felt. The baby writhed in convulsions of pain, reflecting in its tiny body the mental suffering that Hester had endured all day.

The stranger with the unusual appearance entered the dark cell right behind the jailer. His presence in the crowd earlier had deeply startled Hester. The authorities kept him in the prison, not because he was suspected of any crime, but as a convenient place to stay while the magistrates negotiated his ransom with the native chiefs. His name was announced as Roger Chillingworth. After showing him into the room, the jailer stood there for a moment, surprised by the quiet that followed. Hester Prynne had immediately become as still as death, though the baby continued to moan.

“Please, friend, leave me alone with my patient,” the doctor said. “Trust me, jailer, you will soon have peace in this prison. I promise you that Hester Prynne will be much more cooperative than she has been so far.”

“Well, if you can do that, sir,” Master Brackett replied, “I will say you are a man of true skill! The woman has been acting like she is possessed. I was almost ready to whip the devil out of her myself.”

The stranger entered the room with the quiet manner of a doctor. His behavior did not change when the jailer left, leaving him face to face with Hester. Her intense reaction in the crowd had shown how close their relationship was. He focused first on the child. The baby lay crying and twisting in pain on the small bed. It was absolutely necessary to soothe the child before doing anything else. He examined the infant carefully, and then opened a leather case from under his clothes. The case contained medical mixtures, and he mixed one of them into a cup of water.

“My old studies in science,” he remarked, “along with my time living with native people who know the healing properties of herbs, have made me a better doctor than many who hold medical degrees. Here, woman. The child is yours. She is not mine, and she will never recognize my voice or face as her father's. Give her this medicine yourself.”

Hester pushed the medicine away, looking at him with deep fear. “Would you take revenge on an innocent baby?” she whispered.

“Foolish woman!” the physician replied, speaking half coldly and half gently. “Why would I want to harm this poor, miserable child? The medicine is very good. Even if this were my own child, I could do no better for it.”

Since Hester still hesitated in her state of panic, he took the baby in his arms and gave her the medicine himself. The medicine worked quickly, proving the doctor's promise. The baby's moaning subsided and she stopped twisting in pain. In a few moments, she fell into a deep, peaceful sleep. The physician next turned his attention to the mother. He calmly felt her pulse and looked into her eyes. His stare made Hester's heart shrink and shudder, because it was so familiar, yet so cold and strange. Finally, satisfied with his examination, he mixed another medicine.

“I do not have magical potions to make you forget,” he remarked. “But I have learned many secrets in the wilderness. Here is a recipe that a native teacher taught me in exchange for some lessons of my own. Drink it. It may be less soothing than a clear conscience, which I cannot give you. But it will calm your wild passion, like oil poured onto a stormy sea.”

He handed the cup to Hester. She took it, looking slowly and earnestly at his face. Her look was not quite one of fear, but it was filled with doubt and questions about his motives. She also looked at her sleeping baby. “I have thought of death,” she said. “I have wished for it. I would have prayed for it, if someone like me could pray for anything. But if death is in this cup, I ask you to think again before you watch me drink it. See, it is at my lips right now.”

“Drink, then,” he replied with the same cold calmness. “Do you know me so little, Hester Prynne? Are my plans ever that simple? Even if I wanted revenge, the best thing I could do is let you live. I want to give you medicine to protect your life so that this burning shame can continue to blaze on your chest.” As he spoke, he placed his long finger on the scarlet letter. It felt as if it burned into Hester’s chest like a red-hot iron. He noticed her flinch and smiled. “So, live. Carry your doom with you in the eyes of men and women, in the eyes of the man you called your husband, and in the eyes of that child. Drink this so that you can live.”

Without any more arguments or delay, Hester Prynne drank the medicine. The doctor gestured for her to sit on the bed where the child was sleeping, while he took the only chair in the room and sat beside her. She trembled at these preparations. She felt that he had done all he could to ease her physical pain, whether out of kindness, duty, or a refined cruelty. Now he was ready to speak to her as the husband she had deeply and permanently injured.

“Hester,” he said, “I will not ask why or how you fell into this trap of shame. The reason is easy to see. It was my foolishness and your weakness. I was a man of thought, a bookworm in great libraries. I was already growing old, having given my best years to the pursuit of knowledge. I had no business marrying someone as young and beautiful as you. I was deformed from birth. How could I trick myself into thinking that my intelligence could hide my physical shape in a young girl's eyes? People call me wise. If wise men were actually wise in their own lives, I would have foreseen all of this. I would have known that the moment I walked out of the dark forest and entered this Christian settlement, the very first thing I would see would be you, Hester Prynne, standing in shame before the crowd. Indeed, from the moment we walked down the church steps as a married couple, I should have seen this scarlet letter burning at the end of our path.”

“You know,” Hester said, unable to bear this quiet reminder of her shame, “you know that I was honest with you. I did not feel any love for you, and I never pretended to.”

“True,” he replied. “It was my foolishness, just as I said. But up to that point, I had lived in vain. The world had been so cheerless. My heart was like a large, lonely house, cold and without a fireplace. I longed to build a fire there. It did not seem like a wild dream, even though I was old, serious, and deformed, that the simple happiness shared by all mankind could be mine. So, Hester, I welcomed you into the deepest part of my heart. I hoped to find warmth in your presence.”

“I have wronged you deeply,” Hester murmured.

“We have wronged each other,” he answered. “My wrong was the first, when I tied your young youth to my old age. Therefore, since I have studied wisdom, I seek no revenge and plot no evil against you. The scale between us is balanced. But Hester, the man who wronged us both is still alive. Who is he?”

“Do not ask me!” Hester replied, looking firmly into his face. “You will never know!”

“Never, you say?” he replied with a dark, confident smile. “Never know him? Believe me, Hester, there are very few secrets in this world, or in the deep thoughts of men, that remain hidden from a person who devotes himself to solving a mystery. You can hide your secret from the curious crowd. You can also hide it from the ministers and magistrates, just as you did today when they tried to force the name from your heart to place a partner beside you. But I come to this search with different methods than they have. I will seek this man just as I have sought truth in books and science. A deep connection will make me recognize him. I will see him tremble, and I will feel myself shudder suddenly without warning. Sooner or later, he will be mine.”

The wrinkled scholar's eyes glowed so intensely that Hester Prynne pressed her hands over her heart, fearing he might read the secret right there.

“You will not reveal his name? He is still mine,” he continued confidently, as if destiny were on his side. “He does not wear a letter of shame on his clothes as you do, but I will read it on his heart. Yet, do not fear for him. Do not think I will interfere with Heaven’s own punishment, or betray him to the law. Do not imagine that I will plot against his life, or against his reputation if he is a respected man. Let him live. Let him hide behind his public honor if he can. He will still be mine.”

“Your actions are merciful,” Hester said, confused and terrified. “But your words sound like a threat.”

“I must ask one thing of you, my former wife,” the scholar continued. “You have kept the secret of your lover. Keep my secret as well. No one in this land knows me. Do not whisper to any living soul that I was once your husband. I will settle here on this wild edge of the earth. Though I am a wanderer elsewhere, cut off from human interests, I find here a woman, a man, and a child who are closely tied to me. It does not matter whether those ties are of love or hate, right or wrong. You and yours belong to me, Hester Prynne. My home is where you are, and where he is. Do not betray me.”

“Why do you want this?” Hester asked, shrinking from this secret agreement. “Why not announce yourself openly and divorce me at once?”

“Perhaps,” he replied, “because I do not want to face the dishonor of being known as the husband of an unfaithful woman. Or it may be for other reasons. It is enough that I plan to live and die unknown. Let your husband be dead to the world, a man of whom no news will ever come. Do not recognize me by word, sign, or look. Above all, do not whisper this secret to the man you know. If you fail me in this, beware. His reputation, his position, and his life will be in my hands. Beware.”

“I will keep your secret, just as I have kept his,” Hester said.

“Swear it!” he demanded.

And she took the oath.

“And now, Mistress Prynne,” said old Roger Chillingworth, which was the name he would use from then on, “I leave you alone. I leave you alone with your baby and the scarlet letter. How is it, Hester? Does your sentence force you to wear that mark in your sleep? Are you not afraid of nightmares and terrible dreams?”

“Why do you smile at me like that?” Hester asked, troubled by his look. “Are you like the devil that haunts the forest around us? Have you tricked me into an agreement that will ruin my soul?”

“Not your soul,” he answered with another smile. “No, not yours.”

CHAPTER V.

HESTER AT HER NEEDLE.

Hester Prynne’s time in prison was finally over. The prison door was thrown open, and she stepped out into the sunshine. The sun shone on everyone alike, but to Hester’s sad heart, the light seemed to exist only to reveal the scarlet letter on her chest. Walking away from the prison threshold alone for the first time was perhaps even more painful than the public display she had just endured. During that public trial, she had been supported by a state of nervous tension and her own combative energy. This had allowed her to turn the shameful scene into a sort of dark triumph. That display was also a single, unique event in her life. She could call upon all her inner strength to face it, knowing it would happen only once. Even the law that condemned her, like a stern giant with an iron arm, had supported her through the ordeal. But now, as she walked away from the prison door alone, her daily life began. She had to survive it using her ordinary daily strength, or sink under the weight. She could no longer rely on temporary energy to get through her current grief. Tomorrow would bring its own trials, and so would the next day, and the next. Each day would bring the same heavy burden. The years ahead would pass slowly, bringing the same weight for her to carry and never lay down. As the days accumulated, her misery would pile up. She would lose her individuality and become a symbol for preachers and moralists to point at, representing human weakness and sinful passion. Young and pure people would be taught to look at her, with the scarlet letter burning on her chest. They would see a daughter of honorable parents, the mother of a child, a woman who was once innocent, as the very symbol and reality of sin. And over her grave, the shame she carried would be her only monument.

It may seem strange that Hester chose to stay in this remote Puritan settlement. No rule in her sentence forced her to remain. She was free to return to her birthplace in England, or to any other European land. There, she could have hidden her identity under a new name and started a completely new life. She also could have walked into the dark forest, where she could live among native tribes whose customs knew nothing of the law that had condemned her. Yet, she chose to call this place her home, even though it was the only place where she was marked with shame. But there is a powerful feeling, like a sense of doom, that compels human beings to linger around the place where a major event has shaped their lives. This feeling is even stronger when the event is dark and sad. Hester’s sin and shame were the roots she had planted in this soil. It was as if a new birth had tied her to this wild land, making it her dreary, lifelong home. All other places on earth, even the English village of her happy childhood, felt foreign to her now. The chain that bound her to Boston was made of iron, and although it hurt her soul, it could never be broken.

There was another feeling that kept her here, though she hid the secret from herself and grew pale whenever she thought of it. In this town lived the man she felt connected to in a secret union. Although this union was unrecognized on earth, she believed it would bring them together before God's final judgment. She imagined that judgment day would be their marriage altar, bringing them together for eternity. The devil often tempted her with this thought, laughing at the desperate joy she felt before she tried to push the idea away. She barely let herself look at the thought, and quickly locked it away in her mind. Instead, she convinced herself of a different reason for staying in New England. This reason was half-truth and half-delusion. She told herself that since Boston was the scene of her guilt, it should also be the place of her earthly punishment. Perhaps the daily torture of her shame would eventually cleanse her soul and help her achieve a new, saint-like purity born from her suffering.

So Hester Prynne did not flee. On the edge of town, near the shore but far from any neighbors, stood a small thatched cottage. An earlier settler had built it and then abandoned it because the soil was too poor for farming. Its remote location kept it away from the social life of the town. The cottage stood on the shore, looking across the water toward the forest-covered hills to the west. A clump of scrubby trees stood nearby. These trees did not hide the cottage, but they made it look like a place that was meant to be concealed. Hester settled in this lonely cottage with her baby, using the little money she had and with the permission of the magistrates, who still watched her closely. A shadow of suspicion immediately hung over the place. Children who were too young to understand why this woman was isolated would creep close to watch her. They saw her sewing by the cottage window, standing in the doorway, working in her garden, or walking along the path to town. But as soon as they spotted the scarlet letter on her chest, they would run away in sudden fear.

Although Hester was completely lonely and had no friends who dared to support her, she was in no danger of starving. She possessed a skill that allowed her to earn a living and feed herself and her baby, even in a colony with few luxuries. This was the art of needlework, which was then one of the few ways a woman could earn money. The beautifully embroidered letter on her chest was a prime example of her delicate skill. Even wealthy court ladies in Europe would have gladly used her talent to add gold and silk designs to their clothes. In Boston, the Puritans generally dressed in simple, dark clothes, so there was rarely a need for fine fashion. However, the style of that era demanded elaborate designs for special occasions, and this taste influenced even the stern Puritans. Public ceremonies like ordinations and the installations of magistrates were marked by stately events and serious magnificence. Deep ruffs, carefully detailed collars, and embroidered gloves were considered necessary for the official uniforms of men in power. These luxuries were allowed for people of high rank or wealth, even though laws forbade ordinary citizens from wearing them. Hester also found regular work preparing clothes for funerals, where black cloth and white linen represented the sorrow of the family. Finally, baby clothing, which was very formal in those days, provided another source of work and income.

Gradually, and quite quickly, her needlework became highly fashionable. This happened for several reasons. Some people felt pity for her sad fate, while others had a curious interest in things associated with a scandal. Some simply wanted her work because it was unique, or because she was the only skilled embroiderer in the area. In any case, Hester had plenty of well-paying work for as many hours as she chose to sew. People of high standing wore garments made by her sinful hands at formal events. Her needlework was seen on the Governor's collar, on the sashes of military men, and on the collars of ministers. It decorated baby caps, and was buried with the dead in their coffins. But her skill was never used to embroider a white wedding veil for a bride. This single exception showed the relentless severity with which the community viewed her sin.

Hester did not try to earn anything beyond a simple living for herself, though she provided well for her child. Her own dress was made of coarse, dark fabric, with only one ornament: the scarlet letter she was forced to wear. In contrast, her child’s clothes were made with creative and fantastic detail. This heightened the charming, lively nature of the little girl, but the clothing also seemed to have a deeper meaning that we will discuss later. Except for the money she spent on decorating her baby's clothes, Hester gave all her extra income to charity, helping poor people who were less miserable than herself and who often insulted her when she gave them aid. She spent much of her time making simple, rough clothes for the poor. She likely did this as a way to repent, sacrificing her own enjoyment by spending hours on rough handiwork. Hester naturally possessed a love for beautiful, rich things, which she could only express through her fine needlework. Designing clothes was her only creative outlet. This delicate work gave her a way to express and soothe her inner passion. Yet, like all other joys in her life, she felt this pleasure was a sin. This constant guilt over a harmless activity showed that her mind had not found true peace, and that she was still deeply troubled within.

In this way, Hester Prynne carved out a place for herself in the world. Because of her strong character and talent, the community could not completely cast her off, even though they had marked her with a symbol more painful than the mark of Cain. Yet in all her interactions with society, she never felt like she belonged. Every gesture, word, and silence from others reminded her that she was banished. She felt as isolated as if she lived in another world. She stood apart from the community like a ghost visiting a familiar fireplace, unable to make itself seen or felt. The ghost could no longer share in the household joy or mourn with the family. If the ghost tried to show sympathy, it would only awaken terror and disgust. These feelings of disgust and bitter scorn were the only things the community felt toward Hester. It was a harsh era, and she was often reminded of her shame by the rude behavior of others. The poor people she helped often insulted her. Wealthy women whose houses she visited to deliver needlework would drop bitter words into her heart. Sometimes they did this with a quiet malice, turning ordinary details into subtle insults. Other times, they spoke with a coarse cruelty that felt like a rough blow to a wound. Hester had trained herself to endure these attacks. She never responded to these attacks, except for a sudden blush that rose on her pale cheeks and then faded away. She was patient and suffered like a martyr. But she refused to pray for her enemies, fearing that her angry thoughts might turn the words of a blessing into a curse.

She continually felt the painful consequences of her sentence in a thousand ways. Ministers would stop her in the street to lecture her, drawing a crowd of staring people around her. If she entered a church, hoping to share in the worship, she would often find herself used as the subject of the sermon. She grew to fear children. They had learned from their parents that there was something horrible about this quiet woman who walked silently through town with her only child. The children would let her pass and then follow her at a distance, yelling a word that they did not fully understand. Hearing this word from the lips of children was terrible to Hester. It made her feel that her shame was so widespread that all of nature knew about it. It would have hurt no less if the leaves of the trees had whispered her story, or if the winter wind had shrieked it aloud. She also felt a unique pain whenever a stranger stared at her. Strangers always looked curiously at the scarlet letter, branding the shame afresh into her soul. Many times, she wanted to cover the letter with her hand, but she always stopped herself. Even the familiar stares of neighbors had their own pain. Their cool, casual looks were intolerable. From first to last, Hester suffered whenever someone looked at the token. Her heart never grew numb to the pain. Instead, it became more sensitive with each passing day.

But sometimes, once in many days or months, she would feel someone look at the scarlet letter in a way that gave her a moment of relief. It felt as if half of her pain was shared. The next instant, the pain rushed back even stronger, because in that brief moment, she felt she had sinned again. Had Hester sinned alone?

Her lonely and painful life began to affect her imagination. Walking back and forth in her small world, Hester began to feel that the scarlet letter had given her a new sense. She shuddered to think of it, but she could not help believing that the letter allowed her to sense the hidden sins in other people's hearts. She was terrified by these feelings. She wondered if they were simply the whispers of the devil, trying to convince her that everyone's purity was a lie. The devil wanted her to believe that if the truth were known, a scarlet letter would burn on many chests besides her own. She did not know if these sudden feelings were the truth or a delusion. In all her sad experience, nothing was more awful than this new sense. It shocked her because it happened at the most unexpected times. Sometimes the red letter on her chest would seem to throb when she walked near a respected minister or magistrate, men whom the community looked up to as saints. Hester would ask herself what evil was near, only to look up and see no one but this respected leader. Other times, she felt a secret connection when she met the strict frown of a highly respected woman who was known for her lifelong purity. What could Hester's burning shame have in common with that matron's cold heart? Or again, she would feel a sudden thrill of warning, and look up to see a young girl glancing shyly at the scarlet letter. The girl would quickly look away, her cheeks turning red, as if her own purity had been stained by the glance. It seemed as if the devil, using the letter as his symbol, wanted to destroy her faith in everyone, both young and old. Losing her faith was one of the saddest results of her sin. Yet, it was proof that Hester was not completely corrupt that she struggled to believe that other people were not guilty like herself.

Ordinary people in those dark times loved to create scary stories. They started a rumor about the scarlet letter that could easily be turned into a terrifying legend. They claimed that the symbol was not made of simple red cloth, but was red-hot with hellfire. They said it could be seen glowing in the dark whenever Hester walked out at night. And we must say, the letter burned so deeply into Hester’s chest that perhaps there was more truth in that story than we would like to believe today.

CHAPTER VI.

PEARL.

We have barely spoken of the baby yet. She was a little creature whose innocent life had sprung, by the mysterious will of God, like a lovely and immortal flower out of a guilty passion. Hester watched the child’s growth with wonder. Every day, the baby became more beautiful, and her intelligence began to shine over her tiny features. Hester named her Pearl. She did not choose the name because the child possessed a calm, white, or quiet beauty. Instead, she named the infant Pearl because she was of great price. Hester had purchased her with everything she owned, and the child was her mother’s only treasure. It was a strange situation. Society had marked the mother’s sin with a scarlet letter, which isolated her completely from human sympathy. Yet God, as a direct consequence of that same sin, had given her a lovely child to hold against her chest. This child connected Hester to the rest of humanity, and was destined to be a blessed soul in heaven. However, these thoughts filled Hester with worry rather than hope. She knew her action had been sinful, so she had no faith that the result would be good. Day after day, she watched the child’s developing personality with fear. She dreaded finding some dark and wild trait that would reflect the guilt that had brought the child into the world.

Physically, the baby was absolutely perfect. With her healthy shape, her energy, and her natural coordination, she was worthy to have been born in the Garden of Eden. She looked like she belonged there, to be a plaything for angels after the first parents were driven out. Pearl possessed a natural grace that made whatever she wore look beautiful. Her mother did not dress her in simple, plain clothes. Instead, Hester bought the richest fabrics available, and used all her creativity to design gorgeous dresses for the child to wear in public. When dressed in these fine clothes, Pearl looked magnificent. Her own natural beauty shone so brightly through the rich fabric that she seemed to light up the dark cottage floor. Yet, she looked just as perfect in a simple, torn dress after playing outside. Pearl's appearance seemed to change constantly. In this one child, you could see many different personalities. She could look like a sweet wild flower or a tiny royal princess. Through all her changes, however, she kept a passionate nature and a deep, bright energy. If she had ever grown pale or quiet, she would have ceased to be herself. She would no longer be Pearl.

This changing appearance reflected her complex inner life. Her nature possessed both depth and variety. However, Hester feared that the child was completely unsuited for the world she was born into. Pearl could not be made to follow rules. Because a great law had been broken in bringing her to life, the child's mind was beautiful but disorganized. Her thoughts followed a unique order of their own that was impossible to understand. Hester could only explain Pearl’s character by remembering her own state of mind during her pregnancy. The mother's intense emotions had shaped the unborn child. Those feelings had stained the baby's spirit with dark shadows, fiery passions, and intense highlights. Above all, the conflict in Hester's soul during that time lived on in Pearl. Hester recognized her own wild, defiant mood and her flighty temper in the child. She even saw the same dark clouds of sadness that had hung over her own heart. In the young child, these traits were currently brightened by childhood energy. But Hester feared they could grow into storms later in the girl's life.

Family discipline in those days was far more rigid than it is now. Strict frowns, harsh lectures, and physical punishment were common. Parents used them not just to punish bad behavior, but to build good character. However, Hester was a lonely mother with only one child, and she was unlikely to be too harsh. Still, remembering her own mistakes, she tried to establish a gentle but firm control over her daughter. But the task was too difficult. She tried both smiles and frowns, but neither had any effect. Eventually, Hester had to step aside and let the child follow her own impulses. Physical restraint worked only while it lasted. As for any other kind of discipline, Pearl only listened when she felt like it. While Pearl was still a baby, Hester noticed a peculiar look that warned her when it was useless to argue, persuade, or plead. It was a highly intelligent, mysterious look. Sometimes it seemed stubborn or even mischievous, but it was usually accompanied by a wild burst of energy. At these moments, Hester wondered if Pearl was a human child. She looked more like a woodland sprite that would play on the floor and then vanish with a mocking smile. When that look appeared in Pearl’s bright, dark eyes, she seemed distant and unreal. It felt as if she might vanish like a flickering light. Seeing this, Hester would rush toward the child, chasing the little girl as she ran away. She would hold her tight and kiss her, not just out of love, but to prove to herself that Pearl was real and made of flesh and blood. But Pearl's laugh, though musical and happy, only made her mother more doubtful.

Saddened by this baffling distance between herself and her only treasure, Hester would sometimes burst into tears. When this happened, Pearl might react in unpredictable ways. Sometimes she would frown, clench her little fist, and stare coldly at her mother. Often, she would simply laugh louder than before, as if she could not understand human sadness. Other times, she would cry with a sudden rage of grief, sobbing out her love for her mother in broken words. She seemed determined to prove she had a heart by breaking it. But Hester could not rely on these sudden bursts of affection, because they passed as quickly as they arrived. Hester felt like a wizard who had summoned a spirit, but did not know the magic word to control it. Her only comfort was when Pearl was asleep. Then, she was sure of her daughter, and enjoyed hours of quiet, sad happiness, until Pearl woke up, often with that mischievous look returning to her eyes.

Pearl opened up very quickly. She was soon old enough to talk and play with others, rather than just playing with her mother. It would have made Hester so happy to hear Pearl’s clear, bird-like voice playing with other children. But this could never happen. Pearl was born as an outcast in the children’s world. Because she was the product of sin, she was excluded from playing with other baptized children. Pearl seemed to understand her loneliness instinctively. She recognized the circle that isolated her from other children. Ever since leaving the prison, Hester never went out in public without Pearl. The child walked beside her, holding her mother's finger and taking several quick steps for every one of Hester's. Pearl saw the local children playing their serious games on the streets. They played at going to church, whipping Quakers, fighting native tribes, or pretending to be witches. Pearl watched them closely, but she never tried to join them. If they spoke to her, she did not answer. If they gathered around her, she would grow furious. She would throw stones at them and scream in a high, wild voice, making Hester tremble because the cries sounded like witch curses in a foreign language.

The truth was that the Puritan children had learned to look down on Hester and Pearl. They sensed that there was something strange and unearthly about them, so they often called them names. Pearl felt their scorn and returned it with deep hatred. These bursts of anger actually brought some comfort to Hester, because they showed a clear and genuine emotion instead of Pearl's usual unpredictable moods. Still, it terrified Hester to see her own anger reflected in the child. Pearl had inherited this passion directly from Hester’s heart. Mother and daughter stood together in their isolation, and Pearl seemed to carry the same troubled spirit that had tormented Hester before the child was born.

At home, in and around the cottage, Pearl had plenty of playmates. Her creative imagination brought a thousand objects to life, like a torch lighting a fire. The simplest materials, like a stick, a bunch of rags, or a flower, became characters in her games. Her single voice spoke for many imaginary characters, both old and young. The black, solemn pine trees, groaning in the wind, became the Puritan elders. The ugly weeds in the garden became their children, whom Pearl chopped down and uprooted without mercy. It was amazing to see how quickly she changed her games. She danced with endless energy, and then collapsed when she was tired, before starting a new, wild game. In this way, she was like other highly creative children, except that she had no real playmates. What was truly strange was that she was always hostile toward the characters she created. She never imagined a friend. Instead, she seemed to create enemies to fight against. It was deeply sad for Hester to watch this. She knew her own conflict had caused Pearl to see the world as a hostile place at such a young age.

Watching Pearl, Hester would often drop her sewing onto her knees and cry out in pain. “Father in Heaven,” she would whisper, “if you are still my Father, what is this child I have brought into the world?” Pearl, hearing her mother's words, would turn her beautiful face, smile with a mysterious look, and go back to her play.

There was one other strange thing about Pearl’s behavior. The very first thing she noticed in her life was not her mother’s smile. Instead, the first object she became aware of was the scarlet letter on Hester’s chest. One day, as Hester leaned over the cradle, the baby noticed the gold embroidery around the letter. She reached up and grabbed it, smiling with a sharp look that made her face look much older. Hester gasped and instinctively clutched the letter, trying to pull it away from the child's touch. But Pearl looked into her eyes and smiled, as if her mother's pain was a game. From that moment on, Hester never felt safe or at ease with her child, except when Pearl was asleep. Weeks might pass without Pearl looking at the letter, but then she would suddenly stare at it again with that same mysterious smile.

Once, that mysterious look came into Pearl’s eyes while Hester was looking at her own reflection in them. Suddenly, Hester fancied she saw another face in the dark reflection of Pearl’s eye. It was not her own face, but a mischievous, malicious face that looked like someone she knew well, though she had never seen them smile with malice before. It felt as if an evil spirit was peeping out of the child in mockery. Hester was often troubled by this same illusion afterward.

One summer afternoon, after Pearl was old enough to run around, she gathered wild flowers and threw them one by one at her mother’s chest. She danced up and down like an elf whenever she hit the scarlet letter. Hester's first reaction was to cover the letter with her hands. But she stopped herself, choosing to sit pale and still, looking sadly into Pearl's eyes. The flowers kept hitting the mark, causing Hester deep pain. Finally, out of flowers, the child stood and stared at her mother with a mischievous look in her dark eyes. “Child, what are you?” Hester cried. “I am your little Pearl!” the child answered. But as she said it, she laughed and danced around like a little imp.

“Are you really my child?” Hester asked. She did not ask the question completely in jest. Pearl was so intelligent that Hester sometimes wondered if the child knew the secret of her birth. “Yes, I am little Pearl!” the child repeated, continuing to dance. “You are not my child! You are no Pearl of mine,” Hester said half-playfully, as she often tried to joke even in her deepest pain. “Tell me, then, what you are, and who sent you here.”

“Tell me, mother!” the child said seriously, coming close to Hester's knees. “You tell me!” “Your Heavenly Father sent you,” Hester answered. But she hesitated slightly, and the clever child noticed it immediately. Pearl put up her finger and touched the scarlet letter. “He did not send me!” she cried. “I have no Heavenly Father!”

“Hush, Pearl, hush! You must not talk like that!” Hester answered, holding back a groan. “He sent us all into this world. He sent me, and he certainly sent you. If not, you strange child, where did you come from?” “Tell me! Tell me!” Pearl laughed, dancing around the floor. “You must tell me!” But Hester could not answer. She was lost in her own doubts. She remembered how the neighbors rumored that Pearl was a demon child born from sin to serve some wicked purpose. The Puritans often whispered such stories about children born under unusual circumstances, just as old stories claimed that Martin Luther was born from a hellish breed.

CHAPTER VII.

THE GOVERNOR’S HALL.

One day, Hester Prynne went to the mansion of Governor Bellingham to deliver a pair of gloves. She had embroidered and fringed them according to his order, to be worn at a major state event. Although a recent election had caused the former governor to lose his highest office, he still held an honorable and influential position among the colony's magistrates.

Hester had another, far more important reason than delivering gloves to seek an interview with such a powerful leader. She had heard rumors that some of the town's prominent leaders wanted to take her child away. These strict religious citizens believed that Pearl was born from a demon. They argued that for the sake of Hester’s soul, they should remove this obstacle from her path. On the other hand, if the child was capable of growing into a good Christian and being saved, they believed she would have a better chance under the care of wiser and better guardians than Hester. Governor Bellingham was said to be one of the main leaders supporting this plan. It may seem strange, and even ridiculous, that a family matter like this was discussed publicly by major statesmen. Today, such an issue would be handled by local town officials. In those early times, however, minor personal matters were often mixed up with the business of the state. Just a few years earlier, a dispute over who owned a pig had caused a fierce battle in the legislature, and had even changed the structure of the colonial government.

Deeply worried, but confident in her own rights, Hester Prynne set out from her lonely cottage. She felt that a single woman, supported by natural motherly love, could stand equal against the entire community. Little Pearl walked beside her. She was now old enough to run lightly along, and she had enough energy to walk a much longer journey. Often, out of whim rather than tiredness, she would ask to be carried, only to demand to be set down again immediately. She skipped ahead of Hester on the grassy path, tripping and tumbling playfully. Pearl possessed a rich, bright beauty with a healthy complexion, intense dark eyes, and deep brown hair that would turn black as she grew older. She was filled with a fiery energy, like the natural product of a passionate moment. Hester had used all her imagination to design the child’s outfit. She dressed Pearl in a crimson velvet tunic of a unique cut, covered with gold-thread embroidery. This bright color, which would make a pale child look sickly, was perfect for Pearl’s vivid beauty, making her look like a bright little flame dancing along the path.

But the most remarkable thing about Pearl’s outfit, and her entire appearance, was that it reminded everyone of the scarlet letter on Hester’s chest. Pearl was like a living version of the scarlet letter. It was as if the red symbol of shame was burned so deeply into Hester’s mind that she could not help but recreate its shape. Hester had spent hours designing the clothes to create this connection between the child she loved and the symbol of her guilt. In truth, Pearl represented both her mother's love and her mother's shame, and her clothing perfectly matched that reality.

As they entered the town, the Puritan children looked up from their serious play. They spoke solemnly to one another. “Look, there is the woman with the scarlet letter, and the letter itself is running along beside her! Come, let's throw mud at them!” But Pearl was a brave child. She frowned, stamped her foot, and shook her fist threateningly. Then, she suddenly rushed at the group of children and chased them all away. In her fierce pursuit, she looked like a little angel of judgment sent to punish the town's children. She screamed so loudly that the running children were terrified. Once she won the battle, Pearl returned quietly to her mother, looked up, and smiled.

They reached Governor Bellingham’s mansion without any more trouble. It was a large wooden house. In those days, the house looked new and cheerful, with sun shining through its windows. The walls were covered with a stucco plaster mixed with fragments of broken glass. When the sun shone on the front of the house, it glittered and sparkled as if hands full of diamonds had been thrown against it. This brilliant sparkle looked more like Aladdin’s palace than the home of a serious Puritan leader. The plaster was also decorated with strange diagrams and patterns that were popular in that era, which had hardened into durable decorations.

Pearl looked at this bright, glittering house and began to dance around. She demanded that Hester strip the sunshine off the walls and give it to her to play with. “No, my little Pearl!” her mother said. “You must gather your own sunshine. I have none to give you!”

They walked up to the arched door. The entrance was flanked by two small towers with lattice windows and wooden shutters. Hester lifted the iron knocker and knocked. One of the Governor’s servants answered. He was a free-born Englishman who had sold himself into seven years of service, making him the temporary property of his master. He wore the blue coat that was the traditional uniform for servants in England. “Is Governor Bellingham in?” Hester asked. “Yes, he is,” the servant replied, staring with wide eyes at the scarlet letter, which he had never seen before. “His honor is inside. But he has some ministers and a doctor with him, so you cannot see him right now.” “I will come in anyway,” Hester replied. The servant, judging by her confident manner and the glittering symbol on her chest that she must be a lady of high standing, did not try to stop her.

The servant admitted Hester and Pearl into the entrance hall. Governor Bellingham had designed his house to look like the estates of wealthy gentlemen in England. The hall was wide and tall, extending through the entire depth of the house and connecting all the rooms. One end of the hall was lit by the tower windows. The other end had a large bay window with a deep, cushioned seat. A heavy history book lay on the cushion, placed there for guests to look through. The furniture consisted of heavy, dark chairs and a matching table, both carved with oak flowers in the style of the Elizabethan era. These were family heirlooms brought over from England. On the table stood a large pewter mug, containing the foam of a recently finished draft of ale as a sign of English hospitality.

A row of family portraits hung on the wall, showing Governor Bellingham’s ancestors. Some wore armor, while others wore stately robes. All of them looked stern and severe, as if they were ghosts criticizing the lives of modern men. In the center of the oak-paneled wall hung a suit of armor. Unlike the portraits, this armor was not an antique. It had been made by a skilled armorer in London the same year the Governor moved to New England. The suit included a steel helmet, breastplate, collar, shin guards, gloves, and a sword. The helmet and breastplate were polished so highly that they glowed and reflected light onto the floor. The Governor did not keep the armor just for display. He wore it during military drills and had used it in battle during the Pequot War. Although he was trained as a lawyer in England, the demands of the new colony had turned him into a soldier and a statesman.

Little Pearl was delighted by the gleaming armor. She stood in front of the breastplate, using it as a polished mirror. “Mother,” she cried, “I see you in here! Look, look!” Hester looked to please the child. Because the polished breastplate acted as a curved mirror, the scarlet letter looked giant and exaggerated. It became the most prominent feature of Hester's reflection, making her look as if she were hidden behind it. Pearl pointed up at a similar reflection in the helmet, smiling with her usual mischievous look. This look of playfulness was also exaggerated in the reflection. It made Hester feel as if the mirror was showing a little demon instead of her own child.

“Come along, Pearl,” Hester said, drawing her away. “Come and look at the garden. Perhaps we will see beautiful flowers there.” Pearl ran to the bay window at the end of the hall and looked out at a grassy path lined with simple shrubs. It was clear that the Governor had given up trying to grow a traditional, decorative English garden in the tough New England soil. Cabbages grew in plain sight, and a pumpkin vine had stretched across the path, leaving a giant pumpkin right beneath the window. This lump of vegetable gold looked like the richest ornament the New England earth could produce. There were also a few rosebushes and some apple trees, likely planted by the first settler of the area. Pearl saw the rosebushes and began to cry for a red rose, refusing to be quieted. “Hush, Pearl, hush!” her mother whispered. “Do not cry. I hear voices in the garden. The Governor is coming, and some other gentlemen are with him.” Down the garden path, a group of men walked toward the house. Pearl gave a sudden, wild scream, and then became quiet, because she was curious to see who the new visitors were.

CHAPTER VIII.

THE ELF-CHILD AND THE MINISTER.

Governor Bellingham walked first. He wore a loose gown and a comfortable cap, the kind of clothing elderly gentlemen loved to wear in the privacy of their own homes. He seemed to be showing off his estate and explaining his planned improvements in detail. He wore an elaborate, wide collar beneath his gray beard. It was in the old-fashioned style of King James’s reign, and it made his head look a bit like John the Baptist's head on a platter. His appearance was rigid, severe, and worn with old age. This harsh look did not match the comfortable, luxurious things he had gathered around himself. However, it is a mistake to think that our serious ancestors rejected comfort or luxury when they could get it. They did speak and think of human life as a time of trial and struggle, and they were truly ready to sacrifice their property and lives for duty. Still, they did not feel it was against their conscience to enjoy good things when they were available.

For example, the respected pastor John Wilson never taught such a strict rule. His beard, which was as white as a snowdrift, could be seen over Governor Bellingham's shoulder. Mr. Wilson was suggesting that pears and peaches might eventually grow well in the New England climate. He also thought they might train purple grapes to grow against the sunny garden wall. The old clergyman had been raised in the wealthy Church of England, and he had a long-standing, natural appreciation for good and comfortable things. He could be stern in the pulpit, especially when publicly correcting sins like Hester Prynne's. Even so, his kind and warm private life made people love him far more than any other minister of his time.

Two other guests walked behind the Governor and Mr. Wilson. One was the Reverend Arthur Dimmesdale, who had taken a brief and reluctant part in Hester Prynne’s public disgrace. Walking closely beside him was old Roger Chillingworth, a highly skilled doctor who had settled in the town two or three years earlier. Everyone knew that this learned doctor was also a close friend to the young minister. Dimmesdale's health had suffered severely recently because he worked too hard and sacrificed himself for his church duties.

The Governor walked ahead of his guests and went up a couple of steps. He opened the large hall window and found himself standing right next to little Pearl. The shadow of the curtain fell over Hester Prynne, keeping her partly hidden from view.

“What do we have here?” Governor Bellingham said, looking with surprise at the little red figure in front of him. “I must say, I haven't seen anything like this since my younger days in old King James’s time, when I thought it was a great privilege to attend a court masquerade. A crowd of these little figures used to appear during holidays, and we called them children of the Lord of Misrule. But how did such a guest get into my hall?”

“Yes, indeed!” good old Mr. Wilson cried. “What is this little bird with bright red feathers? I think I have seen figures like this when the sun shines through a stained-glass window, casting golden and red patterns across the floor. But that was back in England. Tell me, little one, who are you? And why did your mother dress you up in this strange way? Are you a Christian child? Do you know your catechism? Or are you one of those mischievous elves or fairies that we thought we left behind in old England, along with other Catholic traditions?”

“I am my mother’s child,” the red figure answered, “and my name is Pearl!”

“Pearl? You look more like a Ruby, or Coral, or a Red Rose at the very least, judging by your color!” the old minister replied. He reached out to pat little Pearl on the cheek, but she pulled away. “But where is your mother? Ah, I see,” he added. He turned to Governor Bellingham and whispered, “This is the same child we were discussing earlier. And look, there is the unhappy mother, Hester Prynne!”

“Is that so?” the Governor cried. “Indeed, we might have guessed that such a child's mother would be a scarlet woman, like the notorious woman of Babylon. But she has arrived at a good time. We will investigate this matter immediately.”

Governor Bellingham stepped through the window into the hall, followed by his three guests.

“Hester Prynne,” the Governor said, looking sternly at the woman wearing the scarlet letter. “There has been a lot of discussion about you lately. We leaders have been debating whether we are doing the right thing by leaving an immortal soul, like the one in your child, under the guidance of someone who has fallen into sin. Speak up, as the child's mother. Do you not think it would be better for your little girl's physical and spiritual well-being if she were taken from you? She could be dressed in plain clothes, disciplined strictly, and taught the truths of heaven and earth. What can you do for the child in this regard?”

“I can teach my little Pearl what I have learned from this!” Hester Prynne answered, pointing her finger at the red symbol on her chest.

“Woman, that is your badge of shame!” the stern magistrate replied. “It is because of the sin that the letter represents that we want to place your child in other hands.”

“Even so,” the mother said calmly, though she grew paler. “This badge has taught me, and continues to teach me every day, lessons that can make my child wiser and better, even if they cannot help me.”

“We will judge carefully,” Bellingham said, “and be sure of what we are doing. Good Mr. Wilson, please examine this Pearl, since that is her name, and see if she has received the Christian upbringing that is proper for her age.”

The old minister sat down in an armchair and tried to pull Pearl between his knees. But the child was not used to being touched by anyone except her mother. She escaped through the open window and stood on the top step outside. She looked like a wild tropical bird with bright feathers, ready to fly away into the sky. Mr. Wilson was quite surprised by this behavior, because he was a grandfatherly man who was usually very popular with children. Still, he tried to go on with his questions.

“Pearl,” he said very solemnly, “you must pay attention to lessons so that, in time, you may have a pure soul. Can you tell me, my child, who made you?”

Pearl knew very well who made her. Hester Prynne had grown up in a religious home, and she had taught her daughter about God soon after Pearl was old enough to talk. Children usually absorb these spiritual truths with great interest, and Pearl had learned a lot in her three years of life. She could have easily passed a test on the New England Primer or the Westminster Catechism, even if she had never seen the actual books. However, a stubborn streak, which all children have, took hold of Pearl at that very moment. She had a double share of this stubbornness, and it made her refuse to speak or say the wrong thing on purpose. She put her finger in her mouth and refused to answer good Mr. Wilson's questions. Finally, the child announced that she had not been made at all, but had been plucked by her mother from the wild rosebush that grew by the prison door.

She probably made up this story because she was standing near the Governor's red roses outside the window, and she remembered the rosebush by the prison door that she had passed on her way here.

Old Roger Chillingworth smiled and whispered something into the young minister's ear. Hester Prynne looked at the doctor. Even though her fate was hanging in the balance, she was shocked to see how much his features had changed since she last knew him. He looked much uglier, his dark skin seemed even darker, and his figure was more deformed. She met his eyes for a second, but she had to turn her attention back to what was happening.

“This is terrible!” the Governor cried, slowly recovering from his shock. “Here is a three-year-old child who cannot even tell us who made her. She must be completely ignorant about her soul, its sinful nature, and her eternal destiny. Gentlemen, I do not think we need to ask any more questions.”

Hester grabbed Pearl and pulled her tightly into her arms. She stared at the old Puritan magistrate with a fierce expression. She was alone in the world and cast out by society, and this child was the only treasure she had to keep her heart alive. She felt she had a natural right to her child that the world could not take away, and she was ready to defend that right to the death.

“God gave me the child!” she cried. “He gave her to replace everything else that you took from me. She is my happiness, but she is my torture too. Pearl keeps me alive, but she also punishes me. Don't you see that she is the living version of the scarlet letter? I can love her, and that gives her a million times more power to punish me for my sin. You shall not take her from me. I will die first!”

“My poor woman,” the old minister said, not unkindly, “the child will be well cared for, far better than you can do it yourself.”

“God gave her to me to take care of,” Hester Prynne repeated, raising her voice to a shriek. “I will not give her up!” By a sudden impulse, she turned to the young minister, Mr. Dimmesdale. Up to this point, she had barely looked at him. “Speak for me!” she cried. “You were my pastor, and you had charge of my soul. You know me better than these men do. I will not lose my child. Speak for me. You understand the feelings that these other men lack. You know what is in my heart, and you know a mother's rights. You know how much stronger those rights are when she has nothing but her child and the scarlet letter. Do something. I will not lose my child. Take care of this!”

The young minister came forward at this wild and unusual appeal, which showed that Hester was driven almost to madness by her situation. He was pale and held his hand over his heart, which was his habit whenever he felt anxious or agitated. He looked more worn and thin now than he did during Hester's public disgrace. Whether it was due to failing health or some other cause, his large, dark eyes showed a deep world of pain.

“There is truth in what she says,” the minister began. His voice was sweet, shaking, yet powerful. It echoed through the hall, making the hollow armor ring. “There is truth in what Hester says and in the feelings that drive her. God gave her the child. He also gave her an instinctive understanding of the child's unique nature and needs, which no other person could have. Furthermore, is there not a sacred quality to the bond between this mother and her child?”

“Yes, how is that, good Mr. Dimmesdale?” the Governor interrupted. “Please explain that clearly.”

“It must be so,” the minister continued. “If we think otherwise, we would be saying that God, the Creator of everyone, does not care about sin. We would be saying that He ignores the difference between sinful lust and holy love. This child, born from her father's guilt and her mother's shame, came from the hand of God. She was sent to work on the heart of the mother who pleads so bitterly to keep her. She was meant to be a blessing, the only blessing in Hester's life. But she was also meant to be a punishment, just as the mother told us. She is a source of torture to be felt at unexpected moments, a sting and a constant agony in the middle of a troubled joy. Hester has even expressed this idea in the child's clothing, which reminds us so strongly of the red symbol on her chest.”

“Well said!” good Mr. Wilson cried. “I was afraid the woman only wanted to turn her child into a showpiece!”

“Oh, no, that is not the case,” Mr. Dimmesdale continued. “Believe me, she recognizes the solemn miracle that God has performed in this child's existence. I believe this gift was given above all to keep the mother's soul alive. It protects her from falling into even darker sins that Satan might tempt her with. Therefore, it is good for this poor, sinful woman to have an immortal soul trusted to her care. This child can experience eternal joy or sorrow. Hester can train her to do right. The child will remind her of her fall at every moment, but she is also a promise from the Creator. If Hester can guide her child to heaven, the child will bring her there too. In this way, the sinful mother has a better chance at salvation than the sinful father. For Hester's sake, and for the child's sake, let us leave them together as God has intended.”

“You speak with unusual earnestness, my friend,” old Roger Chillingworth said, smiling at him.

“And there is great meaning in what our young brother has said,” the Reverend Mr. Wilson added. “What do you say, honored Governor Bellingham? Has he not argued well for the poor woman?”

“Yes, he has,” the magistrate answered. “His arguments are strong enough that we will leave the matter as it is, at least as long as the woman causes no further scandal. Still, we must make sure the child is regularly tested on her catechism, either by you or Mr. Dimmesdale. Also, when she is old enough, the local officials must make sure she goes to both school and church services.”

When the young minister finished speaking, he stepped back a few paces and stood with his face partly hidden in the heavy curtain of the window. The shadow of his body on the floor trembled from the intensity of his speech. Pearl, the wild and unpredictable child, walked softly toward him. She took his hand in both of her own and laid her cheek against it. The gesture was so quiet and tender that Hester, who was watching, asked herself if that was really her Pearl. Yet Hester knew there was love in the child's heart, even if it usually showed as passion. Rarely had Pearl been so gentle in her life. The minister looked down. He laid his hand on the child's head, hesitated for a moment, and kissed her forehead. A child's spontaneous affection is sweet, as if it shows we are truly worthy of love. Pearl’s unusual moment of tenderness did not last long. She laughed and skipped down the hall so lightly that Mr. Wilson wondered if her feet even touched the floor.

“The little thing has witchcraft in her, I declare,” Mr. Wilson said to Mr. Dimmesdale. “She doesn't need an old witch's broomstick to fly!”

“A strange child!” Roger Chillingworth remarked. “It is easy to see the mother's influence in her. Do you think, gentlemen, that a wise man could analyze the child's nature and make a good guess at who her father is?”

“No, it would be sinful to use human science for such a question,” Mr. Wilson said. “It is better to fast and pray about it. Perhaps it is best to leave the mystery alone unless God chooses to reveal it himself. That way, every good Christian man has a right to show a father’s kindness to the poor, abandoned child.”

With the matter settled, Hester Prynne and Pearl left the house. As they walked down the steps, a window was opened. Mistress Hibbins, Governor Bellingham’s bitter sister, looked out into the sunny day. A few years later, she would be executed as a witch.

“Hey there!” she called out. Her dark look seemed to cast a shadow over the bright house. “Will you come with us tonight? There will be a lively gathering in the forest, and I almost promised the Devil that the pretty Hester Prynne would join us.”

“Please give him my excuses!” Hester answered with a triumphant smile. “I must stay home and watch over my little Pearl. If they had taken her from me, I would have gladly gone with you into the forest and signed my name in the Devil's book, using my own blood!”

“We will have you there soon enough!” the witch-lady said, frowning as she pulled her head back inside.

If this conversation actually happened, it showed the truth of the young minister's argument. Separating a mother from the child of her sin would only drive her to evil. Even this early, the child had saved her mother from the Devil's trap.

CHAPTER IX.

THE LEECH.

Under the name of Roger Chillingworth, the reader will remember, was hidden another name. The man who wore it had decided that his real name would never be spoken again. He had stood in the crowd and watched Hester Prynne’s public exposure. He was an elderly, travel-worn man who had just come out of the dangerous wilderness. He had hoped to find a warm, cheerful home with Hester, but instead he saw her displayed as a symbol of sin. Her reputation was ruined, and people in the marketplace whispered about her shame. If the news reached her family and old friends, they would only feel the stain of her dishonor. The man whose relationship with Hester had been the most intimate and sacred decided not to claim his place beside her on the platform of shame. Nobody knew his secret except Hester, and he had the power to keep her quiet. He decided to withdraw his name from the world. He vanished from his old life as completely as if he had drowned in the ocean, which is what the rumors claimed. He would find new interests and a new purpose. This purpose might be dark and even sinful, but it would use all his mental strength.

With this plan in mind, he settled in the Puritan town as Roger Chillingworth. He did not need any introduction other than his own intelligence and learning, which were far greater than average. He had studied medicine earlier in his life, so he chose to practice as a physician. The colony welcomed him warmly, because skilled doctors and surgeons were very rare in the settlement. Such professional men rarely shared the religious enthusiasm that drove other emigrants across the Atlantic. They studied the human body so closely that they often lost their spiritual view of life, focusing instead on the complex physical machine. Before Chillingworth arrived, the health of Boston had been in the hands of an elderly deacon who was also an apothecary. This man's piety and holy behavior were his main qualifications, rather than any medical degree. The only other surgeon in town was a barber who occasionally practiced surgery between shaves. Roger Chillingworth was a wonderful addition to this group. He quickly showed his deep knowledge of old-fashioned medical science. In those days, remedies contained many rare and unusual ingredients, mixed together as if they were trying to create the Elixir of Life. During his time as a captive of the Native Americans, Chillingworth had also learned a lot about local herbs and roots. He openly told his patients that these simple natural medicines, taught to him by the native priests, were just as reliable as the European drugs that doctors had spent centuries developing.

This learned stranger lived a very respectable religious life, at least outwardly. Soon after arriving, he chose the Reverend Mr. Dimmesdale as his spiritual guide. The young minister was famous for his scholarship at Oxford. His devoted followers looked at him as a heaven-sent apostle. They believed that if he lived a normal lifespan, he would do great things for the young New England Church, just as the early Church fathers had done for the Christian faith. Around this time, however, Mr. Dimmesdale's health began to fail. People who knew his habits thought his pale cheeks were caused by too much study, his tireless work for his parish, and his frequent fasting. He often stayed awake all night to pray, trying to keep his spiritual mind free from the grossness of the physical body. Some people said that if Mr. Dimmesdale was going to die, it was simply because the world was not worthy of him. The minister himself, with characteristic humility, said that if God chose to take him, it was because he was too unworthy to perform his humble mission on earth. Although everyone had different ideas about the cause of his decline, nobody could deny that he was growing weaker. He became very thin, and his rich, sweet voice had a sad quality that suggested an early death. Whenever he was startled or surprised, he would put his hand over his heart. First his face would flush, and then it would turn pale with pain.

This was the young minister's condition, and it seemed likely that his life would end early when Roger Chillingworth arrived in town. His first appearance in the settlement had a touch of mystery that people easily turned into something miraculous, since nobody knew where he came from. He seemed to drop from the sky or rise up from the earth. Once people knew he was a skilled doctor, they noticed him gathering herbs, wild flowers, roots, and twigs in the forest. He seemed to know the hidden qualities of plants that looked useless to ordinary people. He also mentioned that he had worked with Sir Kenelm Digby and other famous scientists whose discoveries seemed almost supernatural. People wondered why a man of such high standing in the scientific world had come to this wilderness instead of living in a great city. A rumor spread, which some very sensible people actually believed, that God had performed a miracle by flying a famous doctor from a German university through the air and dropping him right at Mr. Dimmesdale’s study door. Wiser people, who knew that God works through natural events rather than dramatic miracles, simply saw the doctor's timely arrival as a sign of divine providence.

This belief grew stronger because the doctor showed a deep interest in the young minister. He joined Dimmesdale's church and tried to win his friendship and trust, respecting the young man's naturally quiet and sensitive nature. Chillingworth expressed great concern about the pastor's health, but he was eager to try to cure him. He felt hopeful about a recovery if they started the treatment early. The church elders, deacons, motherly women, and young ladies of the congregation all urged Dimmesdale to try the doctor's offered skill. But Mr. Dimmesdale gently refused their pleas.

“I do not need medicine,” he said.

Yet the young minister could not deny his illness. Every Sunday, his cheeks looked paler and thinner, and his voice trembled more. It had become a constant habit for him to press his hand against his heart. People wondered if he was tired of his work or if he wanted to die. The older ministers of Boston and the church deacons spoke to Mr. Dimmesdale solemnly about this. They warned him that it was a sin to reject the medical help that God had clearly provided for him. The minister listened in silence, and he finally promised to speak with the doctor.

“If it were God’s will,” the Reverend Mr. Dimmesdale said when he finally asked for Roger Chillingworth’s advice, “I would be happy to let my labors, my sorrows, my sins, and my pains end with me. I would rather let my earthly troubles be buried in my grave, and send my spirit to its eternal home, than have you use your skill to save my life.”

“Ah,” Roger Chillingworth replied with his usual quietness, “that is how a young minister is likely to speak. Young men have not yet taken deep root in the world, so they give up their hold on life very easily. And holy men who walk with God on earth naturally want to leave and walk on the golden streets of heaven.”

“No,” the young minister replied, putting his hand to his heart as a flash of pain crossed his face. “If I were worthier of heaven, I would be more content to stay and work here on earth.”

“Good men always judge themselves too harshly,” the doctor said.

In this way, the mysterious old Roger Chillingworth became the medical adviser to the Reverend Mr. Dimmesdale. The doctor was interested in the disease, but he was also fascinated by the minister's character. These two men of different ages began to spend a great deal of time together. To help the minister's health and let the doctor gather healing plants, they took long walks along the seashore or in the forest. They talked about many things to the sound of the whispering waves and the wind in the treetops. They also visited each other's studies. The minister enjoyed the company of the scientist, recognizing that Chillingworth had a deep intellect and a freedom of thought that could not be found among other ministers. In fact, Dimmesdale was surprised, and almost shocked, to find such broad views in the doctor. Dimmesdale himself was a true priest, deeply religious and respectful of authority. His mind naturally followed the path of his church's creed, and his beliefs grew deeper over time. Under no circumstances would he have been considered a man of liberal views. He needed the structure of his faith to support him, even if it limited his thinking. Still, he felt a quiet pleasure in looking at the universe through the eyes of a different kind of thinker. It was like opening a window to let fresh air into a stuffy study where his life was wasting away among books and dim lamplight. But this intellectual air was too cold to breathe for long. The minister and the doctor would always return to the orthodox beliefs defined by their church.

Roger Chillingworth watched his patient very closely. He observed him in his daily life, following his usual path of familiar thoughts, and also saw how he reacted to new moral ideas that might reveal hidden parts of his character. The doctor felt it was essential to know the man completely before trying to heal his body. Whenever a person has a strong mind and heart, physical diseases are deeply connected to their emotions. In Arthur Dimmesdale, the mind and imagination were very active, and his feelings were intense. His physical illness was likely rooted in his soul. So Roger Chillingworth, acting as a kind and friendly doctor, tried to go deep into his patient’s inner life. He examined his principles, pried into his memories, and tested everything with a cautious touch, like a treasure hunter in a dark cave. Few secrets can escape a skilled investigator who has the opportunity and permission to look for them. A man carrying a secret should especially avoid getting close to his doctor. If the doctor has natural intelligence and intuition, and if he is not too self-centered, he can easily connect his mind with the patient's mind. Before long, the patient will speak aloud things he thought he only kept in his mind. If the doctor receives these confessions quietly, showing understanding with silence or a simple word rather than dramatic sympathy, the patient's soul will eventually open up. It will flow out like a dark but clear stream, bringing all its mysteries into the light of day.

Roger Chillingworth had all of these qualities. As time passed, the two men developed a close relationship. They discussed ethics, religion, public affairs, and human character. They talked about things that seemed personal to them, yet the minister never revealed any secret. The doctor suspected that Dimmesdale had not even told him the true nature of his physical illness. It was a strange and stubborn silence.

After a while, Roger Chillingworth suggested that they move in together, and the minister's friends arranged it. This allowed the doctor to watch the minister's daily life closely. The townspeople were very happy about this arrangement. They believed it was the best thing for the young minister's health, unless he chose to marry one of the many young women in the congregation who admired him. However, there was no sign that Arthur Dimmesdale would ever marry. He rejected all suggestions of marriage, acting as if ministers should remain single. Since he chose to live alone, eating at other people's tables and missing the warmth of his own family, it seemed perfect that this wise, experienced, and kind doctor was always nearby to help him.

The two friends moved into the house of a religious widow. Her house stood near the spot where King’s Chapel was later built. The house sat next to the cemetery, which had originally been Isaac Johnson's land. This location was well suited to bring serious thoughts to both the minister and the doctor. The kind widow gave Mr. Dimmesdale a front room with plenty of sunshine and heavy curtains to block the light when he wanted to rest. The walls were decorated with tapestries showing the biblical story of David, Bathsheba, and Nathan the Prophet. The colors were still bright, making the characters look vivid and dramatic. In this room, the pale minister stacked his library of heavy books, written by church fathers and rabbis, which even Protestant ministers read. On the other side of the house, Roger Chillingworth set up his study and laboratory. It was not a modern laboratory, but it had a distilling apparatus and tools for mixing chemicals and drugs, which the doctor knew how to use well. The two learned men lived in their own spaces but visited each other often, keeping a close eye on each other's work.

The minister's closest friends believed that God had arranged all of this in response to their prayers, hoping to restore the young man's health. But other people in the community began to take a different view of the relationship between Dimmesdale and the mysterious doctor. When ordinary people try to judge things, they are often deceived by appearances. However, when they judge using their instincts and hearts, their conclusions are often very accurate and true. The people in the town could not prove their suspicions about Roger Chillingworth with facts, but they felt uneasy anyway. An old craftsman who had lived in London during a famous murder case thirty years earlier claimed he had seen the doctor there under a different name. He said the doctor had been associated with a famous old magician involved in that murder. Other people whispered that during his captivity, the doctor had learned black magic from Native American priests, who were known for performing strange cures using the dark arts. Many sensible people also noticed that Chillingworth's appearance had changed since he moved in with the minister. At first, his face was calm, thoughtful, and scholarly. Now, there was something ugly and evil in his look, which grew more obvious the more they looked at him. The common people believed that the fire in his laboratory came from hell, and that the smoke was making his face look dark and dirty.

In short, a popular opinion grew that Arthur Dimmesdale was being haunted by the Devil or his messenger in the form of Roger Chillingworth. They believed this evil agent had permission from God to get close to the minister and try to destroy his soul. No sensible person doubted that the minister would win this spiritual battle in the end. The people hoped to see him emerge from the conflict even holier and more glorious than before. Still, it was sad to think of the terrible suffering he had to endure during this struggle.

Unfortunately, the look of gloom and terror in the young minister's eyes showed that the battle was very difficult, and the victory was far from certain.

CHAPTER X.

THE LEECH AND HIS PATIENT.

Throughout his life, Roger Chillingworth had been a calm and kind man. Although he did not have warm feelings, he had always been upright and honest. When he began to study the young minister, he believed he was acting with the fairness of a judge who only wanted the truth. He thought he was solving a simple geometry problem, rather than dealing with human emotions and the wrongs done to himself. But as he went on, a terrible fascination took hold of the old man. A fierce necessity grabbed him and never let him go. He dug into the minister's heart like a miner searching for gold, or like a grave digger searching for a buried jewel, though he was only likely to find decay and corruption. It was a dark search, and it threatened his own soul.

Sometimes a blue, threatening light burned in the doctor's eyes, like the reflection of a furnace. The soil he was digging in had encouraged him.

“This man,” Chillingworth said to himself, “looks pure and holy to everyone. But he has inherited a strong animal nature from his parents. Let us dig a little further in this direction.”

The doctor searched deep into the minister's mind. He found many precious qualities, like high goals for his people, a warm love for others, pure thoughts, and a deep religious devotion strengthened by study. But these valuable things were like rubbish to the doctor, who was looking for something else. He would turn back discouraged and start his search in a different direction. He moved as quietly and cautiously as a thief entering a room where a man lies half-awake, planning to steal his most guarded treasure. In spite of his caution, the floor would sometimes creak or his clothes would rustle. The shadow of his presence would fall across his victim. Arthur Dimmesdale, whose sensitive nerves gave him a kind of spiritual intuition, would feel a vague sense that something dangerous to his peace was near him. But Roger Chillingworth also had quick instincts. Whenever the minister looked up startled, the doctor sat there as a kind, watchful, and sympathetic friend.

Dimmesdale might have seen the doctor's true character if his illness had not made him suspicious of everyone. Because he trusted no one, he could not recognize his actual enemy. He kept up their daily meetings, receiving the doctor in his study or visiting the laboratory to watch weeds being turned into powerful medicines.

One day, Dimmesdale sat with his head in his hand, leaning on the window sill. He looked out at the graveyard while Chillingworth examined a bundle of plants.

“Where did you gather those herbs with the dark, flabby leaves, my kind doctor?” the minister asked. He looked at them sideways, because he rarely looked directly at anything these days.

“Right here in the graveyard,” the doctor answered, without stopping his work. “They are new to me. I found them growing on a grave that had no tombstone. These ugly weeds are the only things left to remember the dead man. They grew out of his heart. Perhaps they represent some dark secret that he buried with him, which he should have confessed while he was alive.”

“Perhaps,” Dimmesdale said, “he wanted to confess but could not.”

“Why not?” the doctor asked. “All the powers of nature call for the confession of sin. These black weeds have grown out of a buried heart to show an unspoken crime.”

“That is just a fantasy of yours, my good doctor,” the minister replied. “Only God’s mercy can reveal the secrets buried in a human heart. The guilty heart must hold its secrets until the day when all hidden things are revealed. I do not believe the Bible teaches that this final revelation is meant as a punishment. That would be a shallow view. I believe these secrets are revealed at the end of time to satisfy the minds of all intelligent beings, who will stand waiting to see the problems of life made clear. A knowledge of men’s hearts is necessary to solve those problems. I believe that those who hold these miserable secrets will yield them up at the last day, not with reluctance, but with unspeakable joy.”

“Then why not reveal them now?” Roger Chillingworth asked, looking sideways at the minister. “Why don't the guilty find that relief today?”

“Most of them do,” the minister said, clutching his chest as if he felt a sudden throb of pain. “Many souls have confessed their sins to me, not only on their deathbed, but while they were still healthy and respected. I have seen the immense relief they feel after pouring out their hearts. It is like drawing fresh air after choking on one's own breath. Why should a guilty man keep a secret buried like a corpse in his heart, rather than throwing it out and letting God take care of it?”

“Yet some men still bury their secrets,” the doctor observed.

“True, there are such men,” Dimmesdale answered. “Some are kept silent by their very nature. Others, even though they are guilty, still want to serve God and help their fellow men. They shrink from showing their sins because they know that once they are exposed, they can do no more good in the world. So, they live with unspeakable torment. They walk among us looking as pure as new snow, while their hearts are spotted with sins they cannot escape.”

“These men are only deceiving themselves,” Chillingworth said, speaking with more energy than usual and gesturing with his finger. “They are afraid to face the shame they deserve. They may still have holy impulses, but if they want to glorify God, they should not lift unclean hands to heaven. If they want to serve their fellow men, they should show the power of conscience by humbling themselves. Do you believe that a false appearance can serve God better than the truth? Trust me, these men are fooling themselves.”

“It may be so,” the young minister said, waving the discussion away as if it were not important. He was skilled at escaping topics that made him anxious. “But now, I want to ask my doctor if his care has helped my weak body.”

Before Roger Chillingworth could answer, they heard a child's wild laughter from the graveyard. They looked out the window and saw Hester Prynne and little Pearl walking along the path. Pearl looked beautiful, but she was in a mischievous mood. She skipped from one grave to another. When she reached a flat, stone tomb, she began to dance on it. Her mother told her to behave, but Pearl ignored her. Instead, she gathered prickly burrs from a tall weed growing near the tomb. She arranged these burrs along the lines of the scarlet letter on Hester’s breast, and the burrs stuck to the fabric. Hester did not pull them off.

Roger Chillingworth came to the window and smiled down grimly.

“That child has no respect for authority or human opinion,” he remarked, speaking as much to himself as to the minister. “The other day, I saw her splash the Governor with water at the trough. What is she? Is she entirely evil? Does she feel affection? Does she have any guiding principles?”

“She only has the freedom of a broken law,” Dimmesdale answered quietly, as if speaking to himself. “I do not know if she is capable of good.”

The child must have heard them, because she looked up at the window with a bright, naughty smile. She threw one of the prickly burrs at the minister. The sensitive clergyman shrank back in fear. Seeing his reaction, Pearl clapped her hands in delight. Hester also looked up. All four of them looked at each other in silence until the child laughed and shouted, “Come away, mother! Come away, or the old Black Man will catch you! He has already caught the minister. Come away, or he will catch you too! But he cannot catch little Pearl!”

She pulled her mother away, dancing and skipping among the graves like a creature that had nothing to do with the dead. It was as if she were a new kind of being who had to live by her own laws.

“There goes a woman,” Chillingworth said after a pause, “who has no mystery of hidden sin. Do you think Hester Prynne is less miserable because the scarlet letter is out in the open?”

“I believe so,” the minister answered. “Yet I saw the pain in her face, and I wish I had not. Still, it must be better for the sufferer to show his pain, as Hester does, than to cover it up in his heart.”

There was another pause, and the doctor began arranging his plants again.

“You asked me earlier about your health,” he said.

“I did,” the minister replied, “and I want to hear your honest opinion, whether it means life or death.”

“To speak plainly,” the doctor said, keeping a close eye on Dimmesdale, “your illness is strange. The physical symptoms are clear enough, and I believe a careful doctor could cure you. But the disease itself is something I seem to know, yet cannot fully understand.”

“You speak in riddles, doctor,” the minister said, looking out the window.

“Let me speak more plainly, and I ask your pardon if I offend you,” the doctor continued. “As your friend and your doctor, let me ask: have you told me everything about this illness?”

“How can you doubt it?” the minister asked. “It would be foolish to call a doctor and then hide the pain.”

“Do I know everything then?” Chillingworth asked, staring intensely at the minister's face. “A doctor who only knows the physical symptoms often knows only half the disease. A physical illness is sometimes just a symptom of a spiritual wound. Forgive me, but you are a man whose body is deeply connected to his spirit.”

“Then I do not need to ask any more,” the minister said, rising quickly from his chair. “I assume you do not practice medicine for the soul.”

“A sickness in your spirit immediately shows itself in your body,” Chillingworth continued, standing up to face the pale minister. “How can I heal your body unless you first show me the wound in your soul?”

“No! Not to you! Not to an earthly doctor!” Dimmesdale cried passionately, staring fiercely at Chillingworth. “If my soul is sick, I will commit myself to God. He can cure or he can kill. Let Him do what is right. But who are you to interfere? How dare you stand between a sufferer and God?”

With a wild gesture, the minister rushed out of the room.

“It was good to take this step,” Chillingworth said to himself, watching the minister leave with a grave smile. “I have lost nothing, and we will be friends again soon. But look how passion drives him. He has done a wild thing in the past, in the hot passion of his heart.”

It was not difficult to restore their friendship. After a few hours alone, the minister realized he had let his temper get the better of him. The doctor had done nothing to deserve such anger. Dimmesdale felt remorseful and apologized to his friend, asking him to continue his care. Chillingworth agreed and went on with his treatment. But every time the doctor left the minister's room, a puzzled smile appeared on his face.

“A rare case,” he muttered. “I must look deeper. There is a strange connection between his body and soul. For the sake of science, I must search this matter to the bottom.”

Not long after this, the minister fell into a very deep sleep while sitting in his chair with a large book open before him. His sleep was usually very light, like a bird on a branch. But his mind had withdrawn so deeply into itself that he did not move when Roger Chillingworth entered the room. The doctor walked up to him, laid his hand on his chest, and pushed aside his clothing.

Mr. Dimmesdale shuddered and moved slightly, but did not wake.

After a moment, the doctor turned away.

His face showed a wild mix of wonder, joy, and horror. He felt a ghastly rapture that showed in his ugly figure. He threw his arms toward the ceiling and stamped his foot in triumph. If anyone had seen him at that moment, they would have known how the Devil looks when he wins a precious soul.

But what made the doctor's joy different from the Devil's was the look of pure wonder on his face.

CHAPTER XI.

THE INTERIOR OF A HEART.

After the incident in the study, the relationship between the minister and the doctor changed. Although their interactions looked the same on the outside, they were very different in reality. Roger Chillingworth now had a clear path before him. He appeared calm, gentle, and passionless, but a quiet depth of malice was active within him. The unfortunate old man imagined a more intimate revenge than any mortal had ever taken on an enemy. He wanted to make himself the minister's one trusted friend. In this way, Dimmesdale would confide all his fear, remorse, and agony to Chillingworth alone. The guilty sorrow, which the world would have pitied and forgiven, was revealed only to this pitiless and unforgiving doctor. All that dark secret was shared with the very man who wanted to destroy him.

The minister's shy reserve had kept this plan from working completely. However, Chillingworth was still satisfied with the situation. He felt that a revelation had been granted to him, and it did not matter whether it came from heaven or hell. With this knowledge, he could see and understand every movement of Dimmesdale's inmost soul. He was no longer just a spectator, but a chief actor in the poor minister's inner world. He could play upon the minister's feelings as he chose. If he wanted to cause him pain, he knew exactly which nerve to touch. If he wanted to startle him with fear, he could easily raise ghosts of death and shame to point at the minister's chest.

All of this was done so subtly that the minister never knew the cause of his suffering. He only felt a vague, evil influence watching over him. Dimmesdale looked at the deformed figure of the doctor with doubt, fear, and even hatred. He hated the doctor's gestures, his walk, his gray beard, and even his clothes. This was a clear sign of a deep hatred in the minister's chest, though he was not willing to admit it to himself. Because he could not find a logical reason for his distrust, Dimmesdale believed the problem lay in his own diseased mind. He blamed himself for these bad feelings and did his best to root them out. Since he could not do so, he forced himself to remain social and friendly with the old man. This gave Chillingworth constant opportunities to carry out his torment.

Despite his physical illness and spiritual torture, Mr. Dimmesdale became incredibly popular in his work. He won this popularity largely because of his sorrows. His intellectual gifts, moral insights, and power to communicate emotion were kept active by his daily pain. His fame grew larger than the reputations of his fellow ministers, even though some of them were highly respected scholars. There were scholars in the colony who had spent more years studying theology than Dimmesdale had been alive. There were also men with sturdier minds and a firmer grasp of doctrine, who made highly respectable and effective leaders. There were saintly fathers who had spent their lives reading books and communicating with the spiritual world. All these holy men lacked only one gift, which was the ability to speak to the human heart in its native language. These apostolic fathers spoke from high peaks of faith, but their voices were distant and hard to understand.

Mr. Dimmesdale naturally belonged to this group of saintly men. He would have climbed to the highest peaks of faith if his secret sin had not kept him down. His burden kept him on a level with the lowest sinners. Yet this very burden gave him a deep sympathy with the sinful brotherhood of mankind. His heart vibrated in unison with theirs, allowing him to share their pain and speak with a sad, persuasive eloquence. The people did not understand the power that moved them. They thought the young minister was a miracle of holiness, a messenger of divine wisdom and love. In their eyes, the very ground he walked on was holy. The young women of his church admired him with a devotion that they believed was entirely religious. The elderly members of his congregation, seeing how weak he was, believed he would die before them. They told their children to bury them close to their young pastor’s grave. Meanwhile, when Dimmesdale thought of his grave, he wondered if grass would ever grow on it, because an accursed thing would be buried there.

The public adoration tortured him. He loved the truth and valued it above all else. He felt like a shadow, completely empty of value. He longed to stand in his pulpit and tell the people what he really was. He wanted to shout that the pastor they respected and trusted was actually a pollution and a lie.

More than once, Mr. Dimmesdale had gone into the pulpit intending not to leave until he had confessed. More than once, he had cleared his throat and taken a deep breath to speak his secret. He had actually spoken to them, telling his congregation that he was vile, a companion of the worst sinners, and an abomination. He told them he was surprised his body did not shrivel up before their eyes under God's wrath. But the people only respected him more. They did not guess the true meaning of his words. They called him a saint on earth, believing that if someone so holy felt so sinful, their own souls must be in a terrible state. The minister knew his confessions would be viewed this way. He had tried to ease his conscience, but he had only added the sin of hypocrisy. He had spoken the truth, but he had turned it into a falsehood. He loved truth and hated lies, so he hated himself most of all.

His guilt drove him to punish himself in secret. In his closet, under lock and key, he kept a whip. He used it on his own shoulders, laughing bitterly as he struck himself. He also fasted rigorously, not to purify his body, but as penance. He fasted until his knees shook beneath him. He stayed awake night after night, sometimes in total darkness, and sometimes looking at his face in a mirror by a bright light. He tortured himself with constant self-examination, but he could not find peace. During these long nights, his brain reeled and he saw visions. He saw demons grinning at him, and angels flying upward. He saw the dead friends of his youth, his stern father, and his mother turning her face away as she passed. Finally, he saw Hester Prynne leading little Pearl, who pointed first at the scarlet letter on Hester's chest and then at the minister's own breast.

None of these visions ever completely fooled him. He could always force himself to realize they were not solid, unlike his oak table or his heavy books. Yet they felt like the only real things in his life. Guilt steals the substance out of reality, making the universe feel false. To the untruthful man, everything is empty. The only truth that gave Dimmesdale a real existence was his inner pain, and the expression of it on his face. If he had ever smiled and looked happy, he would have ceased to exist.

One night, the minister started from his chair. A new thought had struck him, offering a moment of peace. He dressed carefully in his clerical robes, walked softly down the stairs, and went out into the night.

CHAPTER XII.

THE MINISTER’S VIGIL.

Mr. Dimmesdale walked like a sleepwalker, caught in the shadow of a dream. He eventually reached the spot where Hester Prynne had stood in her first hours of public disgrace seven years ago. The platform was black, weather-stained, and worn. The minister went up the steps.

It was a dark night in early May. The sky was covered in thick clouds. If the crowd that had watched Hester's punishment stood there now, they would not have seen him. The town was asleep, so there was no danger of being discovered. The minister could stand there until morning if he wanted. The only risk was that the cold night air might make him sick and ruin his sermon the next day.

Only the eye of God, which had seen him whip himself in his closet, could see him now. He had been driven there by remorse, but his cowardice always held him back from a public confession. A weak man should not carry such guilt. Guilt is for the strong, who can handle it or throw it off. The minister could do neither, and his guilt only led to vain repentance.

Standing on the scaffold, Dimmesdale felt a great horror. He felt as if the universe were staring at a scarlet letter on his naked chest. He felt a sharp physical pain in his heart, and he shrieked aloud. The sound echoed through the hills.

He covered his face, fearing the town would wake and find him there.

But the town did not wake. Some who heard the cry thought it was a bad dream or the sound of witches riding through the air.

Looking out, Dimmesdale saw Governor Bellingham at a window with a lamp. The Governor looked like a ghost in his nightcap and gown. The cry had startled him. At another window, Mistress Hibbins appeared with a lamp, looking sour. She probably thought the noise was made by demons. When she saw the Governor's lamp, she turned hers off and went inside. The Governor also left the window.

Soon, the minister saw a small light approaching. It was Father Wilson carrying a lantern. He was returning from the deathbed of Governor Winthrop, who had died that hour. Dimmesdale imagined calling out to Father Wilson to join him on the scaffold. But he only said it in his mind. Father Wilson walked past without looking up, focused on the muddy path. Once the lantern light faded, Dimmesdale felt the exhaustion of his anxiety.

He began to worry about what would happen in the morning. He imagined the first riser seeing him and bringing a crowd of shocked townspeople to the scaffold. He pictured Governor Bellingham, Mistress Hibbins, Father Wilson, the deacons, and the young women all staring at him in his shame under the morning sun. Overcome by the horror of this image, the minister laughed aloud.

A child's laugh answered him. He recognized Pearl's voice.

“Pearl! Little Pearl!” he called out. Then he whispered, “Hester! Hester Prynne! Are you there?”

“Yes, it is Hester Prynne,” she replied in surprise, walking toward him with Pearl.

Hester explained that she had been at Governor Winthrop's deathbed, measuring him for a shroud, and was walking home.

“Come up here, Hester, you and little Pearl,” the minister said. “You have both stood here before, but I was not with you. Come up, and we will stand together.”

Hester and Pearl went up the steps. The three held hands. As the minister took Pearl's hand, he felt a rush of warmth and energy flow from the mother and child into his cold body.

“Minister!” Pearl whispered.

“What is it, child?” Dimmesdale asked.

“Will you stand here with mother and me tomorrow at noon?” Pearl asked.

“No, my little Pearl,” the minister answered. All his fear of public exposure had returned, and he trembled even as he felt a strange joy. “I will stand with you another day, but not tomorrow.”

Pearl tried to pull away, but the minister held her hand. She asked again if he would promise to hold their hands tomorrow at noon.

“Not then, Pearl,” the minister said, “but another time.”

“What other time?” the child asked.

“At the great judgment day,” the minister whispered. “Then we must stand together before God. But we will not do it in the daylight of this world.”

Pearl laughed.

Suddenly, a bright meteor lit up the sky, illuminating the clouds like a giant lamp. It showed the houses, doorsteps, gardens, and street with the clarity of midday, but with a strange and awful quality. The three stood in this bright light: the minister with his hand over his heart, Hester with her scarlet letter, and Pearl connecting them. Pearl looked at the minister with her usual elvish smile. She pulled her hand away and pointed across the street.

In those days, people saw meteors as messages from God, warning of wars or disease. But the minister, driven by his guilty conscience, saw the meteor as a personal message. He looked up and saw a giant letter “A” written in red light in the sky.

While looking up, the minister realized Pearl was pointing at Roger Chillingworth, who stood near the scaffold. The meteor's light made the doctor look like the devil, standing there with a smile and a scowl. The image was so vivid that it remained in the minister's mind even after the light faded.

“Who is that man, Hester?” Dimmesdale gasped in terror. “I shiver when I look at him. Do you know him? I hate him, Hester!”

Hester remembered her promise to Chillingworth and remained silent.

“I tell you, my soul shivers at him!” the minister muttered. “Who is he? Can you do nothing for me? I have a nameless horror of that man!”

“Minister,” Pearl said, “I can tell you who he is!”

“Quickly, child!” the minister said, leaning down to listen.

Pearl whispered gibberish into his ear.

“Are you mocking me?” the minister asked.

“You were not brave, and you were not true!” the child answered. “You would not promise to hold our hands tomorrow at noon!”

“Pious Master Dimmesdale, can this be you?” Roger Chillingworth called out, walking up to the scaffold. “Scholars often walk in their sleep and need to be looked after. Come, my dear friend, let me lead you home.”

“How did you know I was here?” the minister asked fearfully.

“I knew nothing about it,” Chillingworth replied. “I was caring for Governor Winthrop and was walking home when the light appeared. Come with me, or you will not be able to preach tomorrow. Books trouble the brain, and you should study less to avoid these night fantasies.”

“I will go home with you,” Dimmesdale said. He felt cold and depressed, like someone waking from a nightmare.

The next day was Sunday, and the minister preached a very powerful sermon that brought many souls to God. As he came down the pulpit steps, the sexton handed him a black glove that had been found on the scaffold. The sexton joked that Satan must have dropped it there as a trick, but said a pure hand needs no glove.

“Thank you, my good friend,” the minister said, startled at heart. “Yes, it seems to be my glove.”

“And since Satan stole it, you must handle him without gloves from now on,” the sexton joked. “But did you hear of the sign in the sky last night? A great red letter 'A' was seen, which we interpret to mean 'Angel.' Since Governor Winthrop died last night, it was fitting that a sign should appear.”

“No,” the minister answered, “I had not heard of it.”

CHAPTER XIII.

ANOTHER VIEW OF HESTER.

Hester Prynne was shocked by the minister's condition during their recent meeting. Mr. Dimmesdale's nerves seemed completely shattered. His moral strength had collapsed into a weakness worse than a child's. He felt helpless and defeated, even though his mind remained sharp. In fact, his illness seemed to give his intellect a strange, unhealthy energy. Hester knew secrets that no one else did. She could see that someone was using a terrible system to destroy his peace of mind, adding to the pain of his own guilty conscience. She remembered what this poor, fallen man used to be, and she felt deeply moved by his terror. He had turned to her, an outcast, for help against his hidden enemy. Hester decided that the minister had a right to her help. She had lived away from society for a long time, so she did not judge right and wrong by standard rules. She felt a unique responsibility toward him. All her normal connections to other people, whether made of flowers, silk, or gold, had been broken long ago. Now, only the iron link of their shared crime connected them. Neither of them could break this link, and it brought a duty she had to fulfill.

Hester's position in the community had changed over the years. Pearl was now seven years old. The townspeople had grown used to Hester and the embroidered scarlet letter on her chest. Generally, people respect someone who is prominent but does not interfere with anyone else's interests. Human nature tends to love rather than hate, as long as selfishness does not get in the way. Hatred often turns to love over time, unless something keeps feeding the old anger. Hester never angered the public. She accepted their harsh treatment without complaining, asked for nothing in return for her suffering, and did not demand their sympathy. In addition, she lived a pure and blameless life during her years of shame. Since she had nothing to gain or lose, people believed she chose to live virtuously out of a genuine love for goodness.

Hester never asked for special privileges. She only wanted to breathe the air and earn a living by sewing. Yet, she was always ready to help others. She gave what little she had to the poor, even when they insulted her in return for food and clothes. She was completely devoted when sickness spread through the town. During any crisis, Hester was always there. She entered troubled homes not as a guest, but as someone who belonged there. In those dark rooms, the embroidered letter seemed to glow with a comforting light. The badge of sin became a candle of hope in the sickroom. It even brought peace to dying people in their final moments. In these emergencies, Hester showed her warm and generous nature. She was a constant source of tenderness. Her chest, marked with shame, became a soft pillow for those who needed comfort. She had made herself a Sister of Mercy. The town's harsh judgment had shaped her for this role, even though neither side expected it. The letter became the symbol of her calling. She was so helpful and sympathetic that many people refused to see the scarlet letter as a sign of sin. They said the letter "A" stood for "Able," because Hester was so strong.

Hester only stayed where there was trouble. Once the trouble passed, she vanished. She departed without looking back to see if anyone was grateful. If she met these people on the street, she never raised her head to greet them. If they tried to speak to her, she simply pointed to the scarlet letter and walked past. This behavior could have been pride, but it looked so much like humility that it softened the public's view of her. The public can be demanding. People often refuse to give justice when it is demanded, but they are willing to be generous when someone is humble. The community viewed Hester's quiet attitude as a humble appeal, so they began to treat her with more kindness than she expected or perhaps deserved.

The rulers and educated men took longer than the common people to recognize Hester's good qualities. They had logical reasons to support their prejudices, which made those prejudices harder to change. Still, their harsh attitudes gradually softened over the years. Meanwhile, ordinary citizens had forgiven Hester for her past mistake. They began to view the scarlet letter not as a sign of sin, but as a symbol of her good deeds. They would tell strangers that Hester belonged to the town and was incredibly kind, helpful, and comforting. Of course, they would still whisper about the old scandal, because people like to gossip. But the scarlet letter had become like a cross on a nun's chest. It gave Hester a sacred protection, allowing her to walk safely anywhere. Some believed that if she met thieves, she would remain unharmed. A rumor even spread that an Indian had shot an arrow at the badge, and the arrow had bounced off harmlessly.

The scarlet letter had a powerful and strange effect on Hester's own mind. The warm and delicate parts of her character had withered away under her punishment. She was left with a cold, harsh personality that might have driven friends away, if she had any. Her physical beauty had changed as well. She wore plain, simple clothes, and her manners were very quiet. Her rich, beautiful hair was either cut short or hidden completely under a cap, so that not a single lock shone in the sunlight. Her face no longer invited love, her body seemed majestic but cold like a statue, and her chest no longer offered warmth. She had lost the warmth that makes a woman feel like a woman. This often happens to women who survive extreme hardships. A fragile woman would die under such pressure. A survivor must either lose her tenderness or bury it so deep in her heart that it never shows. The second option was probably true for Hester. A woman who has shut out her feelings can become herself again if she receives the right magic touch. We will see if Hester was ever touched and transformed in this way.

Hester's coldness came from the fact that she spent her life thinking rather than feeling. She stood alone in the world, responsible only for guiding and protecting little Pearl. She had no hope of regaining her place in society, and she did not even want to. She rejected all the rules of the community. In those days, people were beginning to think more freely than they had for centuries. Soldiers had overthrown kings, and thinkers had challenged old prejudices. Hester shared this new spirit. She questioned things in ways that were common in Europe, but the Puritans would have considered her thoughts a worse crime than her sin. In her lonely cottage by the sea, Hester entertained thoughts that no one else in New England dared to think. If others had known about these dangerous ideas, they would have treated them like demons at her door.

People who think the most radical thoughts often live the most quiet and obedient lives. The thoughts themselves satisfy them, so they feel no need to act on them. This was true for Hester. However, she might have acted differently if Pearl had not been born. Without her daughter, Hester might have ended up in history books alongside Anne Hutchinson as the founder of a religious sect. She might have become a prophetess or been executed by the Puritan courts for trying to undermine their church. But raising Pearl gave Hester a practical outlet for her active mind. God had given Hester a little girl to raise under difficult circumstances. The world was hostile, and Pearl's own nature was wild and troubled, showing that she had been born from a forbidden passion. This often made Hester wonder in sadness if it would have been better if the child had never been born.

In fact, Hester wondered the same thing about all women. She asked if life was worth living, even for the happiest of them. She had already decided that her own life was not worth it. Deep thinking can make a woman sad because it reveals how difficult it is to change the world. First, the entire structure of society must be torn down and rebuilt. Then, the very nature of men, which is shaped by centuries of habit, must change before women can be treated fairly. Finally, women themselves must undergo a massive transformation, which might destroy their gentle nature. A woman cannot solve these problems by thinking about them. They can only be resolved through love and feeling. Hester's heart had lost its normal warmth, so she wandered lost in the dark maze of her own mind. She felt surrounded by cliffs and deep chasms, with no comfort or home anywhere. Sometimes, she even wondered if it would be better to send Pearl to heaven and face God's judgment herself.

The scarlet letter had not done its job of curing her thoughts.

However, her meeting with Mr. Dimmesdale on the night of his vigil gave Hester a new purpose. She had seen the intense misery the minister was suffering. He had stopped fighting and was on the verge of going mad. She realized that while his own guilt was painful, his helper was injecting a deadlier poison into his soul. A secret enemy had stayed by his side, pretending to be a friend while playing with the minister's delicate feelings. Hester wondered if she had been wrong to let the minister fall into this trap. Her only excuse was that she saw no other way to save him from ruin except by keeping Roger Chillingworth's identity a secret. She had made a choice back then, and it had turned out to be a terrible mistake. She resolved to fix her error as much as possible. She had grown stronger through years of hardship. She felt ready to face Roger Chillingworth now, unlike the night in the prison when she was overcome by shame. Hester had climbed to a higher place, while the old man had lowered himself by seeking revenge.

Ultimately, Hester resolved to meet her former husband and do everything she could to save his victim. She did not have to wait long. One afternoon, while walking with Pearl in a quiet part of the peninsula, she saw the old doctor. He carried a basket and a staff, and he was searching the ground for roots and herbs to make his medicines.

CHAPTER XIV.

HESTER AND THE PHYSICIAN.

Hester told little Pearl to run down to the water and play with the shells and seaweed while she talked to the herb gatherer. The child ran off like a bird, went barefoot, and walked along the wet shoreline. She stopped occasionally to look into tide pools, which served as mirrors. Pearl saw her own reflection with dark curls and a mischievous smile. Since she had no other playmates, she invited her reflection to hold hands and run a race. The imaginary girl in the pool seemed to beckon her to come in. Pearl stepped into the pool up to her shins and looked at her white feet at the bottom. The water rippled, making her reflection's smile seem to float back and forth.

Meanwhile, Hester walked up to the doctor.

“I want to speak with you,” she said. “It is about something important to both of us.”

“Aha! Does Mistress Hester have something to say to old Roger Chillingworth?” the doctor asked, standing up. “I would be glad to listen. I have heard good news about you. Just yesterday, a magistrate told me that the council has been discussing your situation. They debated whether they could safely allow you to take the scarlet letter off your chest. I urged the magistrate to let them do it immediately.”

“The magistrates do not have the power to take off this badge,” Hester replied calmly. “If I were worthy to be free of it, it would fall off on its own, or it would change into something else.”

“Well, wear it if you prefer,” he said. “A woman must follow her own taste when dressing. The letter is beautifully embroidered and looks very bright on your chest.”

Hester stared at the old man. She was shocked to see how much he had changed over the last seven years. He had grown older, but he still seemed strong and alert. The change was in his expression. The calm, studious look she remembered had completely disappeared. Now, his eyes had an eager, fierce, and secretive glare. He tried to hide this look with a smile, but the smile looked mocking and made his dark expression even more obvious. Occasionally, a red light seemed to flash in his eyes, as if a fire were smoldering in his chest and flared up with anger. He would quickly hide this flash and try to look calm again.

Roger Chillingworth was a clear example of how a person can turn into a devil by doing a devil's work. He had transformed himself by spending seven years analyzing a tortured heart. He enjoyed watching that suffering, and he constantly fed the flames of the minister's pain.

The scarlet letter burned on Hester's chest. She realized she was partly responsible for the ruin of this man's life as well.

“Why do you look at my face so closely?” the doctor asked.

“I see something that would make me cry if I had any tears left,” Hester answered. “But let that go. I want to talk about the minister.”

“What about him?” Roger Chillingworth asked eagerly. He was glad to discuss the subject with the only person who knew his secret. “Actually, I was just thinking about him. Speak freely, and I will answer.”

“When we last spoke seven years ago,” Hester said, “you made me promise to keep your identity a secret. The minister's life and reputation were in your hands, so I felt I had to agree. But I felt guilty because I owed a duty to him. I felt like I was betraying him by keeping your secret. Since then, you have stayed closer to him than anyone else. You follow him everywhere. You are with him when he sleeps and when he wakes. You search his thoughts and torture his heart. You have a hold on his life, causing him to suffer every single day, yet he does not know who you really are. By allowing this, I have been false to the only man I should have protected.”

“What other choice did you have?” Roger Chillingworth asked. “If I had pointed my finger at him, he would have been thrown from his pulpit into a dungeon, or even hanged.”

“It would have been better for him to die then,” Hester said.

“What harm have I done to him?” Chillingworth asked. “No king could have bought the medical care I have given to this miserable priest. Without my help, he would have died from his own guilt within two years of your shared sin. His spirit was not strong enough to bear a burden like your scarlet letter. I could tell you secrets about him, but I will not. I have used all my medical skill to keep him alive. He only breathes today because of me.”

“It would be better if he had died,” Hester repeated.

“You are right,” Chillingworth cried, letting his anger show. “He should have died at once. No man has ever suffered like this minister, and he has suffered right in front of his worst enemy. He has felt a constant evil influence on his life, like a curse. He knew that no friend was watching over him, and that someone was looking into his soul to find evil. But he did not know that person was me. Because he is a minister, he believed he was being tortured by a demon with bad dreams and despair. In reality, it was just my constant presence. I have wronged him, and he has wronged me. I have grown to live only for this revenge. A normal man has turned into a demon to torture him.”

As the doctor spoke, he raised his hands in horror. He looked as if he saw a terrifying monster in a mirror instead of his own face. It was a rare moment when he finally saw his own true moral character. He had probably never looked at himself this way before.

“Haven't you tortured him enough?” Hester asked, seeing his expression. “Has he not paid you back yet?”

“No, he has only increased his debt,” the doctor replied, his anger turning into a dark mood. “Do you remember me as I was nine years ago, Hester? I was already middle-aged, but my life was quiet and peaceful. I spent my time studying to gain knowledge and to help other people. I lived an innocent life and helped many. Do you remember? I was quiet and calm, but I was also kind, true, just, and loving. Was I not like that?”

“You were all that and more,” Hester agreed.

“And what am I now?” he asked, showing all the evil on his face. “I have told you. I am a demon. Who made me this way?”

“I did,” Hester said, shivering. “It was my fault as well as his. Why didn't you seek revenge on me instead?”

“I left you to the scarlet letter,” Chillingworth said, pointing to the badge with a smile. “If that has not punished you, I cannot do any more.”

“It has punished me,” Hester replied.

“I thought so,” the doctor said. “Now, what do you want from me regarding the minister?”

“I must tell him the truth,” Hester said firmly. “He must know who you really are. I do not know what will happen, but I must pay my debt of truth to him. His reputation, his career, and his life are in your hands. The scarlet letter has taught me to tell the truth, even if it is painful. I see no benefit in him living such a miserable, empty life, so I will not beg for your mercy. Do what you want with him. There is no happiness left for him, for me, for you, or for little Pearl. We are all lost in this dark maze.”

“Woman, I almost pity you,” Chillingworth said, admiring her strength and despair. “You have great qualities. If you had met a better love than mine when you were young, this evil might not have happened. I pity you for the good that has been wasted in your life.”

“And I pity you,” Hester answered, “because hatred has turned a wise and good man into a demon. Can you free yourself from this hate and become human again? If not for his sake, then do it for your own. Forgive him, and leave his punishment to God. I said earlier that we are all lost in this maze of guilt. But you still have a chance for good, because you were wronged and you have the power to forgive. Will you reject that chance?”

“Be quiet, Hester,” the old man said sternly. “It is not in my power to forgive. We have no choice in what we do. Your first sin planted the seed of evil, and everything since then has been a dark necessity. We are all just following our fate. Let the black flower grow as it must. Now go, and deal with the minister however you choose.”

He waved his hand and went back to gathering herbs.

CHAPTER XV.

HESTER AND PEARL.

Roger Chillingworth, a deformed old figure whose face haunted people's memories, said goodbye to Hester Prynne. He walked away, stooping toward the ground to gather herbs and dig up roots for his basket. His gray beard almost touched the earth as he crawled along. Hester watched him go, wondering with a strange curiosity if the new spring grass would wither under his feet, leaving a brown trail across the green field. She wondered what kind of herbs he was gathering. She wondered if the earth would produce new poisonous plants just for him, or if his touch would turn healthy plants into poison. She wondered if the sun really shone on him, or if a shadow followed his deformed body wherever he went. Where was he going now? Would he sink into the ground, leaving a dead spot where only poisonous weeds would grow? Or would he sprout bat wings and fly away, looking uglier the higher he flew?

“Whether it is a sin or not,” Hester muttered, watching him go, “I hate the man!”

She felt guilty for feeling this way, but she could not help it. She remembered the old days in England when he would leave his study at night to sit with her by the fire. He used to say he needed her smile to warm his cold heart after studying for so long. Those memories used to seem happy, but now they felt ugly. She wondered how she could have married him. She felt that her greatest mistake was allowing him to hold her hand and letting herself smile at him. It seemed a worse crime for Chillingworth to persuade her that she could be happy with him when she was too young to know better.

“Yes, I hate him,” Hester repeated with more anger. “He betrayed me. He did a worse wrong to me than I did to him.”

Men should be careful when they marry a woman, and they should make sure they win her whole heart. Otherwise, they might share Chillingworth's fate. When a stronger love finally awakens the woman's feelings, she will resent the cold imitation of happiness she was forced to accept. But Hester should have let go of this anger long ago. Her seven years of suffering with the scarlet letter had not yet cured her.

Her thoughts as she watched Chillingworth showed how troubled her mind still was.

Once the doctor was gone, she called her child back.

“Pearl! Little Pearl! Where are you?”

Pearl had kept herself busy while her mother talked to the old man. First, she played with her reflection in a pool, trying to coax the image out. When that did not work, she looked for a way to enter the water. Soon, she realized the image was not real, so she looked for other games. She built little boats out of birch bark and loaded them with snail shells. She launched them into the ocean, though most sank near the shore. She caught a horseshoe crab by its tail, found some starfish, and left a jellyfish to melt in the sun. Then, she threw sea foam into the air, chasing it like snowflakes. She saw a flock of shorebirds feeding near the water, filled her apron with pebbles, and chased them from rock to rock. She was very good at throwing. She thought she hit one gray bird, which flew away with a hurt wing. The little girl felt sad and stopped her game because she did not want to hurt a creature as wild as herself.

Finally, she gathered seaweed of different colors to make a scarf, a cape, and a headdress. She looked like a little mermaid. She had her mother's talent for designing clothes. To finish her outfit, Pearl used green eelgrass to make a letter "A" on her own chest, imitating the scarlet letter she saw on her mother. She looked down at it with great interest, as if she were born to understand what the symbol meant.

“I wonder if mother will ask me what it means,” Pearl thought.

Just then, she heard her mother call. She ran back like a seabird, dancing and laughing as she pointed to the green letter on her chest.

“My little Pearl,” Hester said after a moment, “that green letter on your chest has no meaning. But do you know what the letter on my chest means?”

“Yes, mother,” the child said. “It is the letter A. You taught it to me from my spelling book.”

Hester looked at her closely. Pearl had a strange look in her dark eyes, and Hester could not tell if the child understood the symbol's true meaning. She felt a strong desire to find out.

“Do you know why I wear this letter, Pearl?”

“Yes, I do,” Pearl answered, looking up. “It is the same reason the minister keeps his hand over his heart.”

“And what is that reason?” Hester asked. She tried to smile at the strange answer, but she turned pale. “What does my letter have to do with the minister's heart?”

“That is all I know,” Pearl said seriously. “Ask the old man you were talking to. He might know. But tell me, mother, what does the scarlet letter really mean? Why do you wear it? And why does the minister always keep his hand over his heart?”

She took her mother's hand and stared into her eyes with unusual seriousness. Hester thought the child might be trying to connect with her in a loving, trusting way. Pearl had always been wild and unpredictable, like a spring breeze. Hester loved her deeply but never expected much affection in return. The child would usually play and shout, occasionally kissing her mother's cheek before running off again. But now, Hester thought Pearl was old enough to be a friend and confidant. She saw that Pearl possessed courage, a strong will, pride that could become self-respect, and a hatred of lies. She also had deep feelings, even if they were still undeveloped. Hester believed that Pearl could grow into a noble woman despite her wild nature.

Pearl's interest in the scarlet letter seemed to be a natural part of her character. She had been focused on it since she was a baby. Hester had always thought God designed this to punish her, but now she wondered if it was meant to help her instead. Perhaps Pearl was meant to comfort her mother's cold heart and help her overcome her past passion.

These thoughts ran through Hester's mind. Meanwhile, Pearl held her hand, looked up, and asked the same questions again.

“What does the letter mean, mother? Why do you wear it? And why does the minister keep his hand over his heart?”

Hester wondered what she should say. She decided she could not tell the truth, even if it meant losing her daughter's sympathy.

“Silly Pearl,” Hester said aloud, “what kind of questions are these? Children should not ask about such things. I know nothing about the minister's heart. And I wear the scarlet letter simply for its gold thread.”

In seven years, Hester had never lied about the symbol on her chest. It was as if her guardian spirit left her in that moment, realizing that a new lie had entered her heart. Pearl's serious expression quickly vanished.

But the child did not let the subject go. On the walk home, at supper, and while she was going to bed, Pearl kept asking with a mischievous look in her eyes.

“Mother, what does the scarlet letter mean?”

The next morning, as soon as she woke up, Pearl popped her head up from the pillow and asked the other question.

“Mother, why does the minister keep his hand over his heart?”

“Be quiet, you naughty child,” Hester answered with an anger she had never shown before. “Do not tease me, or I will lock you in the dark closet.”

CHAPTER XVI.

A FOREST WALK.

Hester Prynne remained firm in her decision to tell Mr. Dimmesdale the true character of Roger Chillingworth. For several days, however, she could not find an opportunity to speak to him. She knew he often took quiet walks along the shores of the peninsula or in the nearby hills. There would have been no scandal if she had visited him in his study, because many people went there to confess their sins. However, Hester feared that Chillingworth would interfere. She also felt a nervous guilt that made her imagine suspicion where none existed. Finally, she felt that both she and the minister would need a lot of space to breathe while they had this difficult conversation. For these reasons, Hester decided to meet him only under the open sky.

At last, while helping in a home where the minister had gone to pray, she learned that he had left the day before to visit the Apostle Eliot among his Indian converts. He was expected to return the following afternoon. The next morning, Hester took Pearl and set out. Pearl had to accompany her mother everywhere, even when it was inconvenient.

Once they crossed from the peninsula to the mainland, the road became a simple footpath. It wound into the deep, mysterious forest. The trees stood so thick and dark on both sides, letting in only tiny glimpses of the sky, that Hester was reminded of the moral wilderness she had lived in for so long. The day was cold and gloomy. Thick gray clouds covered the sky, but a light breeze occasionally pushed them aside, letting patches of sunshine dance on the path. This brief sunshine was always far ahead of them. The faint light seemed to slip away as they got close, leaving the path feel even darker.

“Mother,” little Pearl said, “the sunshine does not love you. It runs away and hides because it is afraid of something on your chest. Look, it is playing far ahead. Stand here, and let me run and catch it. I am just a child, so it will not run from me. I do not wear anything on my chest yet.”

“I hope you never will, my child,” Hester said.

“Why not, mother?” Pearl asked, stopping before she started to run. “Won't it grow on me naturally when I am a woman?”

“Run along, child, and catch the sunshine,” her mother replied. “It will soon disappear.”

Pearl ran fast and actually caught the sun. She stood laughing in the middle of the bright light, glowing with energy from her run. The light stayed around the lonely child, as if it enjoyed her company, until her mother walked close enough to step into the light too.

“It will go away now,” Pearl said, shaking her head.

“Look,” Hester said, smiling. “Now I can reach out my hand and catch some of it.”

As she tried, the sunshine vanished. Pearl's face looked so bright that her mother imagined the child had absorbed the light into herself, ready to shine it again when they entered a darker path. Pearl's constant high spirits were a sign of her great energy. She did not suffer from the sadness that many children seemed to inherit in those days. Perhaps her wild energy was a result of the struggle Hester went through before Pearl was born. This energy gave the child a hard, metallic quality. She needed a deep grief to touch her heart and make her capable of sympathy. But there was still plenty of time for Pearl.

“Come, my child,” Hester said, looking around. “We will sit down in the woods and rest.”

“I am not tired, mother,” the little girl replied. “But you can sit if you tell me a story.”

“A story, child?” Hester asked. “About what?”

“Tell me a story about the Black Man,” Pearl said, pulling on her mother's dress and looking up with a mischievous smile. “The old woman said he lives in this forest and carries a heavy book with iron clasps. She said he offers his book and an iron pen to anyone he meets, and they must write their names in blood. Then he puts his mark on their chests. Did you ever meet him, mother?”

“Who told you that story, Pearl?” her mother asked, recognizing the common rumor.

“The old woman by the fireplace in the house where you worked last night,” the child said. “She thought I was asleep. She said thousands of people have met him in these woods and written in his book. She said old Mistress Hibbins was one of them. She also said the scarlet letter is the Black Man's mark on you, and that it glows like fire when you meet him in the forest at midnight. Is that true, mother? Do you go to meet him at night?”

“Have you ever woken up and found me gone?” Hester asked.

“Not that I remember,” Pearl said. “If you are afraid to leave me in our cottage, you can take me with you. I would love to go. But tell me, mother, is there really a Black Man? Did you ever meet him? And is this letter his mark?”

“Wilt thou let me be at peace, if I once tell thee?” asked her mother.

“Yes, if thou tellest me all,” answered Pearl.

“Once in my life I met the Black Man,” Hester said. “This scarlet letter is his mark.”

Talking like this, they walked deep enough into the woods to avoid being seen by travelers. They sat down on a thick bed of moss, which had once been a giant pine tree. They were in a small valley with grassy banks on both sides and a brook flowing through the middle over fallen leaves. Overhanging branches blocked the water, creating dark pools in some places, while in other areas the brook flowed quickly over pebbles and brown sand. The stream faded into the trees and underbrush. The giant trees and rocks seemed to hide the brook, as if they feared its constant babbling would reveal the secrets of the forest. The stream made a gentle, soothing, but sad sound. It sounded like a young child who had never learned to play and spent its life around sad people.

“Oh, foolish, boring little brook,” Pearl cried after listening for a while. “Why are you so sad? Cheer up and stop sighing all the time.”

But the brook had been through such a solemn experience in the forest that it could not stop talking about it. Pearl was like the brook because her life came from a mysterious source and had passed through dark scenes. Unlike the stream, however, Pearl danced, sparkled, and talked happily.

“What is the sad little brook saying, mother?” she asked.

“If you had a sorrow of your own, the brook might tell you about it,” her mother answered. “It is telling me about my own sorrow. But listen, Pearl, I hear footsteps and the sound of someone pushing branches aside. Run and play, and let me speak with the person who is coming.”

“Is it the Black Man?” Pearl asked.

“Go play, child,” her mother repeated. “Do not go too far, and come back as soon as I call you.”

“Yes, mother,” Pearl said. “But if it is the Black Man, can I stay for a moment to look at his big book?”

“Go, silly child,” her mother said impatiently. “It is not the Black Man. You can see him through the trees. It is the minister.”

“So it is,” the child said. “And, mother, he has his hand over his heart. Is that because the Black Man put his mark there when the minister signed the book? Why doesn't he wear the mark on the outside of his clothes like you do?”

“Go now, and you can ask me questions later,” Hester cried. “Do not wander far. Stay close enough to hear the sound of the brook.”

The child walked away singing, following the stream and trying to match its sad tone with her own happy voice. But the stream would not be comforted. It kept telling its sad, mysterious secret. Pearl decided to stop listening to the sad brook. Instead, she began gathering violets, wood anemones, and red flowers growing in the rocks.

Once Pearl was gone, Hester walked toward the forest path but stayed in the shadow of the trees. She saw the minister walking alone, leaning on a staff. He looked tired, weak, and deeply depressed. He looked much worse than he ever did in town, where he knew people were watching him. Here, in the quiet forest, his sadness was completely visible. He walked slowly, as if he had no reason to take another step. He looked like he wanted to lie down at the base of a tree and stay there forever. He did not seem to care about life or death.

To Hester, the minister showed no obvious sign of suffering except for one thing. As Pearl had noticed, he kept his hand over his heart.

CHAPTER XVII.

THE PASTOR AND HIS PARISHIONER.

The minister walked so slowly that he had almost passed before Hester Prynne could speak. Finally, she gathered the strength to call his name.

“Arthur Dimmesdale!” she said quietly at first, then louder and more hoarsely. “Arthur Dimmesdale!”

“Who is speaking?” the minister asked.

He quickly stood up straight, trying to hide his dark mood from any onlookers. He looked toward the voice. Through the dark forest twilight, he saw a figure dressed in dark clothing. He could not tell if it was a woman or a shadow. He felt as if a ghost from his own thoughts had come to haunt him.

He took a step closer and recognized the scarlet letter.

“Hester! Hester Prynne!” he said. “Is it you? Are you alive?”

“I am,” she answered. “I have lived this way for seven years. And you, Arthur Dimmesdale, do you still live?”

They questioned each other's existence because their meeting was so strange. In the dark woods, they felt like two spirits meeting in the afterlife. They stood shivering in fear, not yet used to being ghosts. They were shocked by the sight of each other. The meeting forced them to face their own pasts and feelings. Arthur Dimmesdale slowly and reluctantly reached out his cold hand to touch Hester's. The cold touch helped ease their loneliness. They realized they were both still living in the same world.

Without saying another word, they walked back into the shadow of the forest and sat on the mossy pine trunk where Hester and Pearl had sat earlier. At first, they only spoke about ordinary things like the gloomy sky, the coming storm, and their health. This helped them ease into the deeper subjects in their hearts. Since they had been separated for so long, they needed simple topics to begin their conversation.

After a while, the minister looked at Hester.

“Hester,” he asked, “have you found peace?”

She smiled sadly and looked down at the scarlet letter on her chest.

“Have you?” she asked.

“None,” he answered. “I have found only despair. I could not expect anything else. If I were a man without a conscience or moral feelings, I might have found peace long ago. But God's gifts in me have turned into tools of torture. Hester, I am deeply miserable.”

“The people respect you,” Hester said. “And you do good work among them. Doesn't that bring you comfort?”

“It only makes me more miserable,” the minister replied with a bitter smile. “I cannot believe in the good I seem to do. How can a ruined soul help save others? How can a dirty soul make others clean? I wish the people's respect would turn to hatred. I must stand in my pulpit and look at people who think I am holy. They listen to my words as if I were an apostle, but I know the dark truth about myself. I have laughed in agony at the difference between what I seem to be and what I really am. Even Satan laughs at me.”

“You are too hard on yourself,” Hester said gently. “You have repented deeply. Your sin is in the past. Your life today is just as holy as people believe it is. Your good works show that your repentance is real. Why shouldn't that bring you peace?”

“No, Hester,” the minister said. “My repentance is empty. I have suffered punishment, but I have not found true peace. If I had, I would have confessed my sin to the world long ago. You are lucky to wear the scarlet letter openly on your chest. My letter burns in secret. It is a great relief to look into the eyes of someone who knows who I really am. If I had just one friend, or even an enemy, to whom I could admit my sins, my soul might survive. Even that much truth would save me. But now, my life is completely false, empty, and dead.”

Hester looked at him and hesitated. His words gave her the perfect opportunity to say what she had come to tell him. She overcame her fear and spoke.

“I am the partner of your sin, so I can be the friend you want,” she said with an effort. “But you have also lived with an enemy under your own roof for a long time.”

The minister stood up, gasping for breath and clutching his heart.

“What are you saying?” he cried. “An enemy under my own roof? What do you mean?”

Hester realized how much she had harmed him by letting him live at the mercy of Roger Chillingworth. The presence of his enemy was enough to destroy the peace of a sensitive man like Dimmesdale. In the past, Hester had been too wrapped up in her own troubles to see this. But since the night of his vigil, she understood his feelings better. She knew that Chillingworth had used his role as a doctor to torture the minister's conscience. The old man wanted to drive the minister mad and destroy his soul.

Hester realized she had caused this ruin to the man she still loved deeply. She knew that losing his reputation or even dying would have been better than this torture. She wished she could die at his feet rather than confess this wrong.

“Oh, Arthur,” she cried, “forgive me. I have tried to be true in everything else. Truth was the one virtue I kept, except when your life and reputation were in danger. Then, I agreed to a lie. But a lie is never good, even to avoid death. The old doctor, Roger Chillingworth, was my husband.”

The minister looked at her with sudden anger. It was a dark, fierce look that transformed his face. But he was too weak to stay angry for long. He sank to the ground and buried his face in his hands.

“I should have known,” he whispered. “My heart always shrank from him when I saw him. Why didn't I understand? Oh, Hester, you do not know the horror of this. The shame of exposing my sick heart to his hateful eyes is terrible. You are responsible for this. I cannot forgive you.”

“You must forgive me,” Hester cried, sitting beside him on the leaves. “Let God punish me, but you must forgive.”

She threw her arms around him and pressed his head to her chest, not caring that his cheek touched the scarlet letter. He tried to pull away, but she held him tight so he could not look at her angrily. Hester had survived the town's harsh judgment for seven years, but she could not survive the anger of this weak, pale man.

“Will you forgive me?” she asked again. “Will you stop looking at me with anger? Will you forgive?”

“I do forgive you, Hester,” the minister finally said quietly. He felt only sadness, not anger. “I forgive you freely. May God forgive us both. We are not the worst sinners in the world. The old man's revenge is worse than our sin. He has violated the sanctity of a human heart, but we never did that.”

“Never,” Hester whispered. “What we did had a holiness of its own. We felt it, and we told each other so. Have you forgotten?”

“Quiet, Hester,” Arthur Dimmesdale said, standing up. “No, I have not forgotten.”

They sat down again side by side, holding hands on the fallen tree. It was the darkest hour of their lives, yet they wanted to stay together. The forest around them creaked in the wind, and the branches groaned as if warning them of danger.

Still, they stayed. The path back to town looked gloomy, where Hester would have to face her shame and the minister would have to face his false reputation. For a moment, the dark forest felt better than the town. Here, Hester did not have to hide her scarlet letter from him, and Arthur Dimmesdale could be true for a moment.

Suddenly, a new thought occurred to him.

“Hester,” he said, “there is a new horror. Roger Chillingworth knows you want to reveal his secret. Will he keep our secret now? What will he do for revenge?”

“He is a very secretive man,” Hester replied. “His revenge has made him even more secretive. I do not think he will tell our secret. He will probably look for other ways to satisfy his anger.”

“And what about me?” Arthur Dimmesdale asked. “How can I live in the same house with this deadly enemy?” He pressed his hand to his heart, a gesture that had become automatic. “Think for me, Hester. You are strong. Tell me what to do.”

“You must not live with him anymore,” Hester said firmly. “You cannot stay under his evil influence.”

“That would be worse than death,” the minister said. “But how can I avoid it? What choices do I have? Should I lie down on these leaves and die?”

“You are ruined by your own weakness,” Hester said with tears in her eyes. “There is no other reason for you to die.”

“God's judgment is on me,” the priest replied. “I cannot fight against it.”

“Heaven would show mercy if you were strong enough to take it,” Hester said.

“Be strong for me,” he said. “Tell me what to do.”

“Is the world really so small?” Hester asked, looking into his eyes and using her strength to encourage his weak spirit. “Does the entire universe lie only in that town? Where does this forest path go? It goes back to town, but it also goes deeper into the wilderness. A few miles from here, the path disappears. There, you will be free. You can leave your misery behind and be happy. The forest is large enough to hide you from Roger Chillingworth.”

“Yes, Hester, but only under the fallen leaves,” the minister said with a sad smile.

“Then there is the broad pathway of the sea,” Hester said. “It brought you here. If you choose, it will carry you back. In England, London, Germany, France, or Italy, you would be safe from him. You do not need to care about the opinions of these harsh men in town. They have kept your spirit in prison for too long.”

“I cannot do it,” the minister said, as if he were dreaming. “I do not have the strength to go. Even though I am sinful, I believed I had to stay where God placed me. I want to help other souls, even if my own is lost. I dare not leave my post, even if my reward is death and dishonor.”

“You are crushed by seven years of misery,” Hester said, trying to share her energy with him. “But you can leave it all behind. You do not have to carry it on the path or on a ship. Leave your past here. Start over. You have not run out of options. The future is still full of success and happiness. You can do good and find joy. Change your false life for a true one. You can teach the Indians, or you can be a scholar in Europe. Preach, write, act, and do anything except lie down and die. Give up the name of Arthur Dimmesdale and take a new one that you can wear without shame. Why should you stay here in this torture? Get up and go.”

“Oh, Hester,” Arthur Dimmesdale said, feeling a brief spark of hope before it died out. “You are telling a weak man to run a race. I must die here. I do not have the strength or courage to go into the world alone.”

He was too depressed to reach for a better life.

He repeated the word.

“Alone, Hester.”

“You will not go alone,” she whispered.

Then, everything was settled.

============================================================

CHAPTER XVIII.

A FLOOD OF SUNSHINE.

Arthur Dimmesdale stared into Hester's face with a mixture of hope, joy, and fear. He felt shocked by her boldness. She had spoken the words that he only dared to hint at in his mind.

Hester Prynne possessed a natural courage and an active mind. Since she had been an outcast from society for seven years, she was used to thinking in ways that the minister could not. She had wandered without any guidance in a moral wilderness, which was just as vast and dark as the forest around them. Her mind and heart had lived in lonely places where she could think as freely as a wild Indian in the woods. For years, she had criticized human institutions, laws, and religious rules. Her life of isolation had set her free. The scarlet letter was her passport to enter realms where other women did not dare to go. She had been taught by shame, despair, and solitude. These harsh teachers made her strong, even though they taught her many incorrect things.

The minister, however, had never experienced anything that challenged the rules of society. He had committed one major sin, but it was a sin of passion rather than a calculated choice. Since that time, he had closely watched every single thought and emotion in himself. As a clergyman, he was constrained by the rules and principles of his church. He had kept his conscience painfully sensitive by constantly thinking about his sin. This made him feel that he was safer within the bounds of virtue than if he had never sinned at all.

For Hester, the seven years of shame had prepared her for this moment. But for Arthur Dimmesdale, a new fall seemed to have no excuse. He was broken down by long suffering, and his mind was confused by remorse. He had to choose between running away as a criminal or staying as a hypocrite, and his conscience could not decide. It was natural for him to want to escape death, shame, and his enemy. Finally, this miserable pilgrim saw a chance for love, sympathy, and a new life. The truth is that when guilt breaks a path into a human soul, that path is never completely repaired in this life. The soul can be guarded, but the ruined wall remains, and the enemy is always waiting to attack again.

We do not need to describe the minister's internal struggle. It is enough to say that he decided to escape, and not to go alone.

“If I had felt even one moment of peace during these seven years,” he thought, “I would stay and suffer. But since I am already doomed, why shouldn't I take whatever comfort I can get? If this is the path to a better life, as Hester says, I am not giving up a better future by going. I cannot live without her companionship anymore. She is so strong to support me and so tender to comfort me. Oh, God, please forgive me, even though I do not deserve it.”

“You will go,” Hester said quietly, seeing his look.

Once he made the decision, a feeling of joy brought light to his troubled heart. It was the feeling of a prisoner escaping a dungeon, breathing the free air of a wild and lawless place. His spirit rose and felt closer to heaven than during all his years of misery on earth. Since he was a religious man, his joy had a spiritual quality.

“Do I really feel joy again?” he asked himself in wonder. “I thought that feeling was dead in me. Oh, Hester, you are my better angel. I sat down on these leaves sick and full of sorrow, but I have stood up feeling completely new. This is already a better life. Why didn't we do this sooner?”

“Let's not look back,” Hester answered. “The past is gone. Why should we think about it? Look, with this symbol, I undo everything and make it disappear.”

She undid the clasp of the scarlet letter and threw it into the withered leaves. The token landed on the edge of the stream. If it had gone just a little further, it would have fallen into the water and added another sorrow to the brook's sad story. The embroidered letter lay there, glittering like a lost jewel. Anyone who found it might be haunted by guilt and bad luck.

Once the badge was gone, Hester let out a long sigh, and the weight of her shame left her spirit. She felt a wonderful relief. She had not realized how heavy the burden was until she was free of it. Next, she took off the cap that covered her hair. Her rich, dark hair fell down onto her shoulders, making her face look soft and beautiful. A tender smile appeared on her lips and shone in her eyes. Her face flushed with color, and her youth and beauty returned. The happiness she felt in this hour seemed to banish all the gloom from their lives. Suddenly, the sunshine broke through the clouds and filled the forest, lighting up the green leaves and making the tree trunks glow. The dark shadows disappeared. The little brook sparkled in the sunlight, reflecting the joy of the scene.

Nature itself seemed to share their happiness. Love always creates an inner sunshine that overflows into the outer world. Even if the forest had stayed dark, Hester and Arthur Dimmesdale would have seen it as bright.

Hester looked at him with joy.

“You must meet Pearl,” she said. “Our little Pearl. You have seen her before, but you will see her differently now. She is a strange child, and I do not always understand her. But you will love her, and you can help me raise her.”

“Do you think she will be happy to meet me?” the minister asked nervously. “I have always been afraid of children, because they often distrust me. I have even been afraid of Pearl.”

“That was sad,” the mother said. “But she will love you, and you will love her. She is not far away. I will call her. Pearl! Pearl!”

“I see her,” the minister said. “She is standing in the sunshine on the other side of the brook. Do you really think she will love me?”

Hester smiled and called Pearl again. The child stood in a sunbeam that fell through the branches, looking like a bright spirit. The light shifted, making her look sometimes like a real child and sometimes like a ghost. She heard her mother's call and walked slowly toward them.

Pearl had enjoyed her time in the forest while her mother spoke to the minister. The dark woods welcomed the lonely child. The forest offered her red partridge berries on the leaves, which Pearl gathered and ate. The wild animals did not run from her. A partridge with ten chicks ran toward her but quickly realized she was friendly and clucked to her young not to fear. A pigeon on a branch let Pearl get close, making a friendly sound. A squirrel chattered from a tree and threw a nut at her head. A fox woke up, looked at her, and decided to go back to sleep. Some even said a wolf came up to sniff her dress and let her pat his head. The truth is that the forest and its creatures recognized a wildness in Pearl that was like their own.

She was gentler in the woods than in the town streets or her mother's cottage. The flowers seemed to welcome her, whispering to her to pick them. She gathered violets, anemones, and columbines, along with green twigs. She decorated her hair and waist, looking like a forest nymph that belonged in the woods. Pearl was dressed this way when she heard her mother's voice and walked back.

She walked slowly because she saw the minister.

CHAPTER XIX.

THE CHILD AT THE BROOK-SIDE.

"You will love her dearly," Hester Prynne repeated as she and the minister sat watching little Pearl. "Don't you think she is beautiful? See how naturally and skillfully she has decorated herself with those simple flowers. Even if she had gathered pearls, diamonds, and rubies in the woods, they could not suit her any better. She is a splendid child, and I know exactly who she looks like!"

"Do you know, Hester," Arthur Dimmesdale said with an uneasy smile, "that this dear child has caused me many alarms as she walks by your side? I sometimes thought that my own features were partly repeated in her face. It was a terrible thought to dread. I feared the resemblance was so strong that the world might notice it. But she looks mostly like you."

"No, no! Not mostly!" the mother answered with a tender smile. "Very soon, you will not have to worry about anyone tracing whose child she is. She looks strangely beautiful with those wildflowers in her hair. It is as if one of the fairies from old England has dressed her up to meet us."

Hester and Arthur felt a completely new emotion as they sat and watched Pearl slowly walk toward them. Pearl was the visible connection between them. For the past seven years, she had been presented to the world as a living symbol of their hidden secret. Their secret was written plainly in her, and anyone with the skills of a prophet or a magician could have read it like a message of fire. Pearl was the physical embodiment of their union. Whatever their past sins had been, they could not doubt that their lives and destinies were joined forever. They could see their physical and spiritual union right before them in this child. These thoughts, and perhaps other unacknowledged feelings, made them feel a sense of awe as the child approached.

"Do not let her see anything strange, and do not show any passion or eagerness when you greet her," Hester whispered. "Our Pearl is sometimes a fitful and unpredictable child. She rarely tolerates strong emotions when she does not understand the reason for them. But she is capable of deep affection. She loves me, and she will love you, too."

"You cannot imagine how much my heart dreads this meeting, even as it longs for it," the minister said, looking away from Hester. "As I told you before, children do not easily warm up to me. They will not climb onto my knee, whisper in my ear, or respond to my smile. Instead, they stand apart and watch me strangely. Even little babies cry bitterly when I hold them. Yet Pearl has been kind to me twice in her short life. You know the first time well. The second time was when you brought her to the house of the stern old governor."

"And you argued so bravely for both of us!" the mother answered. "I remember it, and little Pearl will remember it too. Do not be afraid. She may be strange and shy at first, but she will soon learn to love you."

By this time, Pearl had reached the edge of the brook. She stood on the far side, gazing silently at Hester and the minister, who were still sitting on the mossy tree trunk. The brook formed a smooth, quiet pool right where she stopped. It reflected a perfect image of her small figure, highlighting her bright beauty and the flowers in her hair. The reflection made her look even more delicate and spiritual than she did in reality. This clear image seemed to make the real Pearl feel a bit like a ghost herself. She stood and stared steadily at them through the dark forest shadows. At the same time, a ray of sunshine fell directly on her, as if attracted by some natural connection. Another child stood in the brook below, identical to the first, and also lit by a golden beam. Hester felt a strange, unsettling distance between herself and Pearl. It felt as if the child had wandered out of their shared world during her forest walk, and was now trying in vain to get back.

This feeling was partly true and partly false. Hester and Pearl were indeed separated, but it was Hester's fault, not Pearl's. While Pearl was wandering away, Hester had welcomed another person into her heart. This new connection changed everything, so that the returning child could no longer find her usual place, and did not know where she belonged.

"I have a strange feeling," the sensitive minister said, "that this brook is the border between two worlds, and that you will never meet Pearl again. Or is she a fairy spirit who is forbidden from crossing running water, like the legends we heard as children? Please make her hurry, because this delay is making me very nervous."

"Come, dearest child!" Hester said encouragingly, stretching out her arms. "You are being so slow, and you are never usually this sluggish. Here is a friend of mine who must be your friend too. From now on, you will have twice as much love as your mother alone could give you. Leap across the brook and come to us. You can jump like a young deer!"

Pearl did not respond to these sweet words and remained on the other side of the brook. She fixed her bright, wild eyes first on her mother, then on the minister, and then on both of them together. She seemed to be trying to understand their relationship. When Arthur Dimmesdale felt the child's gaze on him, his hand went to his heart in his usual involuntary gesture. Finally, Pearl assumed a strange air of authority. She stretched out her hand and pointed her finger directly at her mother's chest. Below her, the reflection in the brook also showed a sunny image of Pearl, pointing her finger in the same way.

"You strange child, why don't you come to me?" Hester exclaimed.

Pearl kept pointing her finger, and a frown appeared on her brow. The frown looked especially serious on her childish, almost baby-like face. As her mother kept beckoning and smiling unnaturally, the child stamped her foot with an even more demanding look and gesture. In the brook, the beautiful reflection also frowned, pointed its finger, and stamped its foot, mimicking Pearl's anger.

"Hurry, Pearl, or I will be angry with you!" Hester Prynne cried. She was used to this behavior at other times, but she was anxious for her daughter to behave properly now. "Leap across the brook, you naughty child, and run here! Otherwise, I will have to come to you!"

But Pearl was not startled by her mother's threats, nor was she softened by her pleas. She suddenly burst into a fit of passion, waving her arms and twisting her small body wildly. She shrieked loudly, and the woods echoed her cries on all sides. Even though she was alone in her childish anger, the echoes made it sound as if a hidden crowd was encouraging her. In the brook, her reflection showed the same shadow of anger. The flower-crowned image stamped its foot, waved its arms, and kept pointing its finger at Hester's chest.

"I see what is wrong with the child," Hester whispered to the minister. She turned pale, despite her effort to hide her worry. "Children cannot tolerate even the slightest change in the daily things they are used to seeing. Pearl misses something that she has always seen me wear."

"I beg you, if you have any way to calm the child, do it immediately," the minister answered. He tried to smile. "Except for the bitter anger of an old witch like Mistress Hibbins, there is nothing I dread more than a child's temper. In Pearl's young beauty, just like in a wrinkled witch, this anger looks unnatural. Please calm her if you love me."

Hester turned back toward Pearl. She blushed deeply, glanced at the minister, and sighed heavily. Before she could speak, her face turned pale again.

"Pearl," she said sadly, "look down at your feet. Right there, in front of you, on this side of the brook."

The child looked down and saw the scarlet letter. It lay so close to the edge of the stream that its gold embroidery was reflected in the water.

"Bring it here!" Hester said.

"You come and pick it up!" Pearl answered.

"Was there ever such a child?" Hester whispered to the minister. "I have so much to tell you about her. But she is actually right about this hateful badge. I must bear its torture a little longer, just for a few days, until we leave this place. Then we will look back on this time like a bad dream. The forest cannot hide the letter. The middle of the ocean will take it from my hand and swallow it up forever."

She walked to the edge of the brook, picked up the scarlet letter, and pinned it back onto her dress. Only a moment before, she had spoken hopefully about drowning it in the sea. Now, she felt a sense of unavoidable doom as she took the symbol back from the hand of fate. She had thrown it away and breathed freely for an hour, but now the scarlet misery was glittering in the same spot again. This is how it always is with an evil deed, which seems to bring a sense of unavoidable doom. Hester then gathered up her thick hair and hid it under her cap again. The sad letter seemed to carry a fading spell. Her beauty and warmth disappeared like fading sunshine, and a gray shadow seemed to fall over her.

Once this sad change was complete, Hester held out her hand to Pearl.

"Do you know your mother now, child?" she asked, with a quiet, reproachful tone. "Will you come across the brook and accept your mother now that she has her shame back, and now that she is sad?"

"Yes, now I will!" the child answered. She jumped across the brook and threw her arms around Hester. "Now you are my mother indeed, and I am your little Pearl!"

Pearl showed an unusual moment of tenderness. She pulled down her mother's head and kissed her forehead and cheeks. Then, as if she felt a need to mix pain with every comfort she gave, she reached up and kissed the scarlet letter too.

"That was not kind," Hester said. "You show me a little love, and then you mock me."

"Why is the minister sitting over there?" Pearl asked.

"He is waiting to welcome you," her mother replied. "Come and ask for his blessing. He loves you, my little Pearl, and he loves your mother too. Won't you love him? Come, he is eager to greet you."

"Does he love us?" Pearl asked, looking up into her mother's face with sharp intelligence. "Will he walk back into town with us, hand in hand, the three of us together?"

"Not right now, dear child," Hester answered. "But in the future, he will walk hand in hand with us. We will have our own home and fireplace. You will sit on his knee, and he will teach you many things and love you dearly. You will love him, won't you?"

"And will he always keep his hand over his heart?" Pearl asked.

"What a silly question!" her mother exclaimed. "Come and ask for his blessing."

Pearl refused to show any kindness to the minister. She might have been jealous, as children often are when they see a rival for their mother's affection, or perhaps it was just a whim of her wild nature. Hester had to pull her forward. Pearl held back and made strange faces. Ever since she was a baby, she had been able to make many different mischievous expressions. The minister was painfully embarrassed. He hoped a kiss would make her accept him, so he bent forward and kissed her forehead. At this, Pearl broke away from her mother. She ran to the brook, bent over it, and washed her forehead until the unwelcome kiss was completely washed away in the flowing water. She then stood at a distance and watched Hester and the minister in silence while they finished making their plans.

Now this important meeting was over. The forest clearing was left lonely once again among the dark old trees. The trees would whisper about what had happened, though no human would hear them. The sad brook would add this story to its secrets. It continued to babble and murmur, sounding just as melancholy as it had for centuries.

CHAPTER XX.

THE MINISTER IN A MAZE.

The minister walked ahead of Hester Prynne and little Pearl. He looked back, half expecting to see only a faint outline of the mother and child fading into the forest twilight. Such a massive change in his life did not feel real yet. But Hester was still standing there in her gray robe. She stood next to the fallen tree trunk, which had been blown down long ago and covered in moss. That spot had allowed these two fated people, carrying the world's heaviest burden, to sit and find an hour of peace. Pearl was there too, dancing away from the edge of the brook now that the minister had stepped away. She took her usual place by her mother's side. The minister realized he had not simply fallen asleep and dreamed.

To clear his mind of these confusing thoughts, he reviewed the plans that he and Hester had made for their escape. They had decided that Europe, with its crowded cities, offered a much better hiding place than the wilds of America. In America, the only choices were living in an Indian camp or staying in the few tiny European settlements along the coast. Furthermore, the minister's poor health was not strong enough to survive the hardships of the wilderness. His education, talents, and refined personality meant he could only thrive in a civilized society. Fortunately, a ship was currently anchored in the harbor. It was one of those private vessels common at the time, which were not quite pirate ships but sailed with a lot of freedom. The ship had recently arrived from the Spanish Main and would sail for Bristol in three days. Hester Prynne had met the captain and crew through her charitable work. She took it upon herself to secure passage for two adults and a child, keeping their plans completely secret.

The minister had asked Hester exactly when the ship would leave. She told him it would depart on the fourth day. He had thought to himself that this was very fortunate. His reason for thinking so was that he was scheduled to preach the Election Sermon on the third day. This sermon was a major event in the career of a New England minister, and it would be the perfect way to end his public life. He thought that people would at least say he left no duty unfinished or poorly done. It was sad that such a deeply reflective man could deceive himself so completely. This was a pitiable weakness in his character, showing how a subtle moral disease had eaten away at him over the years. A man cannot wear one face in public and another in private for very long without finally forgetting who he really is.

The excitement from his meeting with Hester gave Mr. Dimmesdale unexpected energy, and he walked quickly toward the town. The forest path seemed wilder, rougher, and less traveled than he remembered it. Yet he leaped over muddy spots, pushed through thick bushes, climbed hills, and crossed valleys with an active strength that amazed him. He remembered how slowly he had walked and how often he had stopped to breathe on the same path just two days ago. As he approached the town, the familiar sights looked completely different to him. It felt as if years, rather than days, had passed since he left. The streets and houses were exactly the same, but he felt a strong sense of change. The same was true of the people he met. They were not actually older or younger, but they felt different to him. He felt this strangeness most strongly when he passed his own church. The building looked both so strange and so familiar that he wondered if his whole life had been a dream, or if he was dreaming now.

This feeling of change was entirely within himself. A single day had changed his perspective so deeply that it felt like years had passed. His own will, Hester's will, and their shared fate had transformed him. The town was the same, but he was not the same man who had walked into the forest. He wanted to tell the friends who greeted him that he was not the minister they thought he was. He felt as if he had left his old self in the woods, lying by the mossy tree trunk near the sad brook like a discarded suit of clothes. His friends would have insisted that he was the same person, but they would have been wrong.

Before he reached home, his inner thoughts showed further signs of a revolution. His entire moral code seemed to have changed, giving the minister strange, wild impulses at every step. He felt tempted to do wicked things, acting against his usual habits yet coming from a deep, hidden part of himself. For instance, he met one of his church deacons. The good old man spoke to him with fatherly affection and deep respect, as was fitting for his age and position. During their short conversation, Mr. Dimmesdale had to use extreme self-control to keep from saying blasphemous things about the communion supper. He turned pale and trembled, fearing his tongue would speak these terrible thoughts on its own. Even in his terror, he wanted to laugh at the thought of how shocked the holy deacon would be by his minister's words.

A second incident occurred shortly after. Walking along the street, Mr. Dimmesdale met the oldest woman in his church. She was a pious, poor, and lonely widow whose heart was full of memories of her dead family. Her faith and reading of the Scriptures had turned her heavy sorrow into a quiet joy for over thirty years. Since Mr. Dimmesdale had become her pastor, her greatest comfort was to hear words of Gospel truth from him. But on this day, as he leaned in to speak to her, the minister could not remember a single comforting verse. Instead, he could only think of a short, logical argument against the immortality of the human soul. Whispering this to her would have devastated the old woman, perhaps even killing her from shock. He could never remember what he actually said. There was probably some confusion in his speech that she did not understand, or perhaps God guided her to hear something else. When the minister looked back, he saw an expression of deep gratitude and joy on her wrinkled face.

Then there was a third encounter. He met the youngest female member of his church. She was a young woman who had recently turned to religion after hearing one of his sermons. She was as pure and beautiful as a lily. The minister knew that she held a deep, innocent respect for him in her heart, combining religious devotion with pure affection. It felt as if Satan had led her to him on this afternoon, placing her in the path of this desperate, tempted man. As she approached, a wicked impulse whispered to him to say something that would ruin her innocence and plant a seed of doubt in her mind. He felt a dark sense of power over her trusting soul, feeling that a single bad look or word could destroy her innocence. With a massive struggle, he covered his face with his cloak and hurried past her without a word. The poor girl was left to wonder why he was so rude. She searched her conscience for any tiny mistake she might have made, blaming herself for imaginary faults and crying herself to sleep that night.

Before he could celebrate his victory over that temptation, he felt another impulse that was both ridiculous and horrible. He wanted to stop and teach swear words to a group of young Puritan children playing nearby. Resisting this urge as inappropriate for a minister, he next saw a drunken sailor from the ship in the harbor. Because he had resisted his other urges, Mr. Dimmesdale now wanted to shake hands with the sailor. He longed to trade dirty jokes and shout loud, angry swear words with him. It was his good taste and long habit of acting like a proper minister, rather than his moral principles, that saved him this time.

"What is haunting and tempting me like this?" the minister cried to himself, stopping in the street and hitting his forehead. "Am I losing my mind? Have I given myself over entirely to the devil? Did I sign a contract with him in the forest using my blood, and is he now demanding I fulfill it by suggesting every evil deed possible?"

As Mr. Dimmesdale stood there hitting his forehead, old Mistress Hibbins, the town's reputed witch, passed by. She made a grand appearance in a high headdress and a rich velvet gown. Her collar was styled with the famous yellow starch she had learned to make from her friend Ann Turner, who was later hanged for murder. She stopped and looked closely at the minister, smiling knowingly. Although she rarely spoke to clergymen, she began a conversation.

"So, reverend sir, you have been visiting the forest," the old woman said, nodding her tall headpiece at him. "Next time, please give me some warning, and I will gladly join you. I can promise you that my recommendation will go a long way with the powerful master you met out there."

"I assure you, madam," the minister answered with a formal bow, "I have absolutely no idea what you mean. I did not go into the forest to find any master, and I have no plans to go back there to seek anyone's favor. My only purpose was to visit my holy friend, the Apostle Eliot, and share his joy over the souls he has converted."

"Ha, ha, ha!" the old witch cackled, nodding her headdress. "Well, we must speak this way during the day. You play your part very well. But at midnight in the forest, we will have a different conversation."

She walked away with a dignified air, but she kept turning back to smile at him as if they shared a secret bond.

"Have I really sold myself to the devil?" the minister thought. "Is that the same master this old witch has chosen to serve?"

The miserable minister had indeed made a similar bargain. Tempted by a dream of happiness, he had chosen to yield to what he knew was a deadly sin. The poison of that choice had spread quickly through his soul. It silenced his good impulses and woke up all his bad ones. Bitterness, scorn, a desire to do harm, and a urge to mock holy things all tempted and frightened him. His meeting with Mistress Hibbins, if it really happened, showed his new connection to wicked people and evil spirits.

By this time, he had reached his house next to the graveyard. He hurried upstairs and took shelter in his study. He was relieved to be safe inside without having given away his strange state to the townspeople. He looked around the familiar room at his books, windows, fireplace, and tapestries. Everything looked strange to him now, just as the town had during his walk. In this room, he had studied and written. He had fasted, prayed, and suffered a thousand torments. His Hebrew Bible lay there, representing the word of God. On the table, next to his pen, lay his unfinished Election Sermon, which he had stopped writing two days ago. He knew that he was the same pale minister who had written those pages, but he felt disconnected from that person. That old self was gone. A different, wiser man had returned from the forest, carrying a dark knowledge that his former simple self could never have understood.

While he was thinking about this, someone knocked on the door. The minister said, "Come in," half expecting to see an evil spirit. In a way, he did. Roger Chillingworth entered the room. The minister stood pale and silent, with one hand on his Bible and the other on his chest.

"Welcome home, reverend sir," the physician said. "How was your visit with the Apostle Eliot? You look very pale. Perhaps the long walk through the wilderness was too much for you. Do you need my help to build your strength for the Election Sermon?"

"No, I do not think so," Mr. Dimmesdale replied. "My journey, the visit with the Apostle, and the fresh air have done me a lot of good after being cooped up in my study. I do not think I need any more of your medicine, my good physician, even though you have always offered it with a friendly hand."

Roger Chillingworth watched the minister with the serious gaze of a doctor studying a patient. Despite his calm expression, the minister felt sure that the old man knew or strongly suspected his meeting with Hester. The physician realized that the minister no longer trusted him and saw him as an enemy. Even though they both knew this, they did not say it out loud. People who want to avoid a topic can often talk right around it without ever mentioning it directly. The minister knew Chillingworth would not speak of the secret openly, yet the physician managed to hint at it in his dark way.

"Wouldn't it be better if you let me treat you tonight?" the physician asked. "We must make sure you are strong and healthy for the sermon. The people expect great things from you, especially since they worry that you might not be with them next year."

"Yes, I may be in another world," the minister replied with resignation. "I hope it is a better one, because I do not expect to stay with my congregation for another year. But as for your medicine, I do not need it in my current condition."

"I am glad to hear that," the physician said. "It may be that my remedies are finally working after all this time. I would be very happy, and the people of New England would be grateful, if I could cure you."

"I thank you from my heart, my watchful friend," Mr. Dimmesdale said with a solemn smile. "I can only pay you back with my prayers."

"A good man's prayers are a golden reward," Roger Chillingworth replied as he left. "They are like the gold coins of heaven itself."

Once he was alone, the minister called a servant and asked for food. He ate hungrily when it was brought to him. Then, he threw his unfinished sermon into the fire and immediately began writing a new one. He wrote with such a rush of ideas and emotions that he felt inspired. He could only wonder why heaven would choose to speak through someone as sinful as him. Leaving that mystery aside, he worked through the night with intense focus. The hours flew by quickly. Morning arrived, and the sunrise shone a golden beam of light across his desk. He sat there with his pen in hand, having written a massive amount of pages.

CHAPTER XXI.

THE NEW ENGLAND HOLIDAY.

Early in the morning of the day when the new governor was to take office, Hester Prynne and little Pearl entered the marketplace. The square was already crowded with townspeople and craftsmen. There were also many rough-looking men dressed in deerskins, showing they had traveled from the nearby forest settlements.

On this holiday, as she had for the last seven years, Hester wore a coarse gray dress. The color and simple design of the dress made her blend into the background. However, the bright scarlet letter on her chest drew everyone's eyes back to her, highlighting her shame. Her face showed a quiet, marble-like calmness. It looked like a mask, or even the frozen face of a dead woman. This cold appearance matched her actual state, since she was dead to the sympathy of the community and lived apart from the society around her.

A very close observer might have noticed a tiny change in her expression on this day. Anyone who could read her heart would see that after enduring the town's stares for seven years as a duty and penance, she was facing them voluntarily for one last time. She was turning her long suffering into a quiet victory. She was silently telling the crowd to take their last look at the scarlet letter and its wearer. Soon, she would be out of their reach. In a few hours, the ocean would hide the burning symbol forever. It would also be natural if Hester felt a touch of regret as she prepared to leave the pain that had defined her life for so long. She had lived with bitterness for so many years that she was used to its taste. Her new life would have to be very rich and happy to make her forget the bitter years she had left behind.

Pearl was dressed in bright, airy clothing. It was hard to believe that this cheerful child had come from the same sad, gray mother. The beautiful dress suited Pearl perfectly, looking like a natural part of her character, just like the colors on a butterfly's wing or the petals of a flower. Her outfit perfectly matched her wild nature. On this important day, Pearl was also feeling very excited and restless. She was like a sparkling diamond that flashed with every movement. Children always sense the tension and changes in their parents' lives. Pearl, who was the jewel on her mother's troubled chest, showed her excitement through her dancing movements, revealing the feelings that Hester hid behind her calm face.

This energy made her flit about like a bird instead of walking quietly next to Hester. She kept making wild, happy, and sometimes loud cries. When they reached the marketplace, she became even more active. She noticed all the bustle around the square, which was usually very quiet.

"What is this, mother?" she cried. "Why has everyone stopped working today? Is it a holiday for the whole world? Look, the blacksmith has washed his face and put on his Sunday clothes. He looks like he wants to be happy, if someone would only show him how. And there is Master Brackett, the jailer, nodding and smiling at me. Why is he doing that, mother?"

"He remembers you when you were a baby, my child," Hester answered.

"He should not nod and smile at me, that ugly old man," Pearl said. "He can nod at you if he wants, because you are dressed in gray and wear the scarlet letter. But look, mother, there are so many strangers, Indians, and sailors. What are they all doing in the marketplace?"

"They are waiting to see the parade pass by," Hester said. "The governor, the judges, and the ministers will walk past. All the important and good people will march behind the musicians and the soldiers."

"Will the minister be there?" Pearl asked. "And will he hold out both his hands to me, like when you led me to him by the brook?"

"He will be there, child," her mother answered. "But he will not say hello to you today, and you must not say hello to him."

"What a strange, sad man he is," the child said, almost to herself. "At night, he calls us to him and holds our hands on the scaffold. In the deep forest, where only the trees can hear and the sky can see, he sits on the moss and talks with you. He even kisses my forehead so hard that the brook could barely wash it off. But here, in the daylight and among all the people, he does not know us, and we must not know him. He is a strange, sad man, with his hand always over his heart."

"Be quiet, Pearl, you do not understand these things," her mother said. "Do not think about the minister now. Look around and see how happy everyone is today. The children have left their schools, and the adults have left their work to celebrate. A new governor is starting to rule today, and it is the custom to rejoice as if a wonderful new year is beginning."

It was true that the people looked unusually cheerful. During this annual holiday, the Puritans allowed themselves to enjoy whatever public joy and celebration they could tolerate. They managed to clear away their usual gloomy attitude for one day, so they looked no more serious than other communities might look during a time of national sadness.

We might exaggerate the gloom of that era. The people in the Boston marketplace had not been born into this strict lifestyle. They were native Englishmen whose fathers had lived during the bright and joyful Elizabethan era. If they had followed their natural tastes, the New England settlers would have celebrated with bonfires, feasts, and grand parades. They would have mixed fun with solemn ceremonies. The colony tried to recreate some of this old London splendor during the election day celebrations. The founders of the colony, including the statesmen, priests, and soldiers, believed it was their duty to dress in their finest clothes to show the majesty of their office. They all marched in a procession to give dignity to their new government.

On this day, the people were allowed to rest from their hard work, which was usually just as strict as their religion. There were no plays, singers, dancers, or magicians here, as they would have found in England. Such performers would have been banned by the law. Even so, the crowd was smiling and happy. They held sports that they remembered from England to show their strength and courage. There were wrestling matches in different styles and friendly fights with wooden staffs. Two men even started a sword fight on the pillory platform. However, the town officer stopped the fight to the disappointment of the crowd. He did not want a place of punishment used for entertainment.

Still, these early Puritans knew how to celebrate better than their children would. The next generation became even more strict, casting a dark shadow over the country's culture that lasted for centuries. We are still learning how to enjoy ourselves again.

The crowd in the marketplace was mostly dressed in gray, brown, or black, but there were some colorful groups. A group of Indians stood apart in their decorated deerskins, feathers, and paint. They carried bows, arrows, and spears, and their faces were even more serious than the Puritans' faces. But the wildest people in the square were the sailors from the ship in the harbor. They were rough, bearded men with sun-bronzed skin. They wore wide trousers with belts clasped in gold, and they carried long knives and swords. Their eyes had a wild look even when they laughed. They broke the town rules without fear, smoking tobacco right in front of the town officer and drinking wine from their flasks, which they offered to the crowd. During this era, sailors were allowed a lot of freedom, even though many of their actions at sea would be considered piracy today. This crew had likely attacked Spanish ships, a crime that would have been punished by hanging in later times.

The sea was wild and unregulated by laws back then. A pirate could easily retire and become a respected, religious citizen on land. Even active sailors were not avoided by honest merchants. The Puritan elders in their black cloaks and tall hats simply smiled at the sailors' noisy behavior. Nobody was surprised to see Roger Chillingworth enter the marketplace in close conversation with the ship's captain.

The commander of the Bristol ship was the most colorful figure in the crowd. He wore ribbons on his clothes, gold lace on his hat, and a feather in his cap. He had a sword at his side and a scar on his forehead, which he showed off proudly. A local citizen would have been fined or arrested for dressing and acting this way, but it was accepted as part of a sailor's character.

After speaking with Chillingworth, the captain walked through the marketplace. He approached Hester Prynne, who was standing in her usual isolated spot. A small, empty space always formed around Hester in crowds. The townspeople stayed back, partly because of her quiet dignity and partly out of habit. This isolation allowed Hester and the sailor to talk without being overheard.

"So, mistress," the sailor said, "I have to tell the steward to prepare one more cabin than you requested. We will not have to worry about sickness on this voyage. With our own surgeon and this other doctor on board, our only danger will be taking too many pills. We have plenty of medical supplies that I traded from a Spanish ship."

"What do you mean?" Hester asked, hiding her alarm. "Do you have another passenger?"

"Don't you know?" the captain cried. "This doctor, Chillingworth, wants to travel with you. He told me he is part of your group and a close friend of the gentleman you spoke of, the one who is in trouble with the Puritan rulers."

"They know each other well," Hester replied calmly, though she was terrified. "They have lived together for a long time."

The sailor walked away, but Hester looked across the busy marketplace and saw Roger Chillingworth. He was standing in a far corner, smiling at her with a dark, threatening look.

CHAPTER XXII.

THE PROCESSION.

Before Hester Prynne could gather her thoughts and decide what to do about this alarming news, she heard military music coming from a nearby street. The music announced the arrival of the parade. The judges, officers, and citizens were marching toward the church, where Mr. Dimmesdale was scheduled to deliver the Election Sermon.

The front of the parade soon appeared. The marchers walked slowly and solemnly around the corner and into the marketplace. The musicians led the way, playing drums and horns. Although they were not highly skilled, the music gave the scene a grand and heroic feeling. Little Pearl clapped her hands at first, and then she became quiet, listening to the swelling sounds. Soon, the bright sunshine reflecting off the soldiers' armor drew her attention back to the parade. This military escort was made up of local gentlemen who wanted to learn the skills of war. They walked proudly, showing how much respect the colony had for soldiers. Some of these men had fought in Europe and were experienced veterans. Clad in polished steel helmets with nodding feathers, the soldiers looked incredibly bright and impressive.

The political leaders walking behind the soldiers were even more impressive. Their dignified presence made the soldiers' proud walks look simple by comparison. In those days, people valued stable, dignified character much more than clever talent. The early settlers respected old age, honesty, and wisdom. The statesmen they elected, such as Bradstreet, Endicott, Dudley, and Bellingham, were not necessarily brilliant speakers, but they were serious and dependable men. They had great courage and stood firm in times of danger, defending the colony like cliffs against a stormy sea. These leaders looked strong and carried themselves with a natural authority that would have fit in well among the highest councils in England.

Behind the judges walked the young and famous minister who was to preach the sermon. In that era, the priesthood was the most respected career in the colony, attracting the most ambitious and intelligent men. Successful ministers held immense influence, sometimes even gaining political control.

The crowd noticed that Mr. Dimmesdale walked with more energy than he ever had since arriving in New England. His step was firm, his back was straight, and he did not keep his hand over his heart. However, his strength did not seem to be physical. It looked like a spiritual energy, or perhaps the excitement of his deep thoughts. He was so focused that he did not seem to hear the music or see the crowd around him. His mind was entirely occupied with organizing the thoughts for his sermon. His spirit carried his weak body along, making him forget his illness. Brilliant but troubled men often possess this ability to summon a massive burst of energy for a short time, only to feel completely exhausted afterward.

Hester Prynne watched the minister and felt a cold weight settle on her heart. He seemed so distant and completely out of reach. She had hoped for at least one look of recognition. She remembered their meeting in the forest clearing, where they had held hands and shared their deep love and pain by the murmuring brook. They had known each other so deeply then. Yet now she hardly recognized him as he walked proudly past, surrounded by the town's leaders and the grand music. He looked so far above her, and his thoughts seemed to be in a world where she did not exist. Hester felt a sinking fear that their connection was just a dream. She found it hard to forgive him for being able to detach himself so completely from their shared life, especially now when their fate was closing in on them. She felt as if she were reaching out in the dark and finding nothing.

Pearl sensed her mother's feelings or noticed the minister's distant look himself. She was restless while the parade passed, jumping up and down like a bird ready to fly. Once the crowd had gone by, she looked up at Hester.

"Mother," she said, "was that the same minister who kissed me by the brook?"

"Shh, quiet, dear Pearl," her mother whispered. "We must not talk in public about what happens in the forest."

"I was not sure it was him because he looked so different," the child said. "Otherwise, I would have run to him and asked him to kiss me in front of everyone, just like he did under the trees. What would he have done, mother? Would he have covered his heart, frowned, and told me to go away?"

"He would have told you that this is not the time or place for kisses," Hester answered. "It is a good thing you did not speak to him."

Mistress Hibbins was the only person bold enough to speak to Hester in public. She arrived in a magnificent velvet gown and a gold-headed cane. Because she was rumored to be a witch, the crowd stepped back to avoid touching her clothes. Seeing her stand next to Hester made the crowd move away even faster, leaving the two women alone.

"Who would believe it!" the old woman whispered to Hester. "That saintly man! Everyone thinks he is a saint, and he certainly looks like one. But who would guess that he was just in the forest, pretending to study scriptures while actually taking a walk in the woods? We know what that means, Hester Prynne. I see many church members in the parade who have danced with me in the forest at night. But this minister is a mystery. Tell me, Hester, was he the same man you met on the forest path?"

"Madam, I do not know what you are talking about," Hester answered. She believed Mistress Hibbins was losing her mind, but she was still startled by the old woman's claims. "I should not speak lightly of a respected minister like Mr. Dimmesdale."

"Nonsense!" the old lady cried, shaking her finger at Hester. "Do you think I have visited the forest so often without learning to recognize who else has been there? Even if they wash their hair and wear no leaves, I can tell. I know you, Hester, because I see your badge. It shines openly in the daylight. But this minister is trying to hide his connection. Let me tell you a secret. When the devil sees one of his servants hiding their bond, he has a way of revealing their mark in the broad daylight. What is the minister hiding under his hand?"

"What is it, good Mistress Hibbins?" little Pearl asked eagerly. "Have you seen it?"

"It does not matter, darling," Mistress Hibbins replied, bowing to the child. "You will see it yourself eventually. They say you are related to the prince of the air. Would you like to ride with me tonight to see him? Then you will understand why the minister keeps his hand over his heart."

The old woman walked away, laughing loudly enough for the whole marketplace to hear.

By this time, the opening prayer had ended inside the church, and Mr. Dimmesdale began to speak. A strong feeling kept Hester near the building. Since the church was too crowded for her to enter, she stood next to the pillory scaffold. This spot was close enough for her to hear the sound and rhythm of the minister's voice, even if she could not understand every word.

His voice was a remarkable instrument. Even a listener who did not speak the language could have been moved by its tone and rhythm. The sound carried deep emotion, passion, and tenderness. Although the church walls muffled the words, Hester listened intently and felt the meaning of the speech. The muffled tone seemed to make it more spiritual than if she had heard the words clearly. She heard the low undertone sink down like a dying wind, and then rise in sweetness and power until it filled the air with solemn grandeur. Yet, no matter how majestic the voice became, it always kept a sad, painful tone. It was a cry of human suffering that touched every heart. Even when his voice rose to a powerful command and filled the square outside, a listener could still detect the same cry of pain. It was the sound of a guilty, sorrowful heart sharing its secret with humanity, begging for sympathy and forgiveness. This constant undertone of pain gave the minister his greatest power.

Hester stood like a statue at the base of the scaffold. Even without the minister's voice, the spot held a powerful attraction for her. It was the place where her life of public shame had begun. She felt a heavy, wordless sense that her entire life was connected to this scaffold, which served as the center of her existence.

Meanwhile, Pearl left her mother's side and played around the marketplace. Her bright presence made the serious crowd look more cheerful, like a colorful bird darting through dark trees. She moved with a restless energy that was increased by her mother's tension. She ran toward anything that caught her curiosity, acting as if she owned whatever she looked at. The Puritans watched her, smiling but still believing she was a demon child because of her wild beauty and behavior. She looked directly into the faces of the Indians, who felt she possessed a nature even wilder than their own. She then ran into the middle of a group of sailors. These men watched her with wonder, as if a piece of sea foam had turned into a little girl.

The ship's captain, who had spoken to Hester, was so charmed by Pearl that he tried to kiss her. Since she was as quick and hard to catch as a hummingbird, he took a gold chain from his hat and threw it to her instead. Pearl wrapped it around her neck and waist. It looked so natural on her that it seemed like a part of her outfit.

"Your mother is the woman with the scarlet letter," the sailor said. "Will you carry a message to her?"

"I will if I like the message," Pearl answered.

"Tell her that I spoke again with the dark, hump-shouldered old doctor," he said. "He has promised to bring his friend, the gentleman she knows, on board with him. Tell your mother to only worry about herself and you. Will you tell her that, you little witch?"

"Mistress Hibbins says my father is the prince of the air!" Pearl cried with a mischievous smile. "If you call me names, I will tell him, and he will send a storm after your ship!"

The child ran back to her mother and told her what the sailor had said. Hester's strong spirit almost gave way. Just when a path of escape seemed to open for them, this dark sign of unavoidable doom appeared directly in their way.

While Hester was worrying about the sailor's news, she faced another trial. Many visitors from the countryside who had heard rumors about the scarlet letter but had never seen it now gathered around her out of curiosity. Although they were rude, they stayed a few yards away because of the letter's reputation. The sailors also noticed the crowd and came over to stare at her. Even the Indians watched her, believing the beautifully embroidered letter was a mark of high status. Finally, the townspeople joined in, staring at the familiar symbol of her shame. Hester saw the same group of women who had stood outside the prison seven years ago, except for the youngest, kindest one who had since died. At this final moment, when Hester was so close to throwing the letter away, it had become the center of attention once again, causing her more pain than it had in years.

While Hester stood in this circle of shame in the marketplace, the minister was looking down from the pulpit at the congregation he had deeply moved. The holy minister stood inside the church, while the woman with the scarlet letter stood outside in the square. No one in the crowd would have guessed that both of them carried the very same mark of guilt.

CHAPTER XXIII.

THE REVELATION OF THE SCARLET LETTER.

The minister's eloquent voice finally fell silent, ending the sermon that had deeply moved the crowd. A brief, profound silence followed his words. Then a quiet murmur spread through the audience. The people felt as if they were waking up from a spell. Soon, the crowd began to pour out of the church doors, returning to their everyday lives.

Once outside, the crowd began praising the minister. The streets and marketplace echoed with their applause. Everyone agreed that no one had ever spoken with such wisdom, holiness, and inspiration. The speaker seemed to be possessed by a higher power that led him beyond his written notes, filling him with wonderful ideas. His sermon focused on the relationship between God and humanity, specifically describing the future of New England. Near the end, he spoke like a prophet, predicting a bright and glorious future for the colony. Yet the entire speech carried a sad, painful undertone. The listeners felt it was the natural sadness of a man who knew he was dying. They believed their beloved minister had a premonition of his death and would soon leave them. This thought made the sermon even more powerful.

Mr. Dimmesdale had reached the most successful moment of his career. He stood at the peak of respect, admired for his intelligence, learning, eloquence, and holy reputation. As the sermon ended, he bowed his head on the pulpit cushions. Meanwhile, Hester Prynne stood by the scaffold, the scarlet letter still burning on her chest.

The music began again, and the soldiers marched out of the church doors. The parade was to walk to the town hall for a banquet. The leaders of the colony walked through the crowd, and the people stepped back respectfully to let them pass. When they reached the marketplace, the crowd erupted into a massive shout of enthusiasm. The shout was fueled by the powerful emotions from the sermon. The sound rose into the sky, louder than thunder or the roar of the sea. It was the loudest shout of joy ever heard in New England, and the minister was the most honored man in the colony.

But how was the minister doing? Although the crowd admired him like a saint, he was in a terrible state. As the procession moved forward, all eyes turned to him. The cheers quickly turned to worried whispers as the people saw him. He looked pale and incredibly weak. The energy that had supported him during his sermon was gone now that his duty was done. His face had a deathly color, and he tottered weakly along the path, barely able to stand.

The old minister, John Wilson, stepped forward to offer his arm. Mr. Dimmesdale trembled but firmly pushed the old man's support away. He kept walking slowly, looking like a baby trying to reach its mother's arms. Finally, he reached the dark wooden scaffold where Hester Prynne had stood years ago. Hester was standing there now, holding Pearl's hand. The minister stopped, even though the festival music urged him to keep walking.

Governor Bellingham noticed his weakness and stepped forward to help him. However, something in the minister's look made the governor stop. The crowd watched with wonder, believing his weakness was a sign of his holy devotion. Some half expected him to ascend directly into heaven.

He turned toward the scaffold and held out his arms.

"Hester," he said, "come here. Come, my little Pearl."

His expression was pale and terrible, yet it looked tender and triumphant. Pearl ran to him and wrapped her arms around his knees. Hester slowly approached, as if pulled by fate, but stopped in front of him. At that moment, Roger Chillingworth pushed through the crowd. He looked dark and angry, trying to stop the minister. He rushed forward and grabbed Arthur's arm.

"Stop, madman, what are you doing?" Chillingworth whispered. "Send the woman away. Cast off this child, and all will be well. Do not destroy your reputation and die in dishonor. I can still save you. Would you bring shame on your office?"

"Tempter, you are too late," the minister answered, looking at him with fear but determination. "Your power over me is gone. With God's help, I will escape you now."

He held out his hand to Hester again.

"Hester Prynne," he cried out, "in the name of God, who gives me the strength at this last moment to do what I avoided doing seven years ago, come here. Help me. Let your strength support me, guided by God's will. This wronged old man is trying to stop me with all his power. Come, Hester, support me as I climb the scaffold."

The crowd was confused. The leaders stood in silence, watching what was happening. They saw the minister lean on Hester's shoulder and climb the scaffold steps, holding little Pearl's hand. Roger Chillingworth followed them closely.

"If you had searched the whole earth," Chillingworth said darkly, "there was no place where you could have escaped me, except on this scaffold."

"Thanks be to God who led me here," the minister answered.

He trembled and looked at Hester with a worried smile. "Is this not better than our plans in the forest?" he murmured.

"I do not know," she replied quickly. "Is it better to die together like this?"

"For you and Pearl, let God decide," the minister said. "God is merciful. Let me do what he has commanded. I am dying, Hester, so let me confess my shame."

Supported by Hester and holding Pearl's hand, Mr. Dimmesdale turned to the judges, the ministers, and the crowd. The people watched in shocked silence, filled with sympathy as they realized a major secret was about to be revealed. The sun shone directly on the minister as he prepared to plead guilty.

"People of New England!" he cried, his voice rising solemn and powerful, yet trembling with remorse. "You who have loved me and thought I was holy, look at me now. I am the greatest sinner in the world. At last, I stand on the spot where I should have stood seven years ago. I stand here with this woman, whose arm keeps me from falling on my face. Look at Hester's scarlet letter. You have all shuddered at it. But there was someone else among you who carried a mark of sin, and you did not suspect him."

He seemed about to collapse, but he fought back his weakness. He stepped away from Hester and Pearl and walked forward a pace.

"His mark was hidden from men!" he shouted with determination. "God saw it, the angels knew it, and the devil touched it with his burning finger. But he hid it from you and walked among you like a pure spirit. Now, in his final hour, he stands before you. Look at Hester's letter. He tells you it is only a shadow of what he bears on his own chest. His own red mark is a symbol of what has burned his heart. Does anyone here doubt God's judgment? Look at this witness!"

With a sudden movement, he tore open his shirt to reveal his chest. The crowd stared in horror at what they saw. The minister stood there with a look of triumph, like a man who had won a great victory through extreme pain. Then he collapsed onto the scaffold. Hester held him, resting his head on her chest. Roger Chillingworth knelt next to him, looking defeated.

"You have escaped me," the old man repeated. "You have escaped me."

"May God forgive you," the minister said. "You have also sinned deeply."

He looked away from Chillingworth and turned to Hester and Pearl.

"My little Pearl," he said weakly, a gentle smile appearing on his face. "Will you kiss me now? You refused to kiss me in the forest, but will you now?"

Pearl kissed his lips, and a spell seemed to break. The shared sorrow finally awakened the child's sympathy. Her tears fell on her father's cheek, showing that she would grow up to experience normal human joy and sadness, and live a peaceful life as a woman. Her role as a source of pain for her mother was finished.

"Hester," the minister said, "goodbye."

"Won't we meet again?" she whispered, leaning close to him. "Won't we spend our eternal life together? Surely we have paid for our happiness with all this suffering. You are looking into eternity with your dying eyes. Tell me what you see."

"Shh, Hester, quiet," he said solemnly. "Think only of the law we broke and the sin we have confessed. I fear that when we forgot God and violated the purity of each other's souls, we lost the hope of meeting in a pure reunion. God knows, and he is merciful. He has shown his mercy through my suffering, by giving me this burning pain on my chest, by letting Chillingworth keep the torture alive, and by bringing me here to confess my sin before I die. If I had not suffered these things, I would have been lost forever. Praised be his name. Goodbye."

With that final word, the minister breathed his last. The silent crowd let out a deep, heavy murmur of awe that echoed through the square.

CHAPTER XXIV.

CONCLUSION.

After some time had passed and the townspeople could process what they had seen on the scaffold, several different explanations arose.

Most witnesses swore they saw a scarlet letter, identical to the one worn by Hester Prynne, marked directly into the minister's flesh. People had various theories about its origin. Some believed that Mr. Dimmesdale had carved it into himself as a form of secret penance starting the day Hester first wore her badge. Others claimed that Roger Chillingworth had caused the mark to appear using magic and poisonous drugs. A third group, who understood the minister's sensitive nature, believed that his intense remorse had caused the symbol to grow outward from his heart. The reader may choose which theory to believe. We have shared all the details we could find, and we are glad to finally lay the story to rest.

Surprisingly, some witnesses who watched the entire event claimed that there was no mark at all on his chest. They also reported that his final words did not confess any connection to Hester's guilt. According to these people, the minister had chosen to die in Hester's arms simply to show that even the most righteous men are weak without God. They believed he wanted to teach his followers that all humans are equally sinful in the eyes of God. While this is an important lesson, this version of the story is likely just a sign of how stubbornly a minister's friends will defend his character, even when the truth is as clear as day.

The old manuscript we have followed, which was written based on the accounts of people who knew Hester, supports the version of events we have told. Among the many lessons from the minister's sad life, we offer this simple rule. Be true. Show your real self to the world, or at least show enough that others can guess your weaknesses.

Immediately after the minister's death, Roger Chillingworth changed completely. All his strength and energy left him. He withered away and shriveled like a weed pulled from the ground. He had built his entire life around revenge. Once his victim was gone, he had no purpose left. Within a year, he died and went to his final rest. It is interesting to consider whether hatred and love are not actually very similar. Both passions require a deep understanding of another person, and both make us dependent on them. In the spiritual world, the old doctor and the minister, who were both victims of each other, may have found their hatred turned into love.

Roger Chillingworth left a large fortune in his will to little Pearl, Hester's daughter. Governor Bellingham and John Wilson managed the estate.

Pearl became the wealthiest heiress in the colony. This fortune changed how people viewed her. If she had stayed, she might have married into one of the most respected Puritan families. However, shortly after Chillingworth's death, Hester and Pearl disappeared. For many years, no one heard any real news of them, and the story of the scarlet letter became a legend. The scaffold and Hester's empty cottage remained landmarks. One afternoon years later, children playing nearby saw a tall woman in a gray robe approach the cottage. She opened the door and went inside.

She paused on the threshold, turning back for a moment as if the memories were too heavy to bear. But she hesitated for only an instant, revealing the scarlet letter on her chest.

Hester Prynne had returned to her old home and taken up her shame once again. Nobody knew for sure what had happened to Pearl. She might have died young, or she might have found happiness as a married woman in Europe. Throughout the rest of Hester's life, she received letters with official seals from another country. She also received comfortable and expensive goods that only wealth could buy. Visitors noticed beautiful ornaments in her cottage, showing that someone loved and remembered her. Once, Hester was even seen embroidering a rich baby blanket with gold thread.

Most people believed that Pearl was alive, married, and happy, and that she had invited her mother to live with her.

But Hester chose to stay in New England. This was where she had sinned and suffered, and this was where she wanted to spend her remaining years. She wore the scarlet letter for the rest of her life. Over time, the symbol ceased to be a source of scorn. Instead, it became a symbol of wisdom and sorrow. People brought their troubles to her, asking for advice. Women especially visited her cottage to share their pain over broken relationships or unrequited love. Hester comforted them and assured them that in the future, a new truth would make relationships happier. She had once hoped to bring this new truth herself, but she realized that such a holy mission must be carried by a pure, happy woman who showed the joy of sacred love.

Hester lived out her days in this manner. When she died, she was buried near an old, sunken grave in the churchyard. The two graves had a small space between them, as if their dust could not mix, yet they shared a single tombstone. It was a simple slate headstone marked with a coat of arms. The motto on the stone served as a description of their sad story: "On a field of black, the red letter A."

The author is very surprised, and also quite amused (if he can say so without causing more offense), to find that his introductory sketch of official life in The Scarlet Letter has created an unprecedented stir in the respectable community around him. Indeed, the outrage could hardly have been worse if he had burned down the Custom House. People reacted as if he had extinguished its last smoking ember with the blood of a respected gentleman, whom the author is accused of hating. If the author felt he deserved this public disapproval, it would weigh heavily on him. Therefore, he has carefully reread the introductory pages. He intended to change or remove anything that was inappropriate, wanting to make amends for the "crimes" he is accused of committing. But he believes the only notable features of the sketch are its frank, genuine good humor, and the general accuracy of his descriptions of the people in it. Therefore, the author feels compelled to republish his introductory sketch without changing a single word.

Salem, March 30, 1850.


THE CUSTOM-HOUSE
INTRODUCTORY TO "THE SCARLET LETTER"

It is quite strange that I feel a sudden urge to write about myself to the public. Usually, I do not like to talk too much about myself or my personal life, even when sitting by the fireplace with close friends. Yet, this is the second time in my life that this autobiographical impulse has taken over. The first time was three or four years ago. Back then, I shared a description of my quiet life in the Old Manse with my readers. I did this for no good reason that anyone could think of, and it was probably inexcusable. However, I was lucky enough to find a few readers who enjoyed it, even if I did not deserve them. Because of that success, I am now capturing the public's attention once again. This time, I want to talk about my three years of experience working in a Custom House. I am closely following the example of the famous character, P. P., Clerk of this Parish.

The truth is that when an author publishes their pages and releases them to the world, they are not really writing for the general public who will ignore the book or throw it aside. Instead, they are writing for the few readers who will truly understand them, perhaps even better than their own old schoolmates or lifelong friends. Some authors go even further. They share such deep, personal secrets that their words seem meant only for a single, perfect soulmate. It is as if they expect the printed book, sent out into the wide world, to find their missing half and make them feel complete by connecting them.

However, it is not very polite to share absolutely everything, even when we write under a different name. At the same time, a writer's thoughts can freeze and their words can go numb if they do not feel a connection to their audience. Therefore, it is helpful to imagine that we are speaking to a kind, understanding friend, even if they are not our closest companion. This comforting thought warms up our natural shyness. It allows us to chat about our surroundings, and even about ourselves, while still keeping our deepest, most private self hidden behind a veil. I believe an author can write about their own life within these limits. By doing so, they do not cross any boundaries or violate the rights of the reader or themselves.

This sketch of the Custom House also serves a useful purpose that is well recognized in literature. It explains how I came to possess a large portion of the pages that follow, and it offers proof that the story is authentic. My main reason for speaking directly to the public is simply to establish my role as the editor, and nothing more, of the longest story in this volume. While doing this, I thought it would be interesting to add a few details to describe a way of life that has not been written about before. I also wanted to introduce some of the unique people who lived this life, including myself.

A large brick building stands in my native town of Salem. It is located at the head of a wharf that was once a bustling center of trade fifty years ago, in the days of old King Derby. Today, however, the wharf is dilapidated, crowded with decaying wooden warehouses, and shows almost no signs of business. Occasionally, a single ship might stop halfway down the quiet wharf to unload hides, or a small schooner from Nova Scotia might arrive to dump off a cargo of firewood. The high tide often overflows the wharf, and unhealthy grass grows along the sides of the buildings, showing how quiet the years have been. From the front windows of this brick building, you get a rather depressing view of the wharf and the harbor beyond.

The American flag flies from the highest point of the roof for exactly three and a half hours every morning. It hangs there in the calm air or floats in the breeze. The flag's thirteen stripes run vertically instead of horizontally, which shows that this is a civil government office rather than a military post. The front of the building features a porch with six wooden pillars supporting a balcony. Underneath the balcony, a flight of wide granite steps leads down to the street. An enormous American eagle carved from wood hovers over the entrance. It has outspread wings, a shield on its chest, and holds a bundle of thunderbolts and sharp arrows in its claws. The bird looks fierce and angry, which is typical for this bad-tempered eagle. With its sharp beak and hostile stance, it seems to threaten the peaceful community. It warns all safety-minded citizens not to enter the building under its wings. Despite its fierce look, many people are trying to find shelter under the wings of the federal government at this very moment. They probably imagine that the government's chest is as soft and comfortable as a down pillow. However, this eagle is not very gentle, even in its best moods. Sooner or later, and usually sooner, it is likely to kick people out. When it does, it hurts them with a scratch of its claw, a peck of its beak, or a painful wound from its sharp arrows.

The pavement around this building, which we should name now as the Custom House of the port, has plenty of grass growing in its cracks. This grass shows that the building is not very busy these days. During certain months of the year, however, there are mornings when business moves a bit faster. These moments might remind older residents of the days before the War of 1812. Back then, Salem was a major independent port. It was not ignored like it is today by its own merchants and shipowners. Now, those owners let Salem's wharves crumble into ruins while they send their ships to join the massive trade in New York or Boston.

On a busy morning, when three or four ships arrive at the same time from Africa or South America, or are preparing to depart, you can hear many footsteps walking quickly up and down the granite steps. You might see a sunburned ship captain who has just returned from sea. Before he even greets his own wife, he comes here with his ship's documents in a rusty tin box under his arm. You will also see the ship's owner. He might look happy or gloomy, friendly or grumpy, depending on whether the voyage made a profit. If it went well, he has goods he can easily sell for gold. If it went poorly, he is stuck with unwanted goods that no one wants to buy.

You can also see the smart young clerk who will one day become a stressed, gray-bearded merchant. He gets a taste for trade the way a wolf cub gets a taste for blood. He is already investing in his master's ships when he really should still be sailing toy boats on a pond. Another person in the crowd is a sailor preparing to go abroad who needs a permit, or a sick sailor who just returned and needs a pass to get into the hospital. Finally, we must mention the captains of the rusty little schooners that bring firewood from the British provinces. They are a rough group of sailors who lack the quick, sharp look of local Americans, but they are still a very important part of our quiet trade.

When all these people gathered together, the Custom House became a very lively scene. Most of the time, however, the building was much quieter. If you walked up the steps in the summer, you would see a row of elderly men sitting in the hallway. If it was winter or bad weather, they would sit in their offices. They leaned their old-fashioned chairs back against the wall. They were often asleep. When they did talk, their voices sounded like a mix of speaking and snoring. They had the low energy of people who live in poorhouses or depend on charity and government jobs instead of their own hard work. These old gentlemen were the Custom House officers. They sat like the biblical Matthew at the tax collector's table, but they were not likely to be called away for any holy missions.

If you turn left as you enter the front door, you will find an office that is about fifteen feet square with a high ceiling. Two of its arched windows look out at the decaying wharf. The third window looks across a narrow lane and down part of Derby Street. All three windows offer views of local shops, including grocers, pulley makers, cheap clothing stores, and ship supply shops. Old sailors and other local characters gather around these shop doors to laugh and gossip. The office itself is full of cobwebs and has dirty, old paint. The floor is covered with gray sand, which is a style that has long been out of fashion. Looking at the messy room, it is easy to tell that women rarely enter with their magic tools, the broom and mop.

The furniture in the room is very simple. There is a stove with a large pipe and an old pine desk with a three-legged stool. A few wooden chairs stand nearby, looking very old and fragile. On the shelves, you can find a small library consisting of about forty volumes of congressional records and a heavy book of tax laws. A tin pipe runs up through the ceiling, allowing people to speak to other parts of the building.

Six months ago, you might have seen a familiar face in this office. He walked from corner to corner, or sat on the stool with his elbow on the desk, reading the morning newspaper. This was the same person who once welcomed you into his cheerful study at the Old Manse, where the sun shone through the willow branches. If you go looking for him now, however, you will not find this surveyor. Political changes have swept him out of his job. A new successor now holds his title and receives his salary.

This old town of Salem is my birthplace, even though I have lived away from it for long periods of my childhood and adult life. It has a hold on my feelings that I never truly noticed when I actually lived here. Physically, the town is flat and uninteresting. It is mostly filled with wooden houses that have no real beauty. Its layout is irregular but not in a charming or historic way. Instead, it just looks boring. The main street stretches lazily across the entire peninsula, running from Gallows Hill and the New Guinea neighborhood at one end, to the poorhouse at the other. With these features, it makes about as much sense to love this town as it does to love a messy checkerboard.

Yet, even though I have always been happier living elsewhere, I still have a feeling for Salem that I must call affection. This feeling probably comes from the deep, old roots my family has in this soil. It has been nearly two hundred and twenty-five years since the first English immigrant with my last name arrived in this wild, forest-covered settlement, which later became a city. His descendants were born and died here, and their bodies were buried in this earth. So much of my family is buried here that the very soil feels connected to my own body as I walk the streets. My attachment to this place is partly just the physical connection of dust to dust. Few Americans today experience this feeling. Since moving to new places is often better for a family's growth, they do not really need to experience it.

But this feeling also has a moral side. Family tradition gave my first ancestor a dark and mysterious greatness. I have imagined his figure since early childhood. He still haunts my thoughts and makes me feel connected to the town's past, even though I feel very little connection to its present. I feel a stronger right to live here because of this solemn, bearded ancestor, who wore a black cloak and a pointed hat. He arrived in the early days with his Bible and his sword. He walked the new streets with a stately posture and was a prominent figure in both war and peace. I feel a stronger connection to Salem through him than through my own life, since my name is rarely heard here and my face is barely known. He was a soldier, a legislator, and a judge. He was also a leader in the church, possessing all the good and bad qualities of the Puritans. He was a harsh persecutor as well. The Quakers remembered him in their histories, recording a story of his severe treatment toward a woman of their faith. Unfortunately, this story will probably outlast the memory of his many good deeds. His son inherited this persecuting spirit. He was so active in the Salem witch trials that their blood left a lasting stain on his memory. This stain is so deep that his dry bones in the Charter Street cemetery must still carry it, if they have not completely turned to dust. I do not know if these ancestors ever thought to repent and ask God for forgiveness, or if they are now suffering the heavy consequences of their actions in the afterlife. In either case, as their modern representative, I take the shame of their actions upon myself. I pray that any curse they brought upon our family, which seems likely given how our family has struggled for many years, is now and forever lifted.

However, both of these stern, dark-browed Puritans would have surely considered it a punishment for their sins that their family tree, after so many years, produced a useless idler like me as its final branch. They would not find any of my goals worthy of praise. If I ever achieved any success outside my home life, they would view it as worthless or even shameful. One ghostly ancestor whispers to the other, asking what I am. The other replies that I write storybooks. They wonder what kind of career that is, or how it glorifies God and serves humanity. They think a degenerate person like me might as well be a fiddler. These are the imaginary insults we throw at each other across the generations. Yet, even though they would look down on me, strong parts of their character are still mixed with my own.

These two active, hardworking men established our family in the town's earliest days. Since then, the family has lived here respectably. As far as I know, no member has ever brought disgrace to the name. However, after the first two generations, they rarely performed any memorable deeds or sought public attention. Over time, they faded from view, just as old houses along the streets are slowly buried by rising soil. For more than a century, the men of each generation went to sea. In every generation, a gray-haired captain retired to the family home, while a fourteen-year-old boy took his place on the ship, facing the salty spray and storms that his father and grandfather had faced before him. In time, that boy would rise from deckhand to captain, spend a wild manhood at sea, and eventually return home to grow old, die, and be buried in his native soil. This long connection of a family to one place of birth and burial creates a deep bond between a person and a town. This bond has nothing to do with the beauty of the landscape or the local culture. It is not love, but pure instinct. A new resident, who recently arrived from another country or whose parents did, cannot really understand this connection. They do not know how stubbornly an old settler clings to the spot where generations of his family have lived. It does not matter if the town brings him no joy. It does not matter if he is tired of the old wooden houses, the mud, the dust, the boring landscape, the cold east wind, and the cold social environment. All these flaws are irrelevant. The attachment remains as strong as if his birthplace were paradise. This was true for me. I felt it was my destiny to make Salem my home. I wanted my family's familiar facial features and character traits to remain in the town. Whenever one family member died, another took over the watch along the main street. I wanted people to still recognize my family during my own lifetime. However, this strong attachment is actually a sign that the connection is unhealthy and should be broken. Human beings, like potatoes, will not grow well if they are planted in the same worn-out soil for too many generations. My children were born in other places. As much as I can help it, they will build their lives in new and different soil.

After leaving my home at the Old Manse, it was mainly this strange, lazy, and joyless attachment to my hometown that led me to take a job in the government's brick building. I could have easily gone somewhere else. But it felt like my fate. This was not the first or second time I had left the town, seemingly for good, only to return like a bad penny. Salem felt like the unavoidable center of my universe. So, one fine morning, I walked up the granite steps with my official presidential appointment in my pocket. I was introduced to the group of men who would help me run the Custom House.

I do not doubt at all whether any public official in the United States, in either the civil or military service, has ever managed such an elderly group of veterans as I did. Looking at them, I immediately knew where all the town's oldest residents had gone. For more than twenty years before I arrived, the Collector's secure position had kept the Salem Custom House out of the political struggles that usually make government jobs so unstable. As New England's most distinguished soldier, he stood secure because of his brave service. Secure in his own position through several administrations, he protected his workers during times of political danger. General Miller was very conservative. He was a man of habit who grew attached to familiar faces and resisted change, even when change would have been a clear improvement. Therefore, when I took charge of the department, I found mostly old men. Most of them were former sea captains. After sailing every sea and surviving life's storms, they had drifted into this quiet corner. With little to worry about besides the occasional stress of a presidential election, they all seemed to find new life here. Although they were not immune to aging and sickness, they seemed to have some magic charm that kept death away. I was told that two or three of them had gout or rheumatism, or were even bedridden. They never showed up at the Custom House for most of the year. After a sleepy winter, they would crawl out into the warm sunshine of May or June, slowly do what they called their duties, and then return to bed whenever they pleased. I must admit that I cut short the careers of a few of these elderly servants of the republic. Based on my recommendation, they were allowed to retire from their hard work. Shortly after, as if their only reason for living had been their dedication to their country's service, they passed away. I find comfort in knowing that my actions gave them enough time to repent for any corrupt habits that Custom House officers are assumed to have. After all, neither the front nor the back door of the Custom House leads to heaven.

Most of my officers belonged to the Whig party. It was lucky for this elderly group that I was not an active politician. Although I was a Democrat, I did not receive or keep my job because of political favors. If an active politician had taken this influential position, he could have easily pushed out the Whig Collector, who was too sick to manage the office himself. In that case, almost none of the old staff would have kept their jobs for more than a month. Standard political rules would have required a politician to fire every single one of those elderly men. It was easy to see that the old officers feared I would do exactly that. I was both sorry and amused to see how terrified they were of my arrival. Men with wrinkled faces, weathered by fifty years of storms at sea, turned pale when they looked at someone as harmless as me. When they spoke to me, I could hear their voices tremble. In the past, those same voices had roared through megaphones, loud enough to silence the north wind itself. These good old men knew that, by all standard rules and because of their own inefficiency, they should have been replaced. Younger, politically loyal, and more capable men should have been serving the government instead. I knew this too, but I could never bring myself to fire them. Although it was bad for my reputation and my official duties, they kept their jobs during my time in office. They continued to walk slowly around the wharves and linger on the Custom House steps. They also spent a lot of time sleeping in their favorite corners with their chairs tilted back against the wall. They would wake up once or twice in the morning to bore each other with the same old sea stories and stale jokes they had repeated thousands of times before.

I think they soon realized that I meant them no harm. So, with happy hearts and a sense of being useful, at least to themselves if not to the country, these old gentlemen performed their official duties. They peered into ship cargo holds through their glasses with a wise look. They made a huge fuss over tiny details, but they were often incredibly blind to much larger problems. Whenever a mistake happened, such as a wagon loaded with valuable goods being smuggled ashore in broad daylight right under their noses, they became incredibly active. They would immediately lock and double-lock every entrance to the offending ship, securing them with tape and wax. Instead of being scolded for their laziness, they seemed to deserve praise for their caution after the damage was already done. It was as if they wanted recognition for their sudden enthusiasm the moment it was too late to fix the problem.

Unless people are unusually disagreeable, I have a foolish habit of liking them. I usually focus on the best parts of a person's character, if they have any, and that is how I define them. Most of these old Custom-House officers had good traits. Since my role was to protect and guide them like a father, it was easy to develop friendly feelings, and I soon grew to like them all.

It was pleasant to hear them chatting in the back entry during summer mornings. The intense heat would melt most people, but it only warmed up their slow, elderly bodies. They would lean back against the wall in a row and laugh as they repeated old jokes from past generations. On the outside, the happiness of old men looks a lot like the joy of children. Neither intelligence nor a deep sense of humor has much to do with it. Instead, it is just a superficial brightness that makes both a young child and an old man look cheerful. The child is like a green branch, while the old man is like a decaying tree trunk. For the child, this brightness is real sunshine. For the old man, it is more like the faint glow of decaying wood.

To be fair, I should not describe all my old friends as senile. First of all, my coworkers were not all old. Some of them were in their prime, full of strength, energy, and talent. They were far too good for this slow, dependent lifestyle, which they had only fallen into through bad luck. Also, some of the older men with white hair still had sharp, active minds. However, most of these old veterans were tedious people. They had learned nothing worth keeping from their long lives. They seemed to have thrown away all the useful wisdom they could have gathered over the years, keeping only the worthless details in their memories. They talked with much more excitement about their breakfast or dinner than about a shipwreck from fifty years ago, or any of the world's wonders they had seen when they were young.

A certain permanent inspector was the patriarch of the Custom-House. He was the father figure not only for this small group of officials, but for customs officers all over the country. He was a true product of the revenue system. His father, a Revolutionary War colonel and a former collector of the port, had created the job for him and appointed him to it a very long time ago, in a period that few living people can remember. When I first met this inspector, he was about eighty years old. He was one of the most remarkably healthy and lively old men you could ever find. He had rosy cheeks, a compact figure, and wore a neat blue coat with bright buttons. With his quick, energetic step and healthy appearance, he did not look young, but he seemed like a special creation of nature that old age and sickness could not touch. His voice and laugh echoed through the Custom-House constantly. They did not have the shaky, weak quality of an old man's voice. Instead, they boomed out of his chest like a loud horn or a rooster's crow. If you looked at him simply as a physical creature, and there was not much else to him, he was impressive. He was incredibly healthy and still able to enjoy almost every physical pleasure he had ever cared about. His secure job at the Custom-House, with a steady income and no fear of being fired, certainly helped him stay young. However, the main reasons lay in his perfect physical health, his very average intelligence, and his lack of deep moral or spiritual feelings. In fact, he had just enough of these higher qualities to keep him from acting like an animal. He had no deep thoughts, no strong feelings, and no complex emotions. He only had basic instincts and a cheerful mood that came from feeling physically well. This cheerful attitude served him well enough instead of a heart. He had been married to three wives, who were all long dead, and had fathered twenty children, most of whom had also died at various ages. You would think this would be enough sadness to make even the happiest person deeply depressed. But this was not the case for our old inspector. A single short sigh was enough to clear away all those sad memories. A moment later, he was ready to play like a toddler. He was far more lively than the Collector's young clerk, who was only nineteen but acted much older and more serious than the inspector.

I watched and studied this elderly man with more curiosity than anyone else at the Custom-House. He was truly a rare character. In one way, he was absolutely perfect. In every other way, he was shallow, empty, and practically non-existent. I concluded that he had no soul, no heart, and no mind. He only had instincts. Yet his traits were put together so well that his lack of character did not bother me, and I was entirely content with who he was. It was hard to imagine him having an afterlife because he was so focused on physical pleasures. Still, his life on earth was pleasant. He had no more moral responsibility than a wild animal, but he enjoyed life more than animals do, and he was completely free from the sadness and weakness of old age.

One big advantage he had over animals was his ability to remember the great dinners he had eaten, which made up a large part of his life's happiness. His love of food was a very pleasant trait, and hearing him talk about roast meat made you hungry. Since he had no higher qualities to ruin, and was not wasting any spiritual potential by focusing entirely on his stomach, I enjoyed listening to him talk in detail about fish, poultry, meat, and the best ways to cook them. His memories of good meals, no matter how long ago he ate them, made you feel like you could smell the pig or turkey right in front of you. Some flavors had stayed in his memory for sixty or seventy years, and they seemed as fresh to him as the mutton chop he had just eaten for breakfast. I heard him smack his lips over dinners where every other guest had been dead and buried for a long time. It was amazing to see how the memories of past meals constantly came back to him. They did not return to haunt him, but rather to bring back a continuous stream of enjoyment. He would remember a specific beef tenderloin, veal cutlet, pork rib, chicken, or turkey from the presidency of John Adams. Meanwhile, the history of the world and the events of his own life passed by him like a gentle breeze, leaving no lasting impression. The main tragedy of his life, as far as I could tell, was a bad experience with a goose thirty or forty years ago. The goose looked promising, but it turned out so tough that a carving knife could not cut it, and they had to use an axe and a handsaw to break it apart.

However, it is time to move on from this description. I could talk about him much longer, because of all the men I have known, he was the best suited to be a Custom-House officer. Most people lose their moral character in this kind of job, for reasons I do not have time to explain. The old inspector was immune to this. If he stayed in office until the end of time, he would remain exactly the same, and sit down to dinner with the same great appetite.

There is one more portrait I must include, though I only saw him enough to describe him briefly. This is the Collector, our brave old General. After a brilliant military career, he governed a western territory, and then came here twenty years ago to spend the final years of his honorable life. The brave soldier was almost seventy years old. He was struggling with physical weakness, which even his proud military memories could not ease. His legs shook now, though they had once led the charge in battle. He could only climb the Custom-House steps with a servant's help and by leaning heavily on the iron railing. He would walk slowly and painfully across the floor to his usual chair by the fireplace. He used to sit there, watching people come and go with a calm but distant look. The sounds of rustling paper, legal oaths, business discussions, and office chatter did not seem to reach his mind. His face looked gentle and kind when he was resting. If someone spoke to him, he looked polite and interested. This showed that his mind was still bright, even if his aging body made it hard to express. The more you talked to him, the clearer and sounder his mind seemed. When he did not have to speak or listen, which clearly took effort, his face would relax back into a quiet, peaceful expression. It was not sad to watch him, because his quiet state was not the senility of old age. His strong and massive character had not yet crumbled.

Trying to understand his character in this state was like trying to imagine a complete fortress, like Fort Ticonderoga, just by looking at its ruins. In some places, the walls might still stand. In other places, there might only be a shapeless mound, overgrown with grass and weeds after years of neglect.

Even though we did not talk much, I looked at the old warrior with real affection. In fact, everyone and every animal who knew him felt the same way. I could clearly see the main features of his character. He had noble and heroic qualities, which proved that he had earned his distinguished reputation by right, not by accident. I do not think he was ever naturally restless. At any stage of his life, he probably needed some external push to get him moving. However, once he was motivated, and had obstacles to overcome and a worthy goal to reach, he would never give up or fail. The passion that used to fill his nature was not gone yet. It was never the kind of fire that flashes and flickers in a bright blaze. Instead, it was like the deep, red glow of hot iron in a furnace. Weight, solidity, and firmness characterized his quiet presence, even though old age had begun to weaken him at that time.

Yet, I could still imagine him being deeply stirred by some great excitement. If a trumpet call rang out loud enough to wake his sleeping energy, he could throw off his physical weaknesses like a sick man discarding his robe. He would drop his walking staff, grab a battle sword, and stand up as a warrior once more. Even in such an intense moment, his behavior would remain calm. Of course, this was just something to imagine, not something to actually expect or want.

What I saw in him was a stubborn and heavy endurance, much like the indestructible walls of Old Ticonderoga. In his younger days, this endurance might have been sheer obstinacy. He also possessed a solid mass of honesty, which was as tough and unyielding as a ton of iron ore. Finally, he had a genuine kindness. Even though he had led fierce bayonet charges at Chippewa and Fort Erie, his kindness was far more real than that of the noisy philanthropists of our time. He might have killed men with his own hands for all I know. They certainly fell before his charges like grass before a scythe. But regardless of that, there was never enough cruelty in his heart to brush the dust off a butterfly's wing. I have never known a man whose natural kindness I would trust more.

Many of his traits must have faded or vanished before I met him, including the ones that would have made a portrait of him most vivid. Graceful qualities are usually the first to disappear. Nature does not decorate a decaying human life with new beauties, the way she grows wallflowers in the cracks of the ruined fortress at Ticonderoga. Still, there were still some graceful and beautiful aspects of his character worth mentioning. A flash of humor would occasionally break through his quiet demeanor and bring a pleasant smile to our faces. He also had a natural elegance that is rarely seen in men past their childhood. This was clear from his love for the sight and scent of flowers. You might expect an old soldier to care only for the bloody laurels of war, but he appreciated flowers with the gentle delight of a young girl.

The brave old General used to sit by the fireplace. As the Surveyor, I rarely tried to start a conversation with him because it was difficult. Instead, I liked to stand at a distance and watch his quiet, sleepy face. He seemed very far away, even though he sat just a few yards from us. He was remote when we walked past his chair, and unreachable even if we reached out and touched his hand. Perhaps he lived a more real life in his own thoughts than in the unfitting environment of the collector's office. He might have been visualizing military drills, the chaos of battle, and the heroic music he had heard thirty years before. Meanwhile, merchants, ship captains, neat clerks, and rough sailors walked in and out. The busy murmur of commercial life went on all around him. Yet, the General seemed completely disconnected from both the people and their business. He was entirely out of place there. He looked like an old, rusty sword that had once flashed in battle but still kept a gleam on its blade, sitting among the inkstands, paper folders, and mahogany rulers on the deputy collector's desk.

One memory helped me picture the strong soldier of the Niagara frontier, who was full of simple and genuine energy. I remembered his famous words: "I'll try, sir!" He spoke them just before starting a dangerous and heroic mission. Those words captured the true spirit of New England courage, which understands all dangers and faces them head-on. If our country rewarded bravery with coats of arms, this simple phrase would be the perfect motto for the General's shield. It seems easy to say, but only he said it when facing such a dangerous and glorious task.

It is very good for your mental and moral health to spend time with people who are completely different from you. It helps to be around those who do not care about your interests, forcing you to step outside yourself to appreciate their talents. My life has often given me this opportunity, but never more than during my time in this office. One man in particular showed me a completely new kind of talent. His gifts were those of a perfect businessman. He was fast, sharp, and clear-headed. He could see through any complication, and his organization skills made problems disappear as if by magic. He had worked at the Custom House since he was a boy, so it was his natural environment. The complex business procedures that frustrated outsiders were a simple, orderly system to him. I saw him as the perfect representative of his profession. In fact, he was the Custom House itself, or at least the main spring that kept all its gears turning. In an office like this, where people are appointed for their own profit and convenience rather than their qualifications, employees must look to others for the skills they lack. Naturally, like a magnet attracting steel filings, this businessman drew all the difficult problems to himself. He was patient and kind when dealing with our mistakes, even though our ignorance must have seen like a crime to someone with his sharp mind. With a simple touch of his finger, he could instantly make the most confusing issues clear. The merchants valued him just as much as we, his close colleagues, did. His honesty was absolute. It was a law of nature for him rather than a conscious moral choice. A mind as clear and precise as his had to be honest and orderly in its work. A mistake on his conscience regarding his job would trouble him in the same way, though much more deeply, as an error in a balance sheet or an ink blot on a clean page of a ledger. In short, he was one of those rare people who was perfectly suited for his job.

These were some of the people I was now connected with. I accepted this new position, so different from my old habits, as a gift from Providence. I set out to learn whatever I could from it. I had spent years working on impractical plans with the dreamers at Brook Farm. I had lived for three years under the quiet influence of Emerson's brilliant mind. I had enjoyed wild, free days on the Assabet River, discussing wild theories around a campfire with Ellery Channing. I had talked with Thoreau about pine trees and Native American relics at his cabin by Walden Pond. I had become highly refined by sharing the classical tastes of Hillard, and I had absorbed poetry by Longfellow's fireplace. After all of that, it was finally time for me to use different parts of my mind and experience something new. Even the old inspector was a welcome change of pace for someone who had known Bronson Alcott. I believe it shows my mind is well-balanced that I could leave those intellectual friends behind, mix with people of entirely different characters, and never complain about the transition.

During this time, writing and literature meant very little to me. I did not care about books, and they felt distant. Except for human nature, the natural world of the earth and sky was hidden from me. All the imaginative joy I used to find in nature faded from my mind. My creative writing talent, if it was not completely gone, was temporarily suspended and inactive. This would have been incredibly sad and dreary if I had not known that I could bring back my past whenever I wanted. It is true that I could not live this kind of life forever without paying a price. If I stayed too long, it might have permanently changed who I was without turning me into anyone I actually wanted to be. But I always saw this job as temporary. I had a constant feeling, a quiet whisper in my ear, that a change would come soon, whenever it was necessary for my own good.

Meanwhile, I remained there as the Surveyor of the Revenue. As far as I could tell, I did the job as well as anyone needed to. A thoughtful, imaginative, and sensitive person can always succeed in business if they are willing to make the effort, even if they have ten times more sensitivity than a surveyor needs. My coworkers, as well as the merchants and sea captains I worked with, saw me only as a government official. None of them had ever read anything I wrote, and they would not have cared if they had. It would not have made a difference if those writings had been as great as the work of Robert Burns or Geoffrey Chaucer. Both of those writers also worked as custom house officers in their time. It is a good and sometimes hard lesson for anyone who dreams of literary fame. Stepping outside the small circle of people who recognize your work shows you how unimportant your achievements and goals are to the rest of the world. I do not think I particularly needed this lesson as a warning or a rebuke, but I learned it completely. I am glad to say that this realization never caused me any pain or made me sigh. It is true that the Naval Officer, a great guy who started and left the office around the same time I did, would sometimes talk with me about Shakespeare or Napoleon. Also, the collector's junior clerk was rumored to write poetry on government paper. Now and then, he would mention books to me as something I might know about. This was the extent of my intellectual conversations, and it was more than enough for me.

I no longer cared to see my name printed on the title pages of books. Instead, I smiled to think of its new kind of popularity. The Custom House stencil marker painted my name onto pepper bags, baskets of food dye, cigar boxes, and all kinds of imported goods. This was proof that the taxes had been paid and the goods had cleared the office. Carried by these strange vehicles of fame, my name traveled to places it had never been before, and where I hope it will never go again.

Yet, the past was not dead. Once in a while, the creative thoughts that had been quietly set aside started to wake up again. One of the most notable times this happened is what makes it appropriate for me to share this story with the public now.

There is a large room on the second floor of the Custom-House. In this room, the brickwork and bare rafters have never been covered with paneling and plaster. The building was originally planned on a scale suited to the old commercial trade of the port, and was built with hopes of future prosperity that were never realized. It contains far more space than its occupants know how to use. This airy hall sits above the Collector’s apartments and remains unfinished to this day. Despite the old cobwebs hanging from its dark beams, it still looks like it is waiting for carpenters and masons to finish it. A number of barrels were piled on top of each other in a recess at one end of the room. These barrels contained bundles of official documents. Large amounts of similar trash cluttered the floor. It was sad to think how many days, weeks, months, and years of hard work had been wasted on these dusty papers. They were now just a burden on the earth, hidden away in this forgotten corner, and destined never to be looked at by human eyes again. At the same time, countless other manuscripts had also been forgotten. Unlike these dull official documents, those manuscripts were filled with the thoughts of creative minds and the deep feelings of passionate hearts. Sadly, those writings never even served a temporary purpose like these papers did. Worst of all, they did not earn their writers the comfortable living that the Custom-House clerks made from their own worthless writing. Yet, these documents might not be completely worthless as materials for local history. Here, someone could find statistics about the old trade of Salem, as well as records of its wealthy merchants. These included old King Derby, old Billy Gray, old Simon Forrester, and many other powerful men of their time. However, as soon as their powdered heads were in the grave, their massive fortunes began to shrink. We can also trace the founders of most families that now make up the high society of Salem. We can follow them from their small and humble beginnings in trade, mostly after the Revolution, all the way up to what their children now consider long-established noble rank.

There is a shortage of records from before the Revolution. The earlier documents and archives of the Custom-House were probably carried off to Halifax when the King’s officials fled Boston with the British army. I have often regretted this loss. Those papers, which went back to the days of the Protectorate, must have contained many references to forgotten or remembered men and to ancient customs. Finding them would have given me the same pleasure I felt when I used to pick up Indian arrowheads in the field near the Old Manse.

However, one idle, rainy day, I was lucky enough to make an interesting discovery. I was poking and burrowing through the piled-up trash in the corner. I unfolded various documents and read the names of ships that had long ago sunk at sea or rotted at the docks. I also read the names of merchants who are never heard of today on the stock exchange and whose names are hard to read on their mossy tombstones. I looked at these things with the sad, tired, and reluctant interest that we feel for the remains of past activity. I tried to use my imagination, which was rusty from lack of use, to bring these dry bones to life and picture the old town in its brighter days, back when India was a new region and only Salem knew the way there. As I did this, I happened to place my hand on a small package carefully wrapped in a piece of old yellow parchment. This envelope looked like an official record from a long time ago, when clerks wrote their stiff, formal handwriting on sturdier materials than we use today. Something about it sparked my curiosity. I undid the faded red tape that tied the package, feeling that I was about to discover a treasure. When I opened the stiff folds of the parchment cover, I found a commission signed and sealed by Governor Shirley. It appointed a man named Jonathan Pue as the Surveyor of His Majesty’s Customs for the port of Salem in the Province of Massachusetts Bay. I remembered reading about the death of Mr. Surveyor Pue about eighty years ago, probably in Felt’s Annals. I also remembered a recent newspaper article about his remains being dug up in the small graveyard of St. Peter’s Church while the building was being renovated. If I remember correctly, nothing was left of my respected predecessor except an incomplete skeleton, some scraps of clothing, and a grand, curly wig. Unlike the head it once covered, the wig was in very good condition. But when I examined the papers inside the parchment commission, I found more traces of Mr. Pue’s mind and thoughts than his curly wig had kept of his skull.

These documents were private rather than official, or at least they were written in his personal capacity, and apparently in his own handwriting. I could only explain how they ended up in the pile of Custom-House trash by assuming that Mr. Pue died suddenly. He probably kept these papers in his official desk, so his heirs never knew about them, or perhaps they assumed the papers were related to government business. When the archives were moved to Halifax, this package was left behind because it had no public value. It had remained unopened ever since.

The old Surveyor was probably not very busy with official duties in those early days. He seems to have spent his many free hours researching local history and looking into similar matters. This research gave some light activity to a mind that otherwise would have grown rusty. In fact, some of his findings were very useful to me when I wrote the article "Main Street," which is included in this volume. I might use the rest of the papers for other valuable projects in the future. Alternatively, I might use them to write a proper history of Salem, if my love for my hometown ever inspires me to take on such a project. In the meantime, they are available to any gentleman who is willing and able to take this unpaid labor off my hands. Eventually, I plan to donate them to the Essex Historical Society.

The object that caught my attention the most in the mysterious package was a piece of fine red cloth. It was worn and faded, with traces of gold embroidery that was heavily frayed and damaged, leaving very little of its original shine. It was clearly made with wonderful needlework skill. Women who understand these matters assured me that the stitch shows a forgotten art that cannot be recreated, even by taking the threads apart. This piece of scarlet cloth was little more than a rag, worn out by time, use, and hungry moths. However, on closer inspection, it took the shape of a letter. It was the capital letter A. When I measured it, I found that each side was exactly three and a quarter inches long. There was no doubt that it was meant to be an ornamental decoration on clothing. However, I saw little hope of finding out how it was worn, or what rank, honor, and dignity it represented in the past, because the world's fashions change so quickly. Even so, it interested me strangely. My eyes were fixed on the old scarlet letter, and I could not look away. It certainly had a deep meaning that deserved to be understood. This meaning seemed to flow from the mysterious symbol, quietly touching my emotions but escaping my mind's logical understanding.

While I was wondering about this, I considered several possibilities. I wondered if the letter might have been a decoration that white men made to catch the attention of Native Americans. During these thoughts, I happened to place the letter on my chest. The reader might smile, but you must not doubt my word. It felt to me as if I experienced a sensation that was almost physical. It was a feeling of burning heat, as if the letter were made of red-hot iron instead of red cloth. I shuddered and accidentally dropped it onto the floor.

Because I was so focused on studying the scarlet letter, I had neglected to examine a small roll of dirty paper that had been wrapped around it. I opened this roll now, and I was pleased to find a fairly complete explanation of the whole matter written by the old Surveyor. There were several large sheets of paper containing many details about the life and behavior of a woman named Hester Prynne. She seemed to have been a remarkable person in the eyes of our ancestors. She lived during the period between the early days of Massachusetts and the end of the seventeenth century. Elderly people who were alive during Mr. Surveyor Pue’s lifetime, and whose spoken stories he used to write his account, remembered her from their youth. They described her as a very old but still strong woman, with a dignified and serious appearance. For as long as anyone could remember, she had traveled around the area acting as a volunteer nurse and doing whatever good deeds she could. She also took it upon herself to give advice on all kinds of matters, especially those of the heart. Because of these actions, she naturally earned the respect of many people who viewed her as an angel, though I imagine others looked at her as an intrusive nuisance. Reading further into the manuscript, I found accounts of other experiences and struggles of this unusual woman. For most of these details, I refer the reader to the story titled "The Scarlet Letter." You should keep in mind that the main facts of that story are supported and verified by Mr. Surveyor Pue's document. I still have the original papers and the scarlet letter itself, which is a very interesting relic. I will gladly show them to anyone who wants to see them because of their interest in the story. However, I do not mean to say that I kept strictly to the old Surveyor’s few pages of notes when I wrote the story and imagined the feelings and motives of the characters. On the contrary, I allowed myself almost complete freedom with those details, just as if I had made up the facts myself. What I do insist on is the accuracy of the basic outline.

This incident brought my mind back to its old track to some degree. There seemed to be the foundation of a story here. I felt as if the old Surveyor, dressed in the style of a hundred years ago and wearing his famous wig, had met me in the empty room of the Custom-House. His wig was buried with him, but it did not rot in the grave. In his posture, he carried the dignity of a man who had held the King’s commission. He was illuminated by a reflection of the glory that shone around the throne. This was very different from the ashamed look of a democratic official today, who, as a servant of the public, feels inferior to the lowest of his masters. The dim but majestic figure handed me the scarlet symbol and the small roll of explanatory notes with his own ghostly hand. With his own ghostly voice, he urged me, out of duty and respect for him as my official predecessor, to publish his old, moldy writings. The ghost of Mr. Surveyor Pue nodded his head, which looked impressive in its famous wig, and said, "Do this, and the profit will be all yours! You will need it soon, because in your time, a government job is not a lifelong position or a family inheritance like it was in mine. But I command you, when you tell the story of old Mistress Prynne, give your predecessor's memory the credit it deserves!" And I replied to the ghost of Mr. Surveyor Pue, "I will!"

Therefore, I thought a lot about Hester Prynne’s story. It was the subject of my thoughts for many hours. I paced back and forth across my room, or walked hundreds of times along the path from the front door of the Custom House to the side entrance and back again. The old Inspector, the weighers, and the gaugers felt great weariness and annoyance. My endless footsteps back and forth constantly disturbed their naps. They remembered their own past habits and used to say that the Surveyor was walking the quarter-deck. They probably imagined that my only goal was to build up an appetite for dinner. Indeed, they believed that was the only reason any sane man would ever choose to walk. To tell the truth, an appetite, sharpened by the cold east wind that usually blew through the passage, was the only valuable result of so much tireless exercise. 

The atmosphere of a custom house is so poorly suited to the delicate work of imagination and feeling that I doubt I would have ever published "The Scarlet Letter" if I had stayed there through ten more presidential terms. My imagination was like a dirty mirror. It would not reflect the figures I tried to place in it, or would only show them with miserable dimness. The characters of the story could not be warmed or shaped by any heat I tried to create in my mind. They would not take on the warmth of passion or the tenderness of emotion. Instead, they remained as stiff as corpses, staring back at me with a fixed, ghastly grin of contempt. Their expressions seemed to say, "What do you have to do with us? The small power you once had over imaginary beings is gone. You have traded it for a tiny amount of government gold. Go, then, and earn your wages!" In short, these inactive creatures of my own imagination mocked me for my weakness, and they had good reason to do so.

This miserable numbness did not just control me during the three and a half hours that the government claimed from my daily life. It followed me on my walks along the seashore and my trips into the country. I rarely and reluctantly forced myself to seek out the refreshing charm of nature. In the past, nature used to give me fresh, active thoughts the moment I stepped across the threshold of the Old Manse. The same mental sluggishness followed me home and weighed on me in the room I absurdly called my study. It did not leave me even late at night, when I sat in the empty parlor lit only by the glowing coal fire and the moon. I would try to picture imaginary scenes that might flow onto the page the next day in colorful descriptions.

If the imagination refused to work at such an hour, the case seemed truly hopeless. Moonlight in a familiar room is the perfect medium for a writer of romance to meet his imaginary guests. The light falls white upon the carpet, showing all its patterns clearly. It makes every object visible in detail, yet looks nothing like the light of morning or noon. There is the familiar scene of the room: the chairs, each with its own character; the center table, holding a work basket, a book or two, and an unlit lamp; the sofa, the bookcase, and the picture on the wall. All of these details are seen so completely and are so spiritualized by the unusual light that they seem to lose their physical form and become objects of the mind. Nothing is too small or too trivial to undergo this change and gain dignity. A child's shoe, a doll sitting in her little wicker carriage, or a hobby-horse, in short, anything that has been used or played with during the day is now filled with a sense of strangeness and distance, even though it is still as clearly visible as it is in daylight. Therefore, the floor of our familiar room becomes a neutral territory between the real world and fairyland. Here, reality and imagination can meet and blend together. Ghosts could enter this room without scaring us. It would fit the scene too well to surprise us if we looked around and saw a deceased loved one sitting quietly in a streak of this magical moonshine. They would look so natural that we might doubt whether they had returned from far away or had never left our fireside.

The dim coal fire also plays an essential role in creating this effect. It casts a subtle color throughout the room, showing a faint red glow on the walls and ceiling, and reflecting off the polished furniture. This warmer light mixes with the cold, spiritual moonbeams. It adds a heart and the tenderness of human feeling to the forms that the imagination calls up, converting them from cold figures of snow into real men and women. When we look into the mirror, we see deep within its reflective borders the glowing embers of the dying coal, the white moonbeams on the floor, and a reflection of all the light and shadow in the room. This reflection is one step further from reality and closer to the imagination. If a man sitting all alone with this scene before him at such an hour cannot dream of strange things and make them seem real, he should never try to write romances.

But for me, during my entire time at the Custom House, moonlight, sunshine, and firelight were all the same. None of them was any more useful than the flicker of a cheap candle. An entire range of feelings and the creative gift connected to them was gone from me. It was not of great richness or value, but it was the best I had.

However, I believe that if I had tried a different kind of writing, my mind would not have felt so dull and useless. For example, I could have written down the stories of a veteran shipmaster who served as one of the Inspectors. I would be very ungrateful not to mention him, since hardly a day passed that he did not make me laugh and admire his wonderful storytelling talent. If I could have captured the colorful strength of his style and the humor he naturally brought to his descriptions, I truly believe the result would have been something new in literature. Or I could have easily found a more serious task. It was foolish of me to try to escape into another era while the physical reality of daily life pressed so hard upon me. It was foolish to insist on creating a fictional world out of thin air when the delicate beauty of my imaginary bubble was broken every moment by the harsh reality of my surroundings. A wiser effort would have been to bring thought and imagination to the solid reality of the present day, making it bright and clear. I should have spiritualized the burden that was starting to weigh so heavily. I should have looked determinedly for the true, lasting value hidden in the trivial, tiring events and ordinary characters that I dealt with every day. The fault was mine. The page of life spread out before me seemed dull and commonplace only because I had not understood its deeper meaning. A better book than I will ever write was right there. Page after page presented itself to me, written by the reality of the passing moment, and vanished as fast as it was written. This happened only because my mind lacked the insight and my hand lacked the skill to write it down. Perhaps in the future, I will remember a few scattered fragments and broken paragraphs, write them down, and watch the words turn to gold on the page.

These insights have come too late. At the time, I only knew that what once would have been a pleasure was now a hopeless chore. There was no reason to complain too much about this state of affairs. I had stopped being a writer of mediocre stories and essays, and I had become a reasonably good Surveyor of Customs. That was all. Yet, it is not pleasant to be haunted by the suspicion that your mind is fading away, or evaporating like ether from a bottle without your realizing it, leaving a smaller and less active amount every time you look. There was no doubt about this fact. After examining myself and others, I reached conclusions about the effect of public office on a person's character, and they were not very favorable to this way of life. Perhaps I will write about these effects in more detail in another form. For now, it is enough to say that a long-serving Custom House officer can hardly be a highly respectable figure. This is true for many reasons, including how he keeps his job and the nature of his work. Although I trust it is an honest business, it is the kind of work that does not contribute to the shared efforts of humanity.

One effect is observable, more or less, in everyone who has held this position: while he leans on the strong arm of the government, his own strength leaves him. Depending on the strength of his original character, he loses his ability to support himself. If he has an unusual amount of natural energy, or if the weakening influence of the job does not affect him for too long, his lost abilities might be recovered. The fired officer is fortunate to receive the unkind shove that sends him out early to struggle in a competitive world. He may return to himself and become everything he once was. But this rarely happens. He usually stays in office just long enough to ruin himself. Then he is thrown out, completely weak and helpless, to stumble along the difficult path of life as best he can. He is conscious of his own weakness and knows that his sharp edge and flexibility are gone. Therefore, he forever looks around for external support. His constant and dominant hope is that he will eventually be restored to office. This hope is a hallucination that haunts him while he lives, ignoring all discouragement and impossibilities. I even imagine it torments him for a short time after death, like the convulsions of cholera. This belief, more than anything else, drains the energy and motivation from any project he might dream of starting. Why should he work hard and struggle to pull himself up out of the mud when the strong arm of his Uncle Sam will soon lift him up and support him? Why should he work for a living or go dig for gold in California when he will soon be made happy every month with a small pile of shiny coins from his Uncle's pocket? It is sad and strange to see how small a taste of public office is enough to infect a person with this strange disease. Uncle Sam's gold has an enchanting quality like devil's wages, though I mean no disrespect to the worthy old gentleman. Anyone who touches it must be careful, or they will find the bargain goes against them. It might not take their soul, but it will take many of their best qualities, such as strength, courage, consistency, truth, self-reliance, and everything that makes a strong character.

This was a gloomy prospect in the distance! Not that the Surveyor applied this lesson to himself, or admitted that he could be so completely ruined by either staying in office or being fired. Still, my thoughts were not very comfortable. I began to grow sad and restless, constantly searching my mind to see which of its abilities were gone and how much damage had already been done to the rest. I tried to figure out how much longer I could stay at the Custom House and still leave as a whole man. To tell the truth, my greatest worry was that I was likely to grow gray and weak in this job, becoming just like the old Inspector. It was never standard policy to fire someone as quiet as me, and it is rarely in the nature of a public officer to resign. I wondered if the tedious routine of public service would eventually make me like my old friend, making dinner the highlight of the day and spending the rest of the time like an old dog asleep in the sun or shade. This was a depressing future for a man who believed that true happiness meant using all of his mind and feelings. But during all this time, I was worrying for no reason. Providence had planned better things for me than I could ever imagine for myself.

During my third year as Surveyor, a major event happened. General Taylor was elected to the presidency. To fully understand the reality of holding a government office, you must see what happens to the officeholder when a hostile political administration takes over. The officeholder's position becomes one of the most annoying and uncomfortable situations a person can experience. There are rarely any good options left. However, what seems like the worst possible outcome may actually turn out to be the best. For a person of pride and sensitivity, it is a strange experience to know that their livelihood is controlled by people who do not care about or understand them. Because one or the other must happen, the officer would actually rather be hurt by these people than receive a favor from them. It is also strange for someone who stayed calm during the election to see the cruelty that arises in the hour of victory, and to realize that they are one of the targets. There are few uglier traits in human nature than the tendency to become cruel simply because you have the power to hurt someone. I witnessed this behavior in men who were otherwise no worse than their neighbors. The political guillotine is a common metaphor for firing officeholders. If it were a literal machine, I truly believe that the enthusiastic members of the winning party were angry enough to have chopped off all our heads, and thanked God for the chance. I have been a calm and curious observer in both victory and defeat. It seems to me that this bitter spirit of malice and revenge has never marked the victories of my own party, the Democrats, the way it now marked the victory of the Whigs. Generally, Democrats take government offices because they need the work. Years of practice have made this the standard rule of political warfare. Unless a different system is established, it would be weak and cowardly to complain about it. Furthermore, their long habit of winning has made them generous. They know when to show mercy. When they do fire someone, the blow is quick, but it is rarely done with personal hatred. It is also not their style to insult and kick the person they have just fired.

In short, even though my situation was unpleasant, I had good reason to prefer being on the losing side. I had never been the most passionate supporter of my party. Now, facing this difficult time, I began to feel more clearly where my loyalties lay. I also felt some regret and embarrassment. Based on the odds, it seemed more likely that I would keep my job than my fellow Democrats. But who can predict what comes next? As it turned out, my head was the first to fall.

Meanwhile, the newspapers picked up my story and kept me in the public eye for a week or two. I was described as a man who had lost his head politically, ghastly and grim, like someone desperate to be put to rest but unable to find peace. That was my public image. In reality, the actual man still had his head firmly on his shoulders. He came to the comfortable conclusion that everything had worked out for the best. He bought ink, paper, and pens, opened his long-unused writing desk, and became a writer again.

Now the old notes of my predecessor, Mr. Surveyor Pue, finally became useful. My mind had been idle for so long that it needed time to warm up before it could work on the story in any satisfying way. Even when I was fully absorbed in the task, the story still felt stern and gloomy to me. It was not lit up by enough cheerfulness, and it lacked the softening warmth that human kindness brings to almost every scene in real life. This somber quality probably reflects the time of upheaval and unrest in which the story was written. But it does not mean the writer was unhappy. In fact, while working through the dark themes of this story, he was happier than he had been at any point since leaving the Old Manse. Some of the shorter pieces in this collection were also written after he left his government job. The rest were gathered from old magazines and journals so out of date that they had come back around to feeling fresh again. To continue the idea of a political removal, this whole collection can be thought of as the final papers of a Surveyor who has been dismissed from office. This personal sketch, which closes the introduction, is perhaps too autobiographical for a modest man to publish while he is still alive. But surely it can be forgiven when written by a man who speaks, in a sense, from beyond the grave. Peace to the world. A blessing to my friends. Forgiveness to my enemies. I am at rest.[1]

[Footnote 1: At the time of writing this article the author intended to publish, along with “The Scarlet Letter,” several shorter tales and sketches. These it has been thought advisable to defer.]

The life of the Custom House now lies like a dream behind me. The old Inspector, who I regret to say was knocked down and killed by a horse some time ago, he and all those other old figures who sat with him at the customs counter are now just shadows in my memory. They are white-haired and wrinkled images that my imagination once played with and has now set aside forever. The merchants, Pingree, Phillips, Shepard, Upton, Kimball, Bertram, Hunt, and many other names that felt so familiar to me just six months ago, these men of business who seemed so important in the world, how little time it has taken to separate me from all of them, not just in action but in memory. I can only recall their faces and names with some effort now. Soon, my old hometown will also fade into a haze of memory, surrounded by a kind of mist, as if it were not a real place at all but an overgrown village in the clouds, with only imaginary people living in its wooden houses and walking its plain streets. It is no longer a real part of my life. I am a citizen of somewhere else. My fellow townspeople will not miss me much. I always hoped that my writing would make me matter to them and leave a pleasant memory in this place where so many of my ancestors lived and are buried. But I never found the warm atmosphere there that a writer needs to do his best work. I will do better among new faces, and the familiar ones will do just as well without me.

It may be, however, what a wonderful and exciting thought, that the great-grandchildren of the people living today will sometimes think kindly of this humble writer from long ago. Perhaps a future historian, pointing out the notable sites of the town, will mention the location of THE TOWN PUMP!

A Note on This Modernized Edition

This edition of The Scarlet Letter was adapted to make Hawthorne's masterpiece accessible to language learners, students, and casual readers. We applied the following modernizing guidelines throughout:

* Audiobook and Listening Optimization: Every sentence was structured for natural spoken flow. Subjects come first, and complex poetic inversions were removed so the narrative is easy to follow when listening.

* Linear Idea Progression: Complex or winding thoughts were broken into clear, logical steps. This helps the reader follow the action and emotion of Hester's story without losing the thread of the sentence.

* Clean Punctuation: Visual clutter such as em-dashes and parenthetical hyphens was removed. They were replaced with smooth commas or separate sentences.

* Aesthetic Restoration: All 58 original illustrations, decorative ornaments, and initial drop caps from the 1878 edition were preserved and digitally colorized to bring the visual beauty of the era to life.

The Five Laws of Gold

<rest>A bag heavy with gold, or a clay tablet carved with words of wisdom. If you had the choice, which would you choose?</rest>

By the flickering light of the desert shrub fire, the sun-tanned faces of the listeners gleamed with interest.

<rest>The gold, the gold!</rest> the twenty-seven men answered in a chorus.

Old Kalabab smiled knowingly.

<rest>Listen,</rest> he resumed, raising his hand. <rest>Hear the wild dogs out there in the night. They howl and wail because they are starving. Yet if you feed them, what do they do? They fight and strut. Then they fight and strut some more. They give no thought to tomorrow, which will surely come.

<rest>It is the exact same with men. Give them a choice between gold and wisdom, and what do they do? They ignore the wisdom and waste the gold. Then, when tomorrow comes, they wail because they have no more gold.

<rest>Gold is reserved for those who know its laws and abide by them.</rest>

Kalabab drew his white robe closely around his lean legs. A cool night wind had started blowing.

<rest>You have served me faithfully on our long journey. You cared well for my camels. You worked without complaining across the hot desert sands. You also fought bravely against the robbers who tried to steal my merchandise. Because of all this, I will tell you a story tonight. It is the tale of the Five Laws of Gold. You have never heard a story like this before.

<rest>Pay close attention to the words I speak. If you understand their meaning and follow them, you will have much gold in the days to come.</rest>

He paused for effect. Above them in a canopy of blue, the stars shone brightly in the crystal clear skies of Babylonia. Behind the group stood their faded tents, which were tightly staked to survive desert storms. Beside the tents sat neatly stacked bales of merchandise, covered with animal skins. Nearby, the camel herd sprawled out in the sand. Some chewed their cud contentedly, while others snored loudly.

<rest>You have told us many good tales, Kalabab,</rest> the chief packer spoke up. <rest>We look to your wisdom to guide us tomorrow, when our service with you finally comes to an end.</rest>

<rest>I have only told you about my adventures in strange and distant lands. But tonight, I will tell you about the wisdom of Arkad, the wise and wealthy man.</rest>

<rest>We have heard much about him,</rest> the chief packer acknowledged. <rest>He was the richest man who ever lived in Babylon.</rest>

<rest>He truly was the richest man. That was because he was wiser in the ways of gold than any man before him. Tonight, I will share his great wisdom with you. It was told to me by his son, Nomasir, many years ago in Nineveh, when I was just a young boy.

<rest>My master and I had stayed late into the night at Nomasir's palace. I had helped my master carry great bundles of fine rugs. Nomasir tested each one until he found the exact colors he wanted. Finally, he was completely satisfied. He commanded us to sit down with him and drink a rare, fragrant wine. It was a very warming drink for my stomach, which was not used to such luxury.

<rest>Then, Nomasir told us this tale about the great wisdom of his father, Arkad. I will now tell it to you just as he told it to me.

<rest>In Babylon, as you know, it is customary for the sons of wealthy fathers to live with their parents. They simply wait to inherit the family estate. However, Arkad did not approve of this custom. So, when Nomasir became a man, Arkad sent for him and said:

<rest> 'My son, I want you to inherit my estate. However, you must first prove that you are capable of handling it wisely. Therefore, I want you to go out into the world. You must show your ability to acquire gold and earn the respect of other men.

<rest> 'To give you a good start, I will give you two things. I did not have either of these when I started as a poor youth trying to build my fortune.

<rest> 'First, I give you this bag of gold. If you use it wisely, it will be the foundation of your future success.

<rest> 'Second, I give you this clay tablet. Carved upon it are the Five Laws of Gold. If you apply these laws to your own actions, they will bring you wealth and security.

<rest> 'Ten years from today, come back to your father's house and give an account of yourself. If you prove yourself worthy, I will make you the heir to my estate. Otherwise, I will give everything to the priests. They can use it to bargain with the gods for my soul.'

<rest>So, Nomasir went out to make his own way in the world. He took his bag of gold, his slave, and the horses they rode on. He also took the clay tablet, which was carefully wrapped in a silk cloth.

<rest>Ten years passed. Nomasir kept his promise and returned to his father's house. Arkad threw a great feast in his honor and invited many friends and relatives. After the feast ended, Nomasir's parents sat on their throne-like seats on one side of the great hall. Nomasir stood before them to give an account of himself, just as he had promised.

<rest>It was evening. The room was hazy with smoke from the dimly lit oil lamps. Slaves in white woven jackets and tunics rhythmically fanned the humid air with long palm leaves. The scene felt very dignified and serious. Nomasir's wife and his two young sons sat on rugs behind him. Friends and other family members sat there as well, all eager to listen.

<rest> 'My father,' Nomasir began respectfully. 'I bow before your wisdom. Ten years ago, I stood at the gates of manhood. You told me to go out and become a real man among men, rather than staying here as a servant to your fortune.

<rest> 'You gave me your gold freely. You gave me your wisdom freely. But sadly, I must admit I handled the gold disastrously. It slipped right through my inexperienced hands. It ran away just like a wild rabbit flees from the boy who catches it.'

<rest>His father smiled gently. 'Continue, my son. Your story interests me in every detail.'

<rest> 'I decided to go to Nineveh because it was a growing city. I believed I might find good opportunities there. I joined a caravan, and I made many friends among its members. Two of these men were very well-spoken. They owned a beautiful white horse that could run as fast as the wind.

<rest> 'As we traveled, they told me a secret. They said a wealthy man in Nineveh owned a horse that had never been beaten in a race. Its owner believed no living horse could run faster. Because of this, he would bet any amount of money that his horse could beat any horse in all of Babylonia. My new friends told me that compared to their white horse, the wealthy man's horse was just a slow donkey. They said they could beat it easily.

<rest> 'They offered to let me join them in their bet. They acted as if they were doing me a great favor. I was completely carried away by the idea.

<rest> 'However, our horse was badly beaten, and I lost much of my gold.' Arkad laughed at this. Nomasir continued, 'Later, I discovered it was a scam. These men constantly traveled with caravans looking for victims. The wealthy man in Nineveh was actually their partner. He shared the bets he won with them. That clever trick taught me my first lesson in looking out for myself.

<rest> 'I soon learned another bitter lesson. There was another young man in the caravan who became a close friend of mine. He was also the son of wealthy parents, traveling to Nineveh to find a good place to settle. Shortly after we arrived, he told me that a merchant had died. He said we could buy the man's shop, along with its rich merchandise and loyal customers, for a very low price. He suggested we become equal partners. But first, he said he needed to return to Babylon to get his gold. He convinced me to buy the shop's inventory using my gold. We agreed that his gold would be used later to run our business.

<rest> 'He delayed his trip to Babylon for a long time. Meanwhile, he proved to be a terrible buyer and a foolish spender. I finally kicked him out of the partnership. But by then, the business had already fallen apart. We only had goods that no one wanted to buy, and we had no gold left to buy new merchandise. In the end, I sold whatever was left to an Israelite for a pitiful sum.'</rest>


<rest>Bitter days soon followed, my father. I looked for work but couldn't find any, because I didn't have any trade or training that would allow me to earn a living. I sold my horses. I sold my slave. I sold my extra robes just so I could have food and a place to sleep. But every day, grim poverty crept closer.

<rest>But during those bitter days, I remembered your confidence in me, father. You had sent me out into the world to become a man, and I was determined to achieve that goal.</rest> His mother buried her face in her hands and wept softly. <rest>At that time, I remembered the clay tablet you had given me, the one where you carved the five laws of gold. I read your words of wisdom very carefully. I finally realized that if I had only sought wisdom first, I wouldn't have lost all my gold.

<rest>I learned each law by heart. I made a promise to myself that whenever the goddess of good fortune smiled on me again, I would be guided by the wisdom of age, rather than the inexperience of youth.

<rest>For the benefit of everyone sitting here tonight, I will read my father's wisdom. This is exactly what was engraved on the clay tablet he gave me ten years ago:

**THE FIVE LAWS OF GOLD**

**I.** Gold comes gladly and in increasing quantities to any person who saves at least one-tenth of their earnings. This creates an estate for their future and the future of their family.

**II.** Gold works diligently and contentedly for the wise owner who finds profitable employment for it. It multiplies just like the flocks in the field.

**III.** Gold clings to the protection of a cautious owner who invests it under the advice of people wise in handling money.

**IV.** Gold slips away from the person who invests it in businesses or purposes they are not familiar with, or that are not approved by those skilled in managing money.

**V.** Gold flees the person who tries to force it to impossible earnings. It also flees those who follow the alluring advice of tricksters and schemers, or who trust their own inexperience and romantic desires in investing.

<rest>These are the five laws of gold as written by my father. I proclaim they are actually worth more than gold itself. I will prove this to you as I continue my story.</rest>

He turned to face his father again. <rest>I have told you about the depths of poverty and despair that my inexperience brought me.

<rest>However, every chain of disasters eventually comes to an end. My struggles ended when I found a job managing a crew of slaves. We were working on building the new outer wall of the city.

<rest>Using my knowledge of the first law of gold, I saved a single copper coin from my very first earnings. I added to it at every opportunity until I finally had a piece of silver. It was a slow process, because a person still has to pay for their living expenses. I admit I spent money grudgingly during that time. I was absolutely determined to earn back just as much gold as you had given me before the ten years were up.

<rest>One day, the slave master, who had become a good friend of mine, said, 'You are a thrifty young man. You don't recklessly spend what you earn. Do you have any gold saved up that isn't currently earning anything for you?'

<rest>'Yes,' I replied. 'My greatest desire is to accumulate enough gold to replace what my father gave me, which I foolishly lost.'

<rest>'That is a very worthy ambition,' he said. 'But do you realize that the gold you have saved can actually work for you and earn even more gold?'

<rest>'Unfortunately, my past experiences have been bitter,' I told him. 'My father's gold fled from me. I'm terrified that my own savings might do exactly the same thing.'

<rest>'If you trust me, I can give you a lesson in handling gold profitably,' he replied. 'Within a year, the outer wall will be completed. It will be ready for the massive bronze gates that will be built at every entrance to protect the city from the king's enemies.

<rest>'In all of Nineveh, there isn't enough metal to actually make these gates, and the king hasn't thought ahead to provide it. So, here is my plan. A group of us will pool our gold together and send a caravan far away to the copper and tin mines. We will bring the metal for the gates back to Nineveh. When the king finally gives the order to make the great gates, we will be the only ones who can supply the metal, and he will pay a rich price for it. Even if the king decides not to buy from us, we will still have valuable metal that can be sold for a fair price.'

<rest>I recognized this offer as a perfect opportunity to follow the third law. I could invest my savings under the guidance of wise men. I certainly wasn't disappointed. Our investment pool was a huge success. My small stash of gold was greatly increased by the whole transaction.

<rest>In due time, I was accepted as a member of this same group for other ventures. These were men who were incredibly wise in the profitable handling of gold. They carefully discussed every single plan before getting involved. They refused to take any chances on losing their initial investment. They wouldn't tie their money up in unprofitable investments where their gold couldn't be recovered. Foolish things like the horse race and the bad partnership I had entered into in my youth would have received zero consideration from them. They would have immediately pointed out all the flaws.

<rest>Through my association with these men, I learned how to safely invest gold to bring in profitable returns. As the years went on, my wealth increased more and more rapidly. I didn't just make back what I had lost; I made back much, much more.

<rest>Through my misfortunes, my trials, and my eventual success, I have tested the wisdom of the five laws of gold time and time again, father. They have proven to be true in every single test. For a person who doesn't know the five laws, gold rarely comes, and it goes away quickly. But for the person who actually follows the five laws, gold comes freely and works as their dutiful slave.</rest>

Nomasir stopped speaking and motioned to a slave at the back of the room. The slave brought forward three heavy leather bags, one at a time. Nomasir took one of these bags and placed it on the floor in front of his father. He addressed him again.

<rest>You gave me one bag of Babylon gold. In its place, I am returning a bag of Nineveh gold of equal weight. It is a perfectly equal exchange, as anyone would agree.

<rest>You also gave me a clay tablet inscribed with wisdom. In its place, I am returning two full bags of gold.</rest> Saying this, he took the other two bags from the slave and placed them on the floor in front of his father as well.

<rest>I do this to prove to you, father, that I consider your wisdom to be of far greater value than your gold. But really, who can measure the true value of wisdom in bags of gold? Without wisdom, gold is quickly lost by those who have it. But with wisdom, gold can be secured by those who have nothing, just as these three bags of gold prove.

<rest>It gives me the deepest satisfaction, father, to stand before you today. I can proudly say that because of your wisdom, I have become rich and respected among men.</rest>

His father placed his hand fondly on Nomasir's head. <arkad>You have learned your lessons very well. I am incredibly fortunate to have a son that I can safely entrust my wealth to.</arkad>

Kalabab finished his story and looked critically at his listeners.

<rest>What does this story of Nomasir mean to you?</rest> he continued.

<rest>Who among you can go to your father, or to your wife's father, and give a good account of how wisely you have handled your earnings?

<rest>What would these respected men think if you said to them, 'I have traveled a lot, learned a lot, worked hard, and earned a lot. But sadly, I have very little gold left. Some of it I spent wisely, some of it I spent foolishly, and a lot of it I lost in unwise ways'?</rest>


<rest>Do you still think it is just a quirk of fate that some men have much gold and others have nothing? Then you are wrong. 

<rest>Men have much gold when they know the five laws of gold and follow them. 

<rest>Because I learned these five laws in my youth and followed them, I have become a wealthy merchant. I did not accumulate my wealth by some strange magic. 

<rest>Wealth that comes quickly goes quickly. 

<rest>Wealth that stays to give enjoyment and satisfaction to its owner comes gradually. It is a child born of knowledge and persistent purpose.</rest> 

<rest>Earning wealth is only a slight burden upon the thoughtful man. Bearing the burden consistently from year to year achieves the final goal.</rest> 

<rest>The five laws of gold offer you a rich reward for following them. Each of these five laws is rich with meaning. So you do not overlook this in the briefness of my tale, I will now repeat them. I know them each by heart. In my youth, I could see their value, and I was not content until I knew them word for word.</rest> 



**The First Law of Gold**

*Gold comes gladly and in increasing quantity to any man who will set aside not less than one-tenth of his earnings to create an estate for his future and that of his family.*

<rest>Any man who will consistently set aside one-tenth of his earnings and invest it wisely will surely create a valuable estate. This will provide an income for him in the future. It will also guarantee safety for his family in case the gods call him to the world of darkness. This law always says that gold comes gladly to such a man. I can truly verify this in my own life. The more gold I accumulate, the more readily it comes to me, and in increased quantities. The gold I save earns more, just as yours will. Its earnings earn even more, and this is how the first law works.</rest>

**The Second Law of Gold**

*Gold labors diligently and contentedly for the wise owner who finds profitable employment for it, multiplying even as the flocks of the field.*

<rest>Gold is indeed a willing worker. It is always eager to multiply when the opportunity presents itself. To every man who has a store of gold set aside, opportunity comes for its most profitable use. As the years pass, it multiplies itself in surprising ways.</rest>







**The Third Law of Gold**

*Gold clings to the protection of the cautious owner who invests it under the advice of men wise in its handling.*

<rest>Gold indeed clings to the cautious owner, just as it flees the careless owner. The man who seeks the advice of men wise in handling gold soon learns not to risk his treasure. Instead, he learns to preserve it in safety and to enjoy its consistent increase in contentment.</rest>



**The Fourth Law of Gold**

*Gold slips away from the man who invests it in businesses or purposes with which he is not familiar, or which are not approved by those skilled in its keep.*

<rest>To the man who has gold, yet is not skilled in handling it, many uses for it appear highly profitable. Too often, these are filled with the danger of loss. If properly analyzed by wise men, they show a small possibility of profit. Therefore, the inexperienced owner of gold who trusts his own judgment and invests it in businesses or purposes with which he is not familiar, often finds his judgment imperfect. He pays with his treasure for his inexperience. Wise indeed is the man who invests his treasures under the advice of men skilled in the ways of gold.</rest>



**The Fifth Law of Gold**

*Gold flees the man who would force it to make impossible earnings. It flees the man who follows the alluring advice of tricksters and schemers. It also flees the man who trusts it to his own inexperience and romantic desires in investment.*

<rest>Fanciful proposals that thrill like adventure tales always come to the new owner of gold. These appear to give his treasure magic powers that will enable it to make impossible earnings. Yet, listen to the wise men. They truly know the risks that lurk behind every plan to make great wealth suddenly.</rest> 

<rest>Do not forget the rich men of Nineveh. They would take no chance of losing their principal or tying it up in unprofitable investments. This ends my tale of the five laws of gold. In telling it to you, I have told the secrets of my own success.</rest> 

<rest>Yet, they are not secrets. They are truths that every man must first learn and then follow. That is, if he wishes to step out of the crowd of people who, like wild dogs, must worry each day about finding food to eat. Tomorrow, we enter Babylon. Look! See the eternal fire that burns above the Temple of Bel! We are already in sight of the golden city.</rest> 

<rest>Tomorrow, each of you shall have gold. It is the gold you have so well earned by your faithful services.</rest> 

<rest>Ten years from this night, what will you be able to tell about this gold?</rest> 

<rest>If there are men among you who, like Nomasir, will use a portion of their gold to start an estate for themselves, they will be wise. If they are guided from then on by the wisdom of Arkad, it is a safe bet that ten years from now, they will be rich and respected among men, just like the son of Arkad.</rest> 





<rest>Our wise acts accompany us through life to please us and to help us. Just as surely, our unwise acts follow us to plague and torment us. Alas, they cannot be forgotten. In the front rank of the torments that follow us are the memories of the things we should have done. These are the memories of the opportunities that came to us, which we did not take.</rest> 

<rest>The treasures of Babylon are rich. They are so rich that no man can count their value in pieces of gold. Each year, they grow richer and more valuable. Like the treasures of every land, they are a reward. They are a rich reward awaiting those men of purpose who determine to secure their rightful share.</rest> 

<rest>There is a magic power in the strength of your own desires. Guide this power with your knowledge of the five laws of gold, and you shall share the treasures of Babylon.</rest>

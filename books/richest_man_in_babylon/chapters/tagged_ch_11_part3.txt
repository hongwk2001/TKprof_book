My dear professor:

November 7th, 1936.

If, in your further digging into those ruins of Babylon, you encounter the ghost of a former resident, an old camel trader named Dabasir, do me a favor. Tell him that his scribbling upon those clay tablets, so long ago, has earned for him the lifelong gratitude of a couple of college folks back here in England.

You will possibly remember my writing a year ago that Mrs. Shrewsbury and I intended to try his plan for getting out of debt and at the same time having gold to jingle. You may have guessed, even though we tried to keep it from our friends, our desperate straits.

We were frightfully humiliated for years by a lot of old debts and worried sick for fear some of the tradespeople might start a scandal that would force me out of the college. We paid and paid—every shilling we could squeeze out of income—but it was hardly enough to hold things even. Besides, we were forced to do all our buying where we could get further credit, regardless of higher costs.

It developed into one of those vicious circles that grow worse instead of better. Our struggles were getting hopeless. We could not move to less costly rooms because we owed the landlord. There did not appear to be anything we could do to improve our situation.

Then, here comes your acquaintance, the old camel trader from Babylon, with a plan to do just what we wished to accomplish. He really stirred us up to follow his system. We made a list of all our debts, and I took it around and showed it to everyone we owed.

I explained how it was simply impossible for me to ever pay them the way things were going along. They could readily see this themselves from the figures. Then I explained that the only way I saw to pay in full was to set aside twenty percent of my income each month to be divided proportionally. This would pay them in full in a little over two years. I added that, in the meantime, we would go on a cash basis and give them the further benefit of our cash purchases.

They were really quite decent. Our greengrocer, a wise old man, put it in a way that helped to bring around the rest. <rest>If you pay for all you buy and then pay some on what you owe, that is better than you have done, for you haven't paid down the account at all in three years.</rest>

Finally, I secured all their names to an agreement binding them not to bother us as long as the twenty percent of our income was paid regularly. Then we began scheming on how to live upon seventy percent. We were determined to keep that extra ten percent to jingle. The thought of silver and possibly gold was most alluring.

It was like having an adventure to make the change. We enjoyed figuring this way and that, to live comfortably upon that remaining seventy percent. We started with rent and managed to secure a fair reduction. Next, we put our favorite brands of tea and such under suspicion and were agreeably surprised how often we could purchase superior qualities at less cost.

It is too long a story for a letter, but anyhow it did not prove difficult. We managed, and right cheerfully at that. What a relief it proved to have our affairs in such a shape that we were no longer persecuted by past due accounts.

I must not neglect, however, to tell you about that extra ten percent we were supposed to jingle. Well, we did jingle it for some time. Now don't laugh too soon. You see, that is the sporty part. It is the real fun, to start accumulating money that you do not want to spend. There is more pleasure in running up such a surplus than there could be in spending it.

After we had jingled to our hearts' content, we found a more profitable use for it. We took up an investment upon which we could pay that ten percent each month. This is proving to be the most satisfying part of our regeneration. It is the first thing we pay out of my check.

There is a most gratifying sense of security to know our investment is growing steadily. By the time my teaching days are over, it should be a snug sum, large enough so the income will take care of us from then on.

All this out of my same old check. It is difficult to believe, yet absolutely true. All our debts are being gradually paid, and at the same time our investment is increasing. Besides, we get along financially even better than before. Who would believe there could be such a difference in results between following a financial plan and just drifting along?

At the end of the next year, when all our old bills will have been paid, we will have more to pay upon our investment, besides some extra for travel.

We are determined never again to permit our living expenses to exceed seventy percent of our income. Now you can understand why we would like to extend our personal thanks to that old man whose plan saved us from our "Hell on Earth."

He knew. He had been through it all. He wanted others to benefit from his own bitter experiences. That is why he spent tedious hours carving his message upon the clay. He had a real message for fellow sufferers. It is a message so important that after five thousand years it has risen out of the ruins of Babylon, just as true and just as vital as the day it was buried.

Yours sincerely,

Alfred H. Shrewsbury,
Department of Archaeology.

The Luckiest Man in Babylon

At the head of his caravan proudly rode Sharru Nada, the merchant prince of Babylon. He liked fine cloth and wore rich and becoming robes. He liked fine animals and sat easily upon his spirited Arabian stallion. To look at him, one would hardly have guessed his advanced years. Certainly, they would not have suspected that he was inwardly troubled.

The journey from Damascus is long and the hardships of the desert are many. He did not mind these. The Arab tribes are fierce and eager to loot rich caravans. He did not fear these, for his many fleet-mounted guards were a safe protection.

He was disturbed about the youth at his side, whom he was bringing from Damascus. This was Hadan Gula, the grandson of his partner from other years, Arad Gula. He felt he owed Arad Gula a debt of gratitude that could never be repaid. He would like to do something for this grandson. But the more he considered this, the more difficult it seemed because of the youth himself.

Eyeing the young man's rings and earrings, he thought to himself, <rest>He thinks jewels are for men, yet he has his grandfather's strong face. But his grandfather wore no such gaudy robes. Still, I asked him to come, hoping I might help him get a start for himself and get away from the wreck his father has made of their inheritance.</rest>

Hadan Gula broke in upon his thoughts. <rest>Why do you work so hard, always riding with your caravan upon its long journeys? Do you never take time to enjoy life?</rest>

Sharru Nada smiled. <rest>To enjoy life?</rest> he repeated. <rest>What would you do to enjoy life if you were Sharru Nada?</rest>

<rest>If I had wealth equal to yours, I would live like a prince. I would never ride across the hot desert. I would spend the shekels as fast as they came to my purse. I would wear the richest of robes and the rarest of jewels. That would be a life to my liking, a life worth living.</rest> Both men laughed.

<rest>Your grandfather wore no jewels.</rest> Sharru Nada spoke before he thought, then continued jokingly, <rest>Would you leave no time for work?</rest>

<rest>Work was made for slaves,</rest> Hadan Gula responded.

Sharru Nada bit his lip but made no reply. They rode in silence until the trail led them to the slope. Here he reined his mount and pointed to the green valley far away. <rest>See, there is the valley. Look far down and you can faintly see the walls of Babylon. The tower is the Temple of Bel. If your eyes are sharp, you might even see the smoke from the eternal fire upon its crest.</rest>

<rest>So that is Babylon? I have always longed to see the wealthiest city in all the world,</rest> Hadan Gula commented. <rest>Babylon, where my grandfather started his fortune. I wish he were still alive. We would not be so sorely pressed.</rest>


<rest>Why wish for his spirit to linger on earth beyond its allotted time? You and your father can carry on his good work.</rest>

<rest>Sadly, neither of us has his gift. My father and I do not know his secret for attracting golden shekels.</rest>

Sharru Nada did not reply. He gave rein to his mount and rode thoughtfully down the trail to the valley. The caravan followed behind them in a cloud of reddish dust. Some time later, they reached the Kings' highway and turned south through the irrigated farms.

Three old men plowing a field caught Sharru Nada's attention. They seemed strangely familiar. How ridiculous! You do not pass a field after forty years and find the same men plowing there. Yet, something within him said they were the exact same men. One held the plow with an uncertain grip. The others plodded laboriously beside the oxen. They were ineffectually beating the oxen with their barrel staves to keep them pulling.

Forty years ago, he had envied these men! He would have gladly exchanged places with them! But what a difference he saw now. He looked back with pride at his trailing caravan. He saw well-chosen camels and donkeys, loaded high with valuable goods from Damascus. All of this was just one of his many possessions.

He pointed to the plowers. <rest>They are still plowing the same field where they were forty years ago,</rest> he said.

<rest>They certainly look like it, but why do you think they are the same men?</rest>

<rest>I saw them there,</rest> Sharru Nada replied. Memories were racing rapidly through his mind. Why couldn't he just bury the past and live in the present? Then he saw the smiling face of Arad Gula, as clear as a picture. The barrier between himself and the cynical youth beside him completely dissolved.

But how could he help such an arrogant youth? The young man had spendthrift ideas and bejeweled hands. Sharru Nada could offer plenty of work to willing workers. But he had nothing for men who considered themselves too good for work. Yet, he owed it to Arad Gula to do something. It could not be a half-hearted attempt. He and Arad Gula had never done things that way. They were not that sort of men.

A plan came to him almost in a flash. There were objections. He had to consider his own family and his own social standing. It would be cruel, and it would hurt. But being a man of quick decisions, he waved away his objections and decided to act.

<rest>Would you be interested in hearing how your worthy grandfather and I joined in the partnership that proved so profitable?</rest> he asked.

<rest>Why not just tell me how you made your golden shekels? That is all I really need to know,</rest> the young man parried.

Sharru Nada ignored the reply and continued. <rest>We start with those men plowing. I was no older than you are now. I was marching in a column of men. As we approached, good old Megiddo, a farmer, scoffed at the sloppy way they plowed. Megiddo was chained right next to me. 'Look at those lazy fellows,' he protested. 'The plow holder makes no effort to plow deep. The beaters do not even keep the oxen in the furrow. How can they expect to raise a good crop with such poor plowing?'</rest>

<rest>Did you say Megiddo was chained to you?</rest> Hadan Gula asked in surprise.

<rest>Yes, we had bronze collars around our necks. There was a length of heavy chain between us. Next to him was Zabado, the sheep thief. I had known him back in Harroun. At the end of the line was a man we called Pirate, because he would not tell us his real name. We judged him to be a sailor. He had entwined serpents tattooed on his chest in a sailor's fashion. The column was made up like this so the men could walk in rows of four.</rest>

<rest>You were chained as a slave?</rest> Hadan Gula asked incredulously.

<rest>Did your grandfather not tell you I was once a slave?</rest>

<rest>He often spoke of you, but he never hinted at this.</rest>

<rest>He was a man you could trust with your innermost secrets. You, too, are a man I can trust, am I right?</rest> Sharru Nada looked him squarely in the eye.

<rest>You may rely on my silence, but I am simply amazed. Tell me, how did you come to be a slave?</rest>

Sharru Nada shrugged his shoulders. <rest>Any man may find himself a slave. It was a gaming house and barley beer that brought me disaster. I was the victim of my brother's foolishness. In a brawl, he killed his friend. I was bonded to the widow by my father. He was desperate to keep my brother from being prosecuted under the law. When my father could not raise the silver to free me, the widow sold me to the slave dealer in anger.</rest>

<rest>What a shame and an injustice!</rest> Hadan Gula protested. <rest>But tell me, how did you regain your freedom?</rest>

<rest>We will come to that, but not yet. Let us continue my tale. As we passed, the plowers jeered at us. One took off his ragged hat and bowed low. He called out, 'Welcome to Babylon, guests of the King! He waits for you on the city walls where the banquet is spread. Enjoy your mud bricks and onion soup!' With that, they all laughed uproariously.</rest>

<rest>Pirate flew into a rage and cursed at them roundly. 'What do those men mean about the King awaiting us on the walls?' I asked him.</rest>

<rest>'You march to the city walls to carry bricks until your back breaks,' he said. 'Maybe they will beat you to death before it even breaks. But they won't beat me. I will kill them.'</rest>

<rest>Then Megiddo spoke up. 'It doesn't make sense to me to talk of masters beating willing, hard-working slaves to death,' he said. 'Masters like good slaves. They treat them well.'</rest>

<rest>'Who wants to work hard?' Zabado commented. 'Those plowers are wise fellows. They are not breaking their backs. They are just pretending to work.'</rest>

<rest>'You cannot get ahead by shirking your duties,' Megiddo protested. 'If you plow a hectare, that is a good day's work. Any master knows it. But when you only plow a half, that is shirking. I do not shirk. I like to work, and I like to do good work. Work is the best friend I have ever known. It has brought me all the good things I have ever had. It gave me my farm, my cows, my crops, everything.'</rest>

<rest>'Yeah, and where are all those things now?' scoffed Zabado. 'I figure it pays better to be smart and get by without working. You just watch me. If we are sold to the walls, I will be carrying the water bag or doing some easy job. Meanwhile, you, who loves to work, will be breaking your back carrying bricks.' He laughed his silly laugh.</rest>

<rest>Terror gripped me that night. I could not sleep. I crowded close to the guard rope. When the others slept, I caught the attention of Godoso. He was doing the first guard watch. He was one of those brigand Arabs. He was the sort of rogue who, if he robbed you of your purse, would think he also had to cut your throat.</rest>

<rest>'Tell me, Godoso,' I whispered. 'When we get to Babylon, will we be sold to the walls?'</rest>

<rest>'Why do you want to know?' he questioned cautiously.</rest>

<rest>'Can you not understand?' I pleaded. 'I am young, and I want to live. I do not want to be worked or beaten to death on the walls. Is there any chance for me to get a good master?'</rest>

<rest>He whispered back. 'I will tell you something. You are a good fellow. You gave me no trouble. Most times we go first to the slave market. Listen now. When buyers come, tell them you are a good worker. Tell them you like to work hard for a good master. Make them want to buy you. If you do not make them buy you, the next day you will carry bricks. It is mighty hard work.'</rest>

<rest>After he walked away, I lay in the warm sand. I looked up at the stars and thought about work. Megiddo had said that work was his best friend. This made me wonder if work could be my best friend, too. It certainly would be if it helped me get out of this situation.</rest>

<rest>When Megiddo awoke, I whispered my good news to him. It was our one ray of hope as we marched toward Babylon. Late in the afternoon, we approached the walls. We could see the lines of men, looking just like black ants. They were climbing up and down the steep diagonal paths. As we drew closer, we were amazed at the thousands of men working. Some were digging in the moat. Others mixed the dirt into mud bricks. The greatest number of men were carrying the bricks in large baskets. They carried them up those steep trails to the masons.*</rest>

<rest>Overseers cursed the slow workers. They cracked bullwhips over the backs of those who failed to keep in line. Poor, worn-out fellows staggered and fell beneath their heavy baskets. They were unable to rise again. If the lash failed to bring them to their feet, they were pushed to the side of the paths. They were left writhing in agony. Soon they would be dragged down to join other broken bodies beside the roadway, awaiting unmarked graves. As I beheld the ghastly sight, I shuddered. So this was what awaited my father's son if he failed at the slave market.</rest>

*The famous works of ancient Babylon—its walls, temples, hanging gardens, and great canals—were built by slave labor. These slaves were mainly prisoners of war, which explains the inhuman treatment they received. This force of workmen also included many citizens of Babylon and its provinces. They had been sold into slavery because of crimes or financial troubles. It was a common custom for men to put themselves, their wives, or their children up as a bond. This guaranteed the payment of loans, legal judgments, or other obligations. In case of default, those who were bonded were sold into slavery.

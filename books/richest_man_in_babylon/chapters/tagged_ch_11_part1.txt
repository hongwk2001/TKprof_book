The Camel Trader of Babylon

The hungrier you become, the clearer your mind works—and the more sensitive you become to the smell of food.

Tarkad, the son of Azure, certainly thought so. For two whole days, he hadn't eaten anything except two small figs he stole from a garden wall. He couldn't grab another before an angry woman rushed out and chased him down the street. Her shrill cries were still ringing in his ears as he walked through the marketplace. The memory of her voice helped him stop his restless fingers from snatching tempting fruit from the market women's baskets.

Never before had he realized how much food was brought to the markets of Babylon, or how good it smelled. Leaving the market, he walked across to the inn and paced back and forth in front of the eating house. Perhaps here he might meet someone he knew. He hoped to find someone who could lend him a copper coin. That single coin would win him a smile from the unfriendly innkeeper and a generous helping of food. Without the copper, he knew all too well how unwelcome he would be.

Lost in thought, he unexpectedly found himself face to face with the one man he wished most to avoid. It was the tall, bony figure of Dabasir, the camel trader. Of all the friends and acquaintances from whom he had borrowed small amounts of money, Dabasir made him feel the most uncomfortable. This was because Tarkad had failed to keep his promises to repay the money promptly.

Dabasir's face lit up when he saw him. <rest>Ha! It is Tarkad! You are exactly the person I've been looking for. I want you to repay the two copper coins I lent you a month ago. I also want the silver coin I lent you before that. We are lucky to meet today. I can really use those coins right now. What do you say, boy? What do you say?</rest>

Tarkad stuttered and his face flushed red. He had nothing in his empty stomach to give him the courage to argue with the outspoken Dabasir. <rest>I am sorry, very sorry,</rest> he mumbled weakly, <rest>but today I don't have the copper or the silver to repay you.</rest> <rest>Then get it!</rest> Dabasir insisted. <rest>Surely you can get your hands on a few coppers and a piece of silver. You need to repay the generosity of your father's old friend who helped you when you were in need!</rest>

<rest>It is because bad luck follows me that I cannot pay.</rest>

<rest>Bad luck! Would you blame the gods for your own weakness? Bad luck chases every man who thinks more about borrowing than repaying. Come with me, boy, while I eat. I am hungry, and I want to tell you a story.</rest>

Tarkad flinched from Dabasir's brutal honesty. However, this was at least an invitation to enter the coveted doorway of the eating house.

Dabasir pushed him to a far corner of the room, where they sat down on small rugs.

When Kauskor, the proprietor, appeared with a smile, Dabasir addressed him with his usual casual manner. <rest>Fat lizard of the desert, bring me a leg of goat, browned with plenty of juice! Bring bread and all the vegetables, because I am hungry and want a lot of food. Don't forget my friend here. Bring him a jug of water. Make sure it is cold, because it is a hot day.</rest>

Tarkad's heart sank. Did he have to sit here and drink water while watching this man eat an entire goat leg? He said nothing. He couldn't think of anything to say.

Dabasir, however, didn't know how to stay quiet. Smiling and waving good-naturedly to the other customers, who all knew him, he continued talking.

<rest>I heard a story from a traveler who just returned from Urfa. He spoke of a rich man who owns a piece of stone cut so thin that you can look right through it. He put it in the window of his house to keep out the rain. The traveler said the stone is yellow. When he was allowed to look through it, the entire outside world looked strange and different from reality. What do you say to that, Tarkad? Do you think the whole world could look like a completely different color to a man?</rest>

<rest>I suppose it could,</rest> the young man replied. But Tarkad was much more interested in the fat goat leg that had just been placed in front of Dabasir.

<rest>Well, I know it is true because I myself have seen the world in a completely different color from what it really is. The story I am about to tell you is about how I managed to see the world in its true colors once again.</rest>

<rest>Dabasir is going to tell a story,</rest> a nearby diner whispered to his neighbor, dragging his rug closer. Other diners brought their food and crowded around in a semi-circle. They chewed loudly in Tarkad's ears and brushed against him with their meaty bones. Tarkad was the only one without food. Dabasir didn't offer to share with him. He didn't even point to a small piece of hard bread that had broken off the platter and fallen to the floor.

<rest>The story I am about to tell,</rest> began Dabasir, pausing to bite a huge chunk of meat from the goat leg, <rest>is about my early life and how I became a camel trader. Did anyone here know that I was once a slave in Syria?</rest>

A murmur of surprise rippled through the audience. Dabasir listened to it with satisfaction.

<rest>When I was a young man,</rest> Dabasir continued, after another fierce bite of the goat leg, <rest>I learned my father's trade of saddle-making. I worked with him in his shop and got married.</rest>

<rest>Because I was young and not very skilled, I earned very little. It was barely enough to support my wonderful wife in a modest way. I wanted nice things that I couldn't afford. Soon, I discovered that shopkeepers would let me buy things on credit, trusting me to pay them back later even if I didn't have the money at the time.</rest> <rest>Being young and inexperienced, I didn't realize the danger. A person who spends more than he earns is planting the seeds of unnecessary self-indulgence, and he is guaranteed to harvest a storm of trouble and humiliation. So, I gave in to my desires for fine clothing. I bought luxury items for my good wife and our home that we simply couldn't afford.</rest> <rest>I paid what I could, and for a while, everything was fine. But eventually, I realized I couldn't stretch my earnings to cover both my living expenses and my debts. Creditors began chasing me down, demanding payment for my extravagant purchases. My life became miserable. I borrowed from my friends, but I couldn't repay them either. Things went from bad to worse. My wife went back to live with her father. I decided to leave Babylon and look for a new city where a young man might have better opportunities.</rest>

<rest>For two years, I lived a restless and unsuccessful life, working for caravan traders. Then, I fell in with a group of likable robbers who roamed the desert, attacking unarmed caravans. <rest>We were successful on our first trip. We captured a rich haul of gold, silks, and valuable merchandise. We took this loot to Ginir and quickly wasted it all.</rest>

<rest>The second time, we weren't so lucky. Right after we had captured a caravan, we were attacked by the spearmen of a local tribal chief. The caravans paid him for protection. <rest>I was bought for two silver coins by a Syrian desert chief. With my hair shaved off and wearing nothing but a loincloth, I didn't look any different from the other slaves. Because I was a reckless young man, I thought it was just an adventure. That changed when my master brought me before his four wives and told them they could have me as a eunuch.</rest>

<rest>Then, I truly realized the complete hopelessness of my situation. These desert men were fierce warriors. I was completely at their mercy, with no weapons and no way to escape.</rest>

<rest>I stood there in fear as those four women looked me over. I wondered if I could expect any pity from them. Sira, the first wife, was older than the rest. <rest>It felt like an eternity as I stood there waiting for my sentence. Each woman seemed to want the others to decide. Finally, Sira spoke up in a cold voice.</rest>

<rest>'We have plenty of eunuchs,' she said, 'but we have very few camel tenders, and the ones we do have are worthless. Even today, I need to visit my mother who is sick with a fever, and there is no slave I trust to lead my camel. Ask this slave if he knows how to lead a camel.'</rest>

<rest>My master immediately asked me, 'What do you know about camels?'</rest>

<rest>Trying hard to hide my desperate eagerness, I answered, 'I can make them kneel. I can load them with cargo. I can lead them on long trips without letting them get tired. If necessary, I can even fix their saddles and gear.'</rest>

<rest>'The slave speaks confidently enough,' my master observed. 'If you want, Sira, you can take this man to be your camel tender.'</rest>

<rest>So, I was handed over to Sira. That very day, I led her camel on a long journey to visit her sick mother. I took the opportunity to thank her for saving me. I also told her that I wasn't born a slave, but that I was the son of a free man, a respected saddle maker in Babylon. I explained a lot of my story to her. Her response really caught me off guard, and I thought about her words for a long time afterward.</rest>

<rest>'How can you call yourself a free man when your own weakness brought you to this point?' she asked. 'If a man has the soul of a slave inside him, won't he eventually become a slave no matter how he was born, just as water always seeks its own level? And if a man has the soul of a free man inside him, won't he eventually become respected and honored in his own city, no matter what bad luck he faces?'</rest>

<rest>For over a year, I was a slave and lived among the slaves, but I couldn't bring myself to act like one of them. One day, Sira asked me, 'In the evening, when the other slaves socialize and enjoy each other's company, why do you always sit alone in your tent?'</rest>

<rest>I answered her, 'I am still thinking about what you told me. I am wondering if I truly have the soul of a slave. Because I feel different from them, I can't join them, so I have to sit by myself.'</rest>

<rest>'I have to sit by myself, too,' she confessed. 'My dowry was very large, and my husband only married me because of the money. He doesn't actually want me. <rest>'What do you think of me by now?' I suddenly asked her. 'Do I have the soul of a man, or do I have the soul of a slave?'</rest>


<rest> 'Do you have a desire to repay the fair debts you owe in Babylon?' she parried.</rest>

<rest> 'Yes, I have the desire, but I see no way.'</rest>

<rest> 'If you contentedly let the years slip by and make no effort to repay, then you have nothing but the shameful soul of a slave. No man is anything else if he cannot respect himself, and no man can respect himself if he does not repay his honest debts.'</rest>

<rest> 'But what can I do when I am just a slave in Syria?'</rest>

<rest> 'I am not a weakling,' I denied hotly.</rest>

<rest> 'How?'</rest>

<rest> 'Doesn't your great king fight his enemies in every way he can and with every force he has? Your debts are your enemies. They ran you out of Babylon. <rest>I thought a lot about her unkind accusations. I prepared many defensive phrases to prove that I was not a slave at heart. However, I never got the chance to use them. Three days later, Sira's maid took me to see her mistress.</rest>

<rest>I needed no further urging. I thanked her warmly and hurried away into the night. I did not know this strange country, and I only had a dim idea of the direction toward Babylon. <rest>Late that afternoon, I reached a rough country that was just as uninhabitable as the desert. <rest>From then on, it was a journey that very few men ever live to talk about. We plodded along day after day. Our food and water ran out. The heat of the sun was merciless. <rest>I stretched out upon the ground and slept. I did not wake up until the very first gleam of daylight.</rest>

<rest>I sat up and looked around. There was a refreshing coolness in the morning air. My camels lay dejected not far away. <rest>Could it be that I was facing my end in this peaceful quiet? My mind was clearer than it had ever been before. My body now seemed to be of very little importance.

So ended the tale of Dabasir, the camel trader of old Babylon. He found his own soul when he realized a great truth. It was a truth that had been known and used by wise men long before his time.

This truth has led men of all ages out of difficulties and into success. It will continue to do so for anyone who has the wisdom to understand its magic power. It is here for any man to use who reads these lines.

October 21, 1934,

My dear Professor: The five clay tablets from your recent excavation in the ruins of Babylon arrived on the same boat as your letter. I have been completely fascinated by them. I have spent many pleasant hours translating their inscriptions. I should have answered your letter immediately, but I delayed until I could finish the attached translations.

The tablets arrived without any damage, thanks to your careful use of preservatives and excellent packing.

You will be just as astonished by the story they tell as we were in the laboratory. We usually expect the dim and distant past to speak of romance and adventure. You know, "Arabian Nights" types of stories. But instead, these tablets reveal the struggles of a person named Dabasir trying to pay off his debts. It makes you realize that conditions in this old world have not changed nearly as much in five thousand years as you might expect.

It is strange, but these ancient inscriptions really shook me up, as my students might say. As a college professor, I am supposed to be a thinking human being with a working knowledge of most subjects. Yet, here comes this old fellow straight out of the dust-covered ruins of Babylon. He offers a way to pay off my debts and acquire jingling gold in my wallet that I had never even heard of.

It is a pleasant thought, and it will be very interesting to see if his method works just as well today as it did in old Babylon. Mrs. Shrewsbury and I are planning to try his plan on our own finances, which could certainly use the improvement.

I wish you the best of luck in your worthy expedition. I am eagerly waiting for another opportunity to assist you, and I remain

Yours sincerely,

Alfred H. Shrewsbury,
Department of Archaeology.

Tablet No. I

Now, as the moon becomes full, I, Dabasir, have just returned from slavery in Syria. I am determined to pay off my many fair debts. I want to become a wealthy man worthy of respect in my native city of Babylon. So, I am carving a permanent record of my affairs into this clay. This record will guide and help me achieve my high ambitions.

Under the wise advice of my good friend Mathon, the gold lender, I am determined to follow an exact plan. He says this plan will lead any honorable man out of debt and into wealth and self-respect.

This plan includes three purposes, which represent my hopes and desires.

First, the plan provides for my future prosperity.

**Therefore, one-tenth of everything I earn will be set aside as my own to keep.** Mathon spoke wisely when he said:

"A man who keeps gold and silver in his purse that he does not need to spend is good to his family and loyal to his king.

"A man who has only a few coppers in his purse is indifferent to his family and indifferent to his king.

"But a man who has nothing in his purse is unkind to his family and disloyal to his king, because his own heart is bitter.

"Therefore, the man who wishes to achieve success must have coin that he can keep to jingle in his purse. This way, he will have love for his family and loyalty to his king in his heart."

Second, the plan ensures that I will support and clothe my good wife. She has loyally returned to me from her father's house. Mathon says that taking good care of a faithful wife puts self-respect into a man's heart. It also adds strength and determination to his goals.

**Therefore, seven-tenths of all I earn will be used to provide a home, clothes to wear, and food to eat.** It will also include a little extra to spend, so our lives will not lack pleasure and enjoyment. However, he strongly warned me to be extremely careful. We must not spend more than seven-tenths of what I earn for these worthy purposes. The success of the plan lies right here.

I must live entirely on this portion. I must never use more. I must never buy what I cannot pay for out of this share.

Tablet No. II

Third, the plan ensures that my debts will be paid out of my earnings.

**Therefore, every time the moon is full, two-tenths of all I have earned will be divided honorably and fairly.** I will distribute this among those who trusted me and to whom I owe money. This way, all my debts will surely be paid in due time. So, I am engraving here the name of every man I am indebted to, along with the honest amount of my debt.

Fahru, the cloth weaver, 2 silver, 6 copper.

Sinjar, the couch maker, 1 silver.

Ahmar, my friend, 3 silver, 1 copper.

Zankar, my friend, 4 silver, 7 copper.


Askamir, my friend, 1 silver, 3 copper.

Harinsir, the Jewelmaker, 6 silver, 2 copper.

Diarbeker, my father's friend, 4 silver, 1 copper.

Alkahad, the house owner, 14 silver.

Mathon, the gold lender, 9 silver.

Birejik, the farmer, 1 silver, 7 copper.

(From here on, disintegrated. Cannot be deciphered.)

Tablet No. III

I owe these creditors a total of 119 pieces of silver and 141 pieces of copper. I owed these sums and saw no way to repay them. In my foolishness, I allowed my wife to return to her father. I left my native city to seek easy wealth elsewhere. Instead, I only found disaster and was sold into the degradation of slavery.

Now Mathon has shown me how to repay my debts in small portions of my earnings. I finally realize the great extent of my foolishness in running away from the results of my extravagant spending. Therefore, I visited my creditors. I explained to them that I have no resources to pay them, except for my ability to earn. I intend to apply two-tenths of all I earn toward my debts, dividing it evenly and honestly. This is how much I can pay, and no more. If they are patient, my obligations will eventually be paid in full.

Ahmar, whom I considered my best friend, insulted me bitterly. I left him in humiliation. Birejik, the farmer, pleaded for me to pay him first because he badly needed the help. Alkahad, the house owner, was very disagreeable. He insisted that he would cause trouble for me unless I settled my debt in full soon.

All the rest willingly accepted my proposal. Because of this, I am more determined than ever to carry it through. I am convinced that it is easier to pay one's just debts than to avoid them. Even though I cannot meet the needs and demands of a few of my creditors, I will deal fairly and impartially with everyone.

Tablet No. IV

The moon is full again. I have worked hard with a clear mind. My good wife has supported my intention to pay my creditors. Because of our wise determination, I earned nineteen pieces of silver over the past month. I made this money by buying camels with strong lungs and good legs for Nebatur.

I divided this money according to the plan. I set aside one-tenth to keep for myself. I gave seven-tenths to my good wife to pay for our living expenses. The remaining two-tenths, I divided among my creditors as evenly as possible using copper coins.

I did not see Ahmar, but I left his payment with his wife. Birejik was so pleased that he tried to kiss my hand. Old Alkahad was the only one who grumbled. He said I must pay faster. I replied that if I was allowed to be well-fed and free of worry, that alone would help me pay him faster. All the others thanked me and praised my efforts.

Therefore, at the end of one month, my debt is reduced by almost four pieces of silver. I also possess almost two pieces of silver of my own, which no man can claim. My heart is lighter than it has been in a very long time.

The moon is full again. I worked hard, but with poor success. I was able to buy only a few camels. I earned just eleven pieces of silver. Nevertheless, my good wife and I stood by our plan. We bought no new clothing, and we ate little besides herbs.

Once again, I paid ourselves one-tenth of the eleven pieces of silver, and we lived on seven-tenths. I was surprised when Ahmar praised my payment, even though it was small. Birejik did the same. Alkahad flew into a rage. However, when I told him to give back his portion if he did not want it, he accepted it. The others were content, just as before.

The moon is full again, and I am filled with joy. I intercepted a fine herd of camels and bought many healthy ones. Because of this, my earnings were forty-two pieces of silver. This month, my wife and I bought much-needed sandals and clothing. We also dined well on meat and fowl.

We paid more than eight pieces of silver to our creditors. Even Alkahad did not protest.

The plan is truly great. It leads us out of debt and gives us wealth that is ours to keep.

The moon has been full three times since I last carved upon this clay. Each month, I paid myself one-tenth of all I earned. My good wife and I lived on seven-tenths, even though it was difficult at times. And each month, I paid two-tenths to my creditors.

I now have twenty-one pieces of silver of my own in my purse. This makes me stand tall and feel proud to walk among my friends. My wife keeps our home beautifully and is dressed elegantly. We are very happy living together.

The plan is incredibly valuable. Has it not turned an ex-slave into an honorable man?

Tablet No. V

The moon is full once again. I remember that it has been a long time since I last carved upon the clay. In truth, twelve months have come and gone. But I will not neglect my record today, because today I paid the last of my debts. My good wife and my thankful self will celebrate with a great feast today. We have finally achieved our goal.

Many things happened during my final visit to my creditors that I will remember for a long time. Ahmar begged for my forgiveness for his unkind words. He told me that out of everyone, I was the one he most wanted as a friend.

Old Alkahad is not so bad after all. He said to me, "You were once a piece of soft clay, easily pressed and molded by any hand that touched you. But now you are a piece of bronze, capable of holding a sharp edge. If you ever need silver or gold, come to me."

He is not the only one who holds me in high regard. Many others speak to me with deep respect. My good wife looks at me with a light in her eyes that gives a man true confidence in himself.

Yet, it is the plan that created my success. It enabled me to pay all my debts and to jingle both gold and silver in my purse. I highly recommend it to anyone who wishes to get ahead. Truly, if this plan can enable an ex-slave to pay his debts and have gold in his purse, won't it help any man find independence? I am not finished with the plan myself. I am convinced that if I continue to follow it, it will make me a very rich man.

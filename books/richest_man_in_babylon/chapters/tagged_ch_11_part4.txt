<rest>Godoso had been right. We were taken through the gates of the city to the slave prison. The next morning, we were marched to the pens in the market. Here, the rest of the men huddled in fear. Only the whips of our guards could keep them moving so the buyers could examine them. Megiddo and I eagerly talked to every man who permitted us to speak to him.</rest>

<rest>The slave dealer brought soldiers from the King's Guard. They shackled Pirate and brutally beat him when he protested. As they led him away, I actually felt sorry for him.</rest>

<rest>Megiddo felt that we would soon be separated. When no buyers were near, he spoke to me earnestly. He wanted to impress upon me how valuable work would be to my future. 'Some men hate work,' he told me. 'They make it their enemy. It is better to treat it like a friend. Make yourself like it. Do not mind if it is hard. If you think about the beautiful house you are building, who cares if the beams are heavy? Who cares if it is a long walk to the well to carry water for the plaster? Promise me, boy. If you get a master, work for him as hard as you can. If he does not appreciate all that you do, never mind. Remember that work well done does good for the man who does it. <rest>Megiddo asked about his farm and his crops. He soon convinced the farmer that he would be a valuable worker. After violent bargaining with the slave dealer, the farmer drew a fat purse from beneath his robe. Soon, Megiddo had followed his new master out of sight.</rest>

<rest>A few other men were sold during the morning. At noon, Godoso confided in me. He said the dealer was frustrated and would not stay another night. Instead, he would take everyone who remained at sundown to the King's buyer. I was becoming desperate. Just then, a fat, good-natured man walked up to the wall and asked if there was a baker among us.</rest>

<rest>I approached him and said, 'Why should a good baker like yourself seek out another baker with inferior skills? Would it not be easier to teach a willing man like myself your expert ways? Look at me. I am young, strong, and I like to work. Give me a chance, and I will do my absolute best to earn gold and silver for your purse.'</rest>

<rest>He was impressed by my willingness. He began bargaining with the dealer. The dealer had never noticed me since he bought me, but now he spoke enthusiastically about my abilities, my good health, and my positive attitude. I felt like a fat ox being sold to a butcher. At last, much to my joy, the deal was closed. I followed my new master away, thinking I was the luckiest man in Babylon.</rest>

<rest>My new home was much to my liking. My master, Nana-naid, taught me how to grind the barley in the stone bowl that stood in the courtyard. He showed me how to build the fire in the oven. He then taught me how to grind the sesame flour very fine for the honey cakes. I had a couch in the shed where his grain was stored. The old slave housekeeper, Swasti, fed me well. She was very pleased with the way I helped her with the heavy tasks.</rest>

<rest>Here was the chance I had longed for. I could finally make myself valuable to my master and, I hoped, find a way to earn my freedom.</rest>

<rest>I asked Nana-naid to show me how to knead the bread and bake it. He did this, and he was very pleased with my willingness to learn. Later, when I could do this well, I asked him to teach me how to make the honey cakes. Soon, I was doing all the baking. My master was glad to be idle. However, Swasti shook her head in disapproval. 'Having no work to do is bad for any man,' she declared.</rest>

<rest>I felt it was time for me to think of a way to earn coins to buy my freedom. Since the baking was finished by noon, I thought Nana-naid might approve if I found profitable work for the afternoons. He might even share my earnings with me. Then, a thought came to me. Why not bake extra honey cakes and sell them to hungry men on the streets of the city?</rest>

<rest>I presented my plan to Nana-naid. I asked him, 'If I can use my afternoons, after the baking is finished, to earn coins for you, would it not be fair for you to share those earnings with me? That way, I could have money of my own to spend on the things every man desires and needs.'</rest>

<rest> 'Fair enough, fair enough,' he admitted. When I told him of my plan to sell our honey cakes, he was very pleased. 'Here is what we will do,' he suggested. 'You will sell them at two for a penny. Half of the pennies will be mine to pay for the flour, the honey, and the wood used to bake them. Of the remaining pennies, I will take half, and you will keep half.'</rest>

<rest>I was very pleased by his generous offer. I was allowed to keep one-fourth of my total sales for myself! That night, I worked late to build a tray to display the cakes. Nana-naid gave me one of his worn robes so that I might look presentable. Swasti helped me patch it up and wash it clean.</rest>

<rest>The next day, I baked an extra supply of honey cakes. They looked brown and tempting on the tray as I walked down the street, loudly calling out my wares. At first, no one seemed interested, and I became discouraged. I kept going, though. Later in the afternoon, as men became hungry, the cakes began to sell. Soon, my tray was completely empty.</rest>

<rest>Nana-naid was very pleased with my success and gladly paid me my share. I was delighted to own pennies of my own. Megiddo had been right when he said a master appreciated good work from his slaves. That night, I was so excited over my success that I could hardly sleep. I tried to calculate how much I could earn in a single year, and how many years it would take to buy my freedom.</rest>

<rest>As I went out with my tray of cakes every day, I soon found regular customers. One of these men was none other than your grandfather, Arad Gula. He was a rug merchant who sold his goods to housewives. He traveled from one end of the city to the other. He was always accompanied by a donkey loaded high with rugs, along with a black slave to tend to the animal. He would buy two cakes for himself and two for his slave. He always lingered to talk with me while they ate.</rest>

<rest>Your grandfather said something to me one day that I will always remember. He told me, 'I like your cakes, boy. But better still, I like the fine sense of enterprise with which you offer them. That kind of spirit can carry you far on the road to success.'</rest>

<rest>But how can you understand, Hadan Gula, what such words of encouragement meant to a slave boy? I was lonesome in a great city. I was struggling with everything I had to find a way out of my humiliation.</rest>

<rest>As the months went by, I continued to add pennies to my purse. It began to have a comforting weight upon my belt. Work was proving to be my best friend, just as Megiddo had said. I was happy, but Swasti was worried.</rest>

<rest> 'I am afraid for your master,' she protested. 'He spends far too much time at the gaming houses.'</rest>

<rest>One day, I was overjoyed to meet my friend Megiddo on the street. He was leading three donkeys loaded with vegetables to the market. 'I am doing incredibly well,' he told me. 'My master truly appreciates my hard work. I am now a foreman! You see, he trusts the marketing to me, and he is even sending for my family. Work is helping me recover from my great troubles. Someday, it will help me buy my freedom so I can once more own a farm of my own.'</rest>

<rest>Time went on, and Nana-naid became more and more anxious for me to return from selling. He would be waiting the moment I returned. He would eagerly count and divide our money. He also constantly urged me to seek out new markets and increase my sales.</rest>

<rest>Often, I went outside the city gates to sell to the overseers of the slaves who were building the walls. I hated returning to those disagreeable sights. However, I found that the overseers were very generous buyers. One day, I was surprised to see Zabado waiting in line to fill his basket with bricks. He was gaunt and bent over. His back was covered in welts and sores from the overseers' whips. I felt sorry for him. I handed him a cake, and he crushed it into his mouth like a hungry animal. Seeing the greedy, desperate look in his eyes, I ran away before he could grab my tray.</rest>

<rest> 'Why do you work so hard?' Arad Gula asked me one day. It was almost the exact same question you asked me today, do you remember? I told him what Megiddo had said about work, and how it was proving to be my best friend. I proudly showed him my wallet full of pennies. I explained how I was saving them to buy my freedom.</rest>

<rest> 'When you are free, what will you do?' he inquired.</rest>

<rest> 'Then,' I answered, 'I intend to become a merchant.'</rest>

<rest>At that moment, he confided in me. He revealed something I had never suspected. 'You do not know this,' he said, 'but I, too, am a slave. I am in a partnership with my master.' </rest>

<rest>Stop!</rest> demanded Hadan Gula. <rest>I will not listen to lies that defame my grandfather! He was no slave.</rest> His eyes blazed with anger.

Sharru Nada remained calm. <rest>I honor him for rising above his misfortune to become a leading citizen of Damascus. Are you, his grandson, cast from the same mold? Are you man enough to face the true facts? Or do you prefer to live under false illusions?</rest>

Hadan Gula straightened up in his saddle. In a voice suppressed with deep emotion, he replied. <rest>My grandfather was beloved by everyone. His good deeds were countless. When the famine came, did his gold not buy grain in Egypt? Did his caravans not bring it to Damascus and distribute it to the people so that no one would starve? <rest>If he had remained a slave in Babylon, then he might well have been despised,</rest> Sharru Nada replied. <rest>But through his own efforts, he became a great man in Damascus. The Gods indeed forgave his misfortunes and honored him with their respect.</rest>

<rest>After telling me that he was a slave,</rest> Sharru Nada continued, <rest>he explained how anxious he had been to earn his freedom. Now that he finally had enough money to buy it, he was deeply disturbed about what he should do. He was no longer making good sales, and he was terrified to lose the support of his master.</rest>

<rest>I protested his indecision. I told him, 'Cling to your master no longer! Get the feeling of being a free man once again. Act like a free man, and succeed like one! Decide what you desire to accomplish. Then, let work aid you in achieving it!' He went on his way, saying he was glad I had shamed him for his cowardice.</rest>

<rest>One day, I went outside the gates again. I was surprised to find a massive crowd gathering there. When I asked a man for an explanation, he replied, 'Have you not heard? An escaped slave who murdered one of the King's guards has been brought to justice. He will be flogged to death today for his crime. Even the King himself is going to be here.'</rest>

<rest>The crowd around the flogging post was so dense that I was afraid to go near. I worried my tray of honey cakes would be knocked over. Therefore, I climbed up the unfinished wall so I could see over the heads of the people. I was fortunate enough to get a view of King Nebuchadnezzar himself as he rode by in his golden chariot. I had never beheld such grandeur. His robes and the hangings of his chariot were made of gorgeous gold cloth and velvet.</rest>

<rest>I could not see the actual flogging, though I could hear the shrieks of the poor slave. I wondered how someone as noble as our handsome King could endure watching such suffering. However, when I saw that he was laughing and joking with his nobles, I realized he was cruel. I suddenly understood why such inhuman tasks were demanded of the slaves building the city walls.</rest>

<rest>After the slave was dead, his body was hung upon a pole by a rope attached to his leg so that everyone could see it. As the crowd began to thin out, I went closer. On his hairy chest, I saw a tattoo of two entwined serpents. It was Pirate. The next time I met Arad Gula, he was a completely changed man. He greeted me full of enthusiasm. 'Behold!' he said. 'The slave you knew is now a free man! There was magic in your words. Already, my sales and my profits are increasing. My wife is overjoyed. She was a free woman, the niece of my former master. She strongly desires that we move to a new city where no man will know I was once a slave. That way, our children will be above reproach for their father's past misfortune. Work has truly become my best helper. It has enabled me to recapture my confidence and my skill to sell.'</rest>

<rest>I was absolutely overjoyed. I was so glad that I had been able to repay him, even in a small way, for the wonderful encouragement he had given me.</rest>


*Slave customs in ancient Babylon might seem confusing to us today, but they were strictly regulated by law. For example, a slave could own property of any kind. They could even own other slaves, and their master had no claim over them. Slaves married freely with non-slaves, and children born to free mothers were also free. Most of the city's merchants were actually slaves. Many of these enslaved merchants worked in partnership with their masters and were wealthy in their own right.*

<rest>One evening, Swasti came to me in deep distress. She said, 'Your master is in trouble, and I fear for him. Some months ago, he lost a lot of money at the gambling tables. He hasn't paid the farmer for his grain or his honey. He hasn't paid the money lender. They are all angry, and they are threatening him.'</rest> 

I replied thoughtlessly, <rest>Why should we worry about his foolishness? We aren't responsible for him.</rest>

She said, <rest>Foolish youth, you don't understand. He gave your title to the money lender to secure a loan. Under the law, the money lender can claim you and sell you. I don't know what to do. He is a good master. Why? Oh why, should such trouble come to him?</rest>

<rest>Swasti's fears were not groundless. While I was doing the baking the next morning, the money lender returned with a man he called Sasi. This man looked me over and decided I would do.</rest>

<rest>The money lender didn't wait for my master to return. He just told Swasti to tell him that he had taken me. With only the robe on my back and the purse of pennies hanging safely from my belt, I was hurried away from the unfinished baking.</rest>

<rest>I was swept away from my dearest hopes, just like a hurricane snatches a tree from the forest and casts it into the crashing sea. Once again, a gambling house and barley beer had brought me to disaster.</rest>

<rest>Sasi was a blunt and gruff man. As he led me across the city, I told him about the good work I had been doing for Nana-naid. I said I hoped to do good work for him, too. But his reply offered no encouragement.</rest>

<rest>He told me, 'I don't like this work, and my master doesn't like it either. The King told him to send me to build a section of the Grand Canal. My master told me to buy more slaves, work them hard, and finish quickly. Bah! How can any man finish a big job quickly?'</rest>

<rest>Picture a desert without a single tree. There were just low shrubs and a sun burning with such fury that the water in our barrels became too hot to drink. Then, picture rows of men going down into a deep excavation. They lugged heavy baskets of dirt up soft, dusty trails from daylight until dark. Picture food served in open troughs, where we had to help ourselves like pigs. We had no tents, and we had no straw for beds. That was the situation I found myself in. I buried my wallet in a marked spot, wondering if I would ever dig it up again.</rest>

<rest>At first, I worked with good will. But as the months dragged on, I felt my spirit breaking. Then, the heat fever took hold of my weary body. I lost my appetite and could barely stomach the mutton and vegetables. At night, I tossed and turned in unhappy wakefulness.</rest>

<rest>In my misery, I wondered if Zabado had the best plan after all. He tried to avoid work and keep his back from being broken. But then I recalled my last sight of him, and I knew his plan was not a good one.</rest>

<rest>I thought of Pirate with his bitterness. I wondered if it might be just as well to fight and kill. But the memory of his bleeding body reminded me that his plan was also useless.</rest>

<rest>Then I remembered my last sight of Megiddo. His hands were deeply calloused from hard work, but his heart was light, and there was happiness on his face. His was the best plan.</rest>

<rest>Yet, I was just as willing to work as Megiddo. He couldn't have worked harder than me. So why didn't my work bring me happiness and success? Was it really work that brought Megiddo happiness, or were happiness and success simply left up to the gods? Was I going to work the rest of my life without getting what I wanted, without ever finding happiness or success? All of these questions were jumbled in my mind, and I didn't have an answer. I was deeply confused.</rest>

<rest>Several days later, it seemed like I was at the end of my endurance. My questions were still unanswered when Sasi sent for me. A messenger had come from my master to take me back to Babylon. I dug up my precious wallet, wrapped myself in the tattered remains of my robe, and went on my way.</rest>

<rest>As we rode, the same thoughts of a hurricane whirling me back and forth kept racing through my feverish brain. I felt like I was living the weird words of an old chant from my hometown of Harroun:</rest>

Besetting a man like a whirlwind,
Driving him like a storm,
Whose course no one can follow,
Whose destiny no one can foretell.

<rest>Was I destined to always be punished for things I didn't understand? What new miseries and disappointments were waiting for me?</rest>

<rest>When we rode into the courtyard of my master's house, imagine my surprise when I saw Arad Gula waiting for me! He helped me down and hugged me like a long-lost brother.</rest>

<rest>As we walked together, I tried to follow behind him as a slave should follow his master, but he wouldn't allow it. He put his arm around me and said, 'I hunted everywhere for you. I interrupted, <rest>That was Megiddo's philosophy, not mine.</rest>

<rest>Tears of gratitude filled my eyes. I knew I was the luckiest man in Babylon.</rest>

<rest>You see, in the time of my greatest distress, work proved to be my best friend. My willingness to work saved me from being sold to the slave gangs on the city walls. It also impressed your grandfather so much that he chose me to be his partner.</rest>

Sharru Nada replied, <rest>It was the only key he had when I first met him. Your grandfather enjoyed working. The gods appreciated his efforts and rewarded him generously.</rest>

Hadan Gula said thoughtfully, <rest>I am beginning to see. Work attracted his many friends, who admired his hard work and the success it brought. Sharru Nada and Hadan Gula rode in the shadows of the towering walls, straight up to the massive bronze gates of Babylon. As they approached, the gate guards jumped to attention and respectfully saluted an honored citizen. With his head held high, Sharru Nada led the long caravan through the gates and up the streets of the city.

Hadan Gula confided in him, <rest>I have always hoped to become a man like my grandfather. But I never realized exactly what kind of man he was until you showed me. As he said this, Hadan Gula pulled the jeweled baubles from his ears and the rings from his fingers. Then, pulling the reins of his horse, he dropped back and rode with deep respect behind the leader of the caravan.

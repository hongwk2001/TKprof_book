Meet the Goddess of Good Luck

"If a man is lucky, there is no telling how far his good fortune will reach. Throw him into the Euphrates River, and chances are he will swim out with a pearl in his hand."
— Babylonian Proverb.

The desire to be lucky is universal. It was just as strong in the hearts of men four thousand years ago in ancient Babylon as it is today. We all hope to be favored by the unpredictable Goddess of Good Luck.

Is there some way we can meet her? Can we attract her favorable attention and receive her generous gifts? Is there a reliable way to attract good luck?

That is exactly what the men of ancient Babylon wanted to know, and it is exactly what they decided to find out. They were shrewd men and keen thinkers. That explains why their city became the richest and most powerful in the world at that time.

In that distant past, they did not have schools or colleges. However, they did have a center of learning, and it was a very practical one. Among the towering buildings in Babylon was one that ranked in importance with the King's Palace, the Hanging Gardens, and the temples of the Gods. You will rarely see it mentioned in history books. In fact, it is likely not mentioned at all. Yet, it had a powerful influence on the thinking of that era.

This building was the Temple of Learning. Here, voluntary teachers explained the wisdom of the past, and subjects of popular interest were discussed in open forums. Inside its walls, all men met as equals. Even the humblest slave could freely debate the opinions of a royal prince without fear of punishment.

Among the many who visited the Temple of Learning was a wise, wealthy man named Arkad. He was known as the richest man in Babylon. He had his own special hall. Almost every evening, a large group of men would gather there. Some were old, some were very young, but most were middle-aged. They came together to discuss and argue about interesting subjects. Let's listen in and see if they knew how to attract good luck.

The sun had just set. It looked like a great red ball of fire shining through the hazy desert dust as Arkad strolled up to his usual platform. A full eighty men were already waiting for him, reclining on small rugs spread across the floor, and more were still arriving.

"What shall we discuss tonight?" Arkad asked.

After a brief hesitation, a tall cloth weaver stood up to speak, as was the custom. "I have a subject I would like to hear discussed. However, I hesitate to bring it up, because I fear it might seem ridiculous to you, Arkad, and to my good friends here."

Arkad and the others urged him to speak, so the weaver continued. "Today, I have been very lucky, because I found a purse filled with pieces of gold. I want nothing more than to continue being lucky. I feel that all men share this desire. Therefore, I suggest we debate how to attract good luck, so we can discover ways to entice it to come to us."

"A most interesting subject has been offered," Arkad commented. "It is very worthy of our discussion. To some men, good luck is just a chance happening. They think it is like an accident that occurs without purpose or reason. Others believe that our most generous goddess, Ashtar, is the source of all good fortune, and that she is always eager to reward those who please her with generous gifts. Speak up, my friends! What do you say? Should we try to find out if there are ways to attract good luck to visit every single one of us?"

"Yes! Yes! And a lot of it!" responded the growing group of eager listeners.

Arkad then continued, "To start our discussion, let's first hear from anyone here who has had an experience similar to the cloth weaver's. Has anyone found or received valuable treasures or jewels without putting in any effort?"

There was a pause as everyone looked around expecting someone to reply, but no one did.

"What, no one?" Arkad said. "Then this kind of good luck must be rare indeed. Who will offer a suggestion on where we should look next?"

"I will," said a well-dressed young man as he stood up. "When a man speaks of luck, isn't it natural to think of the gambling tables? Isn't that where many men court the goddess's favor, hoping she will bless them with big winnings?"

As he sat back down, a voice called out, "Don't stop! Keep going with your story! Tell us, did you find favor with the goddess at the gambling tables? Did she turn the dice so the red side landed facing up, allowing you to fill your purse at the dealer's expense? Or did she let the blue sides come up, so the dealer raked in your hard-earned pieces of silver?"

The young man joined the good-natured laughter, then replied, "I don't mind admitting that she didn't even seem to know I was there. But what about the rest of you?

"Have you found her waiting around those places to roll the dice in your favor? We are just as eager to hear and learn."

"A wise start," interrupted Arkad. "We meet here to consider all sides of every question. If we ignored the gambling tables, we would overlook an instinct that is common to most men. That instinct is the love of taking a chance with a small amount of silver in the hope of winning a lot of gold."

"That reminds me of the races just yesterday," another listener called out. "If the goddess hangs around the gambling tables, she surely doesn't overlook the races. The gilded chariots and foaming horses offer far more excitement. Tell us honestly, Arkad. Did she whisper in your ear to bet on those gray horses from Nineveh yesterday? I was standing right behind you, and I could hardly believe my ears when I heard you place your bet on the grays. You know as well as any of us that no team in all of Assyria can beat our beloved bay horses in a fair race.

"Did the goddess whisper in your ear? Did she tell you to bet on the grays because, at the last turn, the inside black horse would stumble and interfere with our bays, allowing the grays to win an unearned victory?"

Arkad smiled indulgently at the teasing. "Why should we think the good goddess would take that much interest in any man's bet on a horse race? To me, she is a goddess of love and dignity whose pleasure is to help those in need and to reward those who deserve it. I don't look for her at the gambling tables or the races, where men lose more gold than they win. Instead, I look for her in other places where the work of men is more worthwhile and more deserving of a reward.

"When a man tills the soil, engages in honest trade, or does any kind of work, he has the opportunity to make a profit from his efforts. He might not be rewarded every single time. Sometimes his judgment might be faulty, and other times the wind and weather might defeat his efforts. But if he is persistent, he can usually expect to make a profit, because the chances of success are always in his favor.

"However, when a man gambles, the situation is entirely flipped. The odds of profiting are always against him and always in favor of the game keeper. The game is specifically designed to favor the house. It is a business, and the keeper plans to make a generous profit from the coins the players bet. Very few players realize how guaranteed the game keeper's profits are, and how uncertain their own chances of winning truly are.

"For example, let's look at bets placed on the dice. Each time it's thrown, we bet on which side will face up. If it's the red side, the game master pays us four times our bet. But if any of the other five sides face up, we lose our money. The math shows that for each throw, we have five chances to lose. But because the red side pays four to one, we only have four chances to win. Over a night of playing, the game master expects to keep one-fifth of all the wagered coins as his profit. How can a man expect to win more than just occasionally against odds that are rigged to make him lose one-fifth of all his bets?"


"Yet some men do win large sums at times," volunteered one of the listeners.

"Quite so, they do," Arkad continued. "Realizing this, I have to ask myself whether money secured in such ways brings permanent value to those who get lucky. I know many of the successful men of Babylon. Yet, among all of them, I cannot name a single one who got his start from the gaming tables.

"You who are gathered here tonight know many more of our prominent citizens. I would be very interested to learn how many of them can credit the gaming tables with their start to success. Suppose each of you tell us about anyone you know. What do you say?"

After a prolonged silence, a joker ventured, "Would your question include the game keepers?"

"If you can think of no one else," Arkad responded.

"If not one of you can think of anyone else, then how about yourselves? Are there any consistent winners among us who would hesitate to recommend gambling as a source of income?"

His challenge was answered by a series of groans from the back. The groans quickly spread into loud laughter.

"It seems we are not looking for good luck in the places where the goddess actually hangs out," he continued. "Therefore, let us explore other fields. We have not found luck in picking up lost wallets. Neither have we found it haunting the gaming tables. As for the horse races, I must confess that I have lost far more coins there than I have ever won.

"Now, suppose we consider our trades and businesses. When we finish a profitable transaction, isn't it natural to view it as a just reward for our efforts, rather than good luck? I am inclined to think we might be overlooking the gifts of the goddess. Perhaps she really does assist us even when we do not appreciate her generosity. Who can suggest something else to discuss?"

At that, an elderly merchant stood up, smoothing his elegant white robe. "With your permission, most honorable Arkad and my friends, I offer a suggestion. You said that we take credit for our business success, blaming our own hard work and ability. So why not consider the successes we almost enjoyed, but which escaped us? I am talking about missed opportunities that would have been highly profitable. They would have been rare examples of good luck if they had actually happened. Because they were not fulfilled, we cannot consider them our just rewards. Surely many men here have similar experiences to share."

"That is a wise approach," Arkad approved. "Who among you has had good luck within your grasp, only to watch it slip away?"

Many hands were raised, including the merchant's. Arkad motioned for him to speak. "Since you suggested this approach, we would like to hear from you first."

"I will gladly tell a story," the merchant resumed. "It shows just how close good luck can get to a man. It also shows how blindly he might let it escape, causing him great loss and deep regret later on.

"Many years ago, I was a young man. I was newly married and earning a good living. One day, my father came to me and strongly urged me to make an investment. The son of one of his good friends had noticed a barren tract of land. It was located not far beyond the outer walls of our city. The land sat high above the canal, completely out of reach of the water.

"This young man had devised a clever plan. He wanted to purchase the land and build three large water wheels. These wheels would be operated by oxen, lifting the life-giving water up to the fertile soil. Once the land was watered, he planned to divide it into smaller tracts. Then, he would sell those tracts to the city residents for herb gardens.

"However, he did not have enough gold to complete such a massive undertaking. Like me, he was just a young man earning a fair wage. His father, like mine, had a large family and very little money. Therefore, he decided to gather a group of men to join the enterprise with him. The group would consist of twelve men. Each man had to be earning a steady income. They all had to agree to invest **one-tenth of their earnings** into the project until the land was ready to be sold. Once the land was sold, everyone would share the profits fairly, in proportion to what they had invested.

"'My son,' my father said to me, 'you are a grown man now. It is my deepest desire that you begin building a valuable estate for yourself. I want you to become a respected man in the community. I want to see you profit by learning from the thoughtless mistakes I made when I was your age.'

"'I want that very much, father,' I replied.

"'Then here is my advice,' he told me. 'Do exactly what I should have done at your age. **Set aside one-tenth of your earnings and put it into favorable investments.** With that ten percent, and the additional money it will earn, you can build a valuable estate long before you reach my age.'

"'Your words are very wise, father,' I told him. 'I greatly desire riches. Yet, there are so many things I need to spend my earnings on right now. Because of that, I hesitate to follow your advice. I am still young. There is plenty of time.'

"'That is exactly what I thought when I was your age,' he warned. 'But look at me now. Many years have passed, and I still haven't even started.'

"'We live in a different age now, father,' I argued. 'I will be sure to avoid your mistakes.'

"'Opportunity stands right in front of you, my son,' he pleaded. 'It is offering you a chance that could lead to great wealth. I beg of you, do not delay. Go tomorrow morning and meet with my friend's son. Agree to pay ten percent of your earnings into this investment. Go promptly. **Opportunity waits for no man.** Today it is here; soon it will be gone. Therefore, do not delay!'

"In spite of my father's passionate advice, I hesitated. Tradesmen from the East had just brought in some beautiful new robes. The fabrics were so rich and beautiful that my wife and I felt we simply had to buy them. If I agreed to pay one-tenth of my earnings into the irrigation project, we would have to deprive ourselves of those robes and other pleasures we dearly wanted. I kept delaying my decision until it was finally too late. I have regretted it ever since. That enterprise ended up being far more profitable than anyone had predicted. So, that is my story. It shows exactly how I allowed good luck to slip through my fingers."

"In this tale, we see that good luck comes to the man who accepts opportunity," commented a dark-skinned man from the desert. "To build an estate, there must always be a beginning. That start might just be a few pieces of gold or silver that a man redirects from his earnings into his first investment. I own many herds of animals myself. I started building my herds when I was just a young boy. I used a single piece of silver to buy a young calf. Because it was the beginning of my wealth, that single purchase was incredibly important to me.

"Taking that first step to build an estate is the best luck that can come to any man. For all men, that first step is the most important one. It transforms them from men who only earn money through their own hard labor, into men who draw dividends from the earnings of their gold. Some men are fortunate enough to take that step when they are young. By doing so, they completely outstrip the financial success of those who start later. They also leave behind unfortunate men, like the father of this merchant, who never take that step at all.

"If our friend, the merchant, had taken that step in his youth when the opportunity first presented itself, he would be blessed with far more wealth today. If our other friend, the cloth weaver, lets his recent good luck inspire him to take that step now, it will certainly be the beginning of much greater fortune for him."

"Thank you! I would like to speak, too." A stranger from another country stood up. "I am a Syrian. I do not speak your language very well. I want to call this merchant friend of ours a specific name. You might think this name is not very polite. Even so, I want to call him that. But, unfortunately, I do not know your word for it. If I say it in Syrian, you won't understand me. Therefore, please, some good gentleman, tell me the right word for a man who constantly puts off doing the things that are very good for him."

"Procrastinator!" called a voice from the crowd.

"That's the word!" shouted the Syrian, waving his hands excitedly. "He does not accept opportunity when she comes! He waits. He says, 'I have too much business right now. I will talk to you later.' Opportunity will not wait for such a slow fellow! She believes that if a man truly desires to be lucky, he will move quickly. Any man who does not move quickly when opportunity knocks is a big procrastinator, just like our friend the merchant!"


The merchant stood up and bowed good-naturedly in response to the laughter. "I admire you, stranger within our gates, because you do not hesitate to speak the truth."

"Now, let us hear another tale of opportunity. Who has another experience to share with us?" Arkad asked.

"I do," responded a middle-aged man in a red robe. "I am a buyer of animals, mostly camels and horses. Sometimes I also buy sheep and goats. The tale I am about to tell will show truthfully how opportunity came one night when I least expected it. Perhaps that is exactly why I let it escape. You will have to be the judge of that."

"I returned to the city one evening after a frustrating ten-day journey searching for camels. I was very angry to find the gates of the city closed and locked. My slaves began setting up our tent for the night. We expected to spend it with very little food and no water. Then, I was approached by an elderly farmer. Just like us, he found himself locked outside the city.

" 'Honored sir,' he said to me, 'from your appearance, I judge you to be a buyer. If that is true, I would very much like to sell you my excellent flock of sheep. We just drove them up here. Unfortunately, my good wife lies very sick with a fever. I must return home as quickly as possible. Please buy my sheep. Then, my slaves and I can mount our camels and travel back without any delay.' "

"It was so dark that I could not see his flock. However, from the sound of their bleating, I knew it must be a large one. I had just wasted ten days searching for camels that I could not find. So, I was glad to bargain with him. In his anxiety to leave, he set a very reasonable price. I accepted his offer. I knew perfectly well that my slaves could drive the flock through the city gates in the morning and sell them for a substantial profit.

"Once the bargain was made, I called my slaves to bring torches. We needed to count the flock, which the farmer claimed contained nine hundred sheep. I will not burden you, my friends, with a description of our struggles. It was incredibly difficult to count so many thirsty, restless, milling sheep. In fact, it proved to be an impossible task. Therefore, I bluntly told the farmer that I would count them at daylight and pay him then.

" 'Please, most honorable sir,' he pleaded. 'Just pay me two-thirds of the price tonight so that I can be on my way. I will leave my most intelligent and educated slave behind to help you make the count in the morning. He is trustworthy, and you can pay the balance to him.'

"But I was stubborn and refused to make any payment that night. The next morning, before I even woke up, the city gates opened. Four buyers rushed out in search of flocks. They were extremely eager and willing to pay high prices. The city was threatened with a siege, and food was running short. The old farmer received nearly three times the price he had offered the flock to me. And that is how a rare piece of good luck was allowed to escape."

"Here is a most unusual tale," Arkad commented. "What wisdom does it suggest?"

"It shows the wisdom of making a payment immediately when we are convinced our bargain is a wise one," suggested an older, respected saddle maker. "If the bargain is good, you need protection against your own weaknesses just as much as against any other man. We mortals are changeable. Sadly, I must say we are more likely to change our minds when we are right than when we are wrong. When we are wrong, we are very stubborn indeed. When we are right, we tend to hesitate and let opportunity escape. My first judgment is usually my best. Yet, I have always found it difficult to force myself to follow through with a good bargain once it is made. Therefore, as a protection against my own weaknesses, I always make a prompt deposit right away. This saves me from later regrets over the good luck that should have been mine."

"Thank you! I would like to speak again." The Syrian was on his feet once more. "These tales are very much alike. Each time, opportunity flies away for the exact same reason. Each time, she comes to a procrastinator, bringing a good plan. Each time, they hesitate. They do not say, 'Right now is the best time, I must do it quickly.' How can men succeed that way?"

"Your words are wise, my friend," responded the buyer. "Good luck fled from procrastination in both of these tales. Yet, this is not unusual. The spirit of procrastination lives inside all men. We all desire riches. However, when opportunity appears before us, that inner spirit of procrastination often urges us to delay our acceptance.

"By listening to it, we become our own worst enemies. In my younger days, I did not know it by this long word that our friend from Syria enjoys using. At first, I thought it was my own poor judgment that caused me to lose so many profitable trades. Later, I blamed it on my stubborn disposition. Finally, I recognized it for exactly what it was. It was a habit of needless delaying when action was required—prompt and decisive action. How I hated it once its true character was finally revealed to me! With the fierce bitterness of a wild donkey hitched to a chariot, I finally broke loose from this enemy of my success."

"Thank you! I would like to ask a question to Mr. Merchant." The Syrian was speaking again. "You wear fine robes, unlike those of a poor man. You speak like a successful man. Tell us, do you still listen now when procrastination whispers in your ear?"

"Like our friend the buyer, I also had to recognize and conquer procrastination," the merchant responded. "To me, it proved to be an enemy, always watching and waiting to ruin my accomplishments. The tale I just shared is only one of many similar examples. I could tell you many more stories to show how it drove away my opportunities. But it is not difficult to conquer, once it is understood. No man willingly allows a thief to rob his bins of grain. Nor does any man willingly allow an enemy to drive away his customers and rob him of his profits. Once I recognized that my enemy was committing these exact crimes against me, I conquered him with fierce determination. Every man must master his own spirit of procrastination in this same way before he can ever expect to share in the rich treasures of Babylon."

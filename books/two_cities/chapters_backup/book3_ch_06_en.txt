CHAPTER VI.
Triumph


The terrible court of five judges, a public prosecutor, and a determined jury met every day. Their list of the condemned went out every evening, read aloud by the jailers to the prisoners in each jail. The jailers' favorite joke was, “Come out and listen to the Evening Paper, you guys inside!”

“Charles Evremonde, called Darnay!”

So at last began the reading of the list at La Force prison.

When a name was called, the prisoner stepped aside into an area reserved for those marked for death. Charles Darnay knew the routine well; he had seen hundreds of prisoners go this way.

His bloated jailer, wearing glasses to read, looked over them to make sure Darnay had stepped aside, and then continued through the list, pausing briefly at each name. There were twenty-three names, but only twenty people answered. One of the summoned prisoners had already died in jail and been forgotten, and two had already been executed and forgotten. The list was read in the same arched room where Darnay had seen the polite crowd on his first night. Every single person from that crowd had died in the massacre, and everyone he had met since then had been executed.

There were quick goodbyes and kind words, but the parting was over fast. It was an everyday event, and the prisoners of La Force were busy planning games and a small concert for that evening. They crowded to the bars to cry, but twenty spots in the evening's show now had to be filled by others. Time was short before lockup, when the rooms and halls would be guarded by the large guard dogs for the night. The prisoners were not cold or heartless; their behavior was just a reaction to the terrible times. Indeed, a strange urge to face the Guillotine unnecessarily and die had infected many people—not out of boasting, but as a mental illness spreading through the population. In times of plague, some people feel a weird urge to get sick, and all of us have hidden instincts that only appear in extreme circumstances.

The trip to the Conciergerie prison was short and dark, and the night spent in its dirty cells was long and cold. The next day, fifteen prisoners were tried before Darnay's name was called. All fifteen were condemned to death, and their trials took only an hour and a half in total.

“Charles Evremonde, called Darnay,” was finally called to the front.

His judges sat on the bench wearing feathered hats, while almost everyone else in the room wore red caps and tri-color ribbons. Looking at the jury and the noisy crowd, one might have thought that criminals were putting honest men on trial. The poorest and cruelest people in the city ruled the courtroom, shouting comments, cheering, booing, and deciding the verdicts without any control. Most of the men were armed, and some of the women carried knives or daggers, eating and drinking while they watched, and many knitted. Among the knitters was one woman sitting in the front row with a spare piece of knitting under her arm. She sat next to a man Darnay had not seen since the city gates, but whom he immediately recognized as Defarge. He noticed that she whispered to him once or twice and seemed to be his wife. But what he noticed most was that, even though they sat very close to him, they never looked at him. They seemed to be waiting for something with stubborn determination, staring only at the jury. Below the president of the court sat Doctor Manette in his usual quiet clothes. As far as Darnay could see, the Doctor and Mr. Lorry were the only men in the room who wore normal clothes instead of the rough revolutionary dress of the Carmagnole.

Charles Darnay was accused of being an emigrant whose life belonged to the Republic under the law that banished all exiles under penalty of death. It did not matter that the law was passed after he had already returned to France. He was there, the law was there, he had been captured in France, and they demanded his execution.

“Cut off his head!” cried the audience. “He is an enemy of the Republic!”

The president rang his bell to quiet the crowd and asked the prisoner if it was true that he had lived for many years in England.

Yes, it was true.

Was he not an emigrant then? What did he call himself?

Not an emigrant, he hoped, according to the spirit of the law.

Why not? the president asked.

Because he had voluntarily given up a title and a position that he hated, leaving his country long before the word "emigrant" was used in this way, to live by his own hard work in England rather than off the labor of the heavily taxed people of France.

What proof did he have of this?

He gave the names of two witnesses: Theophile Gabelle and Alexandre Manette.

But he had married in England, the president reminded him.

Yes, but not an English woman.

A citizeness of France?

Yes, by birth.

What was her name and family?

“Lucie Manette, the only daughter of Doctor Manette, the good physician sitting right there.”

This answer had a great effect on the crowd. Shouts praising the well-known doctor filled the hall. The crowd was so unpredictable that tears rolled down the faces of several fierce-looking people who, just a moment before, had been glaring at the prisoner as if they wanted to drag him outside and kill him.

During these first steps of his trial, Charles Darnay followed Doctor Manette's exact instructions. The same careful plan guided every step that lay ahead of him, prepared in advance.

The president asked why he had returned to France when he did, and not earlier.

He replied that he had not returned sooner because he had no way of making a living in France other than the wealth he had given up, whereas in England he earned his living by teaching French. He returned when he did because of the urgent letter from a French citizen who said his life was in danger because of Darnay's absence. He had come back to save a citizen's life and tell the truth, risking his own safety. Was that a crime in the eyes of the Republic?

The crowd shouted enthusiastically, “No!” and the president rang his bell to quiet them. It didn't work, as they kept shouting “No!” until they decided to stop on their own.

The president asked for the name of that citizen. Darnay explained that the citizen was Gabelle, his first witness. He also pointed to Gabelle's letter, which had been taken from him at the gates but was surely among the papers on the president's desk.

The Doctor had made sure the letter was there. At this point, it was produced and read aloud. Gabelle was called to confirm it, and did so. Gabelle politely mentioned that because the court was so busy dealing with so many enemies of the Republic, he had been slightly forgotten in the Abbaye Prison until three days ago, when he was finally released after the jury decided that his charges were answered by the arrival of Citizen Darnay.

Doctor Manette was questioned next. His great popularity and clear answers made a strong impression. He explained that Charles was his first friend after being released from the Bastille, and that Charles had stayed in England to care for him and Lucie in their exile. He also revealed that, far from being favored by the English government, Charles had actually been tried for his life in London for being a friend to the United States and an enemy to England. As he explained these facts clearly and honestly, the jury and the crowd were completely won over. Finally, when he called on Mr. Lorry to confirm the details of the English trial, the jury declared they had heard enough and were ready to vote.

As each juryman voted aloud, the crowd cheered. Every vote was in the prisoner's favor, and the president declared him free.

Then began one of those wild scenes where the crowd showed their unpredictable generosity and mercy. It was probably a mix of emotions. As soon as the acquittal was announced, tears flowed as freely as blood had on other days. Men and women rushed to hug Darnay, nearly causing him to faint from exhaustion after his long imprisonment. He was well aware that these same people, under a different mood, would have rushed at him just as eagerly to tear him to pieces in the streets.

He was led away to make room for other prisoners, saving him from the crowd for a moment. Five prisoners were to be tried next, accused of being enemies of the Republic because they had not active helped it. The court worked so quickly to make up for freeing Darnay that these five were condemned to die within twenty-four hours before Darnay even left the building. The first one told Darnay the news with the usual prison sign of death—a raised finger—and they all said, “Long live the Republic!”

The five prisoners had no audience to watch their trial, because when Darnay and Doctor Manette walked out the gates, the entire courtroom crowd was outside waiting. Only two faces were missing, whom Darnay looked for in vain. As he came out, the crowd rushed him again, crying, hugging, and shouting, until the scene by the riverbank seemed completely wild.

They placed him in a large chair they had brought from the courthouse. They threw a red flag over the chair and tied a spear with a red cap to the back of it. In this triumphal seat, despite the Doctor's objections, he was carried home on men's shoulders. Surrounded by a sea of red caps and wild faces, Darnay felt so confused that he feared he was actually in a cart on his way to the Guillotine.

In a dreamlike procession, hugging strangers and pointing Darnay out, they carried him along. The red flags and caps colored the snowy streets as they marched toward his apartment. Her father had run ahead to warn Lucie, and when Darnay finally stood before her, she fainted in his arms.

As he held her to his chest and turned her head away from the noisy crowd so he could kiss her unseen, some people began to dance. Instantly, everyone joined in, and the courtyard filled with the Carmagnole. They placed a young woman from the crowd into the empty chair to carry her as the Goddess of Liberty, and then the dancing crowd spilled out into the streets, along the river, and over the bridge, carrying everyone away.

After shaking the hand of the proud, victorious Doctor, shaking the hand of Mr. Lorry, who came in panting from the crowd, kissing little Lucie as she was held up to hug him, and thanking Miss Pross, Charles took his wife in his arms and carried her upstairs to their rooms.

“Lucie! My own! I am safe.”

“Oh, dearest Charles, let me thank God on my knees, just as I prayed to Him.”

They all bowed their heads in prayer. When she was back in his arms, he said to her:

“And now speak to your father, dearest. No other man in France could have done what he did for me.”

She rested her head on her father's chest, just as she had held his poor head long ago. He was happy to have returned the favor, rewarded for his past suffering, and proud of his strength. “You must not be weak, my darling,” he said. “Don't tremble so. I have saved him.”

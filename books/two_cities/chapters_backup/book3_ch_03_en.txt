CHAPTER III.
The Shadow


One of the first things Mr. Lorry thought about when the bank opened was that he had no right to put Tellson's Bank in danger by keeping the wife of a French exile prisoner under the bank's roof. He would have risked his own money, safety, and life for Lucie and her child without hesitation. However, the bank was a trust that did not belong to him, and he had to be a strict businessman about it.

At first, he thought of Defarge, wondering if he should go back to the wine shop to ask him where the safest place to stay would be in this chaotic city. But the same reason that made him think of Defarge also made him change his mind: Defarge lived in the most violent district, was very influential there, and was deeply involved in the dangerous revolution.

When noon came and the Doctor had not returned, and with every delay putting the bank at risk, Mr. Lorry talked with Lucie. She said her father had mentioned renting an apartment for a short time in this district, near the bank. Since there was no business objection to this, and since Mr. Lorry realized that even if Charles were released, they could not leave the city yet, he went out to look for lodgings. He found a suitable place high up in a quiet side street where the closed shutters of the other windows in a tall, gloomy square showed that the houses were empty.

He immediately moved Lucie, her child, and Miss Pross to this apartment, making them as comfortable as possible—much more comfortable than he was himself. He left Jerry Cruncher with them as a guard who could handle a fight if necessary, and returned to his work. He felt anxious and sad, and the day dragged on slowly and heavily.

The day finally ended, leaving him exhausted by the time the bank closed. He was alone again in his room from the night before, thinking about what to do next, when he heard footsteps on the stairs. A moment later, a man entered, looked at him closely, and called him by name.

“At your service,” Mr. Lorry said. “Do you know me?”

The visitor was a strong man with dark, curly hair, between forty-five and fifty years old. For an answer, he repeated the question in the same tone:

“Do you know me?”

“I have seen you somewhere.”

“Perhaps at my wine shop?”

Excited and nervous, Mr. Lorry said, “You come from Doctor Manette?”

“Yes. I come from Doctor Manette.”

“And what does he say? What did he send for me?”

Defarge handed him a folded scrap of paper. It had these words written in the Doctor's handwriting:

    “Charles is safe, but I cannot safely leave this place yet.
     I have arranged for the messenger to carry a short note
     from Charles to his wife. Let the messenger see his wife.”

It was dated from La Force, written less than an hour ago.

“Will you come with me,” Mr. Lorry said, relieved and happy after reading the note, “to where his wife is staying?”

“Yes,” Defarge replied.

Without noticing how cold and robotic Defarge's voice sounded, Mr. Lorry put on his hat, and they went down into the courtyard. There they found two women, one of whom was knitting.

“Madame Defarge, surely!” said Mr. Lorry, who remembered leaving her in that exact same pose seventeen years ago.

“It is she,” her husband remarked.

“Is Madame coming with us?” Mr. Lorry asked, seeing that she walked along with them.

“Yes. So that she can recognize their faces and know who they are. It is for their safety.”

Starting to feel uneasy about Defarge's behavior, Mr. Lorry looked at him doubtfully and led the way. Both women followed them; the second woman was the one known as The Vengeance.

They walked through the streets as quickly as they could, went up the stairs of the new apartment, and were let in by Jerry. They found Lucie crying alone. She was filled with joy at the news of her husband and kissed the hand of Defarge as he handed her the note—never imagining what that hand had been doing near Charles the night before, and what it might have done to him if things had gone differently.

     “DEAREST,—Take courage. I am well, and your father has
      influence here. You cannot reply to this.
      Kiss our child for me.”

That was the entire message. But it meant so much to Lucie that she turned to Madame Defarge and kissed one of the hands that was knitting. It was a loving, thankful gesture, but the hand did not respond—it dropped cold and heavy, and then immediately went back to its knitting.

The cold feel of the hand startled Lucie. She paused as she was putting the note inside her dress, her hands frozen at her collar, and looked at Madame Defarge in fear. Madame Defarge returned her worried look with a cold, blank stare.

“My dear,” Mr. Lorry interrupted to explain, “there are frequent riots in the streets. Although they probably won't bother you, Madame Defarge wants to see the people she has the power to protect during these times, so that she can recognize them. I believe,” Mr. Lorry said, hesitating as the cold behavior of the three visitors began to make him nervous, “I am explaining this correctly, Citizen Defarge?”

Defarge looked gloomily at his wife and answered only with a rough grunt of agreement.

“You had better bring the dear child here, Lucie,” Mr. Lorry said, trying to sound as friendly as possible, “and our good Miss Pross. Our friend Pross, Defarge, is an English lady and doesn't speak French.”

Miss Pross, who firmly believed she was more than a match for any foreigner even in times of danger, came in with her arms crossed. Looking at The Vengeance, she said in English, “Well, I must say, Boldface! I hope _you_ are doing well!” She also coughed loudly at Madame Defarge, but neither woman paid any attention to her.

“Is that his child?” Madame Defarge asked, stopping her knitting for the first time and pointing her needle at little Lucie like the finger of fate.

“Yes, madame,” Mr. Lorry answered. “This is our poor prisoner's darling daughter and only child.”

The threatening presence of Madame Defarge and her companions seemed to cast such a dark shadow over the child that Lucie instinctively knelt down and held her daughter to her chest. The dark shadow seemed to hang over both mother and child.

“It is enough, husband,” Madame Defarge said. “I have seen them. We can go.”

But her quiet behavior carried such a hidden threat that Lucie was frightened into saying, grabbing Madame Defarge's dress pleadingly:

“Please be kind to my poor husband. Do not let them hurt him. Will you help me see him if you can?”

“Your husband is not my concern here,” Madame Defarge replied, looking down at her calmly. “I came here to see the daughter of your father.”

“For my sake, then, show mercy to my husband! For my child's sake! She will fold her hands and beg you to be merciful. We are more afraid of you than of anyone else.”

Madame Defarge took this as a compliment and looked at her husband. Defarge, who had been nervously biting his nail and watching her, forced his face into a stern expression.

“What does your husband say in that little letter?” Madame Defarge asked with a dark smile. “Influence? Did he mention something about influence?”

“He says my father,” Lucie said, quickly pulling out the paper but keeping her eyes fixed on Madame Defarge, “has a lot of influence there.”

“Then surely it will set him free!” Madame Defarge said. “Let it do that.”

“As a wife and mother,” Lucie cried out, “I beg you to have pity on me. Do not use your power against my innocent husband, but help him instead. Oh, think of me as another woman—as a wife and mother!”

Madame Defarge looked at the pleading woman as coldly as ever, and turned to her friend The Vengeance:

“Have the wives and mothers we have seen since we were children ever been treated with mercy? Haven't we seen _their_ husbands and fathers thrown in prison and kept from them for years? All our lives, haven't we watched other women and their children suffer poverty, starvation, sickness, pain, and oppression?”

“We have seen nothing else,” The Vengeance replied.

“We have suffered this for a very long time,” Madame Defarge said, turning back to Lucie. “So tell me: why should the trouble of one single wife and mother matter to us now?”

She went back to her knitting and walked out of the room. The Vengeance followed her, and Defarge left last, closing the door behind him.

“Have courage, my dear Lucie,” Mr. Lorry said as he helped her up. “Courage, courage! So far, everything is going well for us—much better than for many other poor souls. Cheer up and try to be hopeful.”

“I am not ungrateful, but that terrifying woman seems to cast a dark shadow over all my hopes.”

“Come, come!” Mr. Lorry said. “Why is my brave girl losing heart? A shadow is just a shadow! There is nothing real to fear in it, Lucie.”

But the dark attitude of the Defarges worried Mr. Lorry deeply as well, though he kept his fears to himself.

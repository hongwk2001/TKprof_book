CHAPTER XII.
Darkness


Sydney Carton paused in the street, not quite sure where to go next. “At Tellson's Bank at nine,” he said with a thoughtful face. “Would it be a good idea to show myself in the meantime? I think so. It is best that these people know there is someone here who looks like Charles. It is a smart precaution, and it might be a necessary step. But I must be careful, careful, careful! Let me think this through!”

Stopping himself from walking too quickly toward his destination, he paced up and down the darkening street, tracing his plan to its possible consequences. His first instinct was confirmed. “It is best,” he decided at last, “that these people know someone like me is here.” And he turned toward the neighborhood of Saint Antoine.

Defarge had described himself in court that day as a wine-shop owner in Saint Antoine. For someone who knew Paris well, it was not hard to find the shop without asking directions. Once he located it, Carton left the narrow streets, ate dinner at a small restaurant, and fell fast asleep afterward. For the first time in many years, he did not touch any hard alcohol. Since the night before, he had drunk nothing but a little light wine, and last night he had poured his brandy onto Mr. Lorry's hearth, like a man who was finished with drinking for good.

It was seven o'clock when he woke up feeling refreshed and went back out into the streets. On his way to Saint Antoine, he stopped at a shop window with a mirror and adjusted his messy tie, his coat collar, and his wild hair. Once he was presentable, he walked straight to Defarge's wine-shop and went inside.

There were no customers in the shop except Jacques Three, the man with the twitchy fingers and the croaking voice. This man, whom Carton had seen on the jury, stood drinking at the small counter, talking with Monsieur and Madame Defarge. The Vengeance also joined in the conversation as if she were a regular employee.

When Carton walked in, sat down, and asked in very poor French for a small glass of wine, Madame Defarge glanced at him carelessly. Then she looked closer, and even closer, before walking over to him herself to ask what he had ordered.

He repeated his order.

“Are you English?” asked Madame Defarge, raising her dark eyebrows curiously.

He looked at her as if it took him a long time to understand even a single French word, then answered in a thick English accent. “Yes, madame, yes. I am English!”

Madame Defarge went back to her counter to pour the wine. As he picked up a revolutionary newspaper and pretended to struggle through reading it, he heard her whisper, “I swear to you, he looks just like Evrémonde!”

Defarge brought him the wine and said good evening.

“What?”

“Good evening.”

“Oh! Good evening, citizen,” Carton said, filling his glass. “Ah, and good wine. I drink to the Republic.”

Defarge went back to the counter and said, “He does look a little like him.” Madame Defarge snapped back, “I tell you, he looks a lot like him!” Jacques Three calmly remarked, “He is on your mind so much, you see, madame.” The Vengeance laughed and added, “Yes, indeed! And you are looking forward so much to seeing Evrémonde one last time tomorrow!”

Carton followed the lines of the newspaper with his finger, looking completely focused and absorbed. The group at the counter leaned close together, speaking quietly. After a brief silence, during which they all stared at him without breaking his focus on the revolutionary article, they went back to their conversation.

“What madame says is true,” Jacques Three agreed. “Why stop? There is great reason in that. Why stop?”

“Well, yes,” Defarge reasoned, “but we must stop somewhere. After all, the question is: where?”

“At total destruction,” Madame Defarge said.

“Magnificent!” croaked Jacques Three. The Vengeance also strongly agreed.

“Destroying them all is a good policy, my wife,” said Defarge, looking troubled. “Usually, I don't argue against it. But this Doctor has suffered so much. You saw him today; you saw his face when his letter was read.”

“I saw his face!” Madame Defarge repeated angrily and scornfully. “Yes, I saw it. I saw that his face is not the face of a true friend of the Republic. He had better watch his step!”

“And you must have seen, my wife,” Defarge said softly, “the terrible pain of his daughter, which must be agonizing for him!”

“I have watched his daughter,” Madame Defarge repeated. “Yes, I have watched her more than once. I saw her today, and I've seen her on other days. I've watched her in the courtroom, and I've watched her standing in the street near the prison. If I only lift my finger—!” She raised her finger—while Carton pretended to keep reading—and let it drop hard on the counter, making a sound like the heavy blade of the Guillotine falling.

“The citizenness is magnificent!” croaked the juror.

“She is an angel!” said The Vengeance, hugging her.

“As for you,” Madame Defarge continued, looking coldly at her husband, “if it were up to you—which, thank goodness, it is not—you would save this man even now.”

“No!” Defarge protested. “Not even if lifting this glass could do it! But I would stop there. I say, let us stop with Darnay.”

“Listen to me, Jacques,” Madame Defarge said furiously, “and listen to me too, my little Vengeance. Listen to both of us! I have had this whole family name written down in my secret register for a long time, doomed to total destruction for their crimes as cruel tyrants. Ask my husband if that is true.”

“It is true,” Defarge agreed, without even being asked.

“At the start of our great revolution, when the Bastille fell, he found the letter read in court today. He brought it home, and in the middle of the night, when this shop was empty and locked, we read it right here by the light of this lamp. Ask him if that is true.”

“It is true,” Defarge agreed.

“That night, when we finished reading the paper and the lamp went out, as daylight started shining through the shutters and bars, I told him I had a secret to share. Ask him if that is true.”

“It is true,” Defarge agreed again.

“I told him that secret. I beat my chest with my hands, just as I do now, and I told him, ‘Defarge, I was raised among seashore fishermen. That peasant family so horribly treated by the Evrémonde brothers, which the Bastille letter describes, was _my_ family. Defarge, that sister of the dying boy on the ground was my sister. Her husband was my brother-in-law. Their unborn child was my niece or nephew. That boy was my brother, that father was my father. Those dead are my dead, and it is up to me to make sure those crimes are answered for!’ Ask him if that is true.”

“It is true,” Defarge agreed once more.

“Then go tell the Wind and the Fire where to stop,” Madame Defarge replied, “but do not tell me.”

Both of her listeners took a dark pleasure in her deadly anger—Carton could tell how pale and intense she was, even without looking at her—and both praised her. Defarge, standing alone, tried to say a few words in memory of the kind wife of the Marquis, but his wife only repeated her response: “Tell the Wind and the Fire where to stop; not me!”

More customers entered, and the group split up. The English customer paid for his wine, pretended to struggle to count his change, and asked for directions to the National Palace. Madame Defarge walked him to the door, placing her hand on his arm to point down the street. Carton could not help thinking that it would be a good deed to grab that arm, lift it, and plunge a knife deep into her heart.

But he walked away and was soon lost in the shadow of the prison wall. At the agreed time, he left the darkness and went to Mr. Lorry's room, where he found the old gentleman pacing back and forth in great worry. Mr. Lorry said he had been with Lucie until just now, leaving her only to keep his meeting with Carton. Her father had not been seen since he left the bank around four o'clock. Lucie had a tiny bit of hope that her father's influence might save Charles, but it was very small. He had been gone for more than five hours; where could he be?

Mr. Lorry waited until ten o'clock. Since Dr. Manette still had not returned, and Mr. Lorry did not want to leave Lucie alone any longer, they decided that he would go back to her and return to the bank at midnight. In the meantime, Carton would wait by the fire for the Doctor.

Carton waited and waited until the clock struck midnight, but Dr. Manette did not return. Mr. Lorry came back, having heard no news and bringing none. Where could the Doctor be?

They were discussing this and trying to find some small hope in his long absence when they heard him walking up the stairs. The moment he walked into the room, it was clear that all hope was gone.

Whether he had actually met with anyone or had spent all those hours just wandering the streets was never known. As he stood there staring at them, they did not ask any questions because his face told them everything.

“I can't find it,” he said, “ and I must have it. Where is it?”

His head and neck were bare, and as he spoke with a helpless, wandering look, he took off his coat and let it drop onto the floor.

“Where is my shoemaker's bench? I've been looking everywhere for my bench and I can't find it. What have they done with my work? Time is running out; I must finish those shoes.”

Mr. Lorry and Carton looked at each other, their hearts sinking.

“Come on, come on!” Dr. Manette whimpered in a miserable voice. “Let me get to work. Give me my tools.”

When they did not answer, he pulled his hair and stamped his feet on the ground like a panicked child.

“Don't torture a poor, miserable man,” he begged with a terrible cry. “Just give me my work! What will happen to us if these shoes are not finished tonight?”

He was lost, completely lost!

It was so obviously useless to argue with him or try to bring him back to his senses that—as if they had agreed without speaking—they each placed a hand on his shoulder and gently guided him to sit by the fire, promising that he would have his work soon. He sank into the chair, staring sadly at the hot coals and crying. It was as if everything that had happened since his release from the attic room in Paris was just a passing dream; Mr. Lorry saw him shrink back into the exact same broken state he was in when Defarge first kept him.

Although they were both deeply moved and terrified by this sight of his mental collapse, it was not a time to give in to sadness. His poor daughter, now stripped of her last hope and support, needed them too much. Again, as if by silent agreement, they looked at each other with the same thought in their minds. Carton spoke first:

“The last chance is gone; it wasn't much anyway. Yes, he had better be taken back to Lucie. But before you go, will you listen to me carefully for a moment? Don't ask me why I am making these demands, or why I need you to promise what I'm about to ask. I have a reason—a very good one.”

“I don't doubt it,” Mr. Lorry replied. “Tell me.”

The broken doctor sat in the chair between them, rocking back and forth and moaning. They spoke in the quiet tones people use when watching over a sickbed in the middle of the night.

Carton bent down to pick up the Doctor's coat, which was lying near his feet. As he did, a small leather case that the Doctor used to carry his daily schedule fell onto the floor. Carton picked it up and found a folded paper inside. “We should look at this!” he said. Mr. Lorry nodded. Carton opened it and exclaimed, “Thank _God_!”

“What is it?” Mr. Lorry asked eagerly.

“Just a moment! Let me explain that in a second. First,” he reached into his coat and pulled out another paper, “this is the travel pass that allows me to leave the city. Look at it. You see—Sydney Carton, an Englishman?”

Mr. Lorry held it open, looking at Carton's serious face.

“Keep it for me until tomorrow. Remember, I am going to see Charles tomorrow, and it is better if I don't bring this pass into the prison.”

“Why not?”

“I'm not sure; I just prefer not to. Now, take this paper that Doctor Manette had on him. It is a similar travel pass, allowing him, his daughter, and her child to pass through the city gates and cross the border at any time! You see?”

“Yes!”

“Perhaps he got it yesterday as a final precaution. When is it dated? Actually, it doesn't matter; don't waste time looking now. Put it away safely with mine and yours. Listen carefully! I didn't know until a couple of hours ago that he even had such a paper. It is valid until it is canceled. But it might be canceled very soon, and I have reason to believe it will be.”

“Are they in danger?”

“They are in extreme danger. They are about to be accused by Madame Defarge. I know it because I heard it from her own mouth tonight. What I overheard showed me their danger in terrifying detail. I didn't waste any time, and since then I have met with Barsad the spy. He confirmed it. He knows that a wood-cutter living by the prison wall is controlled by the Defarges, and that Madame Defarge has coached him to testify that he saw her”—he avoided saying Lucie's name—“making signals to the prisoners. It is easy to see that they will accuse her of plotting a prison escape. It will cost her life, and probably her child's, and her father's too—since both have been seen with her near the prison. Don't look so horrified. You are going to save them all.”

“May God help me do it, Carton! But how?”

“I am going to tell you how. It will all depend on you, and there is no better man for the job. This new accusation won't happen until after tomorrow—probably not for two or three days, or even a week. You know that it is a crime punishable by death to mourn for or show sympathy for anyone executed by the Guillotine. Lucie and her father will definitely show their grief, and Madame Defarge—who will never stop hunting them—will wait for that to happen to make her case even stronger and doubly sure. Do you understand?”

“I am listening so closely, and with so much trust in you, that for a moment,” Mr. Lorry said, touching the back of the Doctor's chair, “I even forgot about this terrible situation.”

“You have money, and you can buy carriage passage to the coast as fast as possible. You have already finished your preparations to return to England. Early tomorrow, have your horses and carriage ready so they are set to leave at two o'clock in the afternoon.”

“It will be done!”

Carton's manner was so passionate and inspiring that Mr. Lorry caught his excitement, moving as quickly as a young man.

“You have a noble heart. Did I say we could depend on no better man? Tonight, tell her about the danger to her child and her father. Focus on that, because she would gladly die with her husband if it were just her own life at stake.” He paused for a second, then went on. “For the sake of her child and her father, convince her that she must leave Paris with them and you at that exact hour. Tell her it was her husband's last request. Tell her that more depends on this than she can possibly imagine. You believe her father, even in this broken state, will do whatever she asks, don't you?”

“I am sure of it.”

“I thought so. Quietly and steadily make all these arrangements here in the courtyard, even sitting in your seat in the carriage. The moment I come to you, pull me in and drive away.”

“So I must wait for you, no matter what happens?”

“You have my travel pass with the others, and you will save my seat. Wait for nothing except for me to get into that seat, and then drive straight for England!”

“Well, then,” said Mr. Lorry, gripping Carton's eager but steady hand, “it won't all depend on one old man. I will have a young, passionate man at my side.”

“With Heaven's help, you will! Promise me solemnly that nothing will make you change the plan we have just agreed to.”

“Nothing, Carton.”

“Remember these words tomorrow: if you change the plan, or delay it for any reason, no one can be saved, and many lives will be lost.”

“I will remember them. I hope to do my part faithfully.”

“And I hope to do mine. Now, goodbye!”

Although he said this with a serious, earnest smile, and even kissed the old man's hand, he did not leave him just yet. He helped Mr. Lorry wake up the rocking doctor by the dying fire, putting a coat and hat on him, and leading him outside under the pretense of finding the shoemaker's bench and tools he kept crying for. Carton walked on the other side of him, protecting the broken doctor all the way back to the courtyard of the house where Lucie—who had been so happy back when Carton first confessed his lonely heart to her—was staying awake through this terrible night. He entered the courtyard and stood alone for a few moments, looking up at the light in her window. Before he walked away, he whispered a blessing toward the window, and a final goodbye.

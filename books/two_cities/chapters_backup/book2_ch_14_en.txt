CHAPTER XIV.
The Honest Tradesman

To Jeremiah Cruncher, sitting on his stool in Fleet Street with his young son beside him, a vast number of moving things were presented every day. Anyone sitting on Fleet Street during the busy hours would be dazed by the two massive crowds of people—one moving west with the sun, the other moving east from it.

With his piece of straw in his mouth, Mr. Cruncher sat watching these two streams. He had no expectation that they would ever run dry. A small part of his income came from guiding nervous, elderly women across the busy street from Tellson’s Bank. Brief as these encounters were, Mr. Cruncher always took a strong interest in the ladies, wishing he could drink to their health. It was from the tips they gave him for this help that he supported himself, as just noted.

In the old days, poets sat in public places to think and write. Mr. Cruncher, sitting on his stool in public, was no poet; he thought as little as possible and simply watched his surroundings.

He was sitting there during a quiet season when crowds were small, tips were few, and his business was doing poorly. This led him to suspect that Mrs. Cruncher must have been praying against him. Suddenly, a large crowd moving west down Fleet Street caught his attention. Looking closer, Mr. Cruncher saw a funeral procession approaching, and the crowd was shouting and protesting angrily.

"Young Jerry," said Mr. Cruncher to his son, "it’s a burial."

"Hooray, father!" cried Young Jerry.

The young boy shouted this happily. His father took the shout so poorly that he immediately slapped his son on the ear.

"What do you mean? What are you shouting for? What are you trying to tell your own father, you little rascal? This boy is getting to be too much for _me_!" said Mr. Cruncher, looking at him. "Him and his hoorays! Don't let me hear that again, or you'll get another slap. Do you hear?"

"I wasn't doing any harm," Young Jerry protested, rubbing his cheek.

"Quiet then," said Mr. Cruncher. "I won't have any of your 'no harms.' Stand up on that seat and watch the crowd."

His son obeyed, and the crowd came closer. They were shouting and hissing around a dusty hearse and a mourning carriage. Inside the carriage sat a single mourner dressed in black. He looked very uncomfortable as the mob surrounded him, mocking him, making faces, and shouting: "Yah! Spies! Yah! Spies!" along with many other insults.

Funerals always attracted Mr. Cruncher. He became excited whenever one passed Tellson's Bank. Naturally, a funeral surrounded by a mob excited him greatly. He asked the first man who bumped into him:

"What is it, brother? What’s happening?"

"I don’t know," said the man. "Spies! Yah! Spies!"

He asked another man. "Who is it?"

"I don’t know," the man replied, shouting anyway. "Spies! Yah! Spies!"

Finally, a man who knew more bumped into him, and Jerry learned that it was the funeral of Roger Cly.

"Was he a spy?" asked Mr. Cruncher.

"An Old Bailey spy," the man replied. "Yah! Spies!"

"Why, of course!" Jerry exclaimed, remembering the trial he had watched. "I’ve seen him. Is he dead?"

"Dead as mutton," the other replied, "and can’t be dead enough. Pull them out! Spies!"

The crowd caught onto this idea quickly and began mobbing the carriage, forcing it to stop. When they opened the doors, the single mourner scrambled out and was caught by the crowd for a moment. But he was quick and managed to slip away down a side street, shedding his black cloak, hat, and handkerchief to escape.

The people tore his clothes to pieces with great enjoyment. Meanwhile, the local shopkeepers quickly closed their shops, fearing the mob. The crowd had already opened the hearse to pull out the coffin when someone suggested they escort it to the cemetery in celebration. This suggestion was welcomed, and the carriage was immediately filled with people inside and out, while others climbed onto the roof of the hearse. Among the first volunteers was Jerry Cruncher himself, who hid his spiky head in the corner of the carriage to avoid being seen by anyone from Tellson's Bank.

The undertakers protested, but when several voices suggested throwing them into the nearby river to cool them down, their protests stopped. The procession started again, with a chimney sweep driving the hearse and a pie seller driving the carriage. A man leading a mangy black bear joined in, adding to the spectacle.

Drinking beer, smoking pipes, and singing songs, the rowdy crowd went on its way, with shops closing ahead of them. They finally reached the old St. Pancras churchyard in the fields. The mob poured into the cemetery and buried Roger Cly in their own rowdy way.

With the funeral over, the crowd looked for other amusement. Someone suggested accusing random passersby of being spies. They chased and beat several innocent people who had never been near the courthouse. Soon they began breaking windows and looting taverns. Finally, after several hours of rioting, a rumor spread that the Guards were coming. At this, the crowd quickly dispersed, as mobs usually did.

Mr. Cruncher did not join the rioting but stayed behind in the churchyard to talk with the undertakers. The quiet cemetery seemed to soothe him. He got a pipe from a nearby tavern and smoked it, studying the grave site.

"Jerry," Mr. Cruncher said to himself, "you saw that Cly today, and you saw with your own eyes that he was a young, strongly built man."

After finishing his pipe, he headed back to his post at Tellson’s Bank before closing time. On his way, he stopped to speak with his medical adviser, a well-known surgeon.

Young Jerry relieved his father, reporting that no jobs had come in. The bank closed, the old clerks left, and Mr. Cruncher and his son went home for tea.

"I tell you what!" Mr. Cruncher said to his wife as they entered. "If my work goes wrong tonight, I will know you’ve been praying against me, and I will punish you for it."

His wife shook her head sadly.

"Why, you’re doing it right in front of me!" Mr. Cruncher cried.

"I am saying nothing, Jerry."

"Well, don't think anything either. Thinking is the same as praying. You are trying to work against me. Stop it altogether."

"Yes, Jerry."

"Yes, Jerry," Mr. Cruncher repeated as he sat down. "You better say yes, Jerry."

Mr. Cruncher muttered as he ate his bread and butter, looking dissatisfied.

"Are you going out tonight?" his wife asked.

"Yes, I am."

"May I go with you, father?" his son asked.

"No, you may not. I am going—as your mother knows—fishing. That's where I'm going. Fishing."

"Your fishing rod gets rather rusty, doesn't it, father?"

"Never you mind."

"Will you bring any fish home?"

"If I don't, you won't have much to eat tomorrow," his father replied. "That's enough questions. I won't go out until you are asleep."

He spent the rest of the evening watching Mrs. Cruncher closely and talking to her so she couldn't pray. He had his son talk to her too, keeping her busy with complaints rather than leaving her to her thoughts. He showed great respect for the power of prayer by how much he feared her doing it.

"And remember!" Mr. Cruncher said. "No games tomorrow! If I succeed in bringing home some meat, you will eat it. If I bring home beer, you won't drink water. When you're in Rome, do as the Romans do. I am your Rome."

Then he complained:

"You are praying against our food and drink! I don't know how much food you've cost us with your praying. Look at your boy: he is as thin as a rail. Do you call yourself a mother, and not feed your own son?"

This touched Young Jerry, who urged his mother to feed him as his father suggested.

The evening passed, and Young Jerry and his mother were sent to bed. Mr. Cruncher smoked his pipe in silence until nearly one o'clock. At that late hour, he stood up, took a key from his pocket, opened a locked cupboard, and took out a sack, a crowbar, a rope, and a chain. Placing these tools in his pockets, he gave his wife a final warning, put out the light, and went out.

Young Jerry, who had only pretended to undress, followed his father. Under cover of the dark, he followed him down the stairs and out into the street. He knew he could get back in easily because the building was full of renters and the front door was left unlocked.

Wanting to learn his father's trade, Young Jerry kept close to the walls and followed him. His father walked north and was soon joined by another man carrying similar tools.

Within thirty minutes, they had walked past the street lamps and watchmen into the countryside. They met a third man silently along the road.

The three men walked on, with Young Jerry following, until they stopped near a brick wall with an iron railing. They turned up a dark lane beside the wall. Peeping around the corner under the moonlight, Young Jerry saw his father climb over the iron gate. The other two followed him. They dropped quietly inside and began crawling on their hands and knees.

Young Jerry approached the gate, holding his breath. Looking through the bars, he saw the three men creeping through the tall grass of a large cemetery. The gravestones looked like white ghosts, and the church tower looked like a giant monster. The men stopped and stood up, and then began to dig.

They used a spade at first. Then his father began using a tool like a large corkscrew. They worked hard in the dark. Suddenly, the church clock struck the hour, terrifying Young Jerry so much that he ran away, his hair standing on end.

But his curiosity stopped him and drew him back. He peeped through the gate again. The men seemed to have hooked something heavy. They strained and pulled, and slowly a coffin emerged from the earth. Young Jerry knew what it was, but seeing his father prepare to pry it open terrified him, and he ran all the way home.

He ran as fast as he could, fearing the coffin was chasing him down the street, hopping on its end. He imagined it hiding in doorways and shadows, ready to grab him. When he reached his door, he was half dead with fear. The nightmare followed him to bed, weighing heavily on his chest as he slept.

He was awakened after dawn by his father returning. Something had gone wrong, as his father was holding Mrs. Cruncher by her ears and hitting her head against the wooden headboard of the bed.

"I told you I would, and I did," Mr. Cruncher growled.

"Jerry, Jerry, Jerry!" his wife begged.

"You are praying against my business," Jerry said, "and making me and my partners suffer. You promised to honor and obey me; why don't you?"

"I try to be a good wife, Jerry," she cried.

"Is it being a good wife to oppose your husband's work? Is it honoring me to ruin my business?"

"You didn't do this terrible work when we married, Jerry."

"That is none of your business," Jerry retorted. "You are the wife of an honest tradesman. A good wife would leave my work alone. You call yourself religious? I'd rather have an irreligious wife! You have no more sense of duty than the riverbed, and I have to beat it into you."

They argued in low voices. Finally, the messenger kicked off his muddy boots and lay down on the floor to sleep. His son watched him for a moment before falling back asleep himself.

There was no food for breakfast. Mr. Cruncher was in a terrible mood, keeping a heavy pot lid nearby to throw at his wife if she tried to say grace. He washed and dressed, and set off with his son for work.

Young Jerry, carrying the stool under his arm beside his father in the sunny street, felt very different from the terrified boy of the night before. His fears were gone with the daylight.

"Father," Young Jerry said as they walked, keeping his distance behind the stool, "what is a Resurrection-Man?"

Mr. Cruncher stopped on the pavement. "How should I know?"

"I thought you knew everything, father," said the boy. "Well," Mr. Cruncher replied, walking again and lifting his hat, "he is a tradesman."

"What does he sell, father?"

"His goods," said Mr. Cruncher, thinking it over, "are a branch of scientific goods."

"People's bodies, right, father?" the boy asked.

"I believe it is something like that," Mr. Cruncher said.

"Oh, father, I want to be a Resurrection-Man when I grow up!"

Mr. Cruncher was pleased but shook his head in a serious way. "It depends on how you develop your talents. Be careful, don't talk too much, and you might do well." As Young Jerry walked ahead to set up the stool near Temple Bar, Mr. Cruncher muttered to himself: "Jerry, you honest tradesman, there is hope that this boy will be a blessing to you, and make up for his mother!"

CHAPTER V.
The Jackal

Those were drinking days, and most men drank heavily. So much has time improved these habits that a moderate description of the amount of wine and punch a man would drink in a night, without hurting his reputation as a gentleman, would seem ridiculous today. The legal profession was certainly not behind any other in its drinking habits; nor was Mr. Stryver, who was rapidly pushing his way toward a large and profitable practice.

As a favorite at the Old Bailey and other courts, Mr. Stryver had begun to carefully secure his path to success. The courts now frequently called on their favorite lawyer. Daily, Mr. Stryver's red face could be seen in the King's Bench court, standing out among the other lawyers' wigs like a large sunflower pushing its way up in a crowded garden.

It had once been noted by other lawyers that while Mr. Stryver was quick-tongued, bold, and ready, he lacked the ability to find the core facts in a pile of documents—a key skill for a lawyer. However, he had improved remarkably in this. The more work he got, the better he became at finding the main points of a case. No matter how late he sat drinking with Sydney Carton, he always had the facts ready by morning.

Sydney Carton, the idlest and most unpromising of men, was Stryver’s great ally. The amount the two drank together between law terms could have floated a battleship. Stryver never had a case without Carton being there, hands in his pockets, staring at the ceiling. They traveled the same court circuits together, staying up late drinking. Carton was rumored to be seen at dawn, walking unsteadily back to his lodgings like a stray cat. Eventually, those in the legal world realized that while Carton would never be the famous leader—the "lion"—he was an excellent helper, a "jackal," doing the hard background work for Stryver.

"Ten o'clock, sir," said the tavern worker who had been ordered to wake Carton. "Ten o'clock, sir."

"_What's_ the matter?"

"Ten o'clock, sir."

"What do you mean? Ten o'clock at night?"

"Yes, sir. You told me to wake you."

"Oh! I remember. Very well, very well."

After trying to fall back asleep, which the worker prevented by stirring the fire for five minutes, Carton got up, put on his hat, and walked out. He walked to the Temple district, clearing his head by walking around King's Bench Walk and Paper Buildings twice, before entering Stryver's offices.

Stryver's clerk had already gone home, so Stryver himself opened the door. He was wearing slippers and a loose robe, with his collar open for comfort. He had the tired, bloodshot look around his eyes typical of heavy drinkers.

"You are a little late, Memory," said Stryver.

"About the usual time; maybe fifteen minutes late."

They went into a dusty room lined with books and messy papers, where a fire was burning. A kettle steamed on the stove, and on a cleared table sat plenty of wine, brandy, rum, sugar, and lemons.

"I see you've had your bottle already, Sydney."

"Two tonight, I think. I dined with today's client—or watched him dine, it's the same thing!"

"That was a great point you made, Sydney, about the resemblance and the identification. How did you think of it? When did it occur to you?"

"I thought he was a handsome fellow, and I realized I would have looked just like him if I had had any luck in life."

Mr. Stryver laughed so hard his stomach shook.

"You and your luck, Sydney! Let's get to work, get to work."

Sullenly, the jackal loosened his collar, went to the next room, and returned with a large jug of cold water, a basin, and a few towels. Soaking the towels in the water and wringing them out slightly, he wrapped them around his head in a strange, ugly way, sat at the table, and said, "Now I'm ready!"

"Not too much work to summarize tonight, Memory," said Mr. Stryver cheerfully, looking through his papers.

"How much?"

"Only two cases."

"Give me the harder one first."

"Here they are, Sydney. Go ahead!"

The "lion" then lay back on a sofa on one side of the table, while the "jackal" sat at his own messy desk on the other side, with bottles and glasses nearby. Both drank constantly, but in different ways. Stryver lay back with his hands on his belt, staring at the fire or occasionally looking at a document. Carton, with a worried frown and a focused face, was so deep in his work that he didn't even look up when reaching for his glass, often feeling around for it for a minute before finding it. Two or three times, the work became so difficult that Carton had to get up and soak his towels again, returning with damp headgear that looked ridiculous despite his serious expression.

Eventually, the jackal summarized the cases for the lion. Stryver went over the notes carefully, making remarks and adjustments with Carton's help. When they finished, Stryver lay back down to think, while Carton drank another glass, adjusted his head towels, and began working on the second case. They did not finish until the clocks struck three in the morning.

"And now that we are done, Sydney, pour us a glass of punch," said Mr. Stryver.

Carton took the steaming towels off his head, shook himself, yawned, shivered, and poured the drinks.

"You were very sharp about those prosecution witnesses today, Sydney. Every question hit its mark."

"I am always sharp, aren't I?"

"I don't deny it. But what has put you in such a bad mood? Have some punch and cheer up."

With a grunt of protest, Carton drank.

"You are the same Sydney Carton from our old school days," said Stryver, looking at him. "Always going up and down—happy one minute and depressed the next."

"Ah!" Carton sighed. "Yes, the same Sydney with the same bad luck. Even back then, I did the homework for other boys and neglected my own."

"And why did you do that?"

"God knows. It was just my way, I suppose."

He sat with his hands in his pockets and his legs stretched out, staring at the fire.

"Carton," said Stryver in his usual bullying tone, "your way is, and always was, a weak way. You have no energy or purpose. Look at me."

"Oh, stop it!" Sydney laughed, sounding more cheerful. "Don't start preaching to _me_!"

"How do you think I got to where I am?" Stryver asked. "How do I do it?"

"Partly by paying me to help you, I suppose. But there's no need to lecture me; what you want to do, you do. You were always in the lead, and I was always behind."

"I had to work to get to the front; I wasn't born there, was I?"

"I wasn't there to see it, but I bet you were," Carton said, and they both laughed.

"Before school, at school, and ever since," Carton continued, "you have taken your place, and I have taken mine. Even when we were students in Paris, learning French and French law, you were always succeeding and I was always getting nowhere."

"And whose fault was that?"

"Honestly, I think it was yours. You were always so busy pushing and shouldering your way forward that I had no choice but to sit back and rest. But it's depressing to talk about the past at dawn. Change the subject before I go."

"Well then! Let's toast the pretty witness," said Stryver, raising his glass. "Is that a better subject?"

Apparently not, as Carton became gloomy again.

"The pretty witness," he muttered, looking at his glass. "I've seen enough witnesses today. Who do you mean?"

"The doctor’s beautiful daughter, Miss Manette."

"_She_ is pretty?"

"Is she not?"

"No."

"Why, everyone in the courtroom was admiring her!"

"Who cares what the courtroom thinks? Since when is the Old Bailey a judge of beauty? She's just a golden-haired doll!"

"Do you know, Sydney," Mr. Stryver said, looking at him closely, "I actually thought you felt sorry for her, and were very quick to notice when she fainted?"

"Quick to notice! If any girl faints right in front of a man's nose, he doesn't need glasses to see it. I will drink to her, but I deny she is beautiful. And now, no more drinking. I'm going to bed."

As Stryver walked him to the stairs with a candle, the cold morning light was beginning to show through the dirty windows. Outside, the air was cold and gloomy, the sky gray, and the river dark—the whole city looked like a lifeless desert. Wind blew dust around the quiet streets.

With wasted potential inside him and a gloomy world around him, Carton stopped on a bridge. For a moment, he imagined a better life of honest ambition, self-control, and hard work. In that vision, he saw happiness, success, and hope. A moment later, the vision vanished. Climbing the stairs to his dusty room, he threw himself onto his bed in his clothes and wept.

Sadly, the sun rose, looking down on no sadder sight than this talented man, unable to use his abilities, unable to help himself or find happiness, fully aware of his wasted life and resigned to letting it ruin him.

CHAPTER III.
A Disappointment

The Attorney General informed the jury that the prisoner before them, though young in years, was old in the treasonous acts that now threatened his life. He claimed that this communication with the country's enemy was not new, but had been going on for years. He asserted that the prisoner had repeatedly traveled back and forth between France and England on secret business for which he could give no honest explanation. If treason were capable of succeeding—which, fortunately, it never was—the prisoner’s crimes might have remained hidden. However, Providence had inspired a noble and blameless citizen to uncover the prisoner’s schemes and report them to the government. This patriot, the prosecution promised, would testify. The Attorney General described this witness's actions as heroic: he had been the prisoner’s friend, but upon discovering his treason, had decided to sacrifice his personal feelings on the altar of patriotism. If statues were built in Britain for public benefactors as they were in ancient Greece and Rome, this shining citizen would surely get one. Since Britain did not build such statues, he would not have one, but his virtue—patriotism—was contagious. This noble example had even inspired the prisoner’s servant to search his master’s drawers and pockets to find these incriminating documents. The Attorney General anticipated that the defense would try to discredit this excellent servant, but remarked that he personally respected him more than his own family and urged the jury to do the same. The evidence of these two witnesses, combined with the discovered documents, would prove that the prisoner had lists of British military and naval forces to send to the enemy. Even if the lists were not in the prisoner’s handwriting, the prosecution argued that this only proved how clever the prisoner was in hiding his tracks. The proof would show the treason went back five years, starting just before the first battles of the American Revolutionary War. Therefore, the Attorney General argued, the loyal and responsible jury must find the prisoner guilty. He concluded by demanding the prisoner's execution, saying the jury would never sleep peacefully again if they let him live.

When the Attorney General finished, a loud buzz arose in the courtroom, sounding like a swarm of large bluebottles circling the prisoner in anticipation of his death. When the noise died down, the patriotic witness entered the box.

The Solicitor General then questioned the witness, John Barsad. His testimony perfectly matched the Attorney General's description—perhaps a little too perfectly. After sharing his story, he prepared to leave, but the defense lawyer, sitting near Mr. Lorry, stopped him for cross-examination while the other lawyer opposite continued to stare at the ceiling.

Had he ever been a spy himself? No, he rejected the suggestion with disgust. What did he live on? His own property. Where was it? He couldn't remember exactly. What was it? None of anyone's business. Had he inherited it? Yes. From whom? A distant relation. How distant? Quite distant. Had he ever been in prison? No. Never in a debtors' prison? He didn't see what that had to do with anything. Never? Well, yes, once. How many times? Two or three times. Not five or six? Perhaps. What was his profession? A gentleman. Had he ever been kicked? He might have been. Frequently? No. Kicked downstairs? No, he had once been kicked at the top of a staircase and fell down by himself. Was he kicked for cheating at dice? The drunk liar who kicked him claimed that, but it wasn't true. Was he sure? Absolutely. Had he ever lived by gambling? No more than other gentlemen. Had he borrowed money from the prisoner? Yes. Had he paid it back? No. Was his friendship with the prisoner actually a minor acquaintance forced upon the prisoner during travels? No. Was he sure he saw the prisoner with the lists? Yes. Did he know anything else about them? No. Had he put them there himself? No. Did he expect to get paid for his testimony? No. Was he a government spy? Absolutely not. Did he have any motive other than pure patriotism? None at all.

Next came the servant, Roger Cly. He testified that he had started working for the prisoner four years ago, asking him for work on a ship from Calais. He began to suspect the prisoner soon after. While packing his clothes during travels, he had seen lists like the ones in court in the prisoner’s pockets. He had taken them from the prisoner’s desk drawer, denying that he had placed them there himself. He claimed to have seen the prisoner showing these lists to French gentlemen in Calais and Boulogne. He said he loved his country and had to report it. Under cross-examination, he denied ever being suspected of stealing a silver teapot, claiming he was falsely accused over a plated mustard pot. He admitted knowing the previous witness, John Barsad, for seven or eight years, but claimed it was just a coincidence. He also claimed his only motive was true patriotism, hoping there were many other British citizens like him.

The crowd buzzed again, and the Attorney General called Mr. Jarvis Lorry.

"Mr. Jarvis Lorry, are you a clerk at Tellson’s Bank?"

"I am."

"On a Friday night in November 1775, did you travel from London to Dover by the mail coach?"

"I did."

"Were there other passengers?"

"Two."

"Did they get out along the way during the night?"

"Yes."

"Mr. Lorry, look at the prisoner. Was he one of those passengers?"

"I cannot say for sure."

"Does he resemble either of them?"

"They were so wrapped up, it was so dark, and we were all so quiet that I cannot tell."

"Mr. Lorry, looking at his size and height, is it possible he was one of them?"

"Yes, it is possible."

"You won't swear that he was not?"

"No."

"So he might have been one of them?"

"Yes, though both passengers were terrified of highwaymen, and the prisoner does not look like a fearful man."

"Have you ever seen someone pretend to be afraid, Mr. Lorry?"

"Yes, I have."

"Have you seen the prisoner before, to your certain knowledge?"

"Yes."

"When?"

"A few days later, when I was returning from France. The prisoner came on board the ship at Calais and traveled with me."

"What time did he come on board?"

"A little after midnight."

"In the middle of the night. Was he the only passenger to board at that late hour?"

"Yes, he was."

"Were you traveling alone, Mr. Lorry?"

"With two companions. A gentleman and a lady who are here today."

"Did you speak to the prisoner?"

"Hardly at all. The weather was bad, the crossing was rough, and I lay on a sofa the whole time."

"Miss Manette!" was called.

The young lady stood up. Her father rose with her, holding her hand.

"Miss Manette, look at the prisoner."

Facing her pity and beauty was much harder for the accused man than facing the rest of the courtroom. Standing near her, he seemed to lose his composure for a moment. His hand nervously moved the herbs on the desk as if arranging flowers, and his lips trembled as he tried to control his breathing. The crowd buzzed loudly again.

"Miss Manette, have you seen the prisoner before?"

"Yes, sir."

"Where?"

"On the ship we just discussed, on the same journey."

"Are you the young lady Mr. Lorry mentioned?"

"Oh, unfortunately, yes."

Her sad, compassionate tone was interrupted by the Judge, who said sharply, "Just answer the questions and do not make comments."

"Miss Manette, did you speak with the prisoner during the crossing?"

"Yes, sir."

"Describe the conversation."

In a quiet room, she began: "When the prisoner came on board, he noticed that my father," she looked at him lovingly, "was very tired and weak. I had made a bed for him on the deck, and I sat beside him. There were no other passengers except the four of us. The prisoner kindly advised me on how to shelter my father from the wind. He was very gentle and kind, and that is how we began to speak."

"Did he come on board alone?"

"No."

"How many were with him?"

"Two French gentlemen."

"Did they talk?"

"Yes, until the last moment when the French gentlemen had to return to shore in their boat."

"Did they pass papers like these lists?"

"They passed some papers, but I don't know what they were."

"Were they similar in size and shape?"

"Possibly, but I don't know. They stood near the cabin lamp because it was dim, whispering quietly, and I only saw that they were looking at papers."

"What did the prisoner say to you, Miss Manette?"

"He was very open with me, which was comforting in my difficult situation. I hope," she began to cry, "that I do not harm him with my testimony today."

The crowd buzzed again.

"Miss Manette, the prisoner surely understands that you are testifying because it is your legal duty, not because you want to. Please continue."

"He told me he was traveling on difficult, secret business that could get people into trouble, and that he was using a false name. He said this business would require him to travel between England and France frequently."

"Did he mention America?"

"He tried to explain the conflict to me, saying he thought England was wrong and foolish. He joked that George Washington might become as famous in history as King George the Third. But he said it laughingly, just to pass the time."

The crowd unconsciously imitated her anxious expression as she spoke. When the Judge wrote down her words, everyone watched him. When the Judge looked up to glare at her comment about George Washington, he saw a courtroom full of mirrored, worried faces.

The Attorney General then called her father, Doctor Manette.

"Doctor Manette, have you ever seen the prisoner before?"

"Once. He visited my lodgings in London about three years ago."

"Can you identify him from the ship, or speak to his conversation with your daughter?"

"No, I cannot do either."

"Is there a specific reason why you cannot?"

In a low voice, he answered, "Yes. I suffered a long imprisonment in my home country, without trial or charges."

"Were you recently released at the time of the voyage?"

"They tell me so."

"Do you have any memory of it?"

"None. My mind is a blank from my time in prison making shoes until I found myself living in London with my daughter. I do not remember how I got here."

The Attorney General sat down, and the Manettes did as well.

Then, a strange turn occurred. The prosecution called a witness to identify the prisoner as a man who was waiting at a hotel in a dockyard town to collect military secrets. The defense lawyer was cross-examining this witness with no success, when the lawyer who had been staring at the ceiling wrote something on a piece of paper, folded it, and tossed it to him.

Opening the paper, the defense lawyer looked closely at the prisoner, then asked the witness:

"You say again you are quite sure that it was the prisoner?"

The witness was positive.

"Did you ever see anyone who looked very much like the prisoner?"

The witness said not enough to mistake them.

"Look at my colleague here," the lawyer said, pointing to the man who had thrown the paper. "Now look at the prisoner. Are they not very alike?"

Despite the colleague's messy and careless appearance, they looked so similar that the witness and the entire courtroom were shocked. When the Judge allowed the colleague to take off his wig, the resemblance was even more striking. The Judge joked to the defense lawyer, Mr. Stryver, if they should try Mr. Carton (the colleague) for treason next. Mr. Stryver replied no, but asked the witness if he could still be so confident after seeing such a resemblance. This completely destroyed the witness's credibility and ruined that part of the prosecution's case.

By this time, Jerry Cruncher had chewed a lot of rust off his fingers watching the trial. He then listened as Mr. Stryver presented the defense, showing that the witness Barsad was a hired spy, a traitor, and a scoundrel. He argued that the servant Cly was his partner in crime, and that they had targeted the prisoner because his family business required him to travel to France. He explained that the prisoner could not reveal these family matters because they involved others. He showed that Miss Manette's testimony only described polite conversation, and that the joke about George Washington was harmless. He argued that the prosecution was trying to exploit public fear and prejudice, and that the case rested on untrustworthy witnesses. The Judge stopped Mr. Stryver when he criticized the state trials, but the point had been made.

After the defense called its witnesses, the Attorney General argued that the defense was wrong and the prisoner was guilty. Finally, the Judge summarized the case, shaping his words in a way that pointed toward a death sentence.

The jury then withdrew to make their decision, and the crowd buzzed once more.

Mr. Carton, who had been staring at the ceiling, did not move. His colleague, Mr. Stryver, gathered his papers and whispered with others, watching the jury. The Judge paced the bench nervously. Carton sat back with his torn gown half off, his messy wig crooked, his hands in his pockets, staring at the ceiling. His reckless attitude made him look very different from the prisoner, so the resemblance was less noticeable now. Jerry remarked to his neighbor that he bet Carton didn't get much legal work.

Yet Carton was paying closer attention than he seemed. When Miss Manette’s head fell onto her father’s chest, he was the first to notice, calling out: "Officer! Look to that young lady. Help her father take her out. She is going to faint!"

There was much sympathy for her and her father as they left. Questioning had clearly upset the doctor, reminding him of his prison years.

As the jury left, they announced they could not agree and needed to deliberate. The Judge was surprised but allowed them to retire under guard. As night fell, lamps were lit in the court, and it was rumored the jury would take a long time. The spectators left to get food, and the prisoner sat down in the back of the dock.

Mr. Lorry returned and called Jerry over.

"Jerry, get something to eat if you want, but stay close. Let me know as soon as the jury returns. I want you to take the verdict back to the bank immediately, as you are the fastest messenger."

Jerry thanked him, accepted a shilling, and bowed. Mr. Carton then approached Mr. Lorry.

"How is the young lady?"

"She is very distressed, but her father is comforting her, and she is glad to be out of the court."

"I will let the prisoner know. It wouldn't look good for a respectable banker like you to be seen speaking to him."

Mr. Lorry blushed, and Carton walked toward the bar to speak to Darnay. Jerry followed closely to listen.

"Mr. Darnay!"

The prisoner came forward.

"Miss Manette will be fine. You have seen the worst of her distress."

"I am deeply sorry to have caused it. Could you tell her so, with my thanks?"

"Yes, I will." Carton’s manner was careless, almost rude, as he leaned against the bar.

"Thank you," said Darnay.

"What do you expect, Mr. Darnay?" Carton asked.

"The worst."

"That is the safest thing to expect. But the jury taking this long is a good sign."

Jerry had to move along, so he heard no more, leaving the two men—so alike in face, yet so different in manner—standing side by side.

An hour and a half passed in the crowded hallways below. Jerry was dozing when a loud murmur and a rush of people heading back to the courtroom woke him up.

"Jerry! Jerry!" Mr. Lorry called at the door.

"Here, sir! It’s hard to get through, but I'm here!"

Mr. Lorry handed him a paper through the crowd. "Quick! Do you have it?"

"Yes, sir."

Written on the paper was the single word: "ACQUITTED."

"If you had sent the message 'Recalled to Life' again," Jerry muttered as he ran, "I would have understood it this time."

He ran out of the Old Bailey as the crowd poured into the street, the disappointed onlookers dispersing like flies in search of other prey.

CHAPTER XIX.
An Opinion

Exhausted by his anxious watch, Mr. Lorry fell asleep at his post. On the tenth morning of his suspense, he was startled by the sun shining into the room, where a heavy sleep had overtaken him when it was still dark.

He rubbed his eyes and woke up, but at first he doubted if he was actually awake. Looking into the Doctor’s room, he saw that the shoemaker’s bench and tools were put away, and the Doctor himself sat reading by the window. He was dressed in his usual morning clothes, and his face, though still very pale, looked calm and focused.

Even when he was sure he was awake, Mr. Lorry felt uncertain for a few moments whether the shoemaking had been a dream. His friend sat before him in his usual clothes and appearance, working as normal, with no sign that any change had occurred.

But this was only his first confusion. The answer was obvious: if the relapse hadn't happened, why was he, Jarvis Lorry, sleeping in his clothes on the sofa in the Doctor’s office?

Within a few minutes, Miss Pross stood whispering at his side. Her confirmation settled any remaining doubts. Mr. Lorry advised that they should wait until the usual breakfast hour and meet the Doctor as if nothing had happened. If he appeared normal, Mr. Lorry would then carefully ask the Doctor for his professional opinion on the case.

Miss Pross agreed, and they carried out the plan carefully. After dressing methodically, Mr. Lorry arrived for breakfast in his usual neat clothes. The Doctor was summoned normally and joined them.

As far as Mr. Lorry could tell through careful observation, the Doctor at first believed his daughter’s wedding had occurred only the day before. A casual comment about the date set him thinking, which clearly made him uneasy. Otherwise, he was so completely himself that Mr. Lorry decided to seek his advice.

Therefore, when breakfast was cleared away and they were left alone, Mr. Lorry said:

"My dear Manette, I want to ask your confidential opinion on a very strange case in which I am interested. It seems very strange to me, though it might be simple to you."

Looking at his hands, which were stained from his late work, the Doctor looked worried and listened closely. He had already looked at his hands several times.

"Doctor Manette," Mr. Lorry said, touching his arm, "the case concerns a very dear friend of mine. Please advise me for his sake, and especially for his daughter's sake."

"I understand," the Doctor said quietly. "A mental shock?"

"Yes!"

"Tell me everything," said the Doctor. "Spare no detail."

Mr. Lorry saw they understood each other, and continued:

"My dear Manette, it is the case of a severe mental shock that affected the sufferer's mind. He was overwhelmed by it for a long time, though he cannot count the years himself. He eventually recovered through a process he cannot explain. He became a highly intelligent man, capable of hard study and physical exertion, with a large store of knowledge. But unfortunately, he has had a slight relapse."

In a low voice, the Doctor asked, "How long did it last?"

"Nine days and nights."

"How did it show itself? I assume," looking at his hands again, "by returning to some old work associated with his past suffering?"

"Yes, exactly."

"Did you ever see him do that work originally?"

"Once."

"And during the relapse, was he in the same state as before?"

"Yes, in every way."

"You mentioned his daughter. Does she know about the relapse?"

"No. We have kept it from her, and hope to keep it a secret forever. Only I and one other trusted person know."

The Doctor squeezed his hand and murmured, "That was very kind and thoughtful." Mr. Lorry squeezed his hand in return, and they sat in silence.

"Now, my dear Manette," Mr. Lorry said gently, "I am only a businessman, and I don't understand these difficult matters. I need guidance, and there is no one I trust more than you. Tell me, how does such a relapse happen? Is there a danger of it happening again? Can we prevent it? How should we treat it? What can I do for my friend? I want to help him if I only knew how. If your knowledge and experience can guide me, I can do a lot; without guidance, I can do very little. Please explain it to me."

Doctor Manette sat thinking in the silence.

"I think it is likely," the Doctor said, breaking the silence with an effort, "that the relapse was not entirely unexpected by the patient."

"Was he afraid of it?" Mr. Lorry asked.

"Very much," the Doctor said with a shudder. "You cannot imagine how that fear weighs on a person, and how hard it is for him to speak of it."

"Would it help him," Mr. Lorry asked, "if he could bring himself to share that secret fear with someone when it is happening?"

"I think so. But as I said, it is almost impossible. In some cases, completely impossible."

"Now," Mr. Lorry said, laying his hand on the Doctor’s arm, "what caused this attack?"

"I believe," Doctor Manette said, "that there was a sudden and powerful return of the thoughts and memories that caused his original illness. Some distressing associations were vividly recalled. He probably feared those memories would return under certain circumstances—on a particular occasion. He tried to prepare himself in vain, and perhaps the effort to prepare made him more vulnerable."

"Would he remember what happened during the relapse?" Mr. Lorry asked hesitantly.

The Doctor looked around the room, shook his head, and answered quietly, "Not at all."

"And what about the future?" Mr. Lorry asked.

"As for the future," the Doctor said with more confidence, "I would have great hope. Since he recovered so quickly, I would have great hope. Having yielded to a long-feared pressure and recovered after the crisis passed, I would hope the worst is over."

"Well, that is comforting. I am grateful!" said Mr. Lorry.

"I am grateful!" the Doctor repeated reverently.

"There are two other points," Mr. Lorry said. "May I ask?"

"You cannot do your friend a better service," the Doctor said, offering his hand.

"First: he is a studious and energetic man, working hard on his experiments and studies. Does he do too much?"

"I think not. His mind may simply need constant occupation. That may be partly natural, and partly the result of his past suffering. The less his mind is busy with healthy things, the more it is in danger of turning to unhealthy thoughts. He may have realized this himself."

"You are sure he is not under too much strain?"

"Yes, I am sure of it. There has been a violent stress in one direction, and he needs a counterweight."

"Forgive me, as a practical businessman. If he _were_ overworked, would it trigger another relapse?"

"I don't think so. I believe only that one specific set of memories would trigger it. Only a sudden shock to that chord could cause it. After his recovery, I doubt that will happen again. I believe the circumstances likely to cause it are gone."

He spoke with the caution of a doctor who knew how delicate the mind is, but also with the confidence of a man who had survived it himself. Mr. Lorry pretended to be fully relieved, and approached his last point. He knew it was the hardest, but remembered his talk with Miss Pross and what he had seen.

"The work he returned to during this relapse," Mr. Lorry said, clearing his throat, "let us call it blacksmith’s work. Let us say, for example, that he had worked at a small forge in his prison years, and was found at his forge again. Isn't it a pity that he keeps that forge with him?"

The Doctor covered his forehead with his hand and tapped his foot nervously on the floor.

"He has always kept it near him," Mr. Lorry said. "Wouldn't it be better if he let it go?"

The Doctor, still covering his face, kept tapping his foot.

"Is it hard to advise me?" Mr. Lorry asked. "I know it is a difficult question. And yet..." He shook his head and stopped.

"You see," Doctor Manette said after an uneasy pause, "it is very hard to explain the inner workings of this man’s mind. He once longed for that work so much, and it helped relieve his mental pain by keeping his hands busy. Because it helped him survive his mental torture, he could never bear to put it out of reach. Even now, when he feels more confident in himself, the thought of needing that work and not finding it terrifies him, like a lost child."

He looked like a worried child himself as he looked at Mr. Lorry.

"But," Mr. Lorry said, "as a practical man who deals only with money and concrete things, wouldn't keeping the tools keep the memory alive? If the tools were gone, might not the fear go with it? Isn't keeping the forge a sign of doubt in his own recovery?"

Another silence followed.

"You must understand," the Doctor said tremulously, "it is such an old companion."

"I would not keep it," Mr. Lorry said, shaking his head. He grew firmer as he saw the Doctor's hesitation. "I would advise him to sacrifice it. I only want your approval. It does no good. Give me your approval for his daughter’s sake!"

It was clear a great struggle was going on inside the Doctor.

"In her name, then, let it be done; I approve. But do not take it while he is there. Remove it when he is away, so he misses his companion after he leaves."

Mr. Lorry agreed, and their talk ended. They spent the day in the country, and the Doctor seemed completely recovered. Over the next three days he remained well, and on the fourteenth day he left to join Lucie and her husband. Mr. Lorry had explained the excuse they used for his silence, and the Doctor wrote to Lucie accordingly, so she suspected nothing.

On the night after the Doctor left, Mr. Lorry went into his bedroom with a saw, hammer, chisel, and chopper, accompanied by Miss Pross holding a candle. Working in secret behind closed doors, Mr. Lorry chopped the shoemaker’s bench to pieces, while Miss Pross held the light like an accomplice to a murder. They burned the wood in the kitchen fireplace and buried the tools, shoes, and leather in the garden. The work of destruction and secrecy felt so wrong that Mr. Lorry and Miss Pross felt like criminals hiding a body.

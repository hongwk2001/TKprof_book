CHAPTER XV.
The Footsteps Die Out For Ever


Along the streets of Paris, the death carts rumbled, hollow and harsh. Six carts carried that day's victims to the Guillotine. All the greedy and hungry monsters ever imagined throughout history were combined into this one reality: the Guillotine. And yet, in all of France, with its rich variety of soil and weather, there was not a single leaf, root, or plant that grew under conditions more certain than those that had produced this horror. If you crush humanity out of shape again under the same heavy hammers of abuse, it will twist itself into the same tortured shapes. If you sow the same seeds of greedy lawlessness and oppression again, it will surely yield the same bitter fruit.

Six death carts rolled down the streets. Turn them back to what they used to be, powerful magician Time, and they would be seen as the carriages of absolute kings, the coaches of feudal lords, the dressing tables of greedy women, the churches turned into dens of thieves instead of houses of God, and the shacks of millions of starving peasants! But the great magician Time, who carries out the Creator's plan, never reverses his transformations. “If you have been changed into this shape by the will of God,” say the wise men to the cursed in the old Arabian stories, “then stay that way! But if you wear this form because of a passing magic spell, then return to your old self!” Changeless and hopeless, the death carts rolled along.

As the dark wheels of the six carts turned, they seemed to plow a long, crooked path through the crowds in the streets. Waves of faces were pushed to this side and that, and the plows moved steadily forward. The local residents were so used to this sight that many windows were empty, and in others, people did not even stop their work while they watched the faces in the carts. Here and there, a resident had guests over to watch the show; he would point his finger like a tour guide or museum guide, pointing out this cart and that, explaining who had sat there yesterday and who had been there the day before.

Of the people riding in the carts, some watched the crowds and the roadside with a blank stare, while others showed a final interest in the ways of life and humanity. Some sat with slumped heads, lost in silent despair; others were so concerned with how they looked that they cast dramatic glances at the crowd, like actors in theaters or portraits. Several closed their eyes and thought, trying to collect their wandering thoughts. Only one, a miserable man who looked insane, was so broken and crazed by horror that he sang and tried to dance. Not a single person among them begged for the crowd's pity by look or gesture.

A guard of several horsemen rode alongside the carts, and people in the crowd often looked up to ask them questions. It seemed to be always the same question, because it was always followed by a rush of people toward the third cart. The horsemen next to that cart frequently pointed out one man inside with their swords. The crowd's main curiosity was to see which one he was; he stood at the back of the cart with his head bent down, talking to a young girl who sat on the edge of the cart and held his hand. He showed no curiosity or interest in the scene around him, keeping his focus entirely on the girl. Here and there along the long Street of Saint-Honoré, people shouted curses at him. If they affected him at all, it was only to bring a quiet smile to his face, as he shook his hair a little looser. He could not easily touch his face, as his arms were tied.

On the steps of a church, waiting for the carts to arrive, stood Barsad the spy, the prison sheep. He looked into the first cart: Darnay was not there. He looked into the second: not there. He was just starting to ask himself, “Did he betray me?” when his face cleared as he looked into the third cart.

“Which one is Evrémonde?” asked a man behind him.

“That one. At the back there.”

“With his hand in the girl's?”

“Yes.”

The man shouted, “Down with Evrémonde! To the Guillotine with all aristocrats! Down with Evrémonde!”

“Shh, quiet!” the spy begged him nervously.

“And why not, citizen?”

“He is going to pay his price; it will be paid in five minutes. Let him be in peace.”

But the man kept shouting, “Down with Evrémonde!” and the face of the prisoner turned toward him for a second. Carton saw the spy, looked closely at him, and then went on his way.

The clocks were striking three, and the path plowed through the crowd turned into the execution square, coming to an end. The crowds that had been pushed aside now closed in behind the last cart as it passed, for everyone was following them to the Guillotine. In front of the stage, sitting in chairs as if they were in a park for entertainment, were a group of women, busy with their knitting. Standing on one of the front chairs was The Vengeance, looking around for her friend.

“Thérèse!” she cried in her shrill voice. “Who has seen her? Thérèse Defarge!”

“She has never missed it before,” said one of the knitting women.

“No, and she won't miss it now,” cried The Vengeance crossly. “Thérèse!”

“Shout louder,” the woman suggested.

Yes! Shout louder, Vengeance, much louder, and still she will barely hear you. Louder yet, Vengeance, with a curse or two added, and still it will not bring her. Send other women up and down to look for her, thinking she is lingering somewhere; and yet, even though these messengers have done terrifying deeds, it is doubtful if they would dare go far enough to find where she lies!

“Bad luck!” cried The Vengeance, stamping her foot on the chair. “And here are the carts! Evrémonde will be executed in a second, and she isn't here! Look at her knitting in my hand, and her empty chair waiting for her. I could cry with anger and disappointment!”

As The Vengeance got down from the chair, the carts began to empty their passengers. The executioners of Saint Guillotine were ready. Crash!—A head was held up, and the knitting women, who barely looked up at the person who could think and speak just a moment ago, counted One.

The second cart emptied and moved on; the third pulled up. Crash!—And the knitting women, never slowing down or pausing in their work, counted Two.

The man they thought was Evrémonde stepped down, and the seamstress was helped out right after him. He had not let go of her patient hand, holding it just as he had promised. He gently stood her with her back to the crashing blade that kept sliding up and falling, and she looked into his face and thanked him.

“If it weren't for you, dear stranger, I would not be so calm, for I am naturally a poor, weak thing, faint of heart. I wouldn't have been able to lift my thoughts to God, who died so that we might have hope and comfort here today. I believe you were sent to me by Heaven.”

“Or you to me,” said Sydney Carton. “Keep your eyes on me, dear child, and look at nothing else.”

“I don't care about anything while I hold your hand. I won't care about anything when I let it go, if they are quick.”

“They will be quick. Do not fear!”

The two stood in the quickly shrinking crowd of victims, but they spoke as if they were completely alone. Eye-to-eye, voice-to-voice, hand-in-hand, heart-to-heart, these two children of God, who were otherwise so different and lived such separate lives, had met on this dark path to go home together and rest.

“Brave and generous friend, will you let me ask you one last question? I am very uneducated, and it worries me—just a little.”

“Tell me what it is.”

“I have a cousin, my only relative, who is an orphan like me, and I love her very dearly. She is five years younger than I am, and she lives at a farm in the south. Poverty separated us, and she knows nothing of my fate—because I cannot write, and even if I could, how could I tell her! It is better this way.”

“Yes, yes; it is better this way.”

“What I was thinking as we rode along, and what I am still thinking now as I look into your kind, strong face which gives me so much comfort, is this: if the Republic really does good for the poor, and they become less hungry and suffer less, she might live a long time. She might even live to be old.”

“What then, my gentle sister?”

“Do you think,” her patient eyes, which showed so much strength, filled with tears, and her lips trembled slightly, “that it will seem like a long time to me while I wait for her in heaven, where I trust both you and I will find mercy and peace?”

“It cannot feel long, my child. There is no time there, and no trouble there.”

“You comfort me so much! I am so uneducated. Should I kiss you now? Is the moment here?”

“Yes.”

She kissed his lips, and he kissed hers; they solemnly blessed each other. Her thin hand did not shake when he let go of it; there was nothing but a sweet, bright courage on her patient face. She went onto the scaffold before him—and was gone. The knitting women counted Twenty-Two.

“I am the resurrection and the life, says the Lord. He who believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live; and whoever lives and believes in me shall never die.”

The murmur of many voices, the looking up of many faces, the pushing of many feet at the edge of the crowd, swelling forward like a great wave of water, all vanished. Twenty-Three.

They said of him in the city that night that his was the most peaceful face ever seen there. Many added that he looked noble and like a prophet.

One of the most famous victims of the Guillotine—a woman—had asked at the foot of the same platform not long before to be allowed to write down the thoughts that inspired her. If Carton had spoken his thoughts aloud, and if they were prophetic, they would have been these:

“I see Barsad, Cly, Defarge, The Vengeance, the juror, and the judge, along with long lines of the new oppressors who have taken the place of the old ones, dying by this same machine of justice before it stops being used. I see a beautiful city and a brilliant nation rising from this dark pit. Through long years of struggles to be truly free, in their victories and defeats, I see the evil of this time and the past time that caused it gradually paying for itself and fading away.

“I see the lives for which I give my own life—peaceful, useful, prosperous, and happy in that England which I will never see again. I see Lucie with a baby on her chest who carries my name. I see her father, old and bent but with his mind restored, peacefully working as a faithful doctor to heal others. I see the good old man, who was their friend for so long, passing away peacefully in ten years' time and leaving everything he owns to them.

“I see that I hold a sacred place in their hearts, and in the hearts of their descendants for generations to come. I see Lucie as an old woman, crying for me on the anniversary of this day. I see her and her husband, their lives completed, lying side-by-side in their graves, and I know that they honored each other no more than they honored and loved me in their souls.

“I see that child who lay on her chest and carried my name growing up to be a man, succeeding in the same legal career that was once mine. I see him doing so well that my name is made famous and respected by his achievements. I see the mistakes and stains I threw on my name fade away. I see him, as a respected judge, bringing a boy who carries my name—a boy with a forehead I recognize and golden hair—to this very spot, which will then be beautiful and clean, with no trace of today's horror. And I hear him tell the child my story in a gentle, emotional voice.

“It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done; it is a far, far better rest that I go to than I have ever known.”

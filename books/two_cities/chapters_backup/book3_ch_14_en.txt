CHAPTER XIV.
The Knitting Done


At the exact same time the fifty-two prisoners waited for their deaths, Madame Defarge held a dark meeting with The Vengeance and Jacques Three of the Revolutionary Jury. She did not meet with these helpers in her wine-shop, but in the shed of the wood-sawyer, who used to be a road repairer. The wood-sawyer himself did not take part in the meeting. He stood a short distance away, like a servant waiting to be told when to speak or give an opinion.

“But our Defarge,” said Jacques Three, “is definitely a good Republican, isn't he?”

“There is no better in all of France,” the talkative Vengeance declared in her high, squeaky voice.

“Quiet, little Vengeance,” said Madame Defarge, putting her hand over her helper's mouth with a slight frown. “Listen to me speak. My husband, fellow citizen, is a good Republican and a brave man. He has served the Republic well and has its trust. But my husband has his weaknesses, and he is weak enough to feel pity for this Doctor.”

“That is a great pity,” croaked Jacques Three, shaking his head doubtfully while biting his twitching fingers. “That is not like a good citizen; it is very regrettable.”

“Listen,” Madame Defarge said, “I don't care about this Doctor at all. He can keep his head or lose it for all I care; it makes no difference to me. But the Evrémonde family must be wiped out, and the wife and child must follow the husband and father to the Guillotine.”

“She has a beautiful head for it,” croaked Jacques Three. “I have seen blue eyes and golden hair on the platform before, and they looked lovely when Samson the executioner held them up.” Monster that he was, he spoke like a man describing a delicious meal.

Madame Defarge looked down, thinking for a moment.

“The child also,” Jacques Three noted with calm enjoyment, “has golden hair and blue eyes. And we rarely have children on the platform. It is a pretty sight!”

“In short,” Madame Defarge said, snapping out of her thoughts, “I cannot trust my husband in this matter. Since last night, I feel that I cannot tell him the details of my plans. I also know that if I wait, he might warn them, and they might escape.”

“That must never happen,” croaked Jacques Three. “No one must escape. We don't have half enough executions as it is. We should have a hundred and twenty a day.”

“In a word,” Madame Defarge continued, “my husband does not have my personal reason for wanting to destroy this family completely, and I don't have his reason for feeling sympathy for this Doctor. Therefore, I must act on my own. Come here, little citizen.”

The wood-sawyer, who feared her with a deadly terror, stepped forward, touching his red cap respectfully.

“About those signals, little citizen,” Madame Defarge said sternly, “the ones she made to the prisoners; are you ready to testify about them today?”

“Yes, yes, why not!” cried the sawyer. “Every day, in all kinds of weather, from two to four o'clock, she was always signaling—sometimes with the child, sometimes without. I know what I know. I saw it with my own eyes.”

He waved his hands around as he spoke, pretending to copy the signals that he had never actually seen.

“Clearly plotting,” said Jacques Three. “It is obvious!”

“Are you sure about the Jury?” Madame Defarge asked, looking at him with a dark smile.

“Trust the patriotic Jury, dear citizenness. I will answer for my fellow jurors.”

“Now let me think,” said Madame Defarge, pondering again. “One more thing! Can I spare this Doctor for my husband's sake? I don't care either way. Can I spare him?”

“He would count as one more head,” Jacques Three noted in a low voice. “We really don't have enough heads; it would be a pity to lose him, I think.”

“He was signaling with her when I saw her,” Madame Defarge argued. “I cannot accuse one without the other. I must not stay silent and trust this case completely to this little citizen here. After all, I am a very good witness.”

The Vengeance and Jacques Three competed with each other to praise her, declaring she was the most wonderful and marvelous witness. The wood-sawyer, not wanting to be left out, declared she was a witness sent from heaven.

“He must take his chances,” said Madame Defarge. “No, I cannot spare him! You are busy at three o'clock; you are going to watch today's executions—aren't you?”

The question was asked of the wood-sawyer, who quickly answered yes. He took the opportunity to add that he was the most passionate of Republicans, and that he would be the most miserable man in France if anything stopped him from enjoying his afternoon pipe while watching the Guillotine. He was so dramatic about it that one might suspect—and Madame Defarge probably did, looking at him with scorn—that he lived in constant fear for his own head every hour of the day.

“I will be at the same place,” Madame Defarge said. “After it is over—let's say at eight o'clock tonight—come to my house in Saint Antoine, and we will report these people to my revolutionary committee.”

The wood-sawyer said he would be proud and honored to help the citizenness. When she stared at him, he became embarrassed. He looked away like a frightened dog, backed up into his pile of wood, and hid his confusion by working on his saw handle.

Madame Defarge beckoned the juror and The Vengeance closer to the door, explaining the rest of her plan:

“She will be at home now, waiting for the moment of his death. She will be crying and grieving. She will be in a state of mind to insult the justice of the Republic. She will be full of sympathy for its enemies. I will go visit her.”

“What a wonderful woman! What an amazing woman!” Jacques Three cried in delight. “Ah, my darling!” cried The Vengeance, hugging her.

“Take my knitting,” said Madame Defarge, putting it in her assistant's hands, “and have it ready for me in my usual seat. Save my usual chair. Go straight there, because there will probably be a larger crowd than usual today.”

“I happily obey the orders of my leader,” said The Vengeance quickly, kissing her cheek. “You won't be late?”

“I will be there before it starts.”

“And before the death carts arrive. Be sure you are there, my dear,” The Vengeance called after her, as Madame Defarge had already turned into the street, “before the carts arrive!”

Madame Defarge waved her hand slightly to show she heard and could be trusted to arrive on time. She walked through the mud and around the corner of the prison wall. The Vengeance and the juror watched her go, highly admiring her strong figure and her great revolutionary spirit.

There were many women in those days who had been made cruel by the hard times, but none was more feared than this ruthless woman walking down the streets. Strong, fearless, sharp-minded, and determined, she had a kind of beauty that showed her hatred and forced others to recognize her strength. The troubled times would have made her a leader under any circumstances. But since she had been filled since childhood with a deep sense of wrong and a hatred for the rich class, this opportunity had turned her into a tigress. She was absolutely without pity. If she had ever possessed any kindness, it had completely vanished.

It meant nothing to her that an innocent man was going to die for the sins of his ancestors; she did not see him, but only his family name. It meant nothing to her that his wife would be made a widow and his daughter an orphan; that was not enough punishment, because they were her natural enemies and her prey, and they had no right to live. Begging her for mercy was useless because she felt no pity, not even for herself. If she had been killed in the streets during the many fights she had been in, she would not have felt sorry for herself. If she were sentenced to die tomorrow, she would go to the Guillotine without any soft feelings, only a fierce desire to swap places with the judge who sent her there.

Madame Defarge carried this hard heart under her rough dress. Worn carelessly, it was a nice enough outfit in a strange way, and her dark hair looked rich under her coarse red cap. Hidden in her shirt was a loaded pistol. Hidden at her waist was a sharp dagger. Armed like this, walking with the confident step of a strong character and the easy movement of a woman who had walked barefoot on the beach as a girl, Madame Defarge went down the streets.

When the journey of the escape carriage—which was currently waiting for the family to get in—had been planned the night before, Mr. Lorry had worried about how to include Miss Pross. It was not just about avoiding an overloaded carriage, but it was crucial to minimize the time spent at checking stations, since their survival might depend on saving just a few seconds. Finally, after careful thought, he suggested that Miss Pross and Jerry Cruncher, who were free to leave the city legally, should leave at three o'clock in a small, fast light carriage. Carrying no luggage, they would quickly catch up to the escape coach. They could then drive ahead of it, ordering fresh horses in advance at each station to speed up the journey during the dangerous hours of the night.

Seeing that this plan would really help in the emergency, Miss Pross agreed happily. She and Jerry had watched the carriage leave and knew who Solomon was carrying. They had spent ten agonizing minutes waiting in suspense, and were now finishing their plans to follow the carriage, even as Madame Defarge walked through the streets, drawing closer and closer to the apartment where they were talking.

“Now, what do you think, Mr. Cruncher?” said Miss Pross, who was so nervous she could barely speak, stand, or move. “What do you think about us not leaving from this courtyard? Since one carriage has already left this place today, another might make people suspicious.”

“My opinion, miss,” Mr. Cruncher replied, “is that you're right. And I will stand by you, right or wrong.”

“I am so distracted with fear and hope for our dear family,” said Miss Pross, crying wildly, “that I cannot think of any plan. Are _you_ able to make a plan, my dear, good Mr. Cruncher?”

“About my future way of life, miss,” Mr. Cruncher replied, “I hope so. But as for using this brain of mine right now, I don't think I can. Would you do me a favor, miss, and listen to two promises and vows I want to make in this crisis?”

“Oh, for goodness' sake!” cried Miss Pross, still crying. “Make them at once and get them out of the way, like a good man.”

“First,” said Mr. Cruncher, shaking all over and looking pale and serious, “once those poor people are safely out of this, I will never dig up graves again, never again!”

“I am quite sure, Mr. Cruncher,” Miss Pross replied, “that you will never do it again, whatever it is, and I ask you not to mention the details of what it is.”

“No, miss,” Jerry replied, “I won't name it. Second: once those poor people are safe, I will never again yell at Mrs. Cruncher for praying, never again!”

“Whatever religious arrangement that is,” said Miss Pross, trying to dry her eyes and calm down, “I'm sure it's best that Mrs. Cruncher manages it herself. Oh, my poor darlings!”

“I will even go so far as to say, miss,” Mr. Cruncher continued, speaking like a preacher in a church, “and let my words be passed on to Mrs. Cruncher by you—that my opinions about praying have completely changed, and I hope with all my heart that Mrs. Cruncher is praying right now.”

“There, there! I hope she is, my dear man,” cried the worried Miss Pross, “and I hope it brings her what she hopes for.”

“God forbid,” Mr. Cruncher went on, even more slowly and solemnly, “that anything I have said or done in the past should hurt my wishes for those poor people now! God forbid that we shouldn't all pray—if it helped—to get them out of this terrible danger! God forbid, miss! That is what I say: God forbid it!” This was Mr. Cruncher's final statement after trying hard to find a better way to say it.

And still Madame Defarge walked down the streets, coming closer and closer.

“If we ever get back to England,” Miss Pross said, “you can count on me to tell Mrs. Cruncher as much of this as I can remember. In any case, I will testify that you were completely sincere during this terrible time. Now, please let us think! My dear Mr. Cruncher, let us think!”

Still, Madame Defarge walked down the streets, coming closer and closer.

“If you went ahead,” Miss Pross said, “and stopped the carriage and horses from coming here, and waited for me somewhere, wouldn't that be best?”

Mr. Cruncher agreed that it might be best.

“Where could you wait for me?” asked Miss Pross.

Mr. Cruncher was so confused that the only place he could think of was Temple Bar in London. But Temple Bar was hundreds of miles away, and Madame Defarge was getting very close indeed.

“By the cathedral door,” said Miss Pross. “Would it be out of the way to pick me up near the big cathedral door between the two towers?”

“No, miss,” Mr. Cruncher answered.

“Then, like the good man you are,” said Miss Pross, “go straight to the carriage station and change the plan.”

“I don't know,” said Mr. Cruncher, shaking his head. “I'm not sure about leaving you alone. We don't know what might happen.”

“God knows we don't,” Miss Pross replied, “but don't worry about me. Pick me up at the cathedral at three o'clock, or as close to it as you can. I am sure it is better than leaving from this apartment. I'm certain of it. Go on, and God bless you, Mr. Cruncher! Don't think about me—think about the lives that depend on us!”

This speech, along with Miss Pross clutching his hands in desperate begging, convinced Mr. Cruncher. With a couple of nods, he immediately went out to change the arrangements, leaving her by herself to follow as planned.

Having come up with a safety plan that was now being carried out was a great relief to Miss Pross. The need to fix her appearance so she wouldn't look suspicious in the streets was another distraction. She looked at her watch; it was twenty minutes past two. She had no time to lose and had to get ready immediately.

Terrified in the quiet of the empty rooms, and imagining faces peeping out from behind every open door, Miss Pross got a bowl of cold water and began washing her red, swollen eyes. Afraid of being snuck up on, she couldn't stand having her eyes closed for even a second by the water, constantly stopping to look around and make sure no one was watching her. During one of these pauses, she jumped back and screamed, for she saw a figure standing in the room.

The bowl fell to the floor and shattered, and the water flowed to the feet of Madame Defarge. By strange, harsh paths and through much spilled blood, those feet had traveled to meet that water.

Madame Defarge looked at her coldly and said, “The wife of Evrémonde; where is she?”

It flashed in Miss Pross's mind that the open doors would show they had fled. Her first reaction was to close them. There were four doors in the room, and she shut them all. Then she stood directly in front of the door to the bedroom Lucie had used.

Madame Defarge's dark eyes followed her quick movements and stared at her when she stopped. Miss Pross was not a beautiful woman; age had not softened her sharp, stern looks. But she was just as determined in her own way, and she stared right back at Madame Defarge, sizing her up completely.

“You look like you could be the devil's wife,” Miss Pross muttered under her breath. “But you won't defeat me. I am an Englishwoman.”

Madame Defarge looked at her with scorn, but both women realized they were trapped in a standoff. She saw a tough, wiry woman in front of her, just as Mr. Lorry had years ago. She knew very well that Miss Pross was the family's loyal friend; Miss Pross knew very well that Madame Defarge was the family's evil enemy.

“On my way over there,” Madame Defarge said, pointing slightly toward the Guillotine, “where they are saving my chair and knitting, I came to say goodbye to her. I want to see her.”

“I know you mean to do harm,” said Miss Pross, “and you can be sure I will stand my ground against you.”

Each spoke in her own language, and neither understood the other's words. Both were highly alert, trying to figure out from expressions and gestures what the foreign words meant.

“It won't help her to hide from me now,” Madame Defarge said. “Good patriots will know what that hiding means. Let me see her. Go tell her I want to see her. Do you hear?”

“If your eyes were tools to take apart beds, and I was an English wooden bed, you wouldn't get a single splinter out of me. No, you wicked foreign woman, I am a match for you.”

Madame Defarge could not follow these English expressions, but she understood enough to see that Miss Pross was refusing to obey her.

“Foolish, stubborn woman!” Madame Defarge said, frowning. “I won't take no for an answer. I demand to see her. Either tell her I am here, or get away from the door and let me in!” She waved her right arm angrily toward the door.

“I never thought I'd want to understand your gibberish language, but I'd give everything I own, except the clothes on my back, to know if you suspect the truth or any part of it.”

Neither of them broke eye contact for a second. Madame Defarge had not moved since entering the room, but now she took a step forward.

“I am a British woman,” said Miss Pross. “I am desperate. I don't care a penny about my own life. I know that the longer I keep you here, the more time my darling Lucie has to get away. I won't leave a single strand of hair on your head if you touch me!”

Miss Pross said this, shaking her head and flashing her eyes with every quick sentence, speaking in rapid breaths. This was the same Miss Pross who had never hit anyone in her life.

But her bravery was so emotional that tears came to her eyes anyway. Madame Defarge did not understand this kind of courage and mistook it for weakness. “Ha, ha!” she laughed. “You poor wretch! You are nothing! I will speak to the Doctor.” Then she raised her voice and shouted, “Citizen Doctor! Wife of Evrémonde! Child of Evrémonde! Anyone except this miserable fool, answer Citizenness Defarge!”

Perhaps the silence that followed, or something in Miss Pross's face, or a sudden feeling, whispered to Madame Defarge that they were already gone. She quickly opened three of the doors and looked inside.

“These rooms are a mess; someone packed in a hurry. There are scraps left on the floor. There is no one in that room behind you! Let me look.”

“Never!” said Miss Pross, who understood the demand perfectly, just as Madame Defarge understood her refusal.

“If they are not in that room, they have fled and can be chased and brought back,” Madame Defarge said to herself.

“As long as you don't know if they are in that room, you won't know what to do,” Miss Pross thought to herself. “And I will make sure you never find out. Whether you know it or not, you are not leaving this apartment while I can hold you here.”

“I have been fighting in the streets since the start, and nothing has stopped me. I will tear you to pieces to get you away from that door,” Madame Defarge said.

“We are alone at the top of a tall house in a quiet courtyard where no one will hear us. I pray for the physical strength to keep you here. Every minute you waste is worth a fortune to my darling,” Miss Pross thought.

Madame Defarge lunged for the door. In that instant, Miss Pross grabbed her around the waist with both arms and held her tight. It was useless for Madame Defarge to struggle and hit her. Miss Pross, with the fierce strength of love—which is always stronger than hate—held her tightly, even lifting her off the floor as they wrestled. Madame Defarge's hands scratched and beat at her face; but Miss Pross, keeping her head down, held onto her waist like a drowning person clutching for life.

Soon, Madame Defarge stopped hitting and reached down to her waist. “It is under my arm,” Miss Pross said in a muffled voice. “You won't pull that dagger out. I am stronger than you, thank Heaven. I will hold you until one of us faints or dies!”

Madame Defarge's hands went to her own shirt. Miss Pross looked up, saw the pistol, and struck at it. There was a flash and a loud crash, and Miss Pross stood alone, blinded by smoke.

This all happened in a single second. As the smoke cleared, leaving a terrifying silence, the smoke drifted out the window, like the soul of the angry woman whose dead body now lay on the floor.

In her initial panic and horror, Miss Pross ran past the body, keeping as far away as possible, and headed down the stairs to call for help. Fortunately, she realized what would happen if she did that, checked herself, and went back up. It was terrifying to enter the room again, but she did, walking close enough to get her hat and coat. She put these on out on the staircase, locking the door behind her and taking the key. She sat on the stairs for a few moments to catch her breath and cry, then got up and hurried away.

Luckily she had a veil on her hat, or she would have been stopped in the streets immediately. Luckily, too, her unusual appearance made her injuries less obvious than they would be on anyone else. She needed both advantages, because deep finger marks were bruised onto her face, her hair was torn, and her dress—which she had tried to fix with shaking hands—was ripped and messy.

As she crossed the bridge, she threw the door key into the river. She arrived at the cathedral a few minutes before Jerry and waited there, worrying: what if the key was caught in a fishing net? What if it was identified? What if the apartment door was opened and the body discovered? What if she was stopped at the city gates, sent to prison, and charged with murder? In the middle of these panicked thoughts, Jerry arrived with the carriage, picked her up, and drove away.

“Is there any noise in the streets?” she asked him.

“Just the usual noises,” Mr. Cruncher replied, looking surprised by her question and her messy appearance.

“I can't hear you,” said Miss Pross. “What did you say?” It was useless for Mr. Cruncher to repeat himself; Miss Pross could not hear a word. “So I'll just nod my head,” Mr. Cruncher thought in surprise, “at least she will see that.” And she did.

“Is there any noise in the streets now?” Miss Pross asked again soon.

Mr. Cruncher nodded again.

“I don't hear anything.”

“Deaf in just an hour?” Mr. Cruncher thought, deeply worried. “What happened to her?”

“I feel,” said Miss Pross, “as if there was a flash and a crash, and that crash was the very last thing I will ever hear in this life.”

“She is in a very strange state!” Mr. Cruncher said, becoming more worried. “What did she drink to keep her courage up? Listen! There is the rumble of those terrible carts! Surely you can hear that, miss?”

“I can hear nothing,” said Miss Pross, seeing him speak. “Oh, my good man, first there was a great crash, and then a great silence. That silence seems to be permanent, never to be broken again as long as I live.”

“If she can't hear the rumble of those death carts, which are very close to their destination now,” Mr. Cruncher said, looking over his shoulder, “I don't think she will ever hear anything in this world again.”

And indeed, she never did.

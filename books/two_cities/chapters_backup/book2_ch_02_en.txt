CHAPTER II.
A Sight

"You know the Old Bailey courthouse well, no doubt?" said one of the oldest clerks to Jerry the messenger.

"Yes, sir," returned Jerry in a stubborn voice. "I _do_ know the Bailey."

"Just so. And you know Mr. Lorry."

"I know Mr. Lorry, sir, much better than I know the Bailey. Much better," said Jerry, sounding like a reluctant witness in court, "than I, as an honest tradesman, wish to know the Bailey."

"Very well. Find the door where the witnesses go in, and show the doorkeeper this note for Mr. Lorry. He will then let you in."

"Into the courtroom, sir?"

"Into the courtroom."

Mr. Cruncher’s eyes seemed to get a little closer together, as if asking each other, "What do you think of this?"

"Am I to wait in the court, sir?" he asked, after considering the idea.

"I am going to tell you. The doorkeeper will pass the note to Mr. Lorry. You must make some gesture to attract Mr. Lorry’s attention and show him where you are standing. Then, your job is simply to stay there until he needs you."

"Is that all, sir?"

"That’s all. He wants to have a messenger nearby. This note is just to let him know you have arrived."

As the old clerk slowly folded and addressed the note, Mr. Cruncher watched him in silence until he began using the blotting paper to dry the ink. Then Jerry remarked:

"I suppose they’ll be trying forgery cases this morning?"

"Treason!"

"That means drawing and quartering," said Jerry. "Barbarous!"

"It is the law," remarked the old clerk, turning his spectacles toward him in surprise. "It is the law."

"I think it’s harsh for the law to ruin a man's body like that. It’s hard enough to kill him, but it’s very hard to slice him up, sir."

"Not at all," replied the old clerk. "Speak well of the law. Take care of your own health, my friend, and leave the law to take care of itself. That is my advice to you."

"It’s the damp weather, sir, that settles in my chest and voice," said Jerry. "You can imagine what a damp way of earning a living mine is."

"Well, well," said the old clerk. "We all have our different ways of making a living. Some of us have damp ways, and some have dry ways. Here is the letter. Off you go."

Jerry took the letter. Muttering to himself with far less respect than he showed on the outside, "You are a withered old man yourself," he bowed, told his son where he was going as he walked past him, and went on his way.

In those days, executions were carried out at Tyburn, so the street outside Newgate Prison had not yet gained the infamous reputation it would have later. However, the prison itself was a filthy, corrupt place where all kinds of vice and crime were practiced and where terrible diseases bred. These illnesses often came into the courtroom with the prisoners, sometimes spreading directly to the Lord Chief Justice himself and killing him. More than once, a judge wearing the black execution cap had pronounced his own death sentence along with the prisoner's, dying even before the condemned man did. Otherwise, the Old Bailey was famous as a deadly departure point from which pale travelers set out in carts and carriages for a violent journey into the next world, traveling two and a half miles through public streets, to the shame of any good citizens who watched. Such is the power of custom, showing how important it is to establish good customs in the first place. The court was also famous for the pillory, a punishment where no one could predict how much harm the crowd might do; for the whipping post, another traditional institution; and for the practice of paying reward money for catching criminals, which systematically led to terrible crimes committed purely for greed. Overall, the Old Bailey at that time was a prime example of the lazy saying, "Whatever is, is right"—an idea that would be simple and final, if it didn't also mean that nothing in the past had ever been wrong.

Making his way through the dirty crowd gathered around this unpleasant scene, with the skill of a man used to moving quietly, the messenger found the door he was looking for and passed his note through a small slot. In those days, people paid to watch trials at the Old Bailey just as they paid to watch the patients at Bedlam asylum—except the trials cost much more. Therefore, all the doors to the Old Bailey were heavily guarded, except for the doors the criminals entered through, which were always left wide open.

After some delay and discussion, the door opened slightly, allowing Mr. Jerry Cruncher to squeeze himself into the courtroom.

"What's happening?" he whispered to the man standing next to him.

"Nothing yet."

"What case is coming up?"

"The treason case."

"The drawing and quartering one, right?"

"Yes!" the man replied with excitement. "He’ll be dragged on a wooden frame to be half-hanged, then cut down and sliced up while he's still alive. His insides will be pulled out and burned before his eyes, then his head will be chopped off and his body cut into four pieces. That’s the sentence."

"If he’s found guilty, you mean?" Jerry added, to clarify.

"Oh, they’ll find him guilty," said the other. "Don't you worry about that."

Mr. Cruncher’s attention was then drawn to the doorkeeper, whom he saw walking toward Mr. Lorry with the note. Mr. Lorry sat at a table among the lawyers in wigs, not far from the prisoner’s defense lawyer, who had a large stack of papers in front of him. Nearly opposite sat another lawyer in a wig, with his hands in his pockets, who seemed entirely focused on staring at the courtroom ceiling. After some coughing, rubbing his chin, and waving, Jerry finally got the attention of Mr. Lorry, who had stood up to look for him. Mr. Lorry nodded quietly and sat back down.

"What does _he_ have to do with the case?" asked the man Jerry had been talking to.

"I have no idea," said Jerry.

"What do _you_ have to do with it, then, if I may ask?"

"I don't know that either," said Jerry.

The entrance of the Judge and the general movement of the crowd settling into their places stopped their conversation. Soon, the prisoner's dock became the center of attention. Two guards who had been standing there stepped out, and the prisoner was brought in and placed at the bar.

Everyone in the courtroom, except the lawyer looking at the ceiling, stared at him. The breath of the crowd seemed to rush toward him like a wave, a wind, or a fire. Eager faces leaned around pillars and corners to see him; spectators in the back rows stood up so they wouldn't miss a single detail; people on the floor put their hands on the shoulders of those in front of them to help themselves get a better view, standing on tiptoe and climbing onto ledges. Standing out among them, looking like a piece of the spiked wall of Newgate Prison, Jerry stood, breathing out the smell of the beer he had drunk on the way, adding to the thick, damp air of the crowded room that misted up the large windows behind the prisoner.

The target of all this attention was a young man of about twenty-five, tall, well-built, with sunburnt skin and dark eyes. He looked like a young gentleman. He was dressed plainly in black or dark gray, and his long, dark hair was tied back with a ribbon, more to keep it out of the way than for style. Just as strong emotions show through any clothing, the paleness caused by his dangerous situation showed through his sunburned cheeks, proving that his inner feelings were stronger than the sun. Otherwise, he was quite calm, bowed to the Judge, and stood quietly.

The interest with which the crowd stared and breathed on this man was not a noble human feeling. If he had been facing a less terrible sentence, or if there had been any chance he would be spared its brutal details, he would have been far less fascinating to them. The body that was doomed to be so horribly mutilated was the main attraction; the human being about to be butchered was the source of the excitement. Whatever excuses the spectators made for their interest, at its root, it was a savage, monstrous curiosity.

"Silence in court!" was called. Charles Darnay had pleaded not guilty the day before to a long, repetitive indictment charging him with being a traitor to our king. He was accused of having assisted Louis, the King of France, in his wars against England on several occasions. The charges claimed he did this by traveling back and forth between England and France, revealing to the French king what military forces England was preparing to send to Canada and North America. Jerry, whose hair seemed to stand up even more as the complicated legal terms were read, listened with satisfaction, figuring out that this Charles Darnay was indeed the man on trial, that the jury was being sworn in, and that the Attorney General was preparing to speak.

The accused man, who knew that everyone in the room was already imagining him hanged, beheaded, and quartered, did not panic or act dramatically. He was quiet and attentive, watching the start of the trial with serious interest. He stood with his hands resting on the wooden bar in front of him so calmly that he didn't even stir the herbs scattered on it. The courtroom was covered in herbs and sprinkled with vinegar to protect against the bad air and prison fever.

Over the prisoner’s head hung a mirror to reflect light down on him. Over the years, crowds of desperate and criminal people had been reflected in it before leaving both the courtroom and the earth. That terrible place would have been haunted indeed if the glass could show all the faces it had once reflected, just as the ocean will one day give up its dead. Perhaps some thought of the disgrace associated with that mirror crossed the prisoner’s mind. In any case, as he shifted his position and felt a beam of light hit his face, he looked up. When he saw the mirror, his face flushed, and his right hand pushed the herbs aside.

This movement caused him to turn his face to the left side of the court. On a level with his eyes, sitting in the corner of the Judge’s bench, were two people who caught his attention immediately. His expression changed so suddenly when he saw them that all eyes in the room followed his gaze.

The spectators saw a young lady of just over twenty and a gentleman who was clearly her father. He was a man of very striking appearance, with pure white hair and an intense, thoughtful face. When he was lost in thought, he looked quite old; but when he was engaged—as he was now, speaking to his daughter—he became a handsome man who seemed to still be in his prime.

His daughter sat beside him with one hand resting on his arm and the other pressing against it. She had drawn close to him in her fear of the courtroom and her pity for the prisoner. Her face showed deep terror and compassion, focused entirely on the danger facing the accused man. This was so clear and natural that even the spectators who felt no pity for him were touched by her expression, and a whisper went around: "Who are they?"

Jerry the messenger, who had been watching in his own way and sucking the rust off his fingers, stretched his neck to hear the answer. The question was passed through the crowd to the nearest court official and then whispered back until it reached Jerry:

"Witnesses."

"For which side?"

"Against."

"Against who?"

"Against the prisoner."

The Judge, whose eyes had followed the crowd's gaze, looked back, leaned back in his seat, and stared steadily at the man whose life was in his hands, as the Attorney General rose to begin building the case for his execution.

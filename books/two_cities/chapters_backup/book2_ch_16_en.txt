CHAPTER XVI.
Still Knitting

Madame Defarge and her husband returned to the Saint Antoine neighborhood, while the road mender in his blue cap walked back through the dark and dusty miles toward the castle of the late Marquis. The stone faces on the castle walls had plenty of time now to listen to the wind and the fountain. The few starving peasants who wandered near the courtyard to search for herbs or wood thought the expressions on the stone faces had changed. A faint rumor existed in the village that when the Marquis was killed, the stone faces changed from expressions of pride to anger and pain; and when Gaspard was hanged forty feet above the fountain, they changed again to a cruel look of being avenged. On the stone face over the bedroom window where the murder occurred, two nostrils seemed pinched in a way no one had noticed before. Whenever a few peasants sneaked a look at the stone face, they quickly ran back into the woods like frightened rabbits.

The castle and the huts, the stone faces and the hanging body, the bloodstains and the village well—all of France lay under the night sky, looking like a tiny speck from a distance. Just as human knowledge can analyze a ray of light, higher minds could read in the earth's light every thought and action of the people on it.

The Defarges returned in a public carriage to the gates of Paris under the stars. The carriage was stopped at the guardhouse for the usual inspection. Defarge got out, greeting a few soldiers he knew, and warmly hugging a policeman he was friendly with.

As they walked through the dark mud of Saint Antoine toward their shop, Madame Defarge spoke to her husband:

"Tell me, my friend, what did the policeman tell you?"

"Very little tonight, but all he knows. There is a new spy assigned to our neighborhood. There might be more, but he only knows of one."

"Well," Madame Defarge said with a cool, business-like look, "we must register him. What is his name?"

"He is English."

"So much the better. His name?"

"Barsad," said Defarge, pronouncing it in a French way, though he spelled it correctly for her.

"Barsad," madame repeated. "Good. First name?"

"John."

"John Barsad," madame repeated. "Good. What does he look like?"

"About forty years old; about five feet nine inches tall; black hair; dark skin; generally handsome; thin, long, pale face with dark eyes; his nose is hooked but bends toward his left cheek, giving him a sinister expression."

"Well, that is a detailed portrait!" madame laughed. "He will be registered tomorrow."

They entered their closed shop at midnight. Madame Defarge immediately went to her desk, counted the money taken in their absence, checked the inventory, and went over the ledger entries. She checked the servant's work in every way before sending him to bed. Then she poured the coins out again and began tying them into her handkerchief in a chain of separate knots for safekeeping through the night. Defarge smoked his pipe and walked up and down, admiring her work without interfering, which was his habit in both business and family life.

The night was hot, and the closed shop smelled of the dirty neighborhood, mixed with the strong smell of wine, rum, and brandy. Defarge put down his pipe and fanned the air.

"You are tired," madame said, looking up from her knots. "It is just the usual smells."

"I am a little tired," her husband admitted.

"And a little depressed too," said madame, whose quick eyes always watched him. "Oh, you men!"

"But my dear!" Defarge began.

"But my dear!" madame repeated firmly. "You are weak-hearted tonight, my dear!"

"Well," Defarge said, sighing, "it _is_ taking a long time."

"It takes a long time," his wife repeated. "Vengeance always takes a long time; that is the rule."

"It doesn't take long for lightning to strike," said Defarge.

"How long," madame asked calmly, "does it take to make and store the lightning? Tell me."

Defarge looked up thoughtfully.

"It doesn't take long," madame said, "for an earthquake to destroy a town. But tell me how long it takes to prepare the earthquake?"

"A long time, I suppose," said Defarge.

"But when it is ready, it happens and crushes everything. Meanwhile, it is always preparing, even if it is quiet. Remember that."

She tied a tight knot as if strangling an enemy.

"I tell you," madame said, waving her hand, "that although it is taking a long time, the revolution is coming. It never stops or retreats. Look at the anger and discontent growing every day. Can the nobles last? Bah! I laugh at your doubts."

"My brave wife," Defarge said, standing before her with his hands behind his back like an attentive student, "I do not doubt it. But it has lasted a long time, and it is possible that the victory won't come during our lives."

"Well, what of it?" madame asked, tying another tight knot.

"Well," Defarge said, shrugging, "we won't see the victory."

"We will have helped bring it about," madame replied, waving her hand. "Nothing we do is in vain. I believe we will see the victory. But even if we don't, if I were shown a tyrant's neck, I would still—"

Madame set her teeth and tied a very tight, terrible knot.

"Stop!" Defarge said, reddening. "I too will stop at nothing."

"Yes! But you are weak because you need to see the victim and the opportunity to keep your courage up. You must stay strong without that. When the time comes, act like a tiger; but wait for the time with the tiger chained and hidden, yet always ready."

Madame hit the counter with her chain of money to emphasize her point, then calmly gathered her handkerchief and said it was time for bed.

The next noon found Madame Defarge at her usual place, knitting. A rose lay beside her, and she occasionally glanced at it. A few customers were in the shop. The day was very hot, and swarms of flies died at the bottom of the sticky glasses near madame. The other flies ignored them, continuing until they met the same fate.

A figure entering the door cast a new shadow over Madame Defarge. Before looking up, she laid down her knitting and pinned the rose into her headdress.

It was strange: the moment Madame Defarge pinned the rose, the customers stopped talking and gradually left the shop.

"Good day, madame," said the newcomer.

"Good day, monsieur."

She said it aloud, but added to herself: "John Barsad, age about forty, height about five feet nine, black hair, handsome but pale face, dark eyes, hooked nose bending left, sinister look! Good day, spy!"

"Please give me a glass of cognac and some fresh water, madame."

Madame served him politely.

"Excellent cognac, madame!"

It was the first time anyone had complimented the cheap cognac, and Madame Defarge knew its poor quality well. She replied that the cognac was flattered and went back to her knitting. The visitor watched her fingers for a moment and looked around the shop.

"You knit very well, madame."

"I am used to it."

"A pretty pattern too!"

"Do _you_ think so?" madame asked with a smile.

"Definitely. May I ask what it is for?"

"A pastime," madame replied, her fingers moving quickly.

"Not for use?"

"That depends. I might find a use for it one day. If I do, I’ll use it!"

It was remarkable, but the customers clearly did not like the rose in Madame Defarge's hair. Two men who entered to buy drinks saw the rose, hesitated, pretended to look for someone, and walked out. Soon the shop was empty of all previous customers. The spy watched closely but saw no signs of plotting. The customers had left in a natural, casual way.

"John," madame thought, her fingers knitting. "Stay long enough, and I will knit 'BARSAD' before you go."

"Do you have a husband, madame?"

"I do."

"Children?"

"No children."

"Business seems poor?"

"Business is very poor; the people are starving."

"Ah, the poor, oppressed people! As you say."

"As _you_ say," madame corrected him, knitting his name into her register.

"Forgive me; yes, I said it, but you must think so too."

"I think?" madame said loudly. "My husband and I have enough work keeping this shop open without thinking. We only think about how to survive. That keeps us busy enough without worrying about others."

The spy, who was there to gather information, kept his calm, polite expression. He leaned on the counter, sipping his cognac.

"A terrible thing, Gaspard's execution," he sighed. "Poor Gaspard!"

"Well," madame replied coldly, "if people use knives to kill, they must pay the price. He knew the cost; he has paid it."

"I believe," the spy whispered, leaning closer, "that there is a lot of anger and sympathy in this neighborhood for him? Just between us."

"Is there?" madame asked blankly.

"Is there not?"

"Here is my husband," Madame Defarge said.

As Defarge entered, the spy touched his hat and said with a smile, "Good day, Jacques!" Defarge stopped and stared at him.

"Good day, Jacques!" the spy repeated, his smile faltering under the stare.

"You are mistaken, monsieur," Defarge replied. "That is not my name. I am Ernest Defarge."

"It's all the same," the spy said quickly. "Good day!"

"Good day," Defarge replied dryly.

"I was just saying to madame that I hear there is much sympathy and anger in Saint Antoine over Gaspard's death."

"No one has told me so," said Defarge. "I know nothing about it."

He walked behind the counter and stood by his wife's chair, looking at the spy whom they both hated.

The spy calmly finished his glass of cognac and ordered another. Madame Defarge poured it, went back to her knitting, and began humming a tune.

"You seem to know this neighborhood well, better than I do?" Defarge remarked.

"Not at all, but I hope to know it better. I am very interested in these poor people."

"Ah," Defarge muttered.

"Speaking with you, Monsieur Defarge, reminds me," the spy continued, "that I have some interesting associations with your name."

"Indeed?" Defarge said indifferently.

"Yes. When Doctor Manette was released, you, as his old servant, took care of him. He was delivered to you. You see I know the details?"

"That is true," said Defarge. His wife had touched his elbow as she knitted, signaling him to answer briefly.

"It was to you," the spy said, "that his daughter came, and you delivered him to her and that banker in the wig—Lorry—who took them to England."

"That is true," Defarge repeated. "I knew Doctor Manette and his daughter in England," the spy said.

"Yes?"

"You don't hear from them now?"

"No," said Defarge.

"In fact," madame interrupted, looking up, "we never hear from them. We received news of their arrival, but since then we have had no correspondence."

"I see," the spy said. "She is going to be married."

"Married?" madame echoed. "She was pretty enough to marry long ago. You English are very cold."

"Oh! You know I am English."

"I can tell by your accent," madame replied. "A man is what his tongue is."

He did not take it as a compliment but laughed it off. He finished his drink and added:

"Yes, Miss Manette is marrying. But not an Englishman; she is marrying a Frenchman. And speaking of Gaspard, it is a strange coincidence: she is marrying the nephew of the Marquis whom Gaspard killed. In other words, the new Marquis. But he lives quietly in England under the name Charles Darnay. D'Aulnais is his mother's family name."

Madame Defarge knitted steadily, but the news clearly upset her husband. His hand shook as he tried to light his pipe. The spy noticed his reaction and recorded it in his mind.

Having delivered this piece of news, and seeing no other customers, Mr. Barsad paid for his drinks and left, saying politely that he hoped to see them again. The Defarges stood still for a few minutes in case he returned.

"Can it be true," Defarge whispered, "what he said about Miss Manette?"

"Since he said it," madame replied, "it is probably a lie. But it might be true."

"If it is—" Defarge began.

"If it is?"

"—and if the revolution comes in our lifetime, I hope for her sake that her husband stays out of France."

"Her husband’s destiny," Madame Defarge said calmly, "will take him where he must go, and lead him to his end. That is all."

"But it is very strange," Defarge said, wanting his wife to agree, "that after all our sympathy for the Doctor and his daughter, her husband's name should be registered for death by your hand, next to the name of that spy who just left?"

"Stranger things will happen when the revolution comes," madame answered. "They are both on the list for their actions, and that is enough."

She rolled up her knitting and took the rose out of her hair. The neighborhood people, seeing the rose was gone, began coming back into the shop, and it returned to its usual state.

In the evening, when the people of Saint Antoine gathered in the streets for fresh air, Madame Defarge walked among them, carrying her knitting. She was like a missionary of anger, and all the women knitted with her. The work kept their hands busy to distract them from their empty stomachs.

As they knitted, their eyes and thoughts moved quickly. Wherever Madame Defarge walked, she left groups of angry women behind her. Her husband watched her with admiration. "A great, strong, and terrifying woman!" he muttered.

Darkness fell, and the church bells rang while military drums beat in the palace. The women sat knitting in the dark. A greater darkness was coming over France, when these church bells would be melted into cannons, and the drums would beat to drown out the screams of the dying. The women sat knitting, preparing for the day when they would sit by the guillotine, counting the falling heads.

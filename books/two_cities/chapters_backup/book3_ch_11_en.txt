CHAPTER XI.
Dusk


The poor wife of the innocent man condemned to die collapsed when she heard the sentence, as if she had been struck with a deadly blow. But she made no sound. A strong voice inside told her that she, of all people in the world, had to support him in his misery and not make it worse. This thought quickly helped her recover, even from such a terrible shock.

The judges had to take part in a public parade outside, so the court was adjourned. The loud noise and movement of the courtroom emptying through many exits was still going on when Lucie stood up, stretching out her arms toward her husband with nothing but love and comfort on her face.

“If only I could touch him! If only I could hug him just once! Oh, good citizens, if you would only have some pity on us!”

Only a jailer was left, along with two of the four guards who had arrested Darnay the night before, and Barsad. The rest of the crowd had poured out into the streets to watch the parade. Barsad suggested to the others, “Let her hug him, then; it's only for a moment.” They silently agreed, and they helped her over the seats in the hall to a high platform. There, by leaning over the wooden barrier, Darnay could fold her in his arms.

“Goodbye, dear darling of my soul. My parting blessing to you, my love. We will meet again, where the tired are at peace!”

These were her husband's words as he held her close to his chest.

“I can bear it, dear Charles. I am being supported from heaven; do not worry about me. Give a parting blessing for our child.”

“I send it to her through you. I kiss her through you. I say goodbye to her through you.”

“My husband. No! Just a moment!” He was pulling himself away from her. “We will not be separated for long. I feel that this will break my heart soon, but I will do my duty while I can. And when I leave her, God will bring friends to help her, just as He did for me.”

Her father had followed her, and would have fallen to his knees before them, but Darnay reached out and caught him, crying:

“No, no! What have you done, what have you done, that you should kneel to us! We know now what a struggle you went through in the past. We know now what you suffered when you suspected who my family was, and when you finally knew it. We know now the natural hatred you fought against and conquered for her dear sake. We thank you with all our hearts, and all our love and respect. May Heaven be with you!”

Her father's only answer was to run his hands through his white hair and wring them, letting out a cry of agony.

“It could not be any other way,” said the prisoner. “Everything has worked out exactly as it had to. It was my failed attempt to carry out my poor mother's last wishes that first brought my dangerous presence near you. Good could never come from such evil, and a happy ending was not possible after such a sad beginning. Find comfort, and forgive me. May Heaven bless you!”

As he was led away, his wife let go of him and stood watching him go, her hands pressed together in prayer. There was a bright look on her face, and even a comforting smile. As he walked through the prisoner's door, she turned, laid her head lovingly on her father's chest, tried to speak to him, and fainted at his feet.

Then, stepping out from the dark corner where he had stood without moving, Sydney Carton came over and picked her up. Only her father and Mr. Lorry were with her. Carton's arm shook as he lifted her and supported her head. Yet, there was something in his look that was not just pity—there was a glow of pride in it too.

“Shall I carry her to a carriage? She feels as light as a feather to me.”

He carried her easily to the door and laid her down gently in a carriage. Her father and their old friend got inside, and Carton took his seat next to the driver.

When they arrived at the courtyard gate where he had paused in the dark just hours earlier to imagine which paving stones her feet had touched, he lifted her again. He carried her up the stairs to their rooms. There, he laid her down on a couch, where her child and Miss Pross wept over her.

“Don't wake her up yet,” he said softly to Miss Pross. “She is better off like this. Don't bring her back to consciousness while she is only fainted.”

“Oh, Carton, Carton, dear Carton!” cried little Lucie, jumping up and throwing her arms tightly around him in a burst of tears. “Now that you are here, I know you will do something to help mamma and save papa! Oh, look at her, dear Carton! Can you, of all the people who love her, bear to see her like this?”

He bent over the child and rested her warm cheek against his face. He gently set her aside and looked at her unconscious mother.

“Before I go,” he said, and paused, “may I kiss her?”

It was remembered later that when he bent down and touched her face with his lips, he whispered a few words. The child, who was closest to him, told them later, and repeated them to her own grandchildren when she was an old lady, that she had heard him say, “A life you love.”

When he went into the next room, he turned suddenly to Mr. Lorry and her father, who were following him, and said to Dr. Manette:

“You had great influence only yesterday, Doctor Manette. Let us at least try to use it. These judges and all the men in power are very friendly to you and appreciate your services, don't they?”

“Nothing about Charles was kept from me. I had the strongest promises that I would save him, and I did.” He answered in great distress and very slowly.

“Try them again. The hours between now and tomorrow afternoon are few and short, but try.”

“I plan to try. I will not rest for a second.”

“That is good. I have seen energy like yours do great things before—though never,” he added with a smile and a sigh, “anything as great as this. But try! As worthless as life is when we waste it, it is worth this effort. If it weren't, it would be easy to give up.”

“I will go,” said Doctor Manette, “straight to the Prosecutor and the President, and I will go to others whom it is better not to name. I will write letters too, and—but wait! There is a festival in the streets, and no one will be reachable until dark.”

“That is true. Well, it is a desperate hope at best, and not much more desperate for being delayed until dark. I would like to know how you succeed, though remember, I expect nothing! When will you likely have seen these powerful men, Doctor Manette?”

“Right after dark, I hope. Within an hour or two from now.”

“It will be dark soon after four. Let us give it more time. If I go to Mr. Lorry's at nine, will I hear what you have done, either from our friend or from you?”

“Yes.”

“May you succeed!”

Mr. Lorry followed Sydney to the front door, and touching him on the shoulder as he was leaving, made him turn around.

“I have no hope,” said Mr. Lorry in a quiet, sad whisper.

“Neither do I.”

“Even if one of these men, or all of them, wanted to spare him—which is highly unlikely, for what is his life or any man's life to them!—I doubt they would dare to save him after that demonstration in the courtroom.”

“I agree. I heard the sound of the Guillotine's blade falling in those cheers.”

Mr. Lorry leaned his arm against the doorframe and rested his face on it.

“Don't lose hope,” Carton said very gently. “Don't grieve. I encouraged Doctor Manette to try because I felt it might one day bring her comfort. Otherwise, she might always think his life was pointlessly thrown away or wasted, and that thought would torture her.”

“Yes, yes, yes,” Mr. Lorry replied, wiping his eyes. “You are right. But he will die; there is no real hope.”

“Yes. He will die; there is no real hope,” Carton repeated.

And he walked with a steady, determined step downstairs.

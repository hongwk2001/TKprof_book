CHAPTER XV.
Knitting

Drinking began earlier than usual in Monsieur Defarge's wine shop. As early as six o’clock in the morning, pale faces looking through the barred windows could see other customers inside, bending over their wine glasses. Defarge sold a very cheap, thin wine at the best of times, but the wine he served now seemed unusually sour and thin. It had a gloomy effect on the mood of those who drank it. No cheerful laughter came from the grapes of Monsieur Defarge; instead, a quiet, smoldering anger lay hidden in the dregs.

This was the third morning in a row that early drinking had occurred at the shop, starting on Monday and continuing to Wednesday. However, there was more quiet plotting than drinking. Many men who had no money to buy wine came in to whisper and listen. They were just as interested in the place as if they could afford whole barrels, moving from seat to seat, eagerly absorbing every word.

Despite the crowd, the master of the wine shop was nowhere to be seen. He was not missed, as no one looked or asked for him. Everyone was used to seeing Madame Defarge sitting at the counter alone, presiding over the wine sales. In front of her sat a bowl of worn, battered coins, which were as damaged and defaced as the poor pockets they had come from.

A sense of suspense and distraction filled the room, which might have been noticed by any spies looking in. Card games were neglected, domino players built towers with their tiles, and drinkers drew shapes on the tables with spilled wine. Madame Defarge herself picked at her sleeve with a toothpick, looking as if she were seeing and hearing things far away.

This was the state of the Saint Antoine neighborhood until midday. At noon, two dusty men walked under the street lamps: Monsieur Defarge and a road mender wearing a blue cap. Covered in dust and thirsty, they entered the wine shop. Their arrival caused a stir in Saint Antoine, with faces appearing at doors and windows as they walked by. Yet no one followed them inside, and no one spoke when they entered, though all eyes turned toward them.

"Good day, gentlemen!" said Monsieur Defarge.

This signal loosened the tongues in the room, eliciting a chorus of "Good day!"

"It is bad weather, gentlemen," said Defarge, shaking his head.

At this, every man looked at his neighbor, then looked down and sat in silence. One man got up and walked out.

"My wife," Defarge said aloud, addressing Madame Defarge. "I have traveled some distance with this good road mender, named Jacques. I met him by accident a day and a half's journey outside Paris. He is a good fellow, this road mender named Jacques. Give him a drink, wife!"

A second man stood up and left. Madame Defarge poured wine for the road mender, who tipped his blue cap to the room and drank. He took some dark bread from his shirt and ate it, sitting near Madame Defarge's counter. A third man stood up and walked out.

Defarge drank a glass of wine himself—taking less than the guest, since wine was no luxury to him—and waited for the countryman to finish his breakfast. He looked at no one, and no one looked at him, not even Madame Defarge, who was busy with her knitting.

"Have you finished your meal, friend?" Defarge asked.

"Yes, thank you."

"Come then! I will show you the room I told you about. It will suit you perfectly."

They walked out of the wine shop into the street, through a courtyard, up a dark staircase, and into an attic—the very attic where a white-haired shoemaker had once sat working on his bench.

No shoemaker was there now. Instead, the three men who had left the wine shop one by one were waiting. They and the white-haired man were connected by one thing: they had all looked at him through the cracks in the attic wall.

Defarge closed the door carefully and spoke quietly:

"Jacques One, Jacques Two, Jacques Three! This is the witness I met by agreement, I, Jacques Four. He will tell you everything. Speak, Jacques Five!"

The road mender, blue cap in hand, wiped his forehead and said, "Where should I start, monsieur?"

"Start at the beginning," Defarge replied.

"I saw him, messieurs," began the road mender, "a year ago during the summer, hanging by the chain under the Marquis's carriage. It happened like this: I had stopped working on the road at sunset, and as the Marquis's carriage slowly climbed the hill, he was hanging by the chain—like this."

The road mender acted out the scene again. He was very good at it, since it had been the main story in his village for a whole year.

Jacques One asked if he had ever seen the man before.

"Never," answered the road mender.

Jacques Three asked how he recognized him later.

"By his height," the road mender whispered, putting his finger to his nose. "When the Marquis asked me that evening, 'What does he look like?' I answered, 'Tall as a ghost.'"

"You should have said short as a dwarf," Jacques Two remarked.

"But how was I to know? The deed had not been done yet, and he had not told me anything. Even then, I did not volunteer to speak. The Marquis pointed his finger at me by the fountain and ordered his men to bring me to him. I had no choice, messieurs."

"He is right," Defarge muttered to the man who had interrupted. "Go on!"

"Good!" said the road mender. "The tall man disappeared, and they searched for him for months. Was it nine, ten, eleven months?"

"It doesn't matter how long," said Defarge. "He hid well, but at last they found him. Go on!"

"I was working on the hillside again at sunset. I was gathering my tools to go home, where it was already dark, when I looked up and saw six soldiers coming over the hill. In the middle of them was the tall man, his arms tied to his sides—like this!"

Using his blue cap, he showed how the man's elbows were tied tightly behind him with knotted ropes.

"I stood by my heap of stones to watch them pass, as it is a quiet road where any spectacle is worth watching. At first, as they approached, I only saw six soldiers and the bound man. They looked black against the setting sun, which gave them a red edge, messieurs. Their long shadows stretched like giants across the hill. They were covered in dust, marching tramp, tramp! But when they came close, I recognized the tall man, and he recognized me. Ah! He would have gladly thrown himself over the hillside again, as he did the first night we met near that very spot!"

He described it vividly, as if he were still watching it.

"I did not show the soldiers that I knew him, and he did not show them that he knew me. We spoke only with our eyes. 'Come on!' the sergeant said, pointing toward the village. 'Bring him to his grave!' and they marched faster. I followed them. His arms were swollen from the tight ropes, his wooden shoes were clumsy, and he was limping. Because he was slow, the soldiers hit him with their gunstocks—like this!"

He acted out a man being pushed forward by musket butts.

"As they ran down the hill like madmen, he fell. They laughed and pulled him up. His face was bleeding and dusty, but he couldn't wipe it because his hands were tied. The soldiers laughed again. They brought him into the village, and everyone ran to see. They took him past the mill and up to the prison. Everyone saw the prison gates open in the dark and swallow him up—like this!"

He opened his mouth wide and snapped his teeth shut. Seeing he wanted to keep his mouth closed for effect, Defarge said, "Go on, Jacques."

"All the villagers went home," the road mender whispered on tiptoe. "Everyone whispered by the fountain, then went to sleep and dreamed of that poor man locked in the prison on the cliff, never to come out alive. In the morning, with my tools on my shoulder, eating my bread, I walked past the prison. I saw him high up, behind the iron bars of a cage, still bloody and dusty, looking out. He could not wave because his hands were tied. I did not dare call out to him. He looked at me like a dead man."

Defarge and the other three men looked at one another with dark, vengeful expressions. They looked like a secret court. Jacques One and Two sat on the bed, chin in hand, watching the road mender. Jacques Three nervously twitched his fingers near his mouth. Defarge stood between them and the witness in the light of the window.

"Go on, Jacques," Defarge said.

"He stayed in that iron cage for several days. The villagers watched him secretly, fearing the soldiers. But everyone looked up at the prison from a distance. In the evening, when they gathered by the fountain, all faces turned toward the prison instead of the post house. They whispered that he might not be executed. They said petitions had been sent to Paris, explaining that he had gone mad from the death of his child, and that a petition had been given to the King himself. I don't know if it's true. Perhaps yes, perhaps no."

"Listen, Jacques," Jacques One said sternly. "A petition was indeed given to the King and Queen. Everyone here, except you, saw the King take it from his carriage in the street. It was Defarge who ran out before the horses at the risk of his life to hand it to him."

"And listen, Jacques!" the kneeling Jacques Three added, his fingers twitching greedily. "The guards surrounded him and beat him. Do you hear?"

"I hear, messieurs."

"Go on then," said Defarge.

"But on the other hand," the road mender continued, "they whisper by the fountain that he will be executed in our village. They say that because he killed the Marquis, who was the lord of the land, he will be executed as a parent-killer. An old man said that his right hand, which held the knife, will be burned off before his eyes. They will pour boiling oil, melted lead, and sulfur into his wounds, and then tear him apart with four horses. The old man said this was done to Damiens, the man who tried to kill King Louis XV. But I don't know if it's true; I am not educated."

"Listen, Jacques!" the nervous Jacques Three said. "The man was named Damiens, and it was done in broad daylight in the streets of Paris. The wealthy ladies of the court watched the execution until nightfall, when the man was still breathing after losing his limbs! It happened when you were ten years old; you could have seen it."

"Enough!" Defarge said impatiently. "Go on."

"Well, some say this and some say that. On Sunday night, when the village was asleep, the soldiers came down from the prison. Their guns clinked in the street. Workmen dug and hammered while the soldiers sang. In the morning, a gallows forty feet high was standing by the fountain, shadowing the water."

The road mender pointed toward the ceiling as if he could see the gallows in the sky.

"All work stopped, and everyone gathered there, even the cows. At noon, the drums rolled. The soldiers brought him out, bound and gagged with a tight string, making him look like he was laughing." The road mender pulled his mouth back with his thumbs to show them. "At the top of the gallows was the knife, blade up. He was hanged forty feet in the air, left hanging to poison the water."

They looked at one another in silence as the road mender wiped his sweaty face with his blue cap.

"It is terrible, messieurs. How can the women draw water now? Who can sit by the fountain under that shadow? When I left the village on Monday evening and looked back from the hill, the shadow of the gallows stretched across the church, the mill, the prison—across the whole earth!"

Jacques Three chewed on his fingers, his hand shaking.

"That is all, messieurs. I left at sunset as I was told, walked all night and half the next day, until I met this comrade. We rode and walked here, and here I am."

After a quiet pause, Jacques One said, "Good! You have told your story well. Wait for us outside the door."

"Gladly," said the road mender. Defarge led him to the stairs and returned to the attic.

The three men stood with their heads together.

"What do you say, Jacques?" Number One asked. "Should they be registered?"

"To be registered for destruction," Defarge replied.

"Excellent!" Jacques Three croaked.

"The castle and the whole family?" the first asked.

"The castle and the whole family," Defarge answered. "Extermination."

Jacques Three croaked happily, "Excellent!" and chewed on another finger.

"Are you sure," Jacques Two asked Defarge, "that there is no danger in how we keep the record? It is safe because no one else can read it, but will we always be able to read it—or rather, will she?"

"Jacques," Defarge said proudly, "if my wife kept the record in her head alone, she would never lose a word of it. Knitted into her own stitches and symbols, it will always be clear to her. Trust Madame Defarge. It would be easier for a man to erase himself from existence than to erase a single letter from her knitted register."

They murmured in agreement, and Jacques Three asked, "Should we send the countryman back soon? He is very simple; is he dangerous?"

"He knows nothing," said Defarge. "Only enough to get himself hanged if he talks. I will take care of him and set him on his way. He wants to see the King, Queen, and court. I will take him to Versailles on Sunday."

"What?" Jacques Three asked in surprise. "Is it a good sign that he wants to see royalty?"

"Jacques," Defarge explained, "if you want a cat to catch mice, show her milk first. Show a dog his prey if you want him to hunt it down one day."

Nothing more was said. They found the road mender asleep on the stairs and told him to rest on the bed. He fell asleep immediately.

For a poor peasant, Defarge’s shop was a comfortable place to stay. Aside from his fear of Madame Defarge, his time in Paris was very pleasant. Madame sat at her counter all day, acting as if he weren't there, which made him shake in his wooden shoes whenever she looked at him. He felt that if she decided to accuse him of a crime, she would carry it through to the end.

Therefore, when Sunday came, the road mender pretended to be happy to go to Versailles with the Defarges. It was uncomfortable to have madame knitting in the public carriage all the way, and even more uncomfortable to have her knitting in the crowd as they waited to see the King and Queen.

"You work hard, madame," a man nearby remarked.

"Yes," Madame Defarge replied. "I have a lot to do."

"What are you making?"

"Many things."

"For example?"

"For example," she replied calmly, "shrouds."

The man moved away quickly, and the road mender fanned himself with his cap, feeling the heat. Soon the King and Queen arrived in their golden coach, surrounded by their wealthy court of lords and ladies. The road mender was so excited by the jewels, silks, and grandeur that he shouted: "Long live the King! Long live the Queen! Long live everyone!" as if he had never heard of the Jacques movement. He wept with excitement at the gardens, fountains, and royalty. During the three hours of the display, he shouted and cried with the crowd. Defarge held him by the collar the whole time, keeping him from getting too close to the carriage.

"Well done!" Defarge said, clapping him on the back afterward. "You are a good boy!"

The road mender began to worry if he had made a mistake by cheering the King.

"You are exactly the kind of man we need," Defarge whispered. "You make these fools believe their system will last forever. That makes them more insolent, and brings the end closer."

"Ah!" said the road mender. "That is true."

"These fools know nothing. They despise you and would kill you before they killed one of their horses, but they only believe what they see. Let them be deceived a little longer."

Madame Defarge looked at him and nodded.

"As for you," she said, "you would cheer for anything that makes a noise. Wouldn't you?"

"Yes, madame. For the moment."

"If you were shown a pile of dolls and told to tear them to pieces for your own profit, you would choose the finest ones, wouldn't you?"

"Yes, madame."

"And if you were shown a flock of fine birds unable to fly and told to pluck their feathers, you would choose the best ones, wouldn't you?"

"That is true, madame."

"You have seen both dolls and birds today," Madame Defarge said, pointing toward the palace. "Now, go home!"

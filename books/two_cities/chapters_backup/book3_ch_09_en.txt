CHAPTER IX.
The Game Made


While Sydney Carton and the prison spy were in the adjoining back room, speaking so quietly that not a sound could be heard, Mr. Lorry looked at Jerry Cruncher with great doubt and suspicion. Jerry's reaction did not inspire confidence; he shifted his weight from one leg to the other as if he had fifty legs and were trying them all out. He examined his fingernails closely, and whenever Mr. Lorry looked at him, he let out a fake little cough into his hand—a clear sign that he was hiding something.

“Jerry,” Mr. Lorry said. “Come here.”

Jerry stepped forward sideways, keeping one shoulder in front of him.

“What have you been doing, besides working as a messenger?”

After thinking for a moment and looking closely at his boss, Jerry came up with the idea of replying, “I'm an agricultural character.”

“I suspect,” Mr. Lorry said, shaking his finger at him angrily, “that you have used Tellson's Bank as a cover, and that you have been doing some illegal and shameful work. If you have, don't expect me to help you when we get back to England, and don't expect me to keep your secret. Tellson's will not be associated with a criminal.”

“I hope, sir,” the embarrassed Jerry pleaded, “that a gentleman like you, for whom I have worked until I'm gray, would think twice before hurting me, even if it were true—I'm not saying it is, but even if it were. And you have to consider that there are two sides to it. There are doctors right now earning lots of money where an honest worker like me doesn't earn a penny, putting their money in Tellson's and looking down on me while they drive away in their carriages. Well, they are using Tellson's too. You have to treat everyone the same. And my wife back in England was always praying against my business, which was ruining it! Meanwhile, the doctors' wives don't do that. Plus, what with greedy undertakers, clerks, grave-diggers, and watchmen all wanting a cut, a man doesn't make much money from it anyway. And what little he does get never brings him luck, Mr. Lorry. He never gets any good from it and wants to get out of the business if he can, once he's in—even if it were true.”

“Ugh!” Mr. Lorry said, softening a bit anyway. “I am shocked by you.”

“Now, what I would humbly suggest, sir,” Jerry continued, “even if it were true, which I'm not saying it is—”

“Don't lie,” said Mr. Lorry.

“No, I won't, sir,” Jerry replied as if lying were the last thing he would do, “even if it were true. What I would suggest is this: back in London, my son sits on my stool at the bank, ready to do any errands or light jobs for you as hard as he can. If it were true—which I still don't admit—please let my boy keep his job to support his mother. Don't expose him because of his father. Let his father go into honest grave-digging instead, making up for what he dug up by burying people properly from now on. That, Mr. Lorry,” Jerry said, wiping his forehead with his arm to finish his speech, “is what I suggest. A man can't see all this death around him in Paris, with so many headless bodies, without thinking seriously about life. And these are my thoughts, begging you to remember that I spoke up today to help you when I could have kept quiet.”

“That at least is true,” Mr. Lorry said. “Say no more now. Perhaps I will still help you, if you deserve it and show you are sorry through your actions, not just words. I don't want to hear any more.”

Jerry saluted by touching his forehead as Sydney Carton and the spy returned from the back room. “Goodbye, Mr. Barsad,” Carton said. “Now that we have made our agreement, you have nothing to fear from me.”

He sat down in a chair by the fire, opposite Mr. Lorry. Once they were alone, Mr. Lorry asked him what he had accomplished.

“Not much. If things go badly for the prisoner, I have arranged to visit him once.”

Mr. Lorry's face fell.

“It is all I could do,” Carton said. “Asking for too much would have put the spy's own head under the axe, and as he said, nothing worse could happen to him if he were reported. That was the limit of what we could get. We have to accept it.”

“But visiting him,” Mr. Lorry said, “won't save him if he is condemned by the court.”

“I never said it would.”

Mr. Lorry looked back at the fire. His sympathy for Lucie and the shock of Charles's second arrest overwhelmed him. He was an old man, worn out by worry, and he began to cry.

“You are a good man and a true friend,” Carton said in a different, gentler voice. “Forgive me for noticing your tears. I could not watch my own father cry and do nothing, and I could not respect your grief more if you were my father. Though you are spared that misfortune, at least.”

Although Carton made a joke at the end in his usual style, there was a real feeling of respect in his voice and touch. Mr. Lorry, who had never seen this side of him before, was completely surprised. He held out his hand, and Carton squeezed it gently.

“Regarding Darnay,” Carton said. “Don't tell Lucie about this meeting or our agreement. It wouldn't allow her to see him anyway, and she might worry that we are planning for his execution, or trying to smuggle in a way for him to end his life early.”

Mr. Lorry had not thought of that, and he looked at Carton quickly to see if that was what he meant. It seemed to be; Carton returned his look, showing he understood.

“She might worry about a thousand things,” Carton said, “and that would only add to her pain. Don't mention me to her. As I said when I arrived, it is better if I don't see her. I can help her from a distance. You are going to see her now, I hope? She must be very lonely tonight.”

“I am going right now.”

“I am glad. She loves and relies on you so much. How does she look?”

“Anxious and sad, but very beautiful.”

“Ah!”

It was a long, sad sound, like a sigh or a sob. Mr. Lorry looked at Carton's face, which was turned toward the fire. A look of emotion passed over it quickly. Carton raised his foot to push back a burning log that was rolling out. He wore a white coat and boots, and the firelight made him look very pale, with his long, uncombed brown hair hanging loose. He seemed so distracted that he didn't even notice his boot was resting on the hot coals until the wood broke under his foot, prompting Mr. Lorry to warn him.

“I forgot about it,” he said.

Mr. Lorry looked at his face again. Seeing the tired, worn look on Carton's handsome features, he was reminded of the look he had seen on the faces of the prisoners.

“And is your work here finished, sir?” Carton asked, turning to him.

“Yes. As I was telling you last night before Lucie arrived, I have done everything I can here. I hoped to leave them safe and then exit Paris. I already have my travel papers. I was ready to go.”

They were both silent.

“You have a long life to look back on, sir?” Carton asked thoughtfully.

“I am seventy-seven years old.”

“You have been useful all your life, always busy, trusted, respected, and looked up to?”

“I have been a businessman ever since I grew up. Indeed, I was doing business even as a boy.”

“Look at the important place you fill at your age. So many people will miss you when you are gone!”

“I am just a lonely bachelor,” Mr. Lorry replied, shaking his head. “There is no one to cry for me.”

“How can you say that? Wouldn't Lucie cry for you? Wouldn't her child?”

“Yes, yes, thank God. I didn't quite mean that.”

“That is something to be thankful for, isn't it?”

“Of course, of course.”

“If you had to say to yourself tonight, 'No one loves or respects me, I have no place in anyone's heart, and I have done nothing good to be remembered by,' then your seventy-seven years would be a heavy curse, wouldn't they?”

“You are right, Mr. Carton. I think they would be.”

Sydney looked back at the fire and, after a short silence, said:

“I want to ask you: does your childhood seem very far away? Do the days when you sat with your mother feel like a lifetime ago?”

Responding to Carton's gentle tone, Mr. Lorry answered:

“Twenty years ago, yes. But at my age, no. As I get closer to the end of my life, I feel like I am circling back to the beginning. It feels like a gentle way of preparing for death. I keep remembering things from my childhood that I had forgotten, like my beautiful young mother—even though I am so old now—and the days before I grew hard and set in my ways.”

“I understand that feeling!” Carton said, his face flushing. “And does it make you feel better?”

“I hope so.”

Carton ended the conversation by standing up to help him with his coat. “But you,” Mr. Lorry said, returning to the subject, “you are still young.”

“Yes,” Carton said. “I am not old, but the way I lived my youth was never going to lead to a long old age. Enough about me.”

“And enough about me, too,” Mr. Lorry said. “Are you going out?”

“I'll walk with you to her gate. You know my habit of wandering around. If I stay out walking in the streets all night, don't worry. I will see you in the morning. You are going to the court tomorrow?”

“Yes, unfortunately.”

“I will be there too, just as part of the crowd. My spy will find a place for me. Take my arm, sir.”

Mr. Lorry took his arm, and they walked downstairs into the street. A few minutes later, they arrived at Lucie's building. Carton said goodbye, but stayed nearby. After the gate shut, he walked back and touched it. He had heard about her daily walks to the prison. “She stood here,” he said, looking around. “She walked this way, stepping on these very stones. Let me follow her steps.”

It was ten o'clock at night when he stood outside La Force prison, where Lucie had waited so many times. The little wood-sawyer, having closed his shop, was smoking a pipe at his door.

“Good night, citizen,” Sydney Carton said as he walked past, since the man was staring at him curiously.

“Good night, citizen.”

“How is the Republic doing?”

“You mean the Guillotine. Not bad. Sixty-three people today. We will reach a hundred soon. The executioner Samson and his men complain they are tired. Ha, ha, ha! He is so funny, that Samson. What a barber!”

“Do you go to watch him often?”

“Shave? Always. Every day. What a barber! Have you seen him work?”

“Never.”

“Go watch him when he has a big group. Imagine this, citizen: he executed all sixty-three today in less time than it takes to smoke two pipes! Less than two pipes, I swear!”

As the grinning man held out his pipe to show how he timed the executions, Carton felt such a strong urge to punch him that he had to turn away.

“But you aren't French, are you, even though you wear English clothes?”

“Yes,” Carton said, pausing and looking back over his shoulder.

“You speak like a Frenchman.”

“I used to study here.”

“Aha, a perfect Frenchman! Good night, Englishman.”

“Good night, citizen.”

“But go watch that funny guy,” the little man called after him. “And take a pipe with you!”

Sydney walked a short distance, stopped under a street lamp, and wrote on a piece of paper. Then, walking confidently through several dark, dirty streets—which were neglected during the revolution—he stopped at a pharmacy that the owner was closing. It was a tiny, dim, crooked shop on a steep street, owned by a small, crooked man.

Greeting the pharmacist, Carton laid his paper on the counter. The chemist whistled softly as he read it, murmuring, “Oh! My, my!”

Sydney Carton ignored him, and the chemist asked:

“Is this for you, citizen?”

“For me.”

“You will keep them separate, citizen? You know what happens if you mix them?”

“Perfectly.”

The chemist prepared small packets and handed them over. Carton put them in his inside coat pocket, paid for them, and walked out. “There is nothing left to do,” he said, looking up at the moon, “until tomorrow. I can't sleep.”

His voice was not careless or angry. It was the calm voice of a tired man who had been lost for a long time but had finally found his path and could see the end of it.

Long ago, when he was a promising young student, he had attended his father's funeral. His mother had died years before that. The religious words read at his father's grave came back to him now as he walked through the dark, shadowy streets under the moon: “I am the resurrection and the life, saith the Lord: he that believeth in me, though he were dead, yet shall he live: and whosoever liveth and believeth in me, shall never die.”

In a city ruled by the Guillotine, walking alone at night, he felt sad for the sixty-three people executed that day and for the next day's victims waiting in their cells. The words comforted him like an anchor in a storm. He did not think about why they came to him, but simply repeated them as he walked.

He looked at the lit windows where people were going to sleep, escaping the day's horrors for a few hours. He saw the dark church towers where no one prayed anymore, because the people had rejected religion after years of corrupt priests. He thought of the cemeteries with signs reading "Eternal Sleep," the crowded jails, and the streets where cartloads of people rode to their deaths daily. Walking through this city of death as it quieted down for the night, Carton crossed the river again.

There were few carriages on the streets, as riding in them looked suspicious. Wealthy people wore simple red caps and heavy shoes to look like common workers. However, the theaters were packed, and crowds of people walked home chatting happily. Near a theater door, he saw a mother and a little girl trying to cross the muddy street. He carried the child across, and before she let go of his neck, he asked her for a kiss.

“I am the resurrection and the life, saith the Lord: he that believeth in me, though he were dead, yet shall he live: and whosoever liveth and believeth in me, shall never die.”

Now that the streets were quiet and the night grew late, the words seemed to echo in his footsteps and in the air. Calm and steady, he repeated them to himself as he walked, hearing them constantly.

The night ended. As he stood on the bridge listening to the river against the walls of the city, where houses and the cathedral shone under the moon, dawn broke coldly like a dead face in the sky. The stars faded, and for a moment, the world seemed cold and lifeless.

But when the sun rose, its warm rays seemed to bring the words straight to his heart. Shading his eyes, he saw a bridge of light stretching across the sparkling river toward the sun.

The deep, swift river felt like a friend in the quiet morning. He walked along the bank far from any houses and fell asleep in the warm sun. When he woke up, he stayed a bit longer, watching a small whirlpool spin uselessly until the river swept it away toward the sea. “Just like me,” he thought.

A boat with a sail the color of a dead leaf floated past him and disappeared. As the ripples in the water faded, the prayer in his heart for forgiveness for all his past mistakes ended with the words, “I am the resurrection and the life.”

Mr. Lorry was already gone when Carton returned, and it was easy to guess where the old man had went. Carton drank some coffee, ate a bit of bread, washed his face, changed his clothes, and walked to the courtroom.

The courtroom was crowded and noisy when the spy—whom everyone avoided in fear—guided Carton into a quiet corner in the crowd. Mr. Lorry and Doctor Manette were there, and Lucie sat next to her father.

When Charles was brought in, she looked at him with such love, encouragement, and courage that it brought color back to his face and gave him strength. If anyone had looked at Sydney Carton's face at that moment, they would have seen the exact same look of devotion in his eyes.

In this unfair court, there was no real legal procedure to give the accused a fair trial. The Revolution had only happened because the old laws had been so badly abused, and now the angry Republic had thrown all legal rules out the window.

Everyone stared at the jury. They were the same fierce patriots who sat there every day. Prominent among them was one man with a hungry look who kept touching his lips, which pleased the crowd. He was Jacques Three of Saint Antoine, a bloodthirsty juror. The whole jury looked like a pack of wild dogs gathered to try a deer.

Then everyone looked at the five judges and the prosecutor. There was no sympathy there today; they looked grim and ready to execute. The people in the crowd looked at each other and nodded in approval, leaning forward to pay close attention.

Charles Evremonde, called Darnay. Released yesterday. Re-accused and arrested again yesterday. Charges delivered to him last night. Suspected and accused enemy of the Republic, an aristocrat from a family of tyrants, whose class was outlawed for oppressing the common people. Because of this, Charles Darnay was already considered dead under the law.

The public prosecutor spoke to this effect in very few words.

The president asked if the accused was reported openly or in secret.

“Openly, President.”

“By whom?”

“Three voices. Ernest Defarge, wine shop owner of Saint Antoine.”

“Good.”

“Therese Defarge, his wife.”

“Good.”

“Alexandre Manette, physician.”

A huge uproar broke out in the courtroom. In the middle of the noise, Doctor Manette stood up, pale and shaking.

“President, I protest! This is a lie and a fraud!” he cried. “You know the accused is my daughter's husband. My daughter and her family are dearer to me than my own life. Who is the liar saying that I accused my daughter's husband!”

“Citizen Manette, calm down. Refusing to obey the court will make you an outlaw. As for what is dearer than life, nothing can be more precious to a good citizen than the Republic.”

The crowd cheered loudly at this warning. The president rang his bell and continued passionately:

“If the Republic demands the sacrifice of your own child, your duty is to give her up. Listen to what is next, and stay silent!”

The crowd cheered wildly again. Doctor Manette sat down, his eyes wide and his lips trembling, while Lucie drew closer to him. The bloodthirsty juror rubbed his hands together and touched his lips again.

When the room quieted down, Defarge was brought forward. He explained how he had been a boy working for the Doctor, how the Doctor was released, and the state he was in when he was delivered to the wine shop. A short questioning followed, as the court worked quickly.

“You did good work at the capture of the Bastille, citizen?”

“I believe so.”

An excited woman in the crowd LSM screamed, “You were one of the best patriots there! Why not say it? You worked the cannons that day, and you were among the first to enter the fortress when it fell. Patriots, I tell the truth!”

It was The Vengeance shouting this, to the cheers of the crowd. The president rang his bell, but she screamed, “I defy that bell!” which pleased the crowd even more.

“Tell the court what you did inside the Bastille that day, citizen.”

“I knew,” Defarge said, looking down at his wife, who stood below him watching him closely, “that this prisoner had been kept in cell One Hundred and Five, North Tower. I knew it from the Doctor himself. He called himself by that name when he made shoes in my attic. While working my cannon that day, I resolved to search that cell once the Bastille fell. When it did, I went up to the cell with a fellow citizen who is now on the jury. In a hole in the chimney where a stone had been loose, I found a paper. This is that paper. I have compared it with Doctor Manette's handwriting, and they match. I hand this paper over to the President of the court.”

“Let it be read.”

In absolute silence, Charles looked lovingly at his wife, who looked back and forth between him and her father. Doctor Manette kept his eyes on the reader, Madame Defarge stared at the prisoner, Defarge watched his wife, and everyone else looked at the Doctor. The paper was then read aloud as follows.

CHAPTER XII.
The Fellow of Delicacy

Mr. Stryver, having decided to generously offer his hand in marriage to the Doctor’s daughter, resolved to let her know of her good fortune before he left town for the summer vacation. After thinking about it, he decided it would be best to get the preliminary details out of the way. They could then decide at leisure whether to marry a week or two before the autumn court term, or during the short Christmas break.

He had no doubt about the strength of his case, viewing it as a guaranteed success. Argued on practical, worldly grounds—the only grounds he considered worth taking into account—it was a simple case with no weak spots. He viewed himself as the perfect choice, with no possible objections. The decision seemed so obvious that no jury would even need to deliberate. Stryver was entirely satisfied that it was a clear-cut case.

Consequently, Mr. Stryver decided to start his vacation by formally proposing to take Miss Manette to Vauxhall Gardens. If that failed, he would try Ranelagh Gardens. If that also failed, he would go directly to Soho and declare his feelings.

He headed toward Soho, pushing his way through the streets from the Temple. Anyone who saw him walking along the pavement, bumping past weaker people, would have seen how confident and strong he felt.

Since his route took him past Tellson’s Bank, where he kept his money, and knowing Mr. Lorry was a close friend of the Manettes, Stryver decided to stop in and share his happy news. He pushed open the stubborn door, walked down the steps past the two old cashiers, and entered the dusty back office where Mr. Lorry sat working on his ledgers.

"Hello!" said Mr. Stryver. "How are you? I hope you are well!"

Stryver had a way of seeming too big for any room. He was so large for the small office at Tellson's that the old clerks in the corners looked up in protest, as if they were being squeezed against the wall. The bank owners, reading the newspaper in the distance, looked displeased at his loud entrance.

Mr. Lorry said in a quiet, professional voice, "How do you do, Mr. Stryver? How do you do, sir?" and shook hands. He did so in the self-effacing way typical of Tellson's clerks when dealing with customers.

"Can I do anything for you, Mr. Stryver?" Mr. Lorry asked.

"No, thank you. This is a private visit, Mr. Lorry. I have come for a confidential word."

"Oh, indeed!" said Mr. Lorry, leaning forward to listen while keeping an eye on his superiors in the distance.

"I am going," Mr. Stryver said, leaning his arms on the desk so heavily that it seemed too small for him, "to offer myself in marriage to your agreeable friend, Miss Manette, Mr. Lorry."

"Oh dear me!" Mr. Lorry cried, rubbing his chin and looking at his visitor with doubt.

"Oh dear you, sir?" Stryver repeated, drawing back. "What do you mean by that, Mr. Lorry?"

"My meaning is, of course, friendly and appreciative," the banker answered. "It does you great credit, and is everything you could wish. But really, Mr. Stryver—" Mr. Lorry paused and shook his head, as if trying not to say that Stryver was simply too overbearing.

"Well!" said Stryver, slapping the desk. "If I understand you, I'll be hanged!"

Mr. Lorry adjusted his wig and bit his pen.

"Damn it all, sir!" Stryver said, staring at him. "Am I not a good match?"

"Oh, yes! You are a good match," Mr. Lorry said. "If you say so, you are."

"Am I not successful?"

"Yes, you are successful," Mr. Lorry agreed.

"And making progress in my career?"

"No one can doubt that," Mr. Lorry admitted.

"Then what on earth do you mean, Mr. Lorry?" Stryver asked, looking disappointed.

"Well, were you going there now?" Mr. Lorry asked.

"Immediately!" said Stryver, hitting the desk with his fist.

"Then I wouldn't do it if I were you."

"Why not?" Stryver asked, pointing his finger at him like a lawyer in court. "You are a businessman, so you must have a reason. Tell me why I shouldn't go."

"Because," said Mr. Lorry, "I wouldn't propose unless I had reason to believe I would be accepted."

"Damn it!" cried Stryver. "This beats everything."

Mr. Lorry looked at his superiors in the distance, then back at the angry lawyer.

"Here is a man of business, age, and experience in a bank," Stryver scoffed, "and after I list three major reasons for success, he says there is no reason at all!"

"When I speak of success, I mean success with the young lady," Mr. Lorry said, gently tapping Stryver's arm. "And when I speak of reasons for success, I mean reasons that matter to her. The young lady, my good sir, comes first."

"So you are telling me, Mr. Lorry," Stryver said, squaring his elbows, "that in your opinion, the young lady in question is a foolish girl?"

"Not at all. I am telling you, Mr. Stryver," Mr. Lorry said, reddening with anger, "that I will not hear a disrespectful word about that young lady from anyone. If any man spoke disrespectfully of her at this desk, not even my job at Tellson’s would stop me from telling him what I think of him."

Both men were now red-faced and angry, trying to keep their voices quiet in the bank.

"That is what I mean to tell you, sir," said Mr. Lorry. "Please make no mistake about it."

Mr. Stryver chewed on a ruler for a moment, tap-tapping it against his teeth in the awkward silence. Finally, he said:

"This is new to me, Mr. Lorry. You are advising me not to go to Soho and offer myself—Stryver of the King's Bench?"

"Did you ask for my advice, Mr. Stryver?"

"Yes, I did."

"Very well. Then I give it, and you have repeated it correctly."

"And all I can say," Stryver laughed bitterly, "is that this beats everything."

"Now understand me," Mr. Lorry continued. "As a businessman, I know nothing of this matter. But as an old friend who has known Miss Lucie since she was a child, and who cares deeply for her and her father, I have spoken. I did not seek this confidence, remember. Do you still think I am wrong?"

"Not at all!" Stryver said, whistling. "I can't force others to have common sense; I can only have it myself. I assumed the family had sense; you say they don't. I'm sure you are right."

"What I believe, Mr. Stryver, I will define for myself," said Mr. Lorry, flushing again. "I won't have anyone else define it for me."

"There! I beg your pardon!" said Stryver.

"Apology accepted. Thank you. Now, Mr. Stryver, I was going to say: it would be painful for you to be rejected, painful for Doctor Manette to have to tell you, and painful for Lucie to reject you. You know my close relationship with the family. If you like, without committing you to anything, I will go to Soho tonight, observe the situation, and let you know what I find. It might spare everyone an uncomfortable scene. What do you say?"

"How long would I have to wait?"

"Only a few hours. I can go to Soho this evening and come to your offices afterward."

"Then I agree," said Stryver. "I won't go there now. I will expect you tonight. Good morning."

Stryver turned and stormed out of the bank, leaving a draft of air that required the old clerks to hold onto their desks.

The lawyer was smart enough to realize that the banker would not have spoken so strongly without being certain. Though he hated to swallow this pill, he accepted it. "And now," Mr. Stryver said to himself, looking toward the Temple, "my way out of this is to put them all in the wrong."

It was a tactic he often used in court. "You won't reject me, young lady," Mr. Stryver thought. "I will reject you first."

Consequently, when Mr. Lorry arrived at his offices at ten o'clock that night, Mr. Stryver was surrounded by books and papers, acting as if he had entirely forgotten the morning's conversation. He even acted surprised to see Mr. Lorry, behaving in a distracted and busy manner.

"Well," the good-natured Mr. Lorry said after thirty minutes of trying to bring up the subject. "I have been to Soho."

"To Soho?" Mr. Stryver repeated coldly. "Oh, of course! What was I thinking!"

"And I have no doubt," Mr. Lorry said, "that I was right. My opinion is confirmed, and I repeat my advice."

"I assure you," Mr. Stryver replied in a friendly tone, "that I am sorry to hear it on your account, and on her father’s account. I know this must be a sensitive subject for the family; let us say no more about it."

"I don't understand you," said Mr. Lorry.

"I'm sure you don't," Stryver said, nodding dismissively. "No matter, no matter."

"But it does matter," Mr. Lorry urged.

"No, it doesn't. Having assumed there was good sense and healthy ambition where there is none, I am glad to be corrected. No harm is done. Young women have made similar foolish decisions before and regretted them in poverty. On one hand, I am sorry it is over, because it would have been a good thing for them; on the other hand, I am glad, because it would have been a burden to me. I would have gained nothing from it. No harm is done. I have not proposed, and on reflection, I'm not sure I ever would have. Mr. Lorry, you cannot control the vanity and silliness of empty-headed girls; you must expect to be disappointed. Let's say no more. I regret it for their sake, but I am satisfied for my own. Thank you for giving me your advice; you know the young lady better than I do. You were right, it never would have worked."

Mr. Lorry was so surprised that he stared at Stryver in disbelief as the lawyer politely pushed him toward the door, acting as if he were showing great generosity and goodwill toward him.

"Make the best of it, my dear sir," said Stryver. "Say no more about it. Thank you again, and good night!"

Mr. Lorry found himself out in the street before he realized what had happened. Mr. Stryver lay back on his sofa, winking at the ceiling.

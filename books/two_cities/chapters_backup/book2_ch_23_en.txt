CHAPTER XXIII.
Fire Rises


A change came over the village with the fountain, where the road-mender worked every day, breaking stones on the highway just to earn enough bread to keep his poor body and soul together. The prison on the cliff was no longer as powerful as it used to be. There were soldiers guarding it, but not many, and there were officers to watch the soldiers. However, none of the officers knew what their men would do—except they knew the soldiers probably wouldn't obey orders.

All around, the countryside was ruined and desolate. Every green leaf, blade of grass, and stalk of grain was as shriveled and poor as the miserable people themselves. Everything was beaten down, depressed, oppressed, and broken. The houses, fences, farm animals, men, women, children, and even the soil they lived on were completely worn out.

The aristocrats (who were often perfectly nice gentlemen individually) were supposed to be a blessing to the nation, setting a polite example of luxury and fine living. Yet, as a class, they had somehow brought the country to this state. It was strange that the world, which seemed to be made just for them, was so quickly squeezed dry. Surely, there must have been some mistake in the universe's design! But that was the reality. Now that the last drop of blood had been squeezed from the poorest people, and the screws of taxes and oppression could not be tightened any further without breaking, the aristocrats began to run away from this messy, confusing situation.

But this running away was not the real change in this village, or in the many others like it. For decades, the aristocrats had squeezed the people dry, rarely visiting except to go hunting—sometimes hunting wild animals, and sometimes hunting the people themselves, keeping large areas of land wild and useless just for their sports. No, the real change was the appearance of strange, poor-looking faces in the area, rather than the disappearance of the handsome, elegant faces of the lords.

Lately, as the road-mender worked alone in the dust—not thinking about life and death, but mostly about how little he had for dinner and how much more he would eat if he could—he would look up and see a rough traveler approaching on foot. Such travelers used to be rare, but now they were a common sight. As the figure came closer, the road-mender was not surprised to see a tall, shaggy-haired man who looked almost wild. The man wore wooden shoes that looked clumsy even to the road-mender. He was dirty, dark-skinned, covered in the mud and dust of many roads, damp from marshes, and covered in thorns, leaves, and moss from walking through the woods.

Such a man walked up to him like a ghost at noon in the hot July weather, while the road-mender sat on his pile of stones under a bank, sheltering as best he could from a sudden hailstorm.

The traveler looked at him, then at the village in the valley, at the mill, and at the prison on the cliff. Once he had recognized these landmarks in his simple mind, he spoke in a rough dialect that was barely understandable:

“How is it going, Jacques?”

“All well, Jacques.”

“Join hands then!”

They shook hands, and the man sat down on the pile of stones.

“No lunch?”

“Nothing but dinner tonight,” said the road-mender with a hungry look.

“That's the style these days,” growled the man. “I don't find lunch anywhere.”

He pulled out a blackened pipe, filled it, lit it with flint and steel, and puffed until it glowed brightly. Then, he held it away and dropped a tiny pinch of something from his fingers into the bowl, which flared up and went out in a puff of smoke.

“Join hands then,” said the road-mender, after watching this. They shook hands again.

“Tonight?” asked the road-mender.

“Tonight,” said the man, putting the pipe back in his mouth.

“Where?”

“Here.”

He and the road-mender sat on the pile of stones, staring silently at each other while the hail fell between them like a tiny army of bayonets, until the sky began to clear over the village.

“Show me!” said the traveler, walking to the edge of the hill.

“Look!” the road-mender replied, pointing his finger. “You go down here, straight through the street, and past the fountain—”

“To the devil with all that!” the other interrupted, looking out over the landscape. “_I_ am not going through streets or past fountains. What else?”

“Well, it is about six miles past the top of that hill above the village.”

“Good. When do you finish work?”

“At sunset.”

“Will you wake me before you leave? I have walked for two nights without resting. Let me finish my pipe, and I will sleep like a baby. Will you wake me?”

“Of course.”

The traveler finished smoking, put the pipe in his coat, slipped off his heavy wooden shoes, and lay down on his back on the pile of stones. He fell fast asleep immediately.

As the road-mender continued his dusty work, and the hail-clouds moved away to show bright streaks of sky that lit up the landscape with silver gleams, the little man (who now wore a red cap instead of his blue one) was fascinated by the sleeping figure. He looked at the traveler so often that he used his tools automatically and did very little actual work. The tanned face, the shaggy black hair and beard, the coarse red wool cap, the rough mixture of homespun clothes and animal skins, the strong body thinned by starvation, and the tight, desperate look of the lips in sleep filled the road-mender with awe. The traveler had walked a long way; his feet were sore, and his ankles were raw and bleeding. His large wooden shoes, packed with leaves and grass, must have been heavy to drag for so many miles, and his clothes were as full of holes as his body was of sores. Bending down next to him, the road-mender tried to peek at any weapons hidden in his coat, but could see nothing because the man slept with his arms crossed tightly over his chest, as firm as his closed mouth. To the road-mender, fortified towns with their fences, guardhouses, gates, ditches, and drawbridges seemed useless against a man like this. And when he looked up from the sleeper to the horizon, he imagined thousands of similar men, stopped by no obstacles, marching toward targets all over France.

The man slept on, ignoring the hail and the sunshine, the shadows, and the bits of ice on his body that the sun melted into glittering drops, until the sun was low in the west and the sky was glowing. Then, the road-mender gathered his tools, got ready to go down to the village, and woke him up.

“Good!” said the sleeper, sitting up on his elbow. “About six miles past the top of the hill?”

“About that.”

“About that. Good!”

The road-mender walked home, the wind blowing the dust ahead of him. Soon he was at the fountain, squeezing in among the thin cattle brought there to drink, looking as if he were whispering to them just as he was whispering to the rest of the village. Once the villagers had eaten their poor dinner, they did not go to bed as they usually did, but came back outside and stayed there. A strange epidemic of whispering spread among them, and as they gathered in the dark around the fountain, another strange urge made them all look at the sky in the same direction. Monsieur Gabelle, the local official, became nervous. He went out on his rooftop alone and looked in that direction too. He peered down from behind his chimneys at the gloomy faces around the fountain below, and sent a message to the church official who had the keys, telling him they might need to ring the alarm bell soon.

The night grew darker. The trees surrounding the old chateau, which stood isolated and grand, swayed in a rising wind as if threatening the massive, dark building. Rain poured down the two stone terraces and beat against the great door like a messenger trying to wake the people inside. Drafts of wind blew through the hall, rattling the old spears and knives, and swept up the stairs, shaking the curtains of the bed where the late Marquis used to sleep. From the East, West, North, and South, through the woods, four rough, messy figures stepped heavily through the high grass and snapped branches, walking cautiously to meet in the courtyard. Four lights suddenly appeared there, moved off in different directions, and then everything went dark again.

But not for long. Soon, the chateau began to glow with a strange light of its own. A flickering flame danced behind the front of the building, lighting up the arches, balconies, and windows from within. Then the fire rose higher, growing wider and brighter. Soon, flames burst out of twenty large windows, and the stone faces carved on the walls seemed to wake up and stare out from the fire.

A quiet panic arose inside the house among the few servants left behind. A horse was quickly saddled, and someone rode away. Spurring the horse through the dark, splashing in the mud, the rider stopped at the village fountain, the sweating horse panting at Monsieur Gabelle's door. “Help, Gabelle! Help, everyone!” The church alarm bell began to ring urgently, but no help came. The road-mender and two hundred and fifty of his fellow villagers stood by the fountain with their arms crossed, watching the pillar of fire in the sky. “It must be forty feet high,” they said grimly, and none of them moved a muscle to help.

The rider from the chateau, on his sweating horse, clattered out of the village and galloped up the rocky path to the prison on the cliff. At the gate, a group of officers stood watching the fire, and a little distance away, a group of soldiers watched too. “Help, officers! The chateau is on fire! We can save valuable things if we act now! Help!” The officers looked at the soldiers, who just stared at the fire. The officers gave no orders and, shrugging their shoulders and biting their lips, replied, “It must burn.”

As the rider rode back down the hill and through the street, the village was lighting up in celebration. The road-mender and his two hundred and fifty friends, all sharing the same idea, had run into their houses to put candles in every small window pane. Because candles were scarce like everything else, they demanded to borrow them from Monsieur Gabelle. When the official hesitated to give them up, the road-mender—who had once been so obedient—remarked that carriages made great bonfires and carriage-horses would roast nicely.

The chateau was left alone to burn. In the roaring heat of the fire, a scorching wind, looking as if it blew straight from hell, seemed to tear at the building. As the flames flickered, the stone faces carved on the walls looked as if they were crying out in pain. When huge chunks of stone and wood fell, the face with the two dents in the nose was hidden for a moment, then peeked out of the smoke again, looking like the face of the cruel Marquis himself, burning at the stake and fighting the fire.

The chateau burned. The nearest trees caught fire and shriveled up, while trees further away, set on fire by the four mysterious men, surrounded the burning building with a thick forest of smoke. Melted lead and iron bubbled in the marble fountain basin. The water dried up completely. The pointed roofs of the towers melted like ice in the heat, dripping down into four fiery pits. Giant cracks split the solid walls, birds flew in confused circles before falling into the heat, and the four fierce men walked away to the East, West, North, and South along the dark roads, guided by the massive light they had created. In the lit-up village, the people took over the church bell, kicked out the official bell-ringer, and rang the bell in celebration.

Not only that, but the villagers, dizzy from hunger, excitement, and bell-ringing, remembered that Monsieur Gabelle was the one who collected rents and taxes—even though he had collected very little lately. They wanted to speak with him, so they surrounded his house and shouted for him to come out. Monsieur Gabelle quickly locked and barred his doors and sat down to think about what to do. His decision was to climb back onto his roof behind the chimneys. He resolved that if the crowd broke down his door, he—being a small man from the south of France with a stubborn streak—would throw himself off the roof to crush one or two of them on his way down.

Monsieur Gabelle spent a very long night up there, using the burning chateau in the distance as his light, and the pounding on his door and the joyous bell-ringing as his music. Outside his gate, a street lantern hung across the road, and the villagers were dropping hints that they would love to hang him from it. It was a terrifying way to spend a summer night, standing on the edge of death, ready to jump if he had to. But when morning finally came and the villagers' candles burned out, the crowd dispersed, and Monsieur Gabelle was able to climb down, safe for the moment.

Within a hundred miles, by the light of other fires, other officials were not as lucky. That night and over the next few nights, the morning sun found many of them hanging dead over the streets where they had grown up. On the other side, some villagers were less fortunate than the road-mender and his friends, as soldiers and officials caught them and hanged them instead. But the mysterious men continued their march to the East, West, North, and South. No matter who was hanged, the fires of rebellion kept burning, and no official could figure out how to put them out.

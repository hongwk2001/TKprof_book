CHAPTER IV.
Congratulatory

From the dimly lit hallways of the courthouse, the last of the crowd that had been gathered there all day was finally filtering out. Doctor Manette, Lucie Manette, Mr. Lorry, the defense solicitor, and their lead lawyer, Mr. Stryver, stood gathered around Charles Darnay—who had just been released—congratulating him on his narrow escape from death.

Even in much brighter light, it would have been hard to recognize the old shoemaker from the Paris attic in the intellectual-looking and upright Doctor Manette. Yet no one could look at him once without looking again. He still spoke in a sad, grave voice and would occasionally fall into a quiet, distracted state for no apparent reason. While referencing his long years of suffering in prison would always trigger this state—as it had during the trial—it could also happen on its own, casting a shadow over him. To anyone who did not know his story, it was as puzzling as if they were seeing the shadow of the Bastille prison fall upon him in the bright summer sun, even though the prison itself was hundreds of miles away.

Only his daughter had the power to chase away these dark moods. She was the golden thread that connected him to a past and a present beyond his years of misery. The sound of her voice, the look on her face, and the touch of her hand almost always had a powerful, healing effect on him. It was not absolutely always, as there were a few times when her influence had failed, but those moments were rare, and she believed they were behind them.

Mr. Darnay kissed her hand with deep gratitude, then turned to thank Mr. Stryver. Stryver was a man of just over thirty, but he looked twenty years older. He was heavily built, loud, red-faced, blunt, and completely lacking in politeness or tact. He had a pushy habit of forcing his way into groups and conversations, which suggested he would have no trouble pushing his way up in the world.

Still wearing his wig and gown, he stood broad-shouldered before his former client, crowding the group so much that he squeezed the gentle Mr. Lorry right out of the circle. "I am glad to have cleared your name, Mr. Darnay," he said. "It was an infamous prosecution, absolutely disgraceful, but that only made it more likely to succeed."

"You have put me in your debt for life, in more ways than one," said Darnay, shaking his hand.

"I did my best for you, Mr. Darnay, and my best is as good as anyone else's, I believe."

Since someone clearly needed to say "Much better," Mr. Lorry said it, partly in the hope of squeezing himself back into the group.

"Do you think so?" said Mr. Stryver. "Well, you were here all day, so you ought to know. You are a businessman, too."

"And as a businessman," said Mr. Lorry, whom the lawyer had now let back into the group, "I appeal to Doctor Manette to end this meeting and let us all go home. Miss Lucie looks ill, Mr. Darnay has had a terrible day, and we are all exhausted."

"Speak for yourself, Mr. Lorry," said Stryver. "I still have a whole night's work ahead of me."

"I am speaking for myself," answered Mr. Lorry, "and for Mr. Darnay, and for Miss Lucie. Miss Lucie, don't you agree I should speak for us all?" He asked her directly, with a glance at her father.

Doctor Manette's face had frozen into a strange look as he stared at Darnay. It was an intense look that deepened into a frown of dislike and distrust, mixed with a hint of fear. With this strange expression on his face, his mind seemed to wander far away.

"Father," Lucie said softly, laying her hand on his arm.

He slowly shook off the dark mood and turned to her.

"Shall we go home, father?"

Taking a deep breath, he answered, "Yes."

The friends of the acquitted prisoner had already left, believing Darnay would not be released until the next morning. Most of the lights in the hallways were turned off, the iron gates were closing with a loud rattle, and the gloomy courthouse was deserted until the next day's crowd arrived for more hangings, whippings, and brandings. Walking between her father and Mr. Darnay, Lucie Manette walked out into the fresh air. A carriage was called, and the father and daughter drove away.

Mr. Stryver had left them to head back to the dressing room. Another person, who had not joined the group or spoken to anyone, had been leaning against the wall in the darkest shadow. He quietly walked out after them and watched until the carriage drove off. Now he stepped up to where Mr. Lorry and Mr. Darnay stood on the pavement.

"So, Mr. Lorry! Can businessmen speak to Mr. Darnay now?"

Nobody had thanked Mr. Carton for his help during the trial; indeed, nobody seemed to know he had done anything. He had taken off his lawyer's robes and looked no better for it.

"If you knew the conflict that goes on in a businessman's head, torn between a kind impulse and maintaining a professional appearance, you would be amused, Mr. Darnay," Carton said.

Mr. Lorry reddened and said warmly, "You have mentioned that before, sir. We businessmen who serve a firm are not our own masters. We must think of the bank's reputation before ourselves."

"_I_ know, _I_ know," Carton said carelessly. "Don't be upset, Mr. Lorry. I'm sure you are as good as anyone else, probably better."

"And indeed, sir," Mr. Lorry continued, ignoring the comment, "I really don't see what you have to do with this matter. If you will excuse me for saying so, since I am much older than you, I don't think it is any of your business."

"Business! Bless you, _I_ have no business," said Mr. Carton.

"It is a pity you don't, sir."

"I think so, too."

"If you did," Mr. Lorry added, "perhaps you would attend to it."

"Lord help you, no, I wouldn't!" said Mr. Carton.

"Well, sir!" cried Mr. Lorry, thoroughly annoyed by Carton's indifference. "Business is a very good and respectable thing. And if business requires us to be cautious and silent, Mr. Darnay is a generous young gentleman who understands that. Mr. Darnay, good night, and God bless you! I hope you have been saved today for a long and happy life. Carriage, over here!"

Perhaps a little angry with himself as well as with Carton, Mr. Lorry hurried into his carriage and was driven back to Tellson's Bank. Carton, who smelled of port wine and did not seem entirely sober, laughed and turned to Darnay.

"This is a strange twist of fate that brings us together. It must be a strange night for you, standing alone on the street with your double."

"I hardly feel like I belong to this world yet," Charles Darnay replied.

"I don't wonder. It wasn't long ago that you were on your way to the next world. You speak very quietly."

"I think I _am_ about to faint."

"Then why the devil don't you eat? I dined while those fools were deciding whether you should live or die. Let me show you the nearest tavern where you can get a good meal."

Taking Darnay's arm, Carton led him down Ludgate Hill to Fleet Street, and up a covered path into a tavern. They were shown into a small room, where Darnay began to regain his strength with a plain dinner and good wine. Carton sat opposite him at the table with his own bottle of port, maintaining his rude, half-insolent manner.

"Do you feel like you belong to this world again, Mr. Darnay?"

"I am terribly confused about the time and place, but I feel much better now."

"That must be a wonderful feeling!" Carton said bitterly, refilling his glass. "As for me, my greatest wish is to forget that I belong to this world. There is nothing good in it for me except wine like this, and I do no good for it. So we are not very alike in that way. In fact, I'm beginning to think we aren't alike in any way, you and I."

Confused by the emotions of the day and feeling that sitting with this coarse double was like a dream, Charles Darnay did not know how to respond and remained silent.

"Now that you've finished your dinner," Carton said, "why don't you toast a health, Mr. Darnay? Why don't you give a toast?"

"What toast?"

"It's on the tip of your tongue. I know it's there."

"Miss Manette, then!" said Darnay.

"Miss Manette, then!" Carton repeated.

Looking Darnay straight in the face as he drank, Carton threw his empty glass over his shoulder against the wall, shattering it to pieces. Then he rang the bell and ordered another.

"She is a beautiful young lady to help into a carriage in the dark, Mr. Darnay!" he said, filling his new glass.

Darnay frowned slightly and answered with a quiet, "Yes."

"She is a beautiful young lady to be pitied and wept for! How does it feel? Is it worth being tried for your life to receive such sympathy, Mr. Darnay?"

Again, Darnay did not answer.

"She was very pleased to receive your message when I gave it to her. Not that she showed it, but I assume she was."

This reminded Darnay that, despite his unpleasant attitude, Carton had voluntarily helped save him that day. Darnay changed the subject to thank him.

"I don't want any thanks, and I don't deserve any," Carton replied carelessly. "It was nothing to do in the first place, and I don't know why I did it in the second. Mr. Darnay, let me ask you a question."

"Go ahead."

"Do you think I like you?"

"Really, Mr. Carton," replied Darnay, feeling uncomfortable, "I haven't asked myself that question."

"Well, ask yourself now."

"You have acted as if you do, but I don't think you actually do."

"_I_ don't think I do either," said Carton. "I'm beginning to have a high opinion of your intelligence."

"Even so," said Darnay, rising to ring for the bill, "I hope that won't prevent me from paying for the dinner and parting as friends."

Carton agreed, saying, "Not at all." Darnay paid the bill. "Are you paying for the whole thing?" Carton asked. When Darnay said yes, Carton called to the waiter, "Bring me another pint of this wine, and wake me up at ten o'clock."

With the bill paid, Charles Darnay rose and wished him good night. Without returning the greeting, Carton stood up with a challenging look and said, "One last word, Mr. Darnay: do you think I am drunk?"

"I think you have been drinking, Mr. Carton."

"Think? You know I have."

"Since you insist, yes, I know it."

"Then you should also know why. I am a disappointed, hard-working drudge, sir. I care for no one on earth, and no one on earth cares for me."

"That is very regrettable. You could have used your talents better."

"Maybe so, Mr. Darnay, maybe not. But don't let your sober face make you feel too proud; you don't know what you might become. Good night!"

Left alone, Carton took a candle, went to a mirror on the wall, and studied his face closely.

"Do you like this man?" he muttered to his own reflection. "Why should you like a man who looks like you? There is nothing in you to like, and you know it. Ah, damn you! What a waste you've made of your life! It's a fine reason to like a man because he shows you what you used to be and what you could have been! If you changed places with him, would those blue eyes look at you with such pity and love? Let's be honest about it: you hate him."

He drank the rest of his wine in a few minutes to comfort himself and fell asleep with his head on his arms, his hair messy on the table, as the candle melted down beside him.

CHAPTER II.
The Grindstone


Tellson’s Bank in Paris was located in a wing of a large house, accessible through a courtyard and shut off from the street by a high wall and a strong gate. The house belonged to a great nobleman who had lived there until he fled the revolution, disguised in his cook's clothes, and crossed the border. Running away like a wild animal fleeing hunters, he was still the same proud lord who once required three strong men besides that cook just to prepare his morning chocolate.

With the lord gone, and his three servants clearing their consciences of serving him by declaring themselves ready to cut his throat for the new Republic of Liberty, Equality, Fraternity, or Death, the nobleman's house was first seized and then taken by the government. Everything moved so fast, and new laws were passed so quickly, that by the night of September third, revolutionary officials were occupying the house, marking it with the tri-color flag, and drinking brandy in its elegant rooms.

If the London office of Tellson's had looked like the Paris branch, it would have driven the British managers crazy and forced them into bankruptcy. For what would serious, respectable British businessmen think of orange trees in boxes in the courtyard, or a statue of Cupid over the counter? Yet those things existed in Paris. Tellson's had painted over the Cupid, but it could still be seen on the ceiling, aiming its bow at money from morning to night. In serious London, such a decoration, along with a curtained alcove in the back, a mirror on the wall, and young clerks who danced in public, would have spelled ruin. Yet the French branch of Tellson's got along with these features just fine, and as long as times were stable, no one had been scared into withdrawing their money.

No one that night could say how much money would be withdrawn from Tellson's from now on, or how much would lie there lost and forgotten; what gold and jewels would tarnish in the bank's vaults while their owners rotted in prisons or died violent deaths; or how many accounts would never be settled in this world. Mr. Jarvis Lorry couldn't answer these questions either, though he was thinking deeply about them. He sat by a newly lit wood fire—for the poor harvest year had brought an early winter cold—and his honest face showed a deep shadow of horror that was not caused by the hanging lamp or any reflection in the room.

In his loyalty to the bank, Mr. Lorry lived in rooms inside the building, having become as much a part of the place as ivy on a wall. It happened that his rooms were somewhat protected because the patriots had taken over the main building, but the honest old gentleman never thought about his own safety. He only cared about doing his duty. On the opposite side of the courtyard, under a roofed walkway, was a parking area for carriages, where some of the nobleman's carriages still sat. Two large, burning torches were tied to the pillars, and in their light, out in the open air, stood a large grindstone. It was a rough machine that looked like it had been hurriedly brought there from a nearby blacksmith's shop. Standing up and looking out the window at these objects, Mr. Lorry shivered and went back to his seat by the fire. He had opened both the glass window and the wooden shutter outside, closed them both again, and shuddered from head to toe.

From the streets outside the high wall and the gate came the usual night sounds of the city, but with a strange, eerie tone now and then, as if terrible, unusual noises were rising up to the sky.

“Thank God,” said Mr. Lorry, folding his hands, “that no one I love is in this dreadful town tonight. May God have mercy on everyone in danger!”

Soon after, the gate bell rang, and he thought, “They have come back!” and sat listening. But there was no loud crowd rushing into the courtyard as he had expected; he heard the gate shut again, and everything went quiet.

His nervousness and fear made him anxious about the bank's security, which was a natural worry in such times. The bank was well guarded, and he stood up to check on the guards when his door suddenly opened and two people rushed in. At the sight of them, he stepped back in absolute shock.

It was Lucie and her father! Lucie held her arms out to him with a look of intense, desperate seriousness, looking as if her face had been molded just to show this strength at this single moment of her life.

“What is this?” cried Mr. Lorry, breathless and confused. “What is the matter? Lucie! Manette! What happened? What brought you here? What is going on?”

Staring at him wildly, pale and terrified, she gasped in his arms, pleading, “Oh, my dear friend! My husband!”

“Your husband, Lucie?”

“Charles.”

“What about Charles?”

“Here.”

“Here, in Paris?”

“He has been here a few days—three or four, I don't know, I can't think straight. A generous mission brought him here without our knowledge. He was stopped at the border and sent to prison.”

The old man let out a cry he couldn't stop. Almost at the same instant, the gate bell rang again, and the sound of many feet and voices poured into the courtyard.

“What is that noise?” the Doctor asked, turning toward the window.

“Don't look!” Mr. Lorry cried. “Don't look out! Manette, for your life, don't touch the shutter!”

The Doctor turned, with his hand on the window lock, and said with a calm, confident smile:

“My dear friend, I am safe in this city. I was a prisoner in the Bastille. There is no patriot in Paris—or in all of France—who would hurt me if they knew that. Instead, they would hug me or carry me in celebration. My past suffering has given me a status that brought us through the border, got us news of Charles, and brought us here. I knew it would be this way. I knew I could save Charles from danger, and I told Lucie so. What is that noise?” His hand went back to the window.

“Don't look!” Mr. Lorry cried out in desperation. “No, Lucie, my dear, don't you look either!” He wrapped his arm around her and held her tight. “Don't be so scared, my love. I swear to you that I don't know of any harm coming to Charles, and I had no idea he was even in this dangerous city. Which prison is he in?”

“La Force!”

“La Force! Lucie, my child, if you have ever been brave and helpful—and you have always been both—you must calm down now and do exactly as I tell you. More depends on this than you can imagine. You cannot help him by doing anything tonight; you cannot go outside. I say this because what I must ask you to do for Charles's sake is the hardest thing of all: you must be obedient, still, and quiet. Let me put you in a back room. You must leave your father and me alone for two minutes. Since this is a matter of life and death, you must not delay.”

“I will do as you say. I can see in your face that you know I have no other choice. I know you are a true friend.”

The old man kissed her, hurried her into his bedroom, and locked the door. Then he ran back to the Doctor, opened the window, pushed the shutter slightly open, put his hand on the Doctor's arm, and looked out with him into the courtyard.

They saw a crowd of about forty or fifty men and women. The patriots occupying the building had let them through the gate, and they had rushed in to use the grindstone. It had been set up there for them, as it was a convenient and private spot.

But what terrifying workers they were, doing such a terrifying job!

The grindstone had handles on both sides, spun madly by two men. Their long hair flew back as they turned, revealing faces more horrible and cruel than those of wild warriors in war paint. They had glued on fake eyebrows and mustaches, and their ugly faces were covered in sweat and blood, twisted as they howled, their eyes wide with animal excitement and lack of sleep. As these thugs turned the stone, their messy hair flying about, some women held wine to their lips to let them drink. Between the dripping blood, the spilling wine, and the sparks flying from the stone, the whole scene seemed made of fire and blood. Not a single person in the crowd was free from bloodstains. Pushing each other to get to the stone next were men shirtless to the waist, with blood smeared all over their bodies; men in rags stained with blood; and men wearing stolen women's lace, silk, and ribbons, all dyed with blood. Hatchets, knives, bayonets, and swords brought to be sharpened were all covered in red. Some of the bent swords were tied to the owners' wrists with strips of cloth torn from clothing—all stained the same deep red color. As the frantic people grabbed their weapons from the sparks and ran back into the streets, their eyes glowed with the same red madness. Any normal person watching would have given twenty years of life to stop them with a gun.

All of this was seen in a single second, like the quick flash of images a drowning man sees. They stepped back from the window, and the Doctor looked at his friend's pale face for an explanation.

“They are,” Mr. Lorry whispered, looking fearfully toward the locked bedroom door, “murdering the prisoners. If you are sure of what you said—if you really have the influence you think you have, and I believe you do—go show yourself to these monsters and have them take you to La Force. It might be too late already, but don't waste another second!”

Doctor Manette squeezed his hand, ran out of the room without a hat, and was already in the courtyard by the time Mr. Lorry reached the shutter again.

His flowing white hair, his striking face, and his confident manner as he pushed the weapons aside allowed him to reach the center of the crowd by the grindstone immediately. For a few moments, the crowd paused, murmured, and listened to his voice. Then Mr. Lorry saw the Doctor, surrounded by the crowd and walking in the middle of a line of twenty men holding hands and shoulders, being hurried out. The crowd shouted, “Long live the Bastille prisoner! Help the Bastille prisoner's relative in La Force! Make way for the Bastille prisoner! Save the prisoner Evremonde at La Force!” along with thousands of answering cheers.

With a beating heart, Mr. Lorry closed the shutter, the window, and the curtain. He ran to Lucie and told her that her father had the help of the crowd and had gone to find her husband. He found her child and Miss Pross with her, though it did not occur to him to wonder how they had arrived until much later, when he sat watching them in the quiet night.

By that time, Lucie had collapsed onto the floor at his feet, holding his hand in a daze. Miss Pross had put the child to sleep on his bed and rested her head on the pillow next to her. It was a long, terrible night, filled with the quiet cries of the poor wife, with no sign of her father and no news of Charles!

Twice more during the night, the gate bell rang, the crowd rushed in, and the grindstone spun and sparked again. “What is that?” Lucie asked in terror. “Hush! They are sharpening the soldiers' swords,” Mr. Lorry said. “The building belongs to the nation now, and they are using it as a workshop, my love.”

Those were the only times, and the last session of sharpening was short and quiet. Soon dawn broke, and Mr. Lorry gently let go of Lucie's hand and looked out the window again. A man covered in blood and dirt, looking like a wounded soldier waking up on a battlefield, got up from the ground near the grindstone and looked around blankly. Soon, this exhausted killer saw one of the nobleman's carriages parked in the yard. Staggering over to the fancy carriage, he climbed inside and shut the door to sleep on its soft cushions.

The earth had turned, and the sun cast a red glow over the courtyard when Mr. Lorry looked out again. But the other grindstone stood alone in the quiet morning air, stained with a red color that the sun had not created, and would never wash away.

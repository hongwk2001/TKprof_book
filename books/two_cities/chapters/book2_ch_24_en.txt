CHAPTER XXIV.
Drawn to the Loadstone Rock


Three years of storm passed with these rising fires and rising seas—the steady earth shaken by the rushes of an angry ocean that never ebbed but kept rising higher and higher, terrifying and amazing everyone on the shore. During this time, three more birthdays of little Lucie were woven by the golden thread into the peaceful life of her home.

Many a night and day, the family listened to the echoes in the corner of their house, their hearts sinking when they heard the sound of marching feet. In their minds, those footsteps had become the steps of a rioting mob under a red flag, their country declared in danger, transformed into wild beasts by a terrible spell of long-standing anger.

The French aristocrats, as a class, had run away from the fact that they were no longer wanted in France, where they faced the danger of being kicked out of the country and of life itself. Like the legendary peasant who worked hard to summon the Devil but was so terrified when he appeared that he fled without asking a single question, the aristocrats, after spending years doing everything to bring about disaster, ran away in fear the moment they saw the monster they had created.

The glittering court of the King was gone, or else it would have been targeted by a storm of bullets. It had never been a wise or observant court anyway—it had long been blinded by pride, luxury, and ignorance—but now it was completely gone. The royal court, from its inner circle to its outer rings of lies, corruption, and plotting, had vanished. The monarchy was gone, having been besieged in the palace and suspended, according to the latest news.

By August of 1792, the French aristocrats were scattered far and wide.

Naturally, the main gathering place for these exiled French lords in London was Tellson's Bank. Just as ghosts are said to haunt the places they frequented in life, these penniless aristocrats hung around the place where their money used to be kept. It was also the spot where the most reliable news from France arrived quickest. Tellson's was very generous to its old customers who had lost their wealth. Furthermore, the nobles who had foreseen the storm and sent their money to Tellson's in advance could be found there, helping their needy friends. Additionally, almost every new traveler arriving from France reported to Tellson's to share their news. For all these reasons, Tellson's was the central hub for French news, and so many people came to ask questions that the bank sometimes wrote the latest updates on a paper and posted them in the windows for anyone walking by Temple Bar to read.

On a hot, misty afternoon, Mr. Lorry sat at his desk while Charles Darnay stood leaning on it, talking to him quietly. The gloomy room once used for private meetings was now packed with people exchanging news. It was about half an hour before closing time.

“Even though you are the most youthful-spirited man alive,” Charles Darnay said, hesitating a bit, “I still have to suggest to you—”

“I understand. You think I am too old?” said Mr. Lorry.

“Bad weather, a long journey, unreliable transport, a chaotic country, and a city that might not even be safe for you.”

“My dear Charles,” Mr. Lorry said with cheerful confidence, “those are the exact reasons I must go, not stay. I will be safe enough. No one will bother an old man of nearly eighty when there are so many other people worth targeting. As for the chaos, if the city weren't chaotic, we wouldn't need to send someone from our London office to our Paris branch who knows the place and the business, and whom Tellson's trusts completely. As for the difficult travel, the long journey, and the winter weather, if I am not willing to face a few hardships for Tellson's after all these years, who should be?”

“I wish I were going myself,” Charles Darnay said restlessly, as if thinking aloud.

“Really! You are a fine one to complain and give advice!” Mr. Lorry exclaimed. “You wish you were going? A born Frenchman? You are a very wise adviser indeed!”

“My dear Mr. Lorry, it is precisely because I am a Frenchman that the thought has crossed my mind so often, though I didn't mean to say it aloud. Having felt sympathy for the poor people and having given up my property to them,” he spoke thoughtfully, “I cannot help but think that they might listen to me. I might be able to persuade them to show some mercy. Only last night, after you left, I was talking to Lucie—”

“Talking to Lucie,” Mr. Lorry repeated. “Yes! I'm surprised you're not ashamed to mention her name while wishing you were going to France at a time like this!”

“Well, I am not going,” Charles Darnay said with a smile. “It is more important that you are.”

“And I really am. The truth is, my dear Charles,” Mr. Lorry said, looking toward the other bank managers and lowering his voice, “you have no idea how hard it is to do business there, or how much danger our records and papers are in. God only knows what terrible trouble it would cause for many people if our documents were seized or destroyed. And that could happen at any moment—who knows if Paris will be set on fire today or looted tomorrow! Choosing the key papers quickly, burying them, or hiding them safely is something only I can really do without wasting time. Should I refuse, when Tellson's needs me—Tellson's, who has paid my salary for sixty years—just because my joints are a little stiff? Why, I'm a boy compared to half the old men working here!”

“I really admire your youthful courage, Mr. Lorry.”

“Nonsense, sir! And, my dear Charles,” Mr. Lorry said, looking back at the bank managers, “you must remember that getting anything out of Paris right now is almost impossible. Just today, papers and valuables were brought to us here—I tell you this in strict confidence, as it's not professional to gossip, even to you—by the strangest messengers, who were in constant danger of losing their heads as they crossed the borders. Normally, our mail would come and go as easily as in England, but now, everything is blocked.”

“And are you really leaving tonight?”

“I really am, because the situation has become too urgent to wait.”

“Are you going alone?”

“Many people were suggested to me, but I refused them all. I plan to take Jerry. Jerry has been my Sunday night bodyguard for a long time, and I am used to him. No one will suspect Jerry of being anything other than an English bulldog, ready to attack anyone who touches his master.”

“I must say again that I truly admire your youthfulness and courage.”

“And I must say again, nonsense! Once I finish this little job, maybe I will accept Tellson's offer to retire and live comfortably. That will be the time to think about getting old.”

This conversation took place at Mr. Lorry's desk, with French aristocrats gathering just a few feet away, boasting about how they would soon get revenge on the common people. It was typical of the refugees, and of the conservative English public, to talk about this terrible Revolution as if it were a disaster that had come out of nowhere—as if nothing had ever been done to cause it. They ignored the fact that anyone who had observed the starving millions in France and the greed of the rulers had seen it coming years ago and warned about it in plain words. Hearing these empty boasts and their unrealistic plans to restore the old, broken system was hard to endure without speaking up. All this chatter, combined with a deep uneasiness in his own mind, made Charles Darnay feel very restless.

Among the talkers was Stryver, the ambitious lawyer, who was speaking loudly to the aristocrats. He was explaining his ideas for blowing up the French peasants and wiping them off the face of the earth, which was about as realistic as trying to catch eagles by putting salt on their tails. Darnay listened to him with disgust. He was torn between walking away to avoid hearing any more, and staying to speak up, when the next event began to unfold.

One of the bank managers came over to Mr. Lorry and laid a dirty, unopened letter on the desk, asking if he had found any trace of the person to whom it was addressed. The manager placed the letter so close to Darnay that he could read the address—and he recognized it immediately because it was his real name. The address read:

“Very urgent. To Monsieur, formerly the Marquis St. Evremonde, of France. Care of Messrs. Tellson and Co., Bankers, London, England.”

On the morning of his wedding, Doctor Manette had earnestly requested Charles Darnay to keep the secret of his real family name between the two of them. No one else knew it. Even his wife had no idea, and Mr. Lorry certainly did not know.

“No,” Mr. Lorry replied to the manager. “I have asked almost everyone here, and no one knows where this gentleman can be found.”

As the bank's closing hour approached, the crowd of talkers began to move past Mr. Lorry's desk. He held up the letter to show them. One angry refugee after another looked at it, and each had something insulting to say, in French or in English, about the missing Marquis.

“He is the nephew—or at least the worthless successor—of the refined Marquis who was murdered,” said one. “I am glad to say I never knew him.”

“A coward who abandoned his position years ago,” said another—a man who had himself escaped Paris hidden under a load of hay, upside down and half suffocated.

“Infected with those new revolutionary ideas,” said a third, looking at the address through his glass as he passed. “He opposed the late Marquis, abandoned his estate when he inherited it, and left it to the mob. I hope they reward him now as he deserves.”

“Hey?” cried the loud Stryver. “Did he really? Is that the kind of guy he is? Let me see his shameful name. Damn him!”

Darnay, unable to control himself any longer, touched Mr. Stryver on the shoulder and said:

“I know him.”

“Do you, by Jove?” said Stryver. “I am sorry to hear that.”

“Why?”

“Why, Mr. Darnay? Did you hear what he did? Don't ask why in times like these.”

“But I do ask why.”

“Then I tell you again, Mr. Darnay, I am sorry. I am sorry to hear you asking such strange questions. Here is a man who, influenced by the most wicked and ungodly ideas ever known, gave up his property to the worst scum of the earth who murder people wholesale. And you ask me why I'm sorry that a teacher of youth knows him? Well, I will tell you. I'm sorry because I think such a scoundrel is contagious. That's why.”

Remembering his secret, Darnay controlled himself with great effort and said, “You might not understand the gentleman.”

“I know how to put _you_ in a corner, Mr. Darnay,” Stryver bullied, “and I'll do it. If that fellow is a gentleman, then I _don't_ understand him. You can tell him so, with my compliments. You can also tell him from me that since he gave up his wealth and status to this murderous mob, I wonder he isn't leading them. But no, gentlemen,” Stryver said, looking around and snapping his fingers, “I know a bit about human nature, and I tell you that you'll never find a guy like him trusting himself to the mercy of those people. No, gentlemen; he'll run away at the first sign of trouble and hide.”

With those words and a final snap of his fingers, Mr. Stryver pushed his way out into Fleet Street, to the general approval of the listeners. Mr. Lorry and Charles Darnay were left alone at the desk as everyone else left the bank.

“Will you take care of this letter?” Mr. Lorry asked. “Do you know where to deliver it?”

“I do.”

“Will you explain to him that we think it was sent here in the hope that we would know where to find him, and that it has been sitting here for a while?”

“I will. Are you leaving for Paris from here?”

“From here, at eight o'clock.”

“I will come back to see you off.”

Feeling very uncomfortable with himself, Stryver, and everyone else, Darnay walked back to the quiet of the Temple, opened the letter, and read it. This is what it said:

“Abbaye Prison, Paris.”

“June 21, 1792. “MONSIEUR, FORMERLY THE MARQUIS.

“After being in danger for a long time at the hands of the villagers, I have been arrested with great violence and insult, and forced to walk all the way to Paris. I suffered terribly on the road. Not only that, but my house has been completely destroyed and flattened to the ground.

“The crime for which I am locked up, Monsieur, and for which I will be tried and executed (unless you generously help me), is, they say, treason against the people because I managed the estate of an exile. It does no good for me to tell them that I acted in their favor, not against them, following your orders. It does no good for me to explain that, before the exile properties were seized, I had canceled the taxes they stopped paying, collected no rents, and took no legal action against anyone. The only answer I get is that I worked for an exile, and they ask: where is that exile?

“Ah, most gracious Monsieur, where is that exile? I cry out in my sleep, asking where he is. I ask Heaven: will he not come to save me? There is no answer. Ah, Monsieur, I send my desperate cry across the sea, hoping it might reach your ears through the famous bank of Tellson which is known in Paris!

“For the love of Heaven, of justice, of generosity, and for the honor of your noble family name, I beg you, Monsieur, to help and free me. My only crime is that I have been faithful to you. Oh, Monsieur, I pray that you will be faithful to me!

“From this horrible prison, where every hour brings me closer to death, I send you, Monsieur, my sad and miserable respects.

“Your suffering servant,

“Gabelle.”

The quiet anxiety in Darnay's mind flared into intense life upon reading this letter. The danger facing an old and loyal servant, whose only crime was loyalty to him and his family, felt like a direct accusation. As he walked back and forth in the Temple thinking about what to do, he was so ashamed that he almost hid his face from passersby.

He knew very well that in his horror at the family's past cruelty and bad reputation, in his hatred for his uncle, and in his desire to have nothing to do with the estate he was supposed to inherit, he had acted imperfectly. He knew that because of his love for Lucie, his decision to give up his title and property—though not a new idea to him—had been rushed and incomplete. He knew he should have systematically managed the transition and supervised it, and though he had intended to, he had never actually done it.

The happiness of his new English home, the need to stay busy, and the rapid pace of events in France—where the news of one week destroyed the plans of the week before, and the following week changed everything again—had overwhelmed him. He had let things drift, feeling uneasy but not doing anything to stop it. He had waited for the right moment to take action, but the situation had changed so fast that the opportunity had passed. Now, the nobles were fleeing France along every road, their properties were being seized and destroyed, and their very names were being erased—facts he knew just as well as the new French authorities who might accuse him of abandonment.

Yet, he had oppressed no one and imprisoned no one. Far from demanding high taxes or rents, he had given them up voluntarily, started a new life in England with no special help, built his own career, and earned his own living. Monsieur Gabelle had managed the poor estate based on Darnay's written instructions to spare the people and give them whatever they could—such as winter firewood and summer crops. Gabelle would surely use these instructions to prove his innocence, so the truth would have to come out.

This supported the desperate decision Charles Darnay was starting to make: he would go to Paris.

Yes. Like the sailor in the old story, the winds and currents had driven him into the magnetic pull of the Loadstone Rock, and it was drawing him in. He had to go. Every thought pushed him faster and more steadily toward this dangerous attraction. His hidden guilt had been that bad deeds were being done in France by bad people, while he—who knew he had better intentions—was not there to try to stop the bloodshed and call for mercy. With this guilt troubling him, he had compared himself to the brave old Mr. Lorry, whose sense of duty was so strong. Right after that embarrassing comparison, he had been stung by the insults of the refugees and Stryver's coarse sneers. And finally, Gabelle's letter had arrived: an appeal from an innocent prisoner in danger of death, calling on his justice, honor, and family name.

His decision was made. He had to go to Paris.

Yes, the Loadstone Rock was pulling him, and he had to sail on until he reached it. He didn't see the danger. He believed that his good intentions, even if incomplete, would be recognized and appreciated in France once he arrived to explain them. Then, the idealistic dream of doing good—which often misleads well-meaning people—filled his mind, and he even imagined himself having the influence to guide this wild, raging Revolution.

As he walked back and forth, he decided that neither Lucie nor her father must know about his plans until he had left. Lucie should be spared the pain of a long goodbye, and her father, who always avoided thinking about his painful past in France, should only hear about the journey after it had already begun, to avoid any suspense or worry. Darnay did not admit to himself how much of his delay in sorting out his French estate had been due to his fear of upsetting Doctor Manette. But that had certainly played a part in his inaction.

He walked back and forth, his mind racing, until it was time to return to Tellson's and say goodbye to Mr. Lorry. As soon as he got to Paris, he would meet his old friend, but he could say nothing about his plans now.

A carriage with fresh horses was ready at the bank door, and Jerry stood booted and ready.

“I delivered that letter,” Charles Darnay said to Mr. Lorry. “I didn't want you to carry a written reply, but perhaps you can deliver a verbal message?”

“I will gladly do that,” Mr. Lorry said, “if it isn't dangerous.”

“Not at all. Though it is for a prisoner in the Abbaye.”

“What is his name?” Mr. Lorry asked, opening his notebook.

“Gabelle.”

“Gabelle. And what is the message for poor Gabelle in prison?”

“Simply: ‘He received the letter and will come.’”

“Did he mention when?”

“He will start his journey tomorrow night.”

“Did he mention any name?”

“No.”

He helped Mr. Lorry wrap himself in several coats and cloaks, and walked out with him from the warm bank into the misty air of Fleet Street. “Give my love to Lucie and little Lucie,” Mr. Lorry said as they parted, “and take good care of them until I return.” Charles Darnay shook his head and smiled doubtfully as the carriage drove off.

That night—it was August fourteenth—he stayed up late and wrote two emotional letters. One was to Lucie, explaining the heavy duty that forced him to go to Paris and explaining in detail why he was confident he would not be in any personal danger. The other was to the Doctor, entrusting Lucie and their child to his care and repeating the same strong reassurances. He promised both of them that he would send letters proving his safety as soon as he arrived.

It had been a difficult day, spending it with his family while keeping this major secret from them for the first time in their marriage. It was hard to keep up the innocent deception, which they did not suspect at all. But looking affectionately at his happy, busy wife, he remained resolved not to tell her what was happening (though he had been tempted to, since it felt so strange to do anything without her quiet support). The day went by quickly. Early in the evening, he hugged her and their daughter, pretending he would be back soon—making up excuse to go out, having already hidden a small suitcase of clothes. And so he stepped out into the thick mist of the heavy streets with an even heavier heart.

The unseen force was drawing him in quickly now, and all the winds and tides were pushing him directly toward it. He left his two letters with a trusted messenger, to be delivered exactly half an hour before midnight and no earlier. Then he took a horse for Dover and began his journey. “For the love of Heaven, of justice, of generosity, and for the honor of your noble name!” was the poor prisoner's cry that he repeated to strengthen his failing courage, as he left everything he loved behind and floated toward the Loadstone Rock.

The end of the second book.

CHAPTER XVIII.
Nine Days

The wedding day shone brightly. They were waiting outside the closed door of the Doctor's room, where he was speaking with Charles Darnay. The beautiful bride, Mr. Lorry, and Miss Pross were ready to go to church. To Miss Pross, the event would have been perfect happiness, if not for her lingering belief that her brother Solomon should have been the groom.

"And so," said Mr. Lorry, who couldn't stop admiring the bride and walked around her to take in her pretty, simple dress. "And so it was for this, my sweet Lucie, that I brought you across the Channel when you were such a baby! Lord help me! How little I thought what I was doing! How little I valued the service I was doing for my friend Charles!"

"You didn't mean to do it," the practical Miss Pross remarked, "so how could you know? What nonsense!"

"Really? Well, don't cry," said the gentle Mr. Lorry.

"I am not crying," said Miss Pross. "You are."

"I, my Pross?" (By this time, Mr. Lorry dared to joke with her occasionally.)

"You were just now; I saw you do it, and I don't wonder. The silver present you gave them is enough to bring tears to anyone's eyes. There isn't a fork or spoon in the set," said Miss Pross, "that I didn't cry over last night when the box arrived, until I couldn't see them."

"I am very pleased," said Mr. Lorry, "though I certainly didn't mean to make them invisible. Dear me! This is an occasion that makes a man think of everything he has lost. To think that I might have had a Mrs. Lorry at any time during the last fifty years!"

"Not at all!" said Miss Pross.

"You think I never could have had a Mrs. Lorry?" asked the banker.

"Bah!" Miss Pross replied. "You were a bachelor in your cradle."

"Well!" Mr. Lorry smiled, adjusting his wig. "That seems likely too."

"And you were cut out to be a bachelor before you were even put in your cradle," Miss Pross added.

"Then I think I was treated very poorly," said Mr. Lorry, "and I should have had a say in the matter. Enough! Now, my dear Lucie," drawing his arm around her waist, "I hear them moving in the next room. Miss Pross and I, as two practical business people, want to say one last thing to you. You are leaving your good father in hands as loving as your own. He will be taken care of. During the next two weeks, while you are in Warwickshire, even Tellson’s Bank will come second to him. And when he joins you and your husband in Wales in two weeks, you will see that we have sent him to you in the best health and spirits. Now, I hear his footsteps coming to the door. Let me kiss my dear girl and bless her before he comes to claim his bride."

He looked at her face for a moment, remembering her expression, and then held her golden hair against his wig with genuine tenderness.

The door opened, and the Doctor came out with Charles Darnay. He was deadly pale—unlike when they went in—with no color left in his face. But he remained calm, except that Mr. Lorry noticed a faint look of fear and avoidance on his face, like a cold wind.

He gave his arm to his daughter and led her downstairs to the carriage Mr. Lorry had hired. The others followed in another carriage, and soon, in a nearby church with no strangers watching, Charles Darnay and Lucie Manette were happily married.

In addition to the tears of joy among the small group, some bright diamonds shone on the bride’s hand, which Mr. Lorry had just taken from his pocket. They returned home for breakfast, and all went well. In due course, the golden hair that had once mingled with the shoemaker’s white hair in the Paris attic was held against it again in the morning sun as they said goodbye.

It was a difficult parting, though not for long. But her father cheered her, saying as he let her go: "Take her, Charles! She is yours!"

She waved to them from the carriage window, and was gone.

The street corner was quiet and out of the way of the curious, and the preparations had been simple. The Doctor, Mr. Lorry, and Miss Pross were left alone. As they walked back into the cool hall, Mr. Lorry noticed a major change in the Doctor, as if he had received a terrible blow.

He had controlled his feelings for a long time, and some reaction was expected now that the wedding was over. But it was the old scared, lost look that worried Mr. Lorry. Seeing him hold his head and wander off to his room reminded Mr. Lorry of Defarge and the night they rescued him.

"I think," he whispered to Miss Pross after thinking it over, "we should not speak to him right now or disturb him. I must check in at Tellson’s Bank, so I will go there immediately and return soon. Then we will take him for a carriage ride in the country and dine there, and everything will be fine."

It was easier for Mr. Lorry to go to Tellson’s than to leave it. He was delayed there for two hours. When he returned, he went upstairs alone without asking the servant. Entering the Doctor's rooms, he was stopped by a low hammering sound.

"Good God!" he said, starting. "What is that?"

Miss Pross was beside him with a terrified face. "Oh no! Everything is lost!" she cried, wringing her hands. "What will we tell Ladybird? He doesn't recognize me and is making shoes!"

Mr. Lorry tried to calm her and went into the Doctor’s room. The bench was turned toward the window, just as it had been in Paris, and the Doctor was bent over his work, very busy.

"Doctor Manette. My dear friend, Doctor Manette!"

The Doctor looked up for a moment—half questioning, half angry at being interrupted—and went back to his work.

He had taken off his coat and vest. His shirt was open at the throat, just as it used to be in prison, and his face had the same worn, hollow look. He worked hard and quickly, as if impatient at being interrupted.

Mr. Lorry looked at the shoe in his hand, seeing it was the same size and shape as the old ones. He picked up another shoe nearby and asked what it was.

"A young lady’s walking shoe," the Doctor muttered without looking up. "It should have been finished long ago. Let it be."

"But, Doctor Manette. Look at me!"

He looked up in his old submissive way, without stopping his work.

"You know me, my dear friend? Think. This is not your real work. Think, dear friend!"

Nothing could make him speak more. He looked up for only a second when asked, but no persuasion could get a word from him. He worked on in silence, and words fell on him like wind. The only hope Mr. Lorry saw was that he sometimes looked up on his own. In those glances, there seemed a faint look of curiosity or confusion, as if he were trying to understand something.

Two things seemed most important to Mr. Lorry: first, that this must be kept a secret from Lucie; second, that it must be kept a secret from everyone else. He and Miss Pross took steps immediately, telling everyone that the Doctor was unwell and needed a few days of complete rest. To keep the secret from Lucie, Miss Pross was to write to her, saying the Doctor had been called away on professional business, referring to a fake letter they claimed he had written.

These measures were taken in the hope that he would recover soon. If that happened, Mr. Lorry planned to consult a trusted medical specialist about the Doctor's case.

Hoping for his recovery, Mr. Lorry decided to watch him closely without appearing to do so. He arranged to miss work at Tellson's Bank for the first time in his life, and sat by the window in the same room.

He soon realized that speaking to the Doctor only made him anxious. He stopped trying to talk to him on the first day, deciding simply to stay in the room as a silent protest against his delusion. He sat by the window reading and writing, trying to make the room feel normal.

Doctor Manette ate and drank what was given to him and worked on. On that first day, he worked until it was too dark to see—working for thirty minutes after Mr. Lorry could no longer read. When he finally put his tools away, Mr. Lorry stood up and said:

"Will you go out?"

The Doctor looked at the floor, then looked up in his old way, repeating in his low voice:

"Out?"

"Yes, for a walk with me. Why not?"

He made no effort to answer and said nothing more. But as he sat in the dark with his head in his hands, Mr. Lorry thought he saw him asking himself, "Why not?" The banker saw this as a good sign and decided to persist.

Miss Pross and he divided the night into watches, observing him from the next room. The Doctor walked up and down his room for a long time before finally going to sleep. In the morning, he got up early and went straight to his bench to work.

On this second day, Mr. Lorry greeted him cheerfully and spoke of things they had recently discussed. The Doctor did not answer, but he clearly heard and thought about what was said. This encouraged Mr. Lorry to have Miss Pross bring her work into the room several times. They spoke normally of Lucie and her father, acting as if nothing were wrong. They did this briefly so as not to upset him. Mr. Lorry was encouraged to see that the Doctor looked up more often, appearing to notice that something was strange.

When night fell, Mr. Lorry asked again:

"Dear Doctor, will you go out?"

As before, he repeated, "Out?"

"Yes, for a walk with me. Why not?"

This time, when the Doctor didn't answer, Mr. Lorry pretended to go out alone, returning after an hour. While he was gone, the Doctor had sat by the window looking at the plane tree, but when Mr. Lorry returned, he went back to his bench.

Time passed slowly, and Mr. Lorry's hopes began to fade as his heart grew heavier each day. The third day passed, then the fourth, the fifth. Five days, six days, seven days, eight days, nine days.

With fading hope and a heavy heart, Mr. Lorry watched him. The secret was kept from Lucie, who was happy. But Mr. Lorry saw that the Doctor, who had been a bit clumsy at first, was becoming very skilled at shoemaking again, working quickly and expertly by the evening of the ninth day.

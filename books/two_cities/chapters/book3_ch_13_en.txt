CHAPTER XIII.
Fifty-two


In the dark prison of the Conciergerie, the people condemned to die that day waited for their fate. There were fifty-two of them, matching the number of weeks in a year. That afternoon, fifty-two lives would be carried away on the city's tide of death to the endless ocean of eternity. Before they even left their cells, new prisoners were assigned to replace them. Before their blood could join the blood spilled yesterday, the blood of those to be executed tomorrow was already being chosen.

Exactly fifty-two people were counted off. They ranged from a seventy-year-old wealthy tax collector, whose money could not buy his freedom, to a twenty-year-old seamstress, whose poverty and simple life could not save her. Just as physical diseases, born of human vice and neglect, strike down people of all classes, this terrifying moral sickness, born of unspeakable suffering, unbearable oppression, and cruel indifference, struck everyone down without distinction.

Charles Darnay, alone in a cell, had not fooled himself with any false hopes since returning from the courtroom. In every line of the story he had heard read, he heard his death sentence. He fully understood that no personal influence could save him now, that he was essentially condemned by millions of people, and that a few individuals could do nothing to help him.

Still, with the face of his beloved wife fresh in his memory, it was not easy to prepare his mind for what was coming. His grip on life was strong, and it was very, very hard to let go. As he slowly pried one finger loose, another gripped even tighter; and when he forced his hand to open, it clamped shut again. His thoughts were racing, and his heart pounded with a wild energy that fought against giving up. If he felt at peace with his fate for even a second, the thought of his wife and child living on without him made his peace feel selfish.

But that was only at first. Before long, the thought that there was no shame in the death he faced, and that many others went to the same death unjustly and stood strong every day, helped encourage him. Next came the thought that much of his family's future peace of mind depended on his quiet courage. So, step by step, he calmed down, lifting his thoughts to higher things and finding comfort.

Before darkness fell on the night of his sentencing, he had traveled this far along his path to death. Being allowed to buy writing materials and a candle, he sat down to write until the prison lights were put out.

He wrote a long letter to Lucie, explaining that he had known nothing about her father's time in prison until she told him about it. He had been just as ignorant as she was about his father's and uncle's responsibility for that suffering until the paper was read in court. He reminded her that hiding his real family name from her was the one condition—which made complete sense now—that her father had placed on their engagement, and the one promise he had kept on their wedding morning. He begged her, for her father's sake, never to try to find out if her father had forgotten about the paper or had been reminded of it by the story of the Tower on that old Sunday under the plane tree in the garden. Darnay wrote that if Doctor Manette had remembered it at all, he surely thought it was destroyed when the Bastille fell, since it wasn't mentioned among the records found there. He begged her—though he knew he didn't need to ask—to comfort her father by gently showing him that he had done nothing to blame himself for, but had always put himself last for their sakes. After asking her to hold onto his grateful love and blessing, and to overcome her grief to focus on their dear child, he begged her, by their hope of meeting in Heaven, to comfort her father.

To her father, he wrote in the same way, but he explicitly left his wife and child in the Doctor's care. He wrote this very strongly, hoping to keep the Doctor from falling into despair or dwelling on the dangerous past, which Darnay feared might happen.

To Mr. Lorry, he entrusted the family and explained his financial affairs. Once that was done, along with many words of grateful friendship and love, his task was complete. He never thought of Carton. His mind was so full of his family that he did not think of him once.

He had time to finish these letters before the lights were turned off. When he lay down on his straw bed, he believed he was done with this world for good.

But the world pulled him back in his sleep, showing itself in beautiful dreams. Free and happy, back in the old house in Soho (though the dream house looked nothing like the real one), mysteriously released and lighthearted, he was with Lucie again. She told him it was all a dream and that he had never left. After a moment of blankness, he dreamed he had suffered and come back to her, dead and at peace, yet looking exactly the same. Another moment of sleep, and then he woke up in the gloomy morning, not knowing where he was or what had happened, until it flashed in his mind: “This is the day of my death!”

So he had lived through the hours to the day when the fifty-two heads were to fall. Now, though he was calm and hoped to face the end with quiet courage, a new train of thought began in his mind that was very hard to control.

He had never seen the Guillotine that was to end his life. How high was it from the ground? How many steps did it have? Where would he stand? How would they touch him, and would the hands that touched him be stained red? Which way would his face be turned? Would he be the first to die, or the last? These and many similar questions, which he did not want to think about, forced themselves into his mind over and over again. It was not out of fear; he felt no fear. Rather, it came from a strange, nagging desire to know what to do when the moment arrived—a desire far larger than the few quick seconds it concerned. It was a curiosity that felt more like it belonged to some other spirit inside him than to himself.

The hours passed as he walked back and forth, and the clocks struck the hours he would never hear again. Nine o'clock gone forever, ten o'clock gone forever, eleven o'clock gone forever, and twelve o'clock coming and going. After a hard struggle with the strange thoughts that had bothered him, he finally overcame them. He walked up and down, softly repeating the names of his loved ones to himself. The worst of the struggle was over. He could walk back and forth, free from distracting thoughts, praying for himself and for them.

Twelve o'clock gone forever.

He had been told that the execution was at three o'clock, and he knew he would be called earlier, since the death carts traveled heavily and slowly through the streets. Therefore, he decided to focus on two o'clock as the final hour, using the time before then to build up his strength so he could help others when the time came.

Walking steadily back and forth with his arms crossed over his chest—looking very different from the panicked prisoner who had paced in La Force prison—he heard the clock strike one without surprise. The hour had felt like any other hour. Deeply thankful to Heaven for regaining his self-control, he thought, “There is only one hour left now,” and turned to walk again.

Footsteps in the stone hallway outside the door. He stopped.

A key was put in the lock and turned. Before the door opened, a man said in a quiet English voice: “He has never seen me here; I have stayed out of his sight. Go in alone; I'll wait nearby. Hurry!”

The door was quickly opened and closed, and there standing face-to-face with him, quiet and focused, with a gentle smile and a warning finger on his lips, was Sydney Carton.

There was something so bright and unusual in Carton's look that, at first, Darnay thought he was just a ghost created by his own imagination. But Carton spoke, and it was truly his voice; he took Darnay's hand, and it was a real, solid grip.

“Of all the people on earth, I'm the last one you expected to see?” he said.

“I couldn't believe it was you. I can barely believe it now. You aren't”—a sudden fear struck him—“a prisoner?”

“No. By chance, I have leverage over one of the guards here, and that is why I am standing in front of you. I come from her—your wife, dear Darnay.”

Darnay squeezed his hand.

“I bring you a request from her.”

“What is it?”

“A very earnest, urgent, and important plea, spoken in the saddest tones of the voice you love so much and remember so well.”

Darnay turned his face away slightly.

“You don't have time to ask why I bring this request or what it means, and I don't have time to tell you. You must obey—take off your boots and put on mine.”

There was a chair against the cell wall behind the prisoner. Carton pushed forward and, with lightning speed, forced Darnay down into the chair and stood over him, barefoot.

“Put on my boots. Reach down and pull them on; focus your mind. Quick!”

“Carton, there is no escaping from this place; it's impossible. You will only die with me. It is madness.”

“It would be madness if I asked you to escape, but did I? When I actually ask you to walk out that door, then you can call me crazy and stay here. Change your tie for mine, and your coat for mine. While you do that, let me take the ribbon from your hair and shake it out so it looks like mine!”

With incredible speed and a strength of will and action that seemed almost superhuman, Carton forced all these changes on him. Darnay was like a little child in his hands.

“Carton! Dear Carton! This is madness. It can't be done; it has been tried before and always fails. I beg you not to add your death to the bitterness of my own.”

“Am I asking you to walk out the door, my dear Darnay? When I ask that, you can say no. There is a pen, ink, and paper on this table. Is your hand steady enough to write?”

“It was before you came in.”

“Steady it again, and write what I tell you. Quick, friend, quick!”

Holding his confused head in his hand, Darnay sat at the table. Carton stood close beside him with his right hand hidden inside his coat.

“Write exactly what I say.”

“Who is this to?”

“To no one.” Carton still kept his hand inside his coat.

“Should I date it?”

“No.”

Darnay looked up at each question. Carton stood over him, looking down, with his hand still inside his coat.

“‘If you remember,’” Carton dictated, “‘the words we spoke to each other long ago, you will understand this when you see it. I know you remember them. It is not in your nature to forget them.’”

Carton was drawing his hand out of his coat. When Darnay happened to look up in surprise as he wrote, Carton's hand stopped, holding onto something.

“Have you written ‘forget them’?” Carton asked.

“I have. Is that a weapon in your hand?”

“No, I am not armed.”

“What is in your hand?”

“You will find out in a moment. Keep writing; there are only a few words left.” He dictated again. “‘I am thankful that the time has come when I can prove those words. That I am doing this is no reason for regret or sadness.’” As Carton spoke, keeping his eyes fixed on Darnay, his hand slowly and gently drifted down close to Darnay's face.

The pen slipped from Darnay's fingers onto the table, and he looked around blankly.

“What is that smell?” he asked.

“Smell?”

“Something that just passed by me?”

“I don't smell anything; there can't be anything here. Pick up the pen and finish writing. Hurry, hurry!”

As if his mind were failing or his thoughts were confused, Darnay tried to focus. He stared at Carton with foggy eyes, his breathing changing, while Carton—keeping his hand in his coat once more—looked steadily back at him.

“Hurry, hurry!”

Darnay bent over the paper again.

“‘If it had been otherwise,’” Carton's hand crept watchfully and gently down again. “‘I never would have used this longer opportunity. If it had been otherwise,’” Carton's hand was now right at Darnay's face. “‘I would only have had more to answer for. If it had been otherwise—’” Carton looked at the pen and saw that the writing was trailing off into squiggles.

Carton did not hide his hand inside his coat again. Darnay jumped up with a look of surprise and blame, but Carton pressed the drugged cloth firmly over his nose and mouth, catching him around the waist with his left arm. For a few seconds, Darnay struggled weakly against the man who had come to die for him; but within a minute, he collapsed unconscious on the floor.

Quickly, and with hands as steady as his heart, Carton dressed himself in the clothes Darnay had taken off. He combed back his hair and tied it with Darnay's hair ribbon. Then he called softly, “Come in! Come in!” and Barsad the spy entered.

“You see?” said Carton, looking up as he knelt beside the unconscious man, putting the letter into his coat pocket. “Is your risk very big?”

“Mr. Carton,” the spy answered, snapping his fingers nervously, “my risk is not even _that_ much in all the busy rush here, as long as you keep your end of the deal.”

“Do not fear. I will keep my word to the death.”

“You must, Mr. Carton, if our count of fifty-two is to be correct. Since you will replace him in those clothes, I have no fear.”

“Have no fear! I will soon be gone where I cannot harm you, and the others will soon be far away, God willing! Now, get some help and take him to the carriage.”

“You?” the spy asked nervously.

“Him, man, the one I swapped places with! You are going out through the same gate you brought me in, aren't you?”

“Of course.”

“I was weak and dizzy when you brought me in, and I am even weaker now that you are taking me out. The final goodbye was too much for me. This sort of thing has happened here many times. Your life is in your own hands. Quick, call for help!”

“You swear you won't betray me?” the trembling spy asked, pausing one last time.

“Man, man!” Carton replied, stamping his foot. “Haven't I already sworn a solemn oath to go through with this? Why are you wasting precious time? Take him to the courtyard yourself, put him in the carriage yourself, show him to Mr. Lorry yourself, tell Mr. Lorry to give him nothing but fresh air to wake him up, tell him to remember my words and promise from last night, and drive away!”

The spy left, and Carton sat at the table, resting his head in his hands. The spy returned immediately with two helpers.

“What's this?” one of them said, looking down at the unconscious man. “Is he so sad to find that his friend has won a ticket to Saint Guillotine?”

“A good patriot,” said the other, “could hardly be sadder if the aristocrat had escaped.”

They lifted the unconscious Darnay, placed him on a stretcher they had brought to the door, and carried him away.

“Time is short, Evrémonde,” the spy said in a warning voice.

“I know it well,” Carton replied. “Take care of my friend, I beg you, and leave me.”

“Come on, my men,” said Barsad. “Lift him and let's go!”

The door closed, and Carton was left alone. Listening as hard as he could, he waited for any sound of suspicion or alarm. There was none. Keys turned, doors slammed, and footsteps echoed down distant hallways; no one shouted or rushed in a way that seemed unusual. Breathing a little easier after a while, he sat down at the table and listened until the clock struck two.

Sounds that did not frighten him, because he knew what they meant, began to echo. Several cell doors were opened one after another, and finally his own. A jailer holding a list looked in and simply said, “Follow me, Evrémonde!” Carton followed him down to a large, dark room. It was a gloomy winter day, and between the shadows inside and out, he could barely see the other prisoners who had been brought there to have their arms tied. Some were standing, some were sitting. A few were crying and pacing restlessly, but they were the minority. The great majority were silent and still, staring fixedly at the ground.

As Carton stood against the wall in a dark corner while others of the fifty-two were brought in, one man stopped as he passed to hug him, thinking he knew him. This filled Carton with a terrible fear of being discovered, but the man walked on. A few moments later, a young woman with a slender, girlish figure, a sweet, pale face, and large, patient eyes, got up from her seat and walked over to speak to him.

“Citizen Evrémonde,” she said, touching him with her cold hand. “I am a poor seamstress who was in La Force prison with you.”

He whispered in reply: “Yes. I forget, what were you accused of?”

“Plotting. Though Heaven knows I am completely innocent. Is it likely? Who would think to plot with a poor, weak little creature like me?”

The sad little smile she gave touched him so deeply that tears came to his eyes.

“I am not afraid to die, Citizen Evrémonde, but I did nothing wrong. I am willing to die if it helps the Republic, which is supposed to do so much good for us poor people. But I don't see how my death can help anyone, Citizen Evrémonde. I am such a poor, weak little thing!”

As the very last thing on earth to touch his heart, he felt a deep warmth and sympathy for this poor girl.

“I heard you were set free, Citizen Evrémonde. I hoped it was true.”

“It was. But I was arrested again and sentenced to death.”

“If I can ride in the cart with you, Citizen Evrémonde, will you let me hold your hand? I am not afraid, but I am small and weak, and it will give me courage.”

As she raised her patient eyes to his face, he saw a sudden doubt in them, followed by shock. He squeezed her thin, work-worn, hungry fingers and pressed them to his lips.

“Are you dying for him?” she whispered.

“And for his wife and child. Hush! Yes.”

“Oh, will you let me hold your brave hand, stranger?”

“Hush! Yes, my poor sister; all the way to the end.”

The same shadows falling on the prison were also falling, at that very hour of the early afternoon, on the city gates. A crowd stood around as a carriage leaving Paris pulled up to be checked.

“Who goes there? Who is inside? Let me see your papers!”

The papers were handed out and read.

“Alexandre Manette. Doctor. French. Which one is he?”

This was he—the helpless, mumbling, confused old man they pointed to.

“It seems the Citizen-Doctor is not in his right mind? The fever of the Revolution must have been too much for him?”

Much too much for him.

“Hah! Many people suffer from it. Lucie. His daughter. French. Which one is she?”

This was she.

“Clearly it is. Lucie, the wife of Evrémonde, correct?”

Yes, it is.

“Hah! Evrémonde has an appointment elsewhere. Lucie, her child. English. Is this she?”

She and no other.

“Kiss me, child of Evrémonde. Now you have kissed a good Republican—something new in your family! Remember it. Sydney Carton. Lawyer. English. Which one is he?”

He was lying in the corner of the carriage. They pointed him out too.

“It seems the English lawyer has fainted?”

They hoped he would wake up in the fresh air. They explained that he was in poor health and had just said a sad goodbye to a friend who was in trouble with the Republic.

“Is that all? That's not much! Many people are in trouble with the Republic, and have to look out the little window of the Guillotine. Jarvis Lorry. Banker. English. Which one is he?”

“I am he. Naturally, since I'm the last one.”

Jarvis Lorry had answered all the previous questions. He had stepped down from the carriage and stood with his hand on the door, talking to a group of officials. They slowly walked around the carriage and slowly climbed up to check the small amount of luggage on the roof. The local peasants standing nearby crowded closer to the carriage doors, staring greedily inside. A mother held out her little child's hand so it could touch the wife of the aristocrat who was going to the Guillotine.

“Here are your papers, Jarvis Lorry, stamped and signed.”

“We may leave, citizen?”

“You may leave. Go on, drivers! Have a safe trip!”

“Thank you, citizens.—And the first danger is passed!”

These were Mr. Lorry's words as he folded his hands and looked up at the sky. There was terror and crying in the carriage, along with the heavy breathing of the unconscious traveler.

“Aren't we going too slowly? Can't we make them go faster?” Lucie asked, clinging to the old man.

“It would look like we are fleeing, my darling. I must not push them too hard; it would make them suspicious.”

“Look back, look back, and see if anyone is chasing us!”

“The road is empty, my dearest. So far, no one is chasing us.”

Houses passed by in twos and threes, along with lonely farms, ruined buildings, tanneries, open fields, and rows of leafless trees. The carriage rode over rough, bumpy stone roads, with deep mud on both sides. Sometimes we drove into the mud to avoid the stones that shook us so violently; sometimes we got stuck in ruts and puddles. The torture of waiting was so great that in our panic and hurry, we felt like jumping out of the carriage to run and hide—anything was better than sitting still.

Out of the open country and back among ruined buildings, lonely farms, tanneries, cottages, and leafless trees. Had the drivers tricked us and taken us back toward Paris? Was this the same place we passed before? Thank goodness, no. It was a village. Look back, look back, and see if we are pursued! Shh, here is the carriage station.

Slowly, our four horses were unhitched. Slowly, the carriage stood horse-less in the street, looking like it would never move again. Slowly, the new horses appeared one by one. Slowly, the new drivers followed, braiding the leather of their whips. Slowly, the old drivers counted their tips, did their math wrong, and grumbled about the results. All this time, our terrified hearts were beating at a speed that would outrun the fastest horses in the world.

At last, the new drivers were in their saddles and the old ones were left behind. We drove through the village, up and down a hill, and onto wet, low ground. Suddenly, the drivers called out to each other with wild gestures, and the horses were pulled to a sudden halt, nearly sliding on their hind legs. Are we being chased?

“Hey! Inside the carriage! Answer me!”

“What's wrong?” Mr. Lorry asked, looking out the window.

“How many did they say today?”

“I don't understand you.”

“At the last station. How many were sent to the Guillotine today?”

“Fifty-two.”

“I knew it! What a great number! My friend here thought it was only forty-two; ten extra heads are always good to have. The Guillotine is doing fine work. I love it. Move on! Giddy up!”

Night fell dark. The traveler in the corner moved more; he was starting to wake up and speak clearly. He thought they were still in the cell together. He asked Carton, calling him by name, what Carton had in his hand. Oh, have mercy on us, kind Heaven, and help us! Look out, look out, and see if we are being chased.

The wind rushed after us, the clouds flew above us, the moon followed us, and the whole wild night seemed to be chasing us; but, so far, nothing else was pursuing us.

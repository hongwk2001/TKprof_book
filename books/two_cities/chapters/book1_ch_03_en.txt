CHAPTER III.

The Night Shadows

It is a wonderful thing to think about. Every human being is, at heart, a mystery to everyone else. Think about entering a large city at night. Every dark, crowded house holds its own secret. Every room in every house holds its own secret. Every beating heart among the hundreds of thousands of people there—in some of its deepest thoughts—is a secret even to the person standing closest to it. Part of the terror of Death itself comes from this very fact. The pages of a dear book snap shut before we ever finish reading it. The depths of a beautiful pool freeze over before we can see what lies at the bottom. My friend is dead. My neighbor is dead. My love, the darling of my soul, is dead. The secret they carried is now sealed forever inside them. And I will carry my own secret until I die. Walking through any cemetery in this city, I have to wonder: are the people sleeping underground truly more mysterious than the people rushing past the gate above them?

When it came to this—this natural mystery that no one could take away—the messenger on horseback was equal to the King, the Prime Minister, or the richest merchant in London. The three passengers in that cramped old mail coach were no different. Each was a complete mystery to the others. They might as well each have been riding alone in a private carriage, with an entire county of open road between them.

The messenger rode back at an easy trot, stopping pretty often at pubs along the way to drink, but showing a tendency to keep to himself and keep his hat pulled low over his eyes. He had eyes that went well with that look: they were flat black, with no depth in color or shape, and set much too close together—as if they were afraid of being caught doing something separately if they stood too far apart. They had a sinister look, hidden under an old cocked hat that looked like a three-cornered spittoon, and above a large scarf for his chin and throat that hung down almost to his knees. When he stopped for a drink, he pulled this scarf away with his left hand only while pouring the alcohol in with his right; as soon as that was done, he wrapped himself back up.

"No, Jerry, no!" said the messenger, repeating the same idea as he rode. "It wouldn't work out for you, Jerry. Jerry, you honest tradesman, it wouldn't suit *your* line of work! Recalled—! Blow me if I don't think he's been drinking!"

His message confused him so much that he kept having to take off his hat to scratch his head. The top of his head was patchily bald. The rest of his hair was stiff, black, and jagged. It grew forward almost to his broad, flat nose. It looked less like hair and more like the spikes on top of a stone wall. Even the best leapfrog player in the world would have thought twice before jumping over him.

As he trotted back to deliver his message, the night shadows played tricks on his mind. He was heading for the night watchman's booth at the door of Tellson's Bank, near Temple Bar. The watchman would pass the message on to the senior officials inside. As Jerry rode, the word "recalled" haunted him. Strange shapes formed in the dark. Even his mare seemed nervous—she shied away from every shadow on the road, as if the night were full of things only she could see.

Meanwhile, the mail coach rumbled, jolted, rattled, and bumped along its exhausting path, with its three mysterious passengers inside. To them, too, the night shadows showed themselves in whatever shapes their sleepy eyes and wandering thoughts suggested.

Tellson's Bank was never far from the banker's thoughts. He dozed in his seat, his arm hooked through the leather strap overhead to stop himself from slamming into the next passenger every time the coach jolted badly. The dim coach windows, the faint glow of the lamp, and the dark silhouette of the passenger across from him all slowly transformed in his half-sleeping mind into the familiar rooms of the bank. He could hear the harness rattling. In his dream, it became the clinking of coins. More transactions were processed in five minutes of dreaming than Tellson's had managed in three times that duration in real life. Then the underground vaults opened in his mind. He walked through them with a large ring of keys and a dimly burning candle. He found everything exactly as he had left it: safe, secure, and perfectly quiet.

But there was another thought running beneath all of this. It never stopped. It ran all through the night. He was on his way to dig someone out of a grave.

The night shadows refused to show him which face was the true face of that buried person. But every face that appeared was the face of a man around forty-five years old. All the faces were different in expression: some proud, some scornful, some defiant, some broken. But all of them had sunken cheeks, deathly pale skin, and thin, skeletal hands. And every single one of them had hair gone prematurely white. A hundred times that night, the dozing passenger asked the ghost the same question:

"Buried how long?"

The answer was always the same: "Almost eighteen years."

"You had given up all hope of being dug out?"

"Long ago."

"You know that you are brought back to life?"

"They tell me so."

"I hope you care to live?"

"I can't say."

"Shall I show her to you? Will you come and see her?"

The answers to this question were different and contradictory. Sometimes the shaky reply was, "Wait! It would kill me if I saw her too soon." Sometimes it was spoken through a gentle flow of tears: "Take me to her." Sometimes it was wide-eyed and confused: "I don't know her. I don't understand."

After these imagined conversations, the passenger's dream-self would dig. And dig. And dig. Sometimes with a shovel, sometimes with a large key, sometimes with bare hands. The goal was always to pull this miserable creature out of the ground. And when he finally did—when the man emerged, covered in dirt—the figure would suddenly crumble into dust. The passenger would then jolt awake. He would roll down the window and press his cheek against the cold, wet mist to feel something real.

But even with his eyes open—seeing the mist, the moving patch of coach-lamp light, the roadside hedges jerking past—the night shadows outside the coach would always bleed back into the night shadows in his mind. The real bank near Temple Bar. The real business from yesterday. The real vaults. The real messenger. The real reply. All of it was there. And right in the middle of it all, the ghostly face would rise again. And he would ask the same question.

"Buried how long?"

"Almost eighteen years."

"I hope you care to live?"

"I can't say."

Dig. Dig. Dig. Until one of the other passengers shifted restlessly, snapping him back to reality. He would roll the window back up. He would hook his arm through the strap again. He would try to focus on the two sleeping figures nearby. But soon his mind would drift away. Back to the bank. Back to the grave.

"Buried how long?"

"Almost eighteen years."

"You had given up all hope of being dug out?"

"Long ago."

The words were still ringing in his ears when he woke up. They were as clear as any words he had ever heard spoken aloud. He startled awake to the morning light. The night shadows were gone.

He rolled down the window and looked out at the rising sun. There was a ridge of plowed land, with a plow sitting where it had been left the night before when the horses were unharnessed. Beyond that was a quiet woods, where many leaves of burning red and golden yellow still hung on the trees. Although the ground was cold and wet, the sky was clear, and the sun rose bright, calm, and beautiful. "Eighteen years!" said the passenger, looking up at the sun. "Gracious Creator of the day! To be buried alive for eighteen years!"

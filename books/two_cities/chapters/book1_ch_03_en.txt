CHAPTER III.

The Night Shadows

It is a wonderful thing to think about, that every human being is designed to be a deep secret and mystery to everyone else. It is a sobering thought, when entering a large city at night, that every single one of those dark, crowded houses holds its own secret; that every room in every one of them holds its own secret; that every beating heart among the hundreds of thousands of people there, in some of its thoughts, is a secret even to the heart closest to it! Part of the terror, even of Death itself, can be traced back to this. I can no longer turn the pages of this dear book I loved, and hope to read it all eventually. I can no longer look into the depths of this bottomless water, where, as brief flashes of light hit it, I've caught glimpses of buried treasure and other things hidden underwater. It was fated that the book would spring shut, forever and ever, when I had only read one page. It was fated that the water would be frozen solid forever, just as the light was dancing on its surface, and I stood clueless on the shore. My friend is dead, my neighbor is dead, my love, the darling of my soul, is dead; it is the unavoidable sealing and keeping alive of the secret that was always part of that person, and which I'll carry in my own heart until I die. In any of the cemeteries in this city I walk through, is there anyone sleeping there who is more mysterious than its busy residents are, deep down, to me, or than I am to them?

When it came to this—his natural inheritance that couldn't be taken away—the messenger on horseback had exactly the same as the King, the Prime Minister, or the richest merchant in London. It was the same for the three passengers cooped up in the cramped space of one clunky old mail coach; they were complete mysteries to each other, as if each had been in their own private carriage with six horses, or even sixty, with the width of a county between him and the next.

The messenger rode back at an easy trot, stopping pretty often at pubs along the way to drink, but showing a tendency to keep to himself and keep his hat pulled low over his eyes. He had eyes that went well with that look: they were flat black, with no depth in color or shape, and set much too close together—as if they were afraid of being caught doing something separately if they stood too far apart. They had a sinister look, hidden under an old cocked hat that looked like a three-cornered spittoon, and above a large scarf for his chin and throat that hung down almost to his knees. When he stopped for a drink, he pulled this scarf away with his left hand only while pouring the alcohol in with his right; as soon as that was done, he wrapped himself back up.

"No, Jerry, no!" said the messenger, repeating the same idea as he rode. "It wouldn't work out for you, Jerry. Jerry, you honest tradesman, it wouldn't suit *your* line of work! Recalled—! Blow me if I don't think he's been drinking!"

His message confused his mind to such a degree that he felt forced, several times, to take off his hat to scratch his head. Except for the top of his head, which was patchily bald, he had stiff, black hair that stood up jaggedly all over, growing forward almost to his broad, flat nose. It looked so much like metalwork, far more like the top of a spiked wall than a head of hair, that even the best players of leapfrog would have avoided him as the most dangerous man in the world to jump over.

As he trotted back with the message he was to deliver to the night watchman in his booth at the door of Tellson's Bank, near Temple Bar (who would deliver it to higher officials inside), the night shadows took on shapes in his mind that came from the message, and took on shapes in the mare's mind that came from *her* private anxieties. They seemed to be many, because she shied away from every shadow on the road.

Meanwhile, the mail coach rumbled, jolted, rattled, and bumped along its exhausting path, with its three mysterious passengers inside. To them, too, the night shadows showed themselves in whatever shapes their sleepy eyes and wandering thoughts suggested.

Tellson's Bank was very active in the mind of the passenger. As the banker—with his arm slipped through the leather strap, which did its best to keep him from slamming into the next passenger and pushing him into the corner whenever the coach got a particularly bad jolt—dozed in his seat with half-closed eyes, the small coach windows, the coach lamp dimly shining through them, and the bulky silhouette of the passenger opposite him all transformed into the bank, where he conducted a great deal of business. The rattling of the harness became the clinking of coins, and more checks were processed in five minutes than Tellson's, with all its local and international connections, had ever paid out in three times that duration. Then the underground vaults at Tellson's, along with the valuable treasures and secrets known to the passenger (and he knew quite a lot about them), opened up before him. He walked among them with his large keys and a dimly burning candle, finding them safe, secure, intact, and quiet, just as he had last seen them.

But even though the bank was almost constantly in his thoughts, and the coach (in a blurry way, like the feeling of pain under medicine) was always present, there was another stream of thought that never stopped running all through the night. He was on his way to dig someone out of a grave.

Now, the night shadows did not show which of the many faces appearing before him was the true face of the buried person. But they were all the faces of a forty-five-year-old man, differing mainly in the emotions they showed and in how pale, worn, and ruined they looked. Pride, contempt, defiance, stubbornness, surrender, and sorrow followed one another, as did different kinds of sunken cheeks, deathly pale skin, and thin, skeletal hands and bodies. Yet, the face was generally the same face, and every head of hair was prematurely white. A hundred times, the dozing passenger asked this ghost:

"Buried how long?"

The answer was always the same: "Almost eighteen years."

"You had given up all hope of being dug out?"

"Long ago."

"You know that you are brought back to life?"

"They tell me so."

"I hope you care to live?"

"I can't say."

"Shall I show her to you? Will you come and see her?"

The answers to this question were different and contradictory. Sometimes the shaky reply was, "Wait! It would kill me if I saw her too soon." Sometimes it was spoken through a gentle flow of tears: "Take me to her." Sometimes it was wide-eyed and confused: "I don't know her. I don't understand."

After these imagined conversations, the passenger in his mind would dig, and dig, and dig—sometimes with a shovel, sometimes with a large key, and sometimes with his bare hands—to get this miserable creature out. Once finally pulled out, with dirt clinging to his face and hair, the man would suddenly crumble away to dust. The passenger would then startle awake, and roll down the window to feel the cold reality of the mist and rain on his cheek.

Yet even when his eyes were open to the mist and rain, the moving patch of light from the coach lamps, and the roadside hedges passing by in jerks, the night shadows outside the coach would merge with the night shadows in his mind. The actual bank building near Temple Bar, the real business of the day before, the real vaults, the real messenger sent after him, and the real answer sent back would all be there. Out of the middle of them, the ghostly face would rise, and he would speak to it again.

"Buried how long?"

"Almost eighteen years."

"I hope you care to live?"

"I can't say."

Dig—dig—dig—until an impatient shift from one of the other two passengers warned him to roll up the window, slip his arm back securely through the leather strap, and think about the two sleeping figures, until his mind lost its grip on them and they drifted away again into the bank and the grave.

"Buried how long?"

"Almost eighteen years."

"You had given up all hope of being dug out?"

"Long ago."

The words were still ringing in his ears as if they had just been spoken—as clearly as any spoken words he had ever heard in his life—when the tired passenger startled awake to the morning light, finding that the shadows of the night were gone.

He rolled down the window and looked out at the rising sun. There was a ridge of plowed land, with a plow sitting where it had been left the night before when the horses were unharnessed. Beyond that was a quiet woods, where many leaves of burning red and golden yellow still hung on the trees. Although the ground was cold and wet, the sky was clear, and the sun rose bright, calm, and beautiful. "Eighteen years!" said the passenger, looking up at the sun. "Gracious Creator of the day! To be buried alive for eighteen years!"

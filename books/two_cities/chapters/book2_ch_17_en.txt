CHAPTER XVII.
One Night

The sun never set with a brighter glory on the quiet corner in Soho than one memorable evening when the Doctor and his daughter sat together under the plane tree. The moon never rose with a softer light over London than on that night, shining on their faces through the leaves.

Lucie was to be married the next day. She had reserved this last evening for her father, and they sat alone under the tree.

"Are you happy, my dear father?"

"Quite, my child."

They had said very little, though they had been sitting there a long time. When it was still light enough to read and work, she had done neither. Usually, she sat by his side under the tree working or reading to him, but this evening was different from any other.

"I am very happy tonight, dear father. I am deeply happy in my love for Charles, and Charles’s love for me. But if my life were not still dedicated to you, or if my marriage separated us, even by a few streets, I would be very unhappy. Even as it is—"

Her voice faltered, and she could not finish.

In the soft moonlight, she hugged him by the neck and rested her face on his chest. They sat in the moonlight, which always feels a little sad, like human life itself.

"Dearest father! Can you tell me, this last time, that you are sure my new love and duties will never stand between us? I know it, but do you know it in your heart?"

Her father answered with a firm, cheerful conviction: "I am quite sure, my darling! More than that," he added, kissing her tenderly, "my future looks much brighter, Lucie, because of your marriage than it ever did without it."

"If I could only hope that, father!"

"Believe it, my love! Indeed it is true. Think how natural it is. You are young, and you cannot fully understand how much I worried that your life would be wasted—"

She put her hand to his lips to stop him, but he took her hand and repeated the word.

"—wasted, my child, for my sake. Your unselfishness prevents you from seeing how much I thought about this. But ask yourself: how could I be perfectly happy if your happiness was incomplete?"

"If I had never met Charles, father, I would have been perfectly happy with you."

He smiled at her admission that she would be unhappy without Charles now that she had met him, and replied:

"My child, you did meet him. If it hadn't been Charles, it would have been someone else. Or if you had never married, I would have been the cause, and the shadow of my past imprisonment would have ruined your life too."

It was the first time, other than at the trial, that she had heard him speak of his time in prison. Hearing his words gave her a strange feeling, which she remembered long afterward.

"Look!" said the Doctor, pointing toward the moon. "I watched the moon from my prison window when I couldn't bear its light. I watched it when it was torture to think of it shining on everything I had lost, and I beat my head against the prison walls. I watched it when I was so numb and depressed that I could only count the lines I could draw across it. I remember there were twenty lines either way, and the twentieth was hard to squeeze in."

The strange feeling she had hearing him talk of his prison years deepened as he continued, though his tone was calm. He was simply contrasting his present happiness with his past suffering.

"I watched the moon, wondering thousands of times about the child I had been torn from. I wondered if it was alive, or if the shock had killed its mother. I wondered if it was a son who would one day avenge me. (There was a time when my desire for vengeance was unbearable.) I wondered if it was a son who would never know my story, or might believe I had disappeared on purpose. Or if it was a daughter who would grow up to be a woman."

She drew closer, kissing his cheek and hand.

"I imagined my daughter grew up completely ignorant of me. I counted her age, year after year. I imagined her married to a man who knew nothing of my fate. I was entirely forgotten by the living, and my place in the family was a blank."

"My father! Even hearing that you had such thoughts makes my heart ache as if I had been that child."

"You, Lucie? These memories only arise because of the healing and happiness you have brought me. We share them on this last night under the moon. What was I saying just now?"

"You said she knew nothing of you and did not care."

"Yes. But on other nights, when the silence brought a sad peace, I imagined her coming to my cell and leading me out to freedom. I saw her figure in the moonlight, just as I see you now, though I could never hold her. The image stood between the window and the door. But you understand that was not the child I am speaking of?"

"The image was not the real child, but a fancy?"

"No, that was a phantom. The child my mind pursued was more real. I only knew she looked like her mother. The phantom looked like her mother too—as you do—but it was different. Can you follow me, Lucie? Probably not. I think only a prisoner could understand these strange distinctions."

His calm manner did not stop her from feeling a chill as he analyzed his old state of mind.

"In that peaceful state, I imagined her leading me out to show me her married home, which was full of loving memories of her lost father. My picture was on her wall, and I was in her prayers. Her life was active and happy, but my story was part of it."

"I was that child, father. I was not nearly as good, but in my love, that was me."

"And she showed me her children," the Doctor said, "who had been taught to pity me. When they passed a state prison, they looked at the bars and spoke in whispers. She could never rescue me; I imagined she always brought me back to my cell afterward. But then, relieved by tears, I knelt and blessed her."

"I am that child, father. I hope you will bless me just as warmly tomorrow."

"Lucie, I remember these old troubles tonight only because they show how much I love you and how grateful I am to God. My wildest thoughts in prison never imagined the happiness I have found with you, and what lies ahead for us."

He hugged her, blessed her, and thanked God for giving her to him. After a while, they went inside.

No one was invited to the wedding except Mr. Lorry, and Miss Pross was the only bridesmaid. The marriage would not change where they lived. They had simply rented the upper rooms formerly belonging to the invisible lodger, and needed nothing more.

Doctor Manette was very cheerful at dinner. Only the three of them sat at the table: the Doctor, Lucie, and Miss Pross. He regretted that Charles was not there, joking about the custom that kept him away, and drank a toast to him.

Finally, it was time to say good night, and they went to bed. But in the quiet hours of the morning, Lucie came downstairs and crept into his room, feeling a sudden fear.

However, everything was in its place and quiet. He lay asleep, his white hair neat on the pillow, and his hands still. She put her candle in the shadow, went to his bedside, and kissed him.

His face still showed the worn lines of his long imprisonment, but he controlled his expressions even in his sleep. His face showed a quiet, determined struggle with his old memories.

She gently laid her hand on his chest, praying she would always be a good daughter to him. Then she kissed him again and left. Soon the sun rose, and the shadows of the tree leaves moved softly across his face.

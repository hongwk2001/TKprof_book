CHAPTER V.
The Wood-Sawyer


One year and three months passed. During all that time, Lucie was never sure from one hour to the next whether the Guillotine would cut off her husband's head the following day. Every day, heavy carts rumbled through the cobblestone streets, packed with condemned prisoners. Beautiful girls, young women, dark-haired, brown-haired, and gray-haired women, boys, strong men, and the elderly—aristocrats and peasants alike—were all food for the Guillotine. They were brought daily from the dark cells of the filthy prisons and carried through the streets to satisfy its endless thirst. Liberty, equality, fraternity, or death—and death was by far the easiest for the Guillotine to give!

If the sudden shock of this disaster and the chaotic pace of the times had caused the Doctor's daughter to give up in despair, she would have been just like many others. But ever since the day she had held her father's white-haired head in the attic of Saint Antoine, she had always done her duty. She was most loyal during this time of trial, just as all good and quiet people are.

As soon as they moved into their new apartment and her father began his daily routine, she organized the household exactly as if Charles were there. Everything had its place and time. She taught little Lucie as regularly as if they were still in their peaceful English home. The small tricks she played on herself to pretend they would soon be together again—like setting aside his chair and books for his return, and praying at night for her dear prisoner among the many suffering in the shadow of death—were the only ways she could find relief for her heavy heart.

She did not change much in appearance. The simple dark dresses, almost like mourning clothes, that she and her child wore were as neat and clean as their colorful clothes from happier days. She lost some of her color, and her worried, serious expression was now permanent, but otherwise she remained very pretty. Sometimes at night, when kissing her father goodnight, she would cry and let out the grief she had hidden all day, telling him he was her only hope. He always answered firmly, “Nothing can happen to him without my knowing, and I know I can save him, Lucie.”

They had only lived this way for a few weeks when her father said to her one evening upon coming home:

“My dear, there is a high window in the prison that Charles can sometimes reach at three in the afternoon. When he can get to it—which depends on many things—he thinks he might be able to see you in the street if you stand in a specific spot I can show you. But you won't be able to see him, my poor child, and even if you could, it would be unsafe to wave or show that you recognize him.”

“Oh, show me the place, Father, and I will go there every day.”

From then on, in all kinds of weather, she waited there for two hours. She arrived at two o'clock and left at four. When it was not too wet or cold for her daughter, they went together; other times she went alone, but she never missed a day.

The spot was a dark, dirty corner of a narrow, winding street. The shack of a wood-sawyer was the only building there; everything else was just a high wall. On her third day of waiting there, he noticed her.

“Good day, citizeness.”

“Good day, citizen.”

This way of greeting was now required by law. It had started voluntarily among the most loyal patriots, but now everyone had to use it.

“Walking here again, citizeness?”

“You see me, citizen!”

The wood-sawyer was a small man who made a lot of gestures—he was the same man who had once been the road-mender. He looked at the prison, pointed to it, and held up his ten fingers like bars, joking as he peeked through them.

“But it's none of my business,” he said, and went back to sawing his wood.

The next day, he was watching for her and spoke to her the moment she arrived.

“What? Walking here again, citizeness?”

“Yes, citizen.”

“Ah! A child too! That is your mother, isn't it, my little citizeness?”

“Should I say yes, Mama?” whispered little Lucie, holding close to her.

“Yes, dearest.”

“Yes, citizen.”

“Ah! But it's none of my business. My work is my business. Look at my saw! I call it my Little Guillotine. La, la, la; La, la, la! And off his head comes!”

A log fell as he spoke, and he tossed it into a basket.

“I call myself the executioner Samson of the firewood guillotine. Look here again! Loo, loo, loo; Loo, loo, loo! And off _her_ head comes! Now, a child. Tickle, tickle; Pickle, pickle! And off _its_ head comes. The whole family!”

Lucie shivered as he threw two more logs into his basket, but she couldn't stand there without being in his sight. From then on, to keep him friendly, she always spoke to him first and often gave him tip money, which he was happy to take.

He was very curious, and sometimes when she had forgotten all about him while staring up at the prison roof and praying for her husband, she would look over to find him watching her, with his knee on his bench and his saw stopped. “But it's none of my business!” he would usually say, and then start sawing again.

Through snow, winter frost, bitter spring winds, hot summer sun, and autumn rain, Lucie spent two hours there every day. Every day, as she left, she kissed the prison wall. Her husband saw her—according to her father—maybe once every five or six times, sometimes twice in a row, and sometimes not for weeks. Knowing that he could see her when he had the chance was enough, and for that possibility she would have stood there all day, seven days a week.

These daily visits brought her to the month of December, when her father was working calmly amid the city's terrors. One snowy afternoon, she arrived at her usual corner. It was a day of celebration. As she walked, she had seen houses decorated with small toy spears, red caps, tri-color ribbons, and the slogan written in red, white, and blue: "Republic One and Indivisible. Liberty, Equality, Fraternity, or Death!"

The wood-sawyer's tiny shop was too small to fit the long slogan easily. He had paid someone to paint it for him, and they had squeezed the word "Death" onto the end with great difficulty. On his roof, he displayed a toy spear and cap like a good citizen, and in the window he kept his saw labeled "Little Saint Guillotine"—since the crowd now spoke of the machine as if it were a saint. His shop was closed and he was gone, which was a relief to Lucie, leaving her alone.

But he was not far away. Soon, she heard shouting and a loud crowd approaching, which frightened her. A moment later, a mob of people came rushing around the corner. In the middle of them was the wood-sawyer, holding hands with The Vengeance. There were at least five hundred people, dancing like demons. They had no music other than their own singing, chanting the popular revolutionary song to a fierce rhythm that sounded like the grinding of teeth. Men and women danced together in a storm of red caps and rags. They surrounded Lucie and danced wildly around her. They moved forward and backward, clutched at each other's heads, spun around in pairs, and spun in giant circles until many fell from exhaustion. Then they broke into smaller groups, spinning faster and faster, before suddenly stopping, forming a line across the street, and running off screaming. No physical fight could have looked as terrifying as this dance. It was the Carmagnole, a once-innocent pastime now turned into a ritual of anger and madness. Its wild, dark energy showed how twisted the country had become, with young women and girls caught up in this dirty, bloody display.

This was the Carmagnole dance. As it moved away, leaving Lucie frightened and confused in the doorway of the wood-sawyer's shop, the light snow fell quietly and lay white and soft, as if nothing had happened.

“Oh, Father!” she said when she looked up and saw him standing there. “It was such a cruel, horrible sight.”

“I know, my dear, I know. I have seen it many times. Don't be scared! None of them would hurt you.”

“I am not afraid for myself, Father. But when I think of my husband, and the mercy of these people—”

“We will save him from them very soon. I left him climbing to the window, and I came to tell you. No one is watching. You can blow a kiss toward that high roof.”

“I am doing it, Father, and I send him my soul with it!”

“You can't see him, my poor child?”

“No, Father,” Lucie said, crying as she blew a kiss, “no.”

A footstep sounded in the snow. It was Madame Defarge. “Greetings, citizeness,” said the Doctor. “Greetings, citizen,” she replied as she walked by. Nothing more. Madame Defarge vanished like a shadow over the white road.

“Take my arm, my love. Walk away from here looking cheerful and brave, for his sake. That's it,” he said as they left the corner. “It won't be in vain. Charles is summoned for trial tomorrow.”

“For tomorrow!”

“There is no time to lose. I am well prepared, but there are steps we have to take that we couldn't take until he was actually summoned. He hasn't received the notice yet, but I know he will be called tomorrow and moved to the Conciergerie prison. I got the information early. You are not afraid, are you?”

She could barely reply, “I trust you.”

“Trust me completely. The waiting is almost over, my darling. He will be back with you in a few hours. I have arranged every protection for him. I must see Lorry.”

He stopped. They heard the heavy rumble of wheels nearby. They both knew exactly what it meant. One. Two. Three. Three carts carrying their terrible loads away over the silent snow.

“I must see Lorry,” the Doctor repeated, leading her down another street.

The loyal old gentleman was still working at his post. He and his records were often consulted by the government regarding seized property. Whatever he could save for the bank's customers, he saved. There was no better man to guard Tellson's assets and keep his mouth shut.

A cloudy red and yellow sky and mist rising from the Seine River showed that night was falling. It was almost dark when they reached the bank. The lord's grand house was completely ruined and empty. Above a pile of dust and ashes in the yard, painted letters read: "National Property. Republic One and Indivisible. Liberty, Equality, Fraternity, or Death!"

Who could that be with Mr. Lorry—the owner of the coat sitting on the chair—who had to remain hidden? From which newly arrived traveler did Mr. Lorry come out, excited and surprised, to hug Lucie? To whom was he repeating her worried words when he raised his voice, looked back toward the closed door of the private room, and said: “Moved to the Conciergerie, and summoned for trial tomorrow?”

CHAPTER II.

The Mail

On a Friday night late in November, the Dover road lay before the first character of our story. For him, the road stretched out beyond the Dover mail coach as it slowly struggled up Shooter's Hill. He walked up the hill through the mud next to the coach, just like the other passengers. They weren't walking because they enjoyed it. They were walking because everything was too heavy. The hill was steep. The harness was thick. The mud was deep. And the coach itself was enormous. The horses had already stopped three times. Once, they even pulled the coach sideways across the road, clearly planning to turn back toward Blackheath. The reins, whip, coachman, and guard all worked together to say: no. That rebellion was not allowed. The horses reluctantly gave in and returned to their duty.

With drooping heads and shaking tails, they struggled through the thick mud, floundering and stumbling occasionally, as if their main joints were about to fall apart. Every time the driver stopped them to rest, with a cautious "Woah there! Easy now!", the left lead horse violently shook its head and harness—like an especially stubborn horse, protesting that the coach couldn't possibly get up the hill. Whenever the leader made that rattling noise, the passenger flinched, as a nervous traveler might, and felt uneasy.

A steaming mist filled all the low-lying areas, and it had sadly drifted up the hill like an evil spirit, seeking rest but finding none. It was a damp and intensely cold mist, slowly moving through the air in visible ripples that followed and spread over one another like waves on an unhealthy sea. The mist was so thick that the coach-lamps could only light up a few yards of road ahead. Everything else was swallowed up in swirling white fog. The hard-working horses breathed out great clouds of steam. That steam drifted into the mist and became part of it, as if the horses themselves had made the fog.

Two other passengers were trudging up the hill next to the mail coach. All three were bundled up to their cheekbones and over their ears, and wore sturdy riding boots. None of the three passengers could tell, just from looking, what the other two looked like. They were all bundled up completely. Each man was as much of a mystery to his companions as he was to a total stranger. In those days, travelers did not trust strangers quickly. Anyone on the road could be a robber. Even worse, every inn or pub could have a spy on the bandit's payroll. That spy could be the owner, a servant, or the lowliest stable hand. The guard of the Dover mail knew all of this. That Friday night in November 1775, he stood on his platform at the back of the coach. He stamped his feet to stay warm. He kept one hand on the weapons chest. Inside it: a loaded shotgun on top, six or eight loaded horse pistols below, and a cutlass at the very bottom.

The Dover mail was running normally. The guard suspected the passengers. The passengers suspected each other and the guard. They all suspected everyone else. The only person who wasn't suspicious was the coachman. He was simply certain about one thing: the horses were not fit for this journey. He could have sworn that on the Holy Bible.

"Woah there!" said the coachman. "Alright, then! Just one more pull and you’ll be at the top, and damn you, because I’ve had enough trouble getting you this far!—Joe!"

"Hello!" the guard replied.

"What time do you make it, Joe?"

"A good ten past eleven."

"Damn it!" the annoyed coachman burst out, "and we're not even at the top of Shooter's yet! Tsk! Come on! Get moving!"

The lead horse, which had been trying to slow down, was sharply told by the whip to keep going, and it made a determined scramble forward. The three other horses followed suit. Once again, the Dover mail coach struggled onward, with its passengers' boots squelching along beside it. They had stopped when the coach did, and they stayed right next to it. If any of the three had been bold enough to suggest to another that they walk a little ahead into the mist and darkness, he would have been practically asking to be shot instantly as a highwayman.

That last burst of effort carried the mail coach to the top of the hill. The horses stopped to catch their breath, and the guard got down to put the skid-shoe on the wheel for the descent, and open the coach door to let the passengers in.

"Psst! Joe!" the coachman called out in a warning voice, looking down from his seat.

"What do you say, Tom?"

They both listened.

"I hear a horse cantering up, Joe."

"I hear a horse at a gallop, Tom," the guard replied, letting go of the door and nimbly climbing back to his spot. "Gentlemen! In the King's name, everyone!"

After this urgent warning, he cocked his shotgun and got ready to fight.

The passenger this story is about was on the coach step, getting inside; the two other passengers were right behind him, about to follow. He stayed on the step, half in and half out of the coach; they stayed on the road below him. They all looked from the coachman to the guard, then from the guard to the coachman, and listened. The coachman looked back, the guard looked back, and even the lead horse pricked up its ears and listened, without arguing.

The quiet that came after the coach stopped rumbling and working hard, combined with the silent night, made it incredibly still. The horses' panting made the coach shake a little, as if it were nervous. The passengers' hearts were probably pounding loud enough to be heard. Everyone was out of breath. Everyone was holding their breath. Everyone's heart was racing.

The sound of a horse at a gallop came charging up the hill.

"Hey!" the guard shouted as loud as he could. "Hey there! Stop! I'll shoot!"

The horse suddenly stopped. There was a lot of splashing and struggling in the darkness. Then a man's voice called out from the mist. "Is that the Dover mail?"

"Don't you worry about what it is!" the guard shot back. "Who are you?"

"Is that the Dover mail?"

"Why do you want to know?"

"I want a passenger, if it is."

"What passenger?"

"Mr. Jarvis Lorry."

The passenger immediately showed that it was his name. The guard, the coachman, and the two other passengers looked at him suspiciously.

"Stay right there," the guard called to the voice in the mist, "because if I make a mistake, you'll never live to fix it. Mr. Lorry, answer clearly."

"What's going on?" the passenger asked then, with a slightly shaky voice. "Who wants me? Is it Jerry?"

("I don’t like Jerry’s voice, if it is Jerry," the guard grumbled to himself. "His voice is too hoarse for my liking, that Jerry.")

"Yes, Mr. Lorry."

"What is the matter?"

"A message sent after you from over there. T. and Co."

"I know this messenger, guard," said Mr. Lorry. He stepped down onto the road. The other two passengers helped him—less out of politeness and more out of eagerness to be rid of him. The moment he was out, they scrambled back inside, slammed the door, and rolled up the window. "He can come closer," Mr. Lorry called up. "There's nothing wrong."

"I hope not, but I can’t be so sure of that," said the guard, muttering to himself. "Hey you!"

"Well! And hello to you too!" said Jerry, more hoarsely than before.

"Come on at a walking pace! Do you hear me? And if you’ve got holsters on that saddle of yours, don’t let me see your hand go anywhere near them. Because I’m very quick to make a mistake, and when I do, it’s with a bullet. So now let’s get a good look at you."

A horse and rider slowly emerged through the swirling mist and came up to the side of the mail coach, where the passenger was standing. The rider bent down, and, looking up at the guard, handed the passenger a small folded piece of paper. The rider’s horse was winded, and both horse and rider were covered with mud, from the horse’s hooves to the man’s hat.

"Guard!" said the passenger, in a quiet, confident, businesslike tone.

The guard kept his right hand on the stock of his raised shotgun. His left hand held the barrel steady. His eyes stayed fixed on the rider. He answered with a single word. "Sir."

"There’s nothing to worry about. I work for Tellson’s Bank. You must know Tellson’s Bank in London, right? I’m going to Paris on business. Here’s a crown for a drink. Can I read this?"

"As long as you’re quick about it, sir."

He opened it by the light of the coach-lamp on that side, and read it—first to himself and then aloud: "'Wait at Dover for Mademoiselle.' It’s not long, you see, guard. Jerry, tell them my answer was, RECALLED TO LIFE."

Jerry jumped in his saddle. "That's a really strange answer, too," he said, his voice raspy.

"Take that message back, and they'll know I got this, just as if I'd written it myself. Get going. Good night."

With those words, the passenger opened the coach door and got in. His fellow travelers didn't help him at all; they had quickly hidden their watches and purses in their boots and were now all pretending to be asleep. Their only goal was to avoid having to do anything else.

The coach rumbled on again. Thicker swirls of mist wrapped around it as it started going downhill. The guard put his shotgun back in the weapons chest. He checked the rest of the chest's contents. Then he checked the extra pistols on his belt. Finally, he checked a smaller chest under his seat. Inside it: a few blacksmith's tools, a couple of torches, and a tinderbox. He was very well-equipped. If the coach lamps blew out in the wind and rain—which sometimes happened—he could handle it. He just had to close himself in, make sure no sparks landed on the straw, and with a bit of luck he could get a light going in about five minutes.

"Tom!" softly from the coach roof.

"Hello, Joe."

"Did you hear the message?"

"I did, Joe."

"What did you make of it, Tom?"

"Nothing at all, Joe."

"That's a coincidence, too," the guard thought, "because I didn't get anything from it either."

Jerry was left alone in the mist and darkness. He got off his horse. He wanted to give the tired animal a rest. He also needed to wipe the mud from his own face. He shook the water out of his hat brim. The hat looked like it could hold about half a gallon. After standing with the bridle draped over his heavily-splashed arm until he couldn't hear the mail coach's wheels anymore and the night was completely quiet again, he turned to walk down the hill.

"After that gallop from Temple Bar, old lady, I won't trust your front legs until we're on flat ground," said the hoarse messenger, glancing at his mare. "'Recalled to life.' That's a really strange message. A lot of that wouldn't work out for you, Jerry! Hey, Jerry! You'd be in a really bad way if bringing people back to life became popular, Jerry!"

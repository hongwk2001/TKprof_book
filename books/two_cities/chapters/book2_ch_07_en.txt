CHAPTER VII.
Monseigneur in Town

Monseigneur, one of the most powerful lords at Court, held a reception every two weeks at his grand mansion in Paris. Monseigneur was in his private inner room—his holy sanctuary—while a crowd of worshippers waited in the outer rooms. Monseigneur was preparing to drink his morning hot chocolate. Monseigneur was capable of swallowing many things with ease. A few bitter minds accused him of rapidly swallowing up the wealth of France. But his morning chocolate could not even reach his throat without the help of four strong men in addition to the cook.

Indeed, it took four men to get the chocolate to his lips. All four were decorated in gold and fine clothing. Their leader required at least two gold watches in his pocket—just to copy the noble fashion set by Monseigneur. One servant carried the chocolate pot; a second frothed the drink with a small whisk; a third offered a napkin; and a fourth (the one with the two gold watches) poured it out. It was impossible for Monseigneur to do without any of these servants and still maintain his high social standing. It would have been a terrible disgrace to his family name if his chocolate had been served by only three men; he would have died of shame if there were only two.

Monseigneur had attended a private dinner party the night before, where actresses and opera singers were present. He went out to such parties almost every night with charming company. Monseigneur was so polite and easily influenced that comedies and opera singers had far more influence on him regarding state affairs and secrets than the actual needs of all France. This was a happy circumstance for France, as such things always are for countries favored with similar rulers—just as it had been for England when the merry King Charles II sold his country to France.

Monseigneur had one noble idea about public business in general: to let everything take care of itself. As for his own private business, he believed everything must go his way to increase his power and wealth. Regarding his pleasures, he was convinced that the world had been created solely for his enjoyment. His motto was: "The earth and everything in it belong to me, says Monseigneur."

Yet Monseigneur had slowly realized that financial troubles were creeping into both his public and private affairs. To handle this, he had been forced to partner with a Farmer-General—a wealthy tax collector. Since Monseigneur knew nothing about public finances, he had to lease them to someone who did. For his private finances, because he was growing poor after generations of luxury, he relied on the Farmer-General's wealth. Therefore, Monseigneur had taken his sister out of a convent. Letting her become a nun would have been the cheapest way to support her—but instead, he married her off to a very rich tax collector from a low-class family. This tax collector, carrying a cane with a golden apple on top, was now in the outer rooms. The crowd bowed low before him—except for the noble relatives of Monseigneur, who, along with his own wife, looked down on him with utter contempt.

The tax collector was a very wealthy man. He kept thirty horses in his stables, twenty-four male servants in his halls, and six ladies-in-waiting for his wife. As someone whose only job was to plunder and gather wealth wherever he could, he was at least the most honest reality among the guests at Monseigneur’s mansion that day.

Although the rooms were beautiful and decorated with the finest art of the time, they did not represent a healthy society. Nearby lived starving people in rags and nightcaps. The towers of Notre Dame Cathedral could easily see both worlds—the mansion and the misery—standing between the two extremes. If anyone at Monseigneur's house ever bothered to think about it, the contrast would have seemed like an uncomfortable business. The guests included military officers with no knowledge of war, naval officers who knew nothing of ships, civil servants with no idea how to govern, and hypocritical priests with greedy eyes and loose morals. All of them were completely unfit for their jobs, but because they were related to Monseigneur's class, they were given public positions purely for profit. There were dozens of them. There were also doctors making fortunes by prescribing expensive remedies for imaginary illnesses, smiling at their wealthy patients in the waiting rooms. Inventors proposed solutions for minor state problems, ignoring the real need to root out corruption. Non-believing philosophers talked of remodeling the world with words, while chemists talked of turning lead into gold. Exquisite gentlemen showed their high breeding by acting completely bored by everything. The spies among the guests—who made up at least half the crowd—would have struggled to find a single wife who acted like a real mother to her children. In high society, children were sent away to be raised by peasant women, while grandmothers of sixty dressed and dined like young women of twenty.

A sense of unreality clung to everyone around Monseigneur. In the outermost room stood a few people who had begun to worry that things in France were going wrong. To fix this, some of them had joined a strange religious sect that fell into fits and convulsions, hoping to find guidance for the future. Others had joined a group that talked about "the Center of Truth," claiming that men could be saved by fasting and talking to spirits.

Fortunately, all the guests were dressed perfectly. If the Day of Judgment were a fashion show, everyone there would have been saved. They had powdered hair, painted faces, fine swords, and expensive perfumes. They looked capable of keeping the system going forever. The gentlemen wore gold trinkets that jingled as they walked like little bells. Between the jingling and the rustling of silk and fine linen, the air was filled with a flurry that blew the memory of the starving people of Saint Antoine far away.

Fashionable dress was the magic charm used to keep everything in its place. Everyone acted as if they were at a never-ending costume ball. This fashion extended from the royal palace down to the executioner himself, who was required to perform his duties with powdered hair, a gold-trimmed coat, and white silk stockings. Monsieur Paris, the head executioner, dressed in this elegant fashion at the gallows. No one at the reception in 1780 doubted that a system run by a powdered, silk-stockinged executioner would last forever.

Having finished his chocolate, Monseigneur opened his doors and walked out. The crowd bowed, flattered, and humbled themselves before him. They showed so much devotion that they left nothing for God—which was probably why they never bothered to pray.

Giving a promise here and a smile there, whispering to one happy guest and waving to another, Monseigneur walked through the rooms. He then turned and returned to his private room, shut in once more by his servants, and was seen no more.

With the reception over, the guests hurried downstairs, their gold trinkets jingling. Soon only one man was left, slowly walking past the mirrors on his way out, holding his hat and snuffbox.

"I condemn you," he muttered, stopping at the door and looking back toward Monseigneur's room, "to the Devil!"

He shook the snuff from his fingers as if shaking dust from his shoes, and walked downstairs.

He was a man of about sixty, handsomely dressed, proud, with a face like a mask. His face was very pale, with sharp features and a cold expression. His nose was well-shaped, but pinched at the nostrils. This pinching was the only part of his face that ever changed, turning white or twitching slightly when he was angry, giving him a look of cruelty and treachery. Despite this, he was a handsome and striking man.

He went down to the courtyard, got into his carriage, and drove away. Few people had spoken to him at the reception, and Monseigneur had been cold to him. Under the circumstances, he seemed to enjoy seeing the common people scatter before his horses, barely escaping with their lives. His driver drove recklessly, as if charging into battle, and the master did not stop him. Sometimes people complained that the fast driving in the narrow streets without sidewalks injured and killed poor citizens. But few wealthy people cared, and the poor were left to get out of the way as best they could.

With a loud clatter and a complete lack of concern, the carriage sped through the streets and around corners. Women screamed, and men grabbed their children to pull them out of the way. Suddenly, near a fountain at a street corner, a wheel hit something with a sickening jolt. The horses reared, and the crowd screamed.

If the horses hadn't stopped, the carriage would have driven on, leaving the injured behind. But the driver quickly got down, and several people grabbed the horses' reins.

"What is the matter?" the master asked, looking out calmly.

A tall man in a nightcap had picked up a bundle from under the horses' hooves and laid it by the fountain. He was kneeling in the mud, crying out like a wild animal.

"Pardon, Monsieur the Marquis!" said a poor man, bowing. "It is a child."

"Why is he making that terrible noise? Is it his child?"

"Excuse me, Monsieur the Marquis—yes, unfortunately."

The street opened into a small square around the fountain. As the tall man suddenly stood up and ran toward the carriage, the Marquis put his hand on his sword.

"Killed!" the man shrieked, raising his arms. "Dead!"

The crowd gathered around and looked at the Marquis. Their eyes showed only watchfulness and curiosity, not anger or threats. They remained silent after their first cry. The man who had spoken sounded humble and frightened. The Marquis looked at them as if they were rats coming out of their holes.

He took out his purse.

"It is strange to me," he said, "that you people cannot take care of yourselves and your children. You are always in the way. For all I know, you have injured my horses. Here! Give him this."

He threw a gold coin out for the driver to pick up, and the crowd stared at it as it fell. The tall man cried out again, "Dead!"

He was stopped by the arrival of another man, for whom the crowd made way. Seeing him, the father fell on his shoulder, sobbing and pointing to the fountain where some women were looking at the still bundle. The women were as quiet as the men.

"I know, I know," said the newcomer. "Be brave, Gaspard. It is better for the poor child to die like this than to live in misery. It died in a moment, without pain. Could it have lived a happy life?"

"You are a philosopher," the Marquis said, smiling. "What is your name?"

"They call me Defarge."

"What is your trade?"

"I sell wine, Monsieur the Marquis."

"Pick that up, philosopher and wine seller," said the Marquis, throwing him another gold coin. "Spend it as you like. Are the horses ready?"

Without looking at the crowd again, the Marquis leaned back, preparing to drive away like a gentleman who had broken a cheap object and paid for it. Suddenly, a coin was thrown back into the carriage, ringing on the floor.

"Stop!" the Marquis called out. "Hold the horses! Who threw that?"

He looked toward where Defarge had been standing. The father was on the pavement, crying. Next to him stood a stout, dark woman—knitting.

"You dogs!" the Marquis said calmly, his nostrils twitching. "I would gladly run over all of you and wipe you off the earth. If I knew who threw that coin, I would crush him under the wheels."

The crowd was so cowed and used to his power that no one raised a hand or said a word. But the woman who stood knitting looked the Marquis straight in the eye. He ignored her, looking over them like rats, and told his driver, "Go on!"

He drove away. Other carriages followed in quick succession—the ministers, tax collectors, doctors, and lawyers. They were all part of the grand costume ball. The poor people stayed in the street for hours, watching through the barrier of soldiers and police. The father had carried his dead child away. The women sat by the fountain, watching the water flow and the carriages pass, while the woman who had stood knitting continued to knit like the figure of Fate. The water ran, the river flowed, day turned to evening, and life in the city turned to death. The poor went back to their dark rooms, the rich lit up their dinners, and everything continued as before.

CHAPTER X.
The Substance of the Shadow


“I, Alexandre Manette, an unfortunate doctor from Beauvais who later lived in Paris, write this sad paper in my dark cell in the Bastille during the last month of the year 1767. I write it in secret moments, facing every difficulty. I plan to hide it in the chimney wall, where I have slowly and carefully made a hiding place for it. Some kind hand may find it there when I and my sorrows are nothing but dust.

“These words are written with a rusty iron point, which I use with great difficulty, using soot and charcoal scraped from the chimney and mixed with my own blood. It is now the last month of the tenth year of my imprisonment. Hope has completely left my heart. I know from terrible warning signs in myself that my mind will not stay clear for much longer. However, I solemnly declare that right now I am in my right mind—that my memory is exact and detailed—and that I write the truth as I will have to answer for these last recorded words at God's final judgment, whether they are ever read by men or not.

“One cloudy, moonlit night in the third week of December (I think it was the twenty-second) in the year 1757, I was walking on a quiet part of the path along the Seine River to get some fresh, frosty air. I was about an hour's walk from my home on the Street of the School of Medicine when a carriage came up behind me, driving very fast. I stepped aside to let the carriage pass, worried that it might run me over, when a head popped out of the window and a voice called for the driver to stop.

“The carriage stopped as soon as the driver could pull back his horses, and the same voice called out my name. I answered. The carriage was so far ahead of me that two gentlemen had time to open the door and step down before I reached it.

“I noticed that they were both wrapped in cloaks and seemed to be hiding their identities. As they stood side by side near the carriage door, I also noticed that they both looked to be about my age, or perhaps younger, and they were extremely similar in height, behavior, voice, and (from what I could see) face as well.

“‘Are you Doctor Manette?’ one asked.

“I am.”

“‘Doctor Manette, formerly of Beauvais,’ said the other, ‘the young doctor, originally a skilled surgeon, who has made a growing reputation in Paris over the last year or two?’

“‘Gentlemen,’ I replied, ‘I am indeed the Doctor Manette you speak of so kindly.’

“‘We went to your home,’ said the first man, ‘but we were not lucky enough to find you. When we were told you were probably walking in this direction, we followed you, hoping to catch up. Please get into the carriage.’

“The manner of both men was very commanding, and as they spoke, they moved to trap me between them and the carriage door. They were armed. I was not.

“‘Gentlemen,’ I said, ‘excuse me, but I usually ask who is asking for my help, and what kind of medical case I am being called to see.’

“The second man replied to this. ‘Doctor, your patients are people of high social standing. As for the nature of the illness, we are so confident in your skill that we know you will figure it out for yourself better than we could describe it. That is enough. Please get into the carriage.’

“I had no choice but to obey, and I got in silently. They both followed me—the last one jumping in after folding up the carriage steps. The carriage turned around and drove off at the same high speed as before.

“I repeat this conversation exactly as it happened. I have no doubt it is word-for-word correct. I describe everything exactly as it occurred, forcing my mind not to wander from the task. Wherever I make the star marks that follow, it means I stopped writing for a while and put my paper back in its hiding place.

        *****

“The carriage left the city streets, passed through the North Gate, and went onto a country road. About two miles from the gate—I did not measure the distance then, but did so later when I traveled it again—the carriage turned off the main road and soon stopped at a lonely house. The three of us got out and walked down a damp, soft garden path, past a neglected, overflowing fountain, to the door. It was not opened right away when we rang the bell, so one of my guides struck the servant who finally opened it across the face with his heavy riding glove.

“There was nothing unusual in this to catch my attention, for I had seen common people beaten more often than dogs. But the other gentleman, who was also angry, struck the servant in the same way with his arm. The looks and actions of the two men were so identical that I realized for the first time they were twin brothers.

“From the moment we got out at the outer gate (which we found locked, and which one of the brothers unlocked to let us in and then locked again), I had heard screams coming from an upstairs room. I was taken straight to this room, the screams growing louder as we climbed the stairs, and I found a patient with a severe brain fever lying on a bed.

“The patient was a very beautiful young woman, certainly not much over twenty. Her hair was torn and messy, and her arms were tied to her sides with sashes and handkerchiefs. I noticed that these ties were all parts of a gentleman's clothing. On one of them, which was a fringed scarf used for formal wear, I saw the coat of arms of a nobleman and the letter E.

“I saw this during my very first minute of examining the patient. In her restless struggling, she had turned over onto her face at the edge of the bed, drawing the end of the scarf into her mouth, and was in danger of choking. My first act was to reach out and help her breathe, and as I moved the scarf away, I saw the embroidered coat of arms in the corner.

“I turned her over gently, placed my hands on her chest to calm her and keep her still, and looked into her face. Her eyes were wide and wild, and she kept screaming and repeating the words, ‘My husband, my father, and my brother!’ She would then count up to twelve and say, ‘Hush!’ For just a second, she would stop to listen, and then the piercing screams would start all over again. She would repeat the cry, ‘My husband, my father, and my brother!’ count to twelve, and say, ‘Hush!’ She never changed the order or the way she said it. She never stopped, except for that regular, brief moment of listening.

“‘How long,’ I asked, ‘has this been going on?’

“To tell the brothers apart, I will call them the older and the younger. By the older, I mean the one who showed the most authority. It was the older brother who replied, ‘Since about this time last night.’

“‘Does she have a husband, a father, and a brother?’

“‘A brother.’

“‘Am I speaking to her brother?’

“He answered with great scorn, ‘No.’

“‘Has she recently associated something with the number twelve?’

“The younger brother added impatiently, ‘With twelve o'clock?’

“‘Look, gentlemen,’ I said, keeping my hands on her chest, ‘I am useless to you since you brought me here like this! If I had known what kind of case I was coming to see, I could have brought the right supplies. Now we must waste time. There are no medicines to be found in this lonely place.’

“The older brother looked at the younger, who said proudly, ‘There is a box of medicines here,’ and brought it out of a closet, placing it on the table.

        *****

“I opened some of the bottles, smelled them, and put the stoppers to my lips. If I had wanted to use anything other than strong sedatives—which are poisons in large doses—I would not have used any of them.

“‘Do you doubt the medicines?’ asked the younger brother.

“‘You see, monsieur, I am about to use them,’ I replied, and said nothing more.

“With great difficulty and after many attempts, I got the patient to swallow the dose I wanted to give her. Since I planned to give it to her again later and needed to watch how it affected her, I sat down next to the bed. There was a quiet, timid woman helping (the wife of the servant downstairs) who had shrunk into a corner. The house was damp, decaying, and poorly furnished—clearly it had been occupied recently and only for temporary use. Thick old curtains had been nailed over the windows to muffle the sound of her screams. She kept screaming in the same regular order: the cry of ‘My husband, my father, and my brother!’ the count to twelve, and ‘Hush!’ Her madness was so violent that I did not loosen the bandages holding her arms, though I checked to make sure they were not hurting her. The only encouraging sign was that my hand on the poor girl's chest had a soothing effect, quietening her body for minutes at a time. However, it did not stop her cries; no clock pendulum could have been more regular.

“Probably because my hand had this calming effect, I sat by the bedside for half an hour with the two brothers watching before the older one spoke:

“‘There is another patient.’

“I was startled and asked, ‘Is it an urgent case?’

“‘You had better see for yourself,’ he answered carelessly, and picked up a candle.

        *****

“The other patient was in a back room across a second staircase, which was a kind of loft over a stable. Part of the room had a low, plastered ceiling, while the rest was open to the tiled roof with wooden beams running across. Hay and straw were stored in that part of the loft, along with firewood and a pile of apples stored in sand. I had to walk through that storage area to reach the patient. My memory is detailed and firm. I test it with these details, and I can see them all here in my Bastille cell, near the end of the tenth year of my imprisonment, just as I saw them that night.

“On some hay on the ground, with a cushion placed under his head, lay a handsome peasant boy—he could not have been more than seventeen years old. He lay on his back with his teeth clenched, his right hand pressed tightly against his chest, and his staring eyes looking straight up. I could not see where his wound was as I knelt beside him, but I could tell he was dying from a puncture wound.

“‘I am a doctor, my poor boy,’ I said. ‘Let me look at your wound.’

“‘I don't want it examined,’ he answered. ‘Leave it alone.’

“The wound was under his hand, and I calmed him until he let me move his hand away. It was a sword wound received twenty to twenty-four hours earlier, but no medical skill could have saved him even if he had been treated immediately. He was dying fast. When I looked up at the older brother, I saw him looking down at this handsome, dying boy as if he were just a wounded bird, a hare, or a rabbit—not a fellow human being at all.

“‘How did this happen, monsieur?’ I asked.

“‘A crazy young peasant dog! A serf! He forced my brother to draw his sword against him, and fell by my brother's blade—like a gentleman.’

“There was no trace of pity, sadness, or human feeling in his answer. The speaker spoke as if it were simply inconvenient to have this lower kind of creature dying there, and that it would have been better if he had died in the usual quiet way of his vermin class. He was completely unable to feel any sympathy for the boy or his fate.

“The boy's eyes had moved slowly to the man as he spoke, and now they slowly turned to me.

        “‘Doctor, they are very proud, these Nobles; but we common dogs are proud too, sometimes. They rob us, abuse us, beat us, and kill us, but we still have a little pride left sometimes. She—have you seen her, Doctor?’

“Her screams and cries could be heard there, though they were quieted by the distance. He spoke of her as if she were lying right there with us.

“I said, ‘I have seen her.’

“‘She is my sister, Doctor. For many years, these Nobles have taken their shameful rights with our sisters' modesty and virtue, but we have had good girls among us. I know it, and I've heard my father say so. She was a good girl. She was engaged to a good young man, too, who was a tenant of his. We were all tenants of his—that man standing there. The other one is his brother, the worst of a bad family.’

“It was extremely hard for the boy to find the strength to speak, but his spirit spoke with terrifying power.

“‘We were robbed so badly by that man standing there, just as all of us common dogs are by those superior Beings—taxed by him without mercy, forced to work for him without pay, forced to grind our grain at his mill, forced to feed dozens of his tame birds on our poor crops, while we were forbidden under pain of death to keep a single tame bird of our own. We were plundered to the point that if we happened to have a piece of meat, we ate it in fear with the door locked and the shutters closed so his servants wouldn't see it and take it from us. I tell you, we were so robbed and hunted, and made so poor, that our father told us it was a terrible thing to bring a child into the world. He said we should pray that our women would be barren and our miserable family line would die out!’

“I had never before seen the pain of oppression burst out like a fire. I had assumed it must be hidden in the common people somewhere, but I had never seen it break out until I saw it in this dying boy.

“‘Even so, Doctor, my sister married. Her lover was sick at the time, poor guy, and she married him so she could care for and comfort him in our cottage—our dog-hut, as that nobleman would call it. She had only been married a few weeks when that man's brother saw her, took a liking to her, and asked that nobleman to lend her to him—because what are husbands to people like us! The nobleman was willing enough, but my sister was good and pure, and she hated the brother as much as I did. So what did the two of them do to force her husband to make her give in?’

“The boy's eyes, which had been fixed on mine, turned slowly toward the older brother, and I could see from both of their faces that everything he said was true. Even now in the Bastille, I can still picture those two opposite kinds of pride facing each other: the nobleman's careless indifference, and the peasant's crushed feelings and passionate desire for revenge.

“‘You know, Doctor, that these Nobles have the legal right to harness us common dogs to carts and drive us like horses. They harnessed him and drove him that way. You know it is their right to keep us in their swamps all night, beating the water to quiet the frogs so their noble sleep won't be disturbed. They kept him out in the unhealthy night mists and ordered him back to work during the day. But he would not give in. No! Taken out of the harness one day at noon to eat—if he could find any food—he sobbed twelve times, once for each strike of the clock, and died in her arms.’

"Nothing human could have kept the boy alive except his determination to tell of his wrongs. He fought off the approaching shadows of death, just as he forced his clenched right hand to stay closed over his wound.

“‘Then, with that nobleman's permission and even his help, his brother took her away. In spite of what I know she must have told the brother—and you will find out what that was soon enough, Doctor, if you don't know already—his brother took her away for his own pleasure and amusement for a little while. I saw her pass me on the road. When I brought the news home, our father's heart broke; he died without saying a word of the pain that filled him. I took my youngest sister (for I have another one) to a place far away out of this man's reach, where she will never be _his_ servant. Then I followed the brother here, and last night I climbed in—a common dog, but with a sword in my hand. Where is the loft window? It was around here somewhere.’

“The room was growing dark to his eyes; the world was closing in on him. I looked around and saw that the hay and straw were scattered all over the floor, as if there had been a fight.

“‘She heard me and ran in. I told her not to come near us until he was dead. He came in and first threw some coins at me, then struck at me with a whip. But even though I am a common dog, I attacked him so hard that I forced him to draw his sword. Let him break that sword into a thousand pieces—the sword he stained with my common blood—but he had to draw it to defend himself and fight with all his skill for his life.’

“Just a few moments earlier, my eyes had fallen on the pieces of a broken sword lying in the hay. That weapon belonged to a gentleman. In another spot lay an old sword that looked like it had belonged to a soldier.

“‘Now lift me up, Doctor; lift me up. Where is he?’

“‘He is not here,’ I said, holding the boy up, thinking he was talking about the brother.

“‘Him! As proud as these nobles are, he is afraid to look at me. Where is the man who was standing here? Turn my face toward him.’

“I did so, resting the boy's head against my knee. But suddenly filled with an unbelievable strength, he pulled himself all the way up, forcing me to stand up too so I could keep supporting him.

“‘Marquis,’ the boy said, turning to him with wide eyes and his right hand raised, ‘in the days when all these things must be answered for, I summon you and your family, to the very last of your evil race, to answer for them. I mark this cross of blood upon you as a sign of my summons. In the days when all these things must be answered for, I summon your brother, the worst of your evil family, to answer for them separately. I mark this cross of blood upon him as a sign of my summons.’

“Twice he touched the wound in his chest and drew a cross in the air with his index finger. He stood for a moment with his finger still raised, and when it fell, he fell with it. I laid him down, dead.

        *****

“When I went back to the young woman's bedside, I found her screaming in the exact same order as before. I knew this might go on for many hours, and that it would likely end only in death.

“I gave her another dose of the medicine and sat by the bed until late in the night. She never stopped her piercing screams, and never made a mistake in the clear order of her words. They were always: ‘My husband, my father, and my brother! One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight, nine, ten, eleven, twelve. Hush!’

“This went on for twenty-six hours from the time I first saw her. I had left and returned twice, and was sitting by her again when her strength began to fail. I did what little I could to help her rest, and soon she fell into a deep sleep and lay as still as a corpse. It was as if the wind and rain had finally stopped after a long, terrifying storm. I untied her arms and called the servant's wife to help me straighten her body and the clothes she had torn. It was then I realized she was pregnant, and at that moment I lost the little hope I had left for her.

“‘Is she dead?’ asked the Marquis, the older brother, walking into the room still wearing his riding boots.

“‘Not dead,’ I said, ‘but close to it.’

“‘What strength there is in these peasant bodies!’ he said, looking down at her with curiosity.

“‘There is enormous strength,’ I answered him, ‘in grief and despair.’

“He laughed at my words at first, then frowned. He kicked a chair over near mine, ordered the woman to leave, and said in a quiet voice:

“‘Doctor, when I found my brother in this trouble with these peasants, I suggested we call for your help. Your reputation is high, and as a young man trying to make a career, you probably care about your own success. The things you see here are meant to be seen, but never spoken of.’

“I listened to the patient's breathing and avoided answering him.

“‘Are you listening to me, Doctor?’

“‘Monsieur,’ I said, ‘in my profession, whatever a patient tells a doctor is always kept strictly confidential.’ I was careful with my reply because I was deeply troubled by what I had seen and heard.

“Her breathing was so faint that I carefully checked her pulse and heartbeat. She was barely alive. Looking up as I sat back down, I saw both brothers watching me closely.

        *****

“I write with so much difficulty, the cold is so bitter, and I am so afraid of being caught and thrown into a dark dungeon that I must shorten this story. There is no confusion or memory loss; I can remember and detail every single word spoken between me and those brothers.

“She hung onto life for a week. Near the end, I could make out a few words she whispered by putting my ear close to her lips. She asked me where she was and who I was, and I told her. I tried in vain to ask for her family name. She just shook her head weakly on the pillow, keeping her secret just as her brother had done.

"I had no chance to ask her any questions until I told the brothers she was dying and could not last another day. Until then, even though she only saw the servant's wife and me, one of the brothers had always sat watchfully behind the curtain at the head of the bed whenever I was there. But when she was near death, they didn't seem to care what I said to her—as if, a sudden thought struck me, they planned for me to die too.

“I noticed that their pride was deeply hurt by the fact that the younger brother had fought a peasant boy. The only thing that seemed to bother either of them was that this fight was degrading to their family name and made them look ridiculous. Whenever I met the younger brother's eyes, I could see that he hated me for knowing what the dying boy had told me. He was more polite and pleasant to me than the older brother, but I could see right through him. I also realized that the older brother saw me as an annoying problem to be dealt with.

“My patient died two hours before midnight—almost to the exact minute I had first seen her a week earlier. I was alone with her when her poor, young head fell gently to the side, and all her earthly pain and sorrows ended.

“The brothers were waiting in a room downstairs, eager to ride away. While I sat alone by the bedside, I had heard them pacing up and down, tapping their boots with their riding whips.

“‘Is she finally dead?’ the older one asked when I went downstairs.

“‘She is dead,’ I said.

“‘Congratulations, brother,’ he said, turning to his twin.

“He had offered me money earlier, which I had put off taking. Now he handed me a roll of gold coins. I took it from his hand but set it down on the table. I had thought it over and decided not to accept anything from them.

“‘Please excuse me,’ I said. ‘Under these circumstances, I cannot take it.’

“They looked at each other, but bowed their heads to me as I bowed to them, and we parted without saying another word.

        *****

“I am so tired, tired, tired—worn down by misery. I can barely read what my thin hand has written.

“Early the next morning, the roll of gold was left at my door in a small box with my name written on it. From the beginning, I had worried about what I should do. That day, I decided to write a private letter to the government Minister, explaining the two patients I had been called to see, the location of the house, and all the details. I knew how much influence the Nobles had at Court and how they were above the law, so I expected nothing would be done about it; however, I wanted to clear my own conscience. I had kept the secret completely from my wife, and I decided to mention that in the letter too. I had no fear for my own safety, but I knew there could be danger for others if they were dragged into this by knowing what I knew.

“I was very busy that day and couldn't finish the letter that night. I woke up long before my usual time the next morning to complete it. It was the last day of the year. The letter was lying finished on the desk in front of me when I was told that a lady was waiting to see me.

        *****

“I am becoming less and less able to finish this task. It is so cold and dark, my senses are so numb, and the sadness around me is so terrible.

“The lady was young, charming, and beautiful, but she looked pale and weak. She was extremely upset. She introduced herself as the wife of the Marquis St. Evrémonde. I connected the title the dying boy had used for the older brother with the letter embroidered on the scarf, and immediately realized I had met her husband very recently.

“My memory is still clear, but I cannot write down all the words of our conversation. I suspect I am being watched more closely now, and I do not know when they are spying on me. She had guessed some of the facts and discovered the rest of the cruel story—her husband's part in it, and how they had called for my help. She did not know the young woman had died. She told me in great distress that she had hoped to show the poor girl a woman's sympathy in secret. She had hoped to turn away God's anger from a noble family that had long been hated by the suffering people.

“She had reasons to believe there was a younger sister still alive, and her greatest wish was to help her. I could only tell her that the sister did exist; beyond that, I knew nothing. She had come to me, trusting my confidentiality, hoping I could tell her the girl's name and where she lived. But to this miserable hour, I still know neither.

        *****

“I am running out of paper. One piece was taken from me yesterday, along with a warning. I must finish my record today.

“She was a good, kind lady, and she was not happy in her marriage. How could she be! The brother-in-law distrusted and disliked her, and his influence was completely against her; she was terrified of him, and of her husband too. When I walked her down to the carriage, I saw a child inside—a pretty little boy about two or three years old.

        “‘For his sake, Doctor,’ she said, pointing to the boy through her tears, ‘I want to do everything I can to make up for this. Otherwise, he will never find happiness in his inheritance. I have a feeling that if no other innocent person pays for this crime, it will one day be demanded of him. The little I have of my own—which is hardly more than a few jewels—I will make it his first duty in life to give to this injured family, along with the love and sorrow of his dead mother, if the sister can be found.’

“She kissed the boy and said as she held him close, ‘It is for your own dear sake. You will be faithful to this promise, little Charles?’ The child answered her bravely, ‘Yes!’ I kissed her hand, and she took him in her arms and drove away. I never saw her again.

“Since she had mentioned her husband's name, assuming I already knew it, I did not add it to my letter. I sealed the envelope, and not trusting anyone else, I delivered it myself that day.

“That night, the last night of the year, around nine o'clock, a man dressed in black rang my doorbell, asked to see me, and quietly followed my young servant, Ernest Defarge, upstairs. When my servant entered the room where I sat with my wife—oh, my beloved wife, the joy of my heart, my beautiful young English wife!—we saw the man standing silently behind him.

“He said there was an urgent case on Rue Saint-Honoré. He promised it wouldn't take long and said a carriage was waiting.

“It brought me here; it brought me to my grave. As soon as I stepped out of the house, a black scarf was pulled tightly over my mouth from behind, and my arms were tied. The two brothers walked across the road from a dark corner and pointed me out with a simple gesture. The Marquis took the letter I had written from his pocket, showed it to me, burned it in the light of a lantern, and stamped out the ashes with his foot. Not a single word was spoken. I was brought here—to my living grave.

“If it had pleased _God_ to put it in the hard hearts of either of the brothers during all these terrible years to give me any news of my dearest wife—even just a single word to tell me if she was alive or dead—I might have believed that He had not completely abandoned them. But now I believe that the mark of the red cross has doomed them, and that they have no share in His mercy. So, on this last night of the year 1767, in my unbearable agony, I, Alexandre Manette, an unhappy prisoner, denounce them and their descendants to the very last of their family line, to the time when all these crimes must be answered for. I denounce them to Heaven and to earth.”

A terrifying noise arose when the reading of this document finished. It was a sound of hungry eagerness, with no clear words except a cry for blood. The story stirred up the most vengeful feelings of the time, and there was not a noble head in the country that would not have fallen after hearing it.

There was no need, in front of that court and audience, to explain why the Defarges had not made the paper public with the other captured Bastille records during the parades. They had kept it hidden, waiting for the perfect time. There was no need to show that this hated family name had long been cursed by the people of Saint Antoine, and was already written in the secret registry. No man had ever lived whose good deeds and service could have saved him in that courtroom today against such an accusation.

And it was all the worse for the doomed man that his accuser was a respected citizen, his own close friend, and his wife's father. The crowd's crazy desire was to imitate the harsh public virtues of ancient times, offering up human sacrifices on the altar of the people. Therefore, when the President declared (knowing his own head would roll if he didn't), that the good doctor of the Republic would serve the country even better by wiping out a hated family of aristocrats, and would surely feel a holy joy in making his own daughter a widow and her child an orphan, there was wild excitement and patriotic passion, but not a single drop of human sympathy.

“Does that Doctor have a lot of influence now?” whispered Madame Defarge, smiling at The Vengeance. “Save him now, my Doctor, save him!”

With every juror's vote, a roar went up. Another and another. Roar after roar.

The vote was unanimous. By birth and by nature an aristocrat, an enemy of the Republic, and a well-known oppressor of the people. Back to the Conciergerie prison, and death within twenty-four hours!

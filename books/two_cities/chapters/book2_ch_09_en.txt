CHAPTER IX.
The Gorgon’s Head

The Marquis’s castle was a heavy stone building, with a large stone courtyard in front, and two curved stone staircases meeting at a stone terrace before the main entrance. It was a stony place altogether, with heavy stone balustrades, stone urns, stone flowers, and stone faces of men and lions in every direction. It looked as if the mythical Gorgon’s head had looked at it when it was finished two hundred years ago, turning the whole place to stone.

Up the broad, shallow steps walked the Marquis, preceded by a servant carrying a torch. His arrival disturbed the darkness enough to make an owl in the stable roof hoot loudly. Everything else was so quiet that the torch carried up the steps and another held at the entrance burned steadily, as if they were in a closed room rather than the open night air. The only sounds were the owl's hooting and the splashing of the fountain into its stone basin. It was one of those dark nights that seem to hold their breath for hours, sighing softly before holding their breath again.

The great door slammed shut behind him. The Marquis walked through a hall decorated with old boar spears, swords, and hunting knives. It was made even grimmer by the heavy riding whips on the walls, many of which had been felt by the local peasants when their lord was angry.

Avoiding the larger rooms, which were dark and locked for the night, the Marquis followed his torchbearer upstairs to a room in the corridor. This door opened into his private suite of three rooms: his bedroom and two others. They were high-ceilinged, vaulted rooms with cool stone floors and large wood-burning fireplaces for winter. They were filled with luxuries suitable for a nobleman of that wealthy era. The style of King Louis XIV was visible in the rich furniture, alongside decorations showing older pages of French history.

A dinner table was set for two in the third room, which was a round room located in one of the castle's towers. It was a small, high room with its window wide open and the wooden blinds closed, so the dark night showed only as thin black lines between the blinds.

"My nephew," said the Marquis, looking at the table. "They said he had not arrived."

He had not, but he had been expected to arrive with the Marquis.

"Ah! He probably won't arrive tonight, but leave the table as it is. I will be ready in fifteen minutes."

Fifteen minutes later, the Marquis sat down alone to his dinner. His chair faced the window. He had finished his soup and was raising his glass of Bordeaux wine to his lips when he suddenly put it down.

"What is that?" he asked calmly, looking at the blinds.

"Monseigneur? What?"

"Outside the blinds. Open them."

The servant did so.

"Well?"

"Monseigneur, there is nothing. Just the trees and the night."

The servant had opened the blinds and looked out into the darkness, then stood waiting for instructions.

"Good," said the calm master. "Close them again."

This was done, and the Marquis went on eating. He was halfway through his meal when he stopped again, glass in hand, hearing the sound of wheels. A carriage drove up quickly to the front of the castle.

"Find out who has arrived."

It was the Marquis's nephew. He had been traveling behind his uncle earlier in the afternoon, closing the distance but not catching up on the road. At the post houses, they had told him his uncle was ahead of him.

He was told that dinner was waiting for him, and he was asked to join. Soon he entered. In England, he was known as Charles Darnay.

The Marquis received him politely, but they did not shake hands.

"You left Paris yesterday, sir?" Darnay asked as he sat down.

"Yesterday. And you?"

"I came straight here."

"From London?"

"Yes."

"You took a long time to make the journey," the Marquis said with a smile.

"On the contrary, I came directly."

"Excuse me! I don't mean the journey took long; I mean it took you a long time to decide to come."

"I was delayed by various business," the nephew replied.

"No doubt," said the polite uncle.

As long as the servant was in the room, they said nothing else. Once coffee was served and they were alone, the nephew looked at his uncle's mask-like face and began to speak.

"I have returned, sir, to finish the business that took me away. It led me into great and unexpected danger, but it is a sacred duty. Even if it had led to my death, I hope it would have supported me."

"Not to death," the uncle said. "There is no need to say that."

"I doubt, sir," the nephew replied, "that if I had been on the brink of death, you would have done anything to save me."

The pinched nostrils and the sharp lines on the uncle's face tightened ominously. He made a polite gesture of protest, but it was purely a social formality and not reassuring.

"Indeed, sir," the nephew continued, "for all I know, you may have helped make the suspicious circumstances around me look even worse."

"No, no, no," the uncle said pleasantly.

"However that may be," the nephew said, looking at him with deep distrust, "I know you would stop me by any means, and would have no moral objections to those means."

"My friend, I told you so," said the uncle, his nostrils twitching. "Please remember that I told you so long ago."

"I remember."

"Thank you," the Marquis said sweetly.

His polite tone lingered in the air like a musical note.

"In fact, sir," the nephew continued, "I believe it is both your misfortune and my good luck that I am not in a French prison right now."

"I don't quite understand," the uncle said, sipping his coffee. "Would you explain?"

"I believe that if you were not in disgrace with the royal court, you would have used a secret arrest warrant to throw me into a fortress indefinitely."

"It is possible," the uncle said calmly. "For the honor of the family, I would gladly lock you up. Please excuse me!"

"I see that, fortunately for me, your reception at court the day before yesterday was cold as usual," the nephew remarked.

"I wouldn't say fortunately, my friend," the uncle replied politely. "A period of quiet reflection in prison might help you more than you help yourself. But there is no point in discussing it. As you say, I am at a disadvantage now. Those little warrants and favors are hard to get now because so many ask for them. It didn't use to be this way, but France has changed for the worse. Our ancestors held the power of life and death over the peasants. From this very room, many peasants have been taken out to be hanged. In the next room, my bedroom, a man was stabbed to death for protesting when we took his daughter. We have lost many privileges. A new philosophy is fashionable, and asserting our high position today might cause us real inconvenience. It is all very bad!"

The Marquis took a pinch of snuff and shook his head, looking elegantly sad about a country that still had him to guide it.

"We have asserted our high position so much, both in the past and now," the nephew said gloomily, "that I believe our family name is detested more than any other in France."

"Let us hope so," said the uncle. "The hatred of the lower classes is the involuntary respect they pay to the high."

"There is not a single face in this area," the nephew continued, "that looks at me with respect. They only show the fear and submission of slaves."

"A compliment to the family's power," said the Marquis, "which we have maintained well. Ah!" He took another pinch of snuff and crossed his legs.

But when his nephew leaned on the table and covered his eyes in depression, the Marquis looked at him sideways with sharp dislike, dropping his mask of indifference.

"Repression is the only philosophy that lasts. The fear and submission of slaves, my friend," the Marquis remarked, "will keep these dogs obedient to the whip as long as this roof," looking up, "stands."

That might not be as long as the Marquis thought. If he could have seen his castle and fifty others like it a few years in the future, he would not have recognized the burnt, ruined remains. As for the roof he bragged about, it would shut out the sky forever for the men who would be shot by the lead melted from it.

"Meanwhile," said the Marquis, "I will preserve the family honor and peace, even if you won't. But you must be tired. Shall we end our talk for the night?"

"A moment more."

"An hour, if you like."

"Sir," said the nephew, "we have done wrong, and we are paying the price."

"_We_ have done wrong?" the Marquis repeated with a smile, pointing to his nephew and himself.

"Our family; our noble family. Even in my father's time, we did terrible wrongs, injuring anyone who stood in the way of our pleasure. I cannot separate my father from his twin brother, his partner and successor."

"Death has done that!" said the Marquis.

"And left me," the nephew answered, "tied to a system I hate, responsible for it but powerless to change it. I am trying to carry out my mother's last request to show mercy and correct our wrongs, but I am powerless."

"You will never find help or power from me, my nephew," said the Marquis, touching him on the chest with his finger as they stood by the fire.

Every line on his pale face was tightened in a cruel, sharp expression as he looked at his nephew, holding his snuffbox. He touched him on the chest again, as if his finger were a sword running him through, and said:

"My friend, I will die supporting the system under which I have lived."

Having said this, he took a pinch of snuff and put the box in his pocket.

"It is better to be sensible," he added, ringing a bell, "and accept your destiny. But you are lost, Charles, I see."

"This property and France are lost to me," said the nephew sadly. "I give them up."

"Are they yours to give up? France might be, but is the property? It is hardly worth mentioning, but is it yours yet?"

"I did not mean to claim it yet. If it passed to me tomorrow—"

"Which I hope is not likely."

"—or twenty years from now, I would abandon it and live elsewhere. It is little to give up. It is just a wilderness of misery and ruin!"

"Ah!" said the Marquis, looking around the luxurious room.

"It looks beautiful here," the nephew said, "but in reality, it is a crumbling tower of waste, debt, oppression, hunger, and suffering."

"Ah!" the Marquis said again, looking very satisfied.

"If it ever becomes mine, I will put it into hands that can slowly free it from this debt, so the poor people who cannot leave can suffer less in the next generation. But it is not for me. There is a curse on this land."

"And you?" the uncle asked. "Forgive my curiosity, but how do you plan to live?"

"I must do what other noblemen may have to do one day—work."

"In England, for example?"

"Yes. The family name will be safe from me there, because I do not use it."

The ringing of the bell had summoned the servant to light the bedroom next door, which now shone through the open entrance. The Marquis looked that way, listening for his servant's footsteps.

"England is very attractive to you, considering how poorly you have done there," he remarked with a smile.

"I have already said that my difficulties there may be due to you, sir. For the rest, it is my refuge."

"The English boast that their country is a refuge for many. Do you know a fellow countryman who has found refuge there? A Doctor?"

"Yes."

"With a daughter?"

"Yes."

"Yes," said the Marquis. "You are tired. Good night!"

He bowed politely, but there was a secret, mocking look on his face as he spoke those words, which startled his nephew. His thin lips and eyes curved into a diabolical smile.

"Yes," the Marquis repeated. "A Doctor with a daughter. Yes. So begins your new philosophy! You are tired. Good night!"

It would have been useless to ask him any questions. The nephew looked at him in vain as he walked to the door.

"Good night!" said the uncle. "I look forward to seeing you in the morning. Rest well! Show my nephew to his room—and burn him in his bed if you like," he added to himself, before summoning his servant to his own room.

Once the servant left, the Marquis walked around his bedroom in his robe, getting ready for bed on this hot night. Moving quietly in his slippers, he looked like a tiger—or an enchanted marquis from a fairy tale who was about to turn into a beast.

He walked around the luxurious room, thinking of the day's journey: the slow climb up the hill, the sunset, the mill, the prison on the cliff, the poor village, the peasants by the fountain, and the road mender pointing out the man under the carriage. That fountain reminded him of the one in Paris, the dead child, the crying father, and the women looking at the body.

"I am cool now," the Marquis said, "and can go to bed."

Leaving only one light burning, he let his gauze curtains fall around his bed and went to sleep as the night fell silent.

The stone faces on the walls stared blindly into the dark night for three hours. In the stables, the horses rattled their chains, the dogs barked, and the owl hooted.

For three hours, the stone faces of the castle, both human and lion, stared into the night. Darkness lay over the land, and the cemetery's graves were hidden in the dark. In the village, both the tax collectors and the poor peasants were fast asleep, dreaming of food and rest as yoked oxen do.

The village fountain flowed unseen in the dark, and the castle fountain splashed quietly—both counting the minutes of the three dark hours. Then, the water began to look gray in the dawn light, and the stone faces on the castle seemed to open their eyes.

The light grew stronger until the sun touched the trees and shone over the hill. In the red light, the castle fountain water looked like blood, and the stone faces turned red. The birds sang loudly, and a little bird sang on the Marquis's bedroom window sill. The nearest stone face seemed to stare in shock, with its mouth open.

With the sun up, the village awoke. Windows were opened, doors unlocked, and people walked out shivering in the morning air. The day's hard work began. Some went to the fountain, some to the fields to dig, and some led their thin cows to find grass by the roadside. At the cemetery cross, a few people knelt to pray.

The castle awoke later, but gradually. The sun shone on the boar spears and hunting knives, doors and windows were opened, the horses looked around in their stables, and the dogs barked to be let loose.

These were normal morning events. But the sudden ringing of the great castle bell, the sound of people running up and down the stairs, and the panic on the terrace were not normal. Servants saddled horses and rode away in a hurry.

The news quickly reached the road mender working on the hill. He ran as fast as he could down the hill through the dust, stopping only when he reached the village fountain.

All the villagers were gathered there, whispering in suspense and curiosity. The cows stood by, and the castle servants and tax officials stood armed across the street in confusion. The road mender joined them, beating his chest with his blue cap. Gabelle, the tax collector, was hastily mounted on a horse behind a servant and ridden away at a gallop.

It meant there was one stone face too many at the castle.

The Gorgon had looked at the building during the night, adding the final stone face it had waited two hundred years for.

It lay on the Marquis's pillow. It looked like his mask-like face, but frozen in anger and petrified. Driven deep into the stone figure's heart was a knife. Around the handle was a piece of paper, on which was written:

"Drive him fast to his tomb. This, from Jacques."

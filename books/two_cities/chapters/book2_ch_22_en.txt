CHAPTER XXII.
The Sea Still Rises

The starving people of Saint Antoine had only one week of celebration, softening their hard bread with hugs and congratulations, before Madame Defarge sat at her counter as usual, presiding over her customers. Madame Defarge wore no rose in her headdress now, for the spies had become very cautious about coming into the neighborhood. The street lamps had an ominous swing to them.

Madame Defarge sat in the morning light with her arms folded, watching the shop and the street. In both, there were groups of poor, dirty people, but they now felt a new sense of power. The raggedest nightcap worn by the poorest man seemed to say: "It has been hard for me to survive, but it has become very easy for me to kill you." Every lean arm that had been jobless before now had work always ready—to strike. The fingers of the knitting women were sharp and dangerous. There was a change in Saint Antoine; the neighborhood had been prepared for this for hundreds of years, and the final blows had changed its face.

Madame Defarge watched with quiet approval, as a leader of the women should. Another woman knitted beside her: the short, plump wife of a grocer, who was also a mother of two. This assistant had earned the name The Vengeance.

"Listen!" said The Vengeance. "Who is coming?"

A fast-spreading murmur came down the street, like a lit fuse rushing toward the shop.

"It is Defarge," madame said. "Silence, patriots!"

Defarge entered out of breath, pulled off his red cap, and looked around. "Listen!" madame ordered. "Listen to him!" Defarge stood panting, surrounded by eager faces at the door, while everyone in the shop stood up.

"Tell us, husband. What is it?"

"News from the other world!"

"What?" madame asked contemptuously. "The other world?"

"Does everyone remember old Foulon, who told the starving people they could eat grass, and who died and went to hell?"

"Everyone!" the crowd shouted.

"The news is about him. He is here among us!"

"Among us!" the crowd shouted. "And not dead?"

"Not dead! He feared us so much that he pretended to be dead and had a fake funeral. But they found him alive, hiding in the country, and have brought him in. I just saw him on his way to the City Hall, a prisoner. I said he had reason to fear us. Am I right?"

If the wicked old man of seventy-four had never known it, he would have known it in his heart if he could have heard the crowd's response.

A deep silence followed. Defarge and his wife looked at each other. The Vengeance reached down and pulled a drum to her feet behind the counter.

"Patriots!" Defarge said firmly. "Are we ready?"

Instantly, Madame Defarge put her knife in her belt, the drum began beating in the street, and The Vengeance ran screaming from house to house to rouse the women.

The men looked out of their windows with bloody anger, grabbed whatever weapons they had, and poured into the streets. But the women were a terrifying sight. Leaving their poor chores, their children, and their sick, they ran out with messy hair, screaming and driving one another to madness. "Foulon is caught, sister! Old Foulon is caught, mother! Foulon is caught, daughter!" Others ran into the crowd, screaming: "Foulon is alive! Foulon who told the starving people to eat grass! Foulon who told my old father to eat grass when I had no bread! Foulon who told my baby to suck grass when my breasts were dry! I swear on my knees to avenge you on Foulon! Give us his blood, his head, his heart! Tear him to pieces and bury him, so grass grows from him!" Many women, driven to a frenzy, began hitting and tearing at their own friends until they fainted, saved from being trampled only by their husbands.

No time was lost. Foulon was at the City Hall and might be released. Never, if Saint Antoine could help it! Armed men and women poured out of the neighborhood so fast that within fifteen minutes, only a few old women and crying children were left.

The crowd packed the examination room where the wicked old man was held, overflowing into the streets. The Defarges, The Vengeance, and Jacques Three were in the front of the crowd, close to him.

"Look!" madame cried, pointing her knife. "See the old villain tied with ropes. They tied a bunch of grass to his back! That was well done. Let him eat it now!" Madame put her knife under her arm and clapped her hands as if watching a play.

The people behind her explained her reaction to those behind them, and soon the streets clapped in response. During the hours of legal delays, Madame Defarge's gestures of impatience were copied by the crowd outside. Men who had climbed the walls to look through the windows signaled her reactions to the mob below.

Finally, the sun rose high, shining on the old prisoner’s head. The crowd could wait no longer. In an instant, the guards were pushed aside, and Saint Antoine grabbed him!

The news spread instantly. Defarge leaped over a railing and grabbed the old man in a tight embrace. Madame Defarge followed, wrapping her hand in the ropes he was tied with. The Vengeance and Jacques Three followed, while the crowd screamed: "Bring him out! To the lamp post!"

Dragged up and down the steps, now on his knees, now on his back, beaten and choked with grass and straw stuffed into his face, the old man begged for mercy. The crowd drew back to watch him struggle. They dragged him to the nearest street corner where a lamp post hung. Madame Defarge let him go like a cat releasing a mouse, watching quietly while they prepared the rope, as he begged her for help. The women screamed at him, and the men called for him to be hanged with grass in his mouth. Twice they hoisted him up, and twice the rope broke, dropping him screaming to the ground. The third time, the rope held him, and his head was soon placed on a pike with grass stuffed in his mouth, while Saint Antoine danced around it.

This was not the end of the day's violence. The crowd danced until they heard that Foulon's son-in-law, another enemy of the people, was being brought to Paris under a guard of five hundred cavalry. The mob wrote his crimes on paper, seized him from the soldiers, cut off his head and heart, and carried the three bloody trophies on pikes through the streets.

Only at dark did the men and women return to their hungry children. They stood in long lines at the bakeries to buy bad bread. As they waited with empty stomachs, they comforted themselves by talking about the day's triumphs. Gradually, the lines disappeared, and small fires were lit in the streets where neighbors cooked and ate together at their doors.

It was a poor supper with no meat, but sharing it brought some comfort and cheer. Fathers and mothers who had taken part in the violence played gently with their children, and lovers hoped for a better future.

It was almost morning when the last customers left Defarge’s shop. Monsieur Defarge closed the door and said quietly to his wife:

"At last it is here, my dear!"

"Well," madame replied, "almost."

Saint Antoine went to sleep, and the drum was quiet. The drum was the only thing in the neighborhood that the violence had not changed; its sound was the same as before the Bastille fell. But the voices of the men and women of Saint Antoine were now hoarse and altered.

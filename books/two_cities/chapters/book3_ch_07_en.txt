CHAPTER VII.
A Knock at the Door


“I have saved him.” This was not another of the dreams in which he had so often returned; he was really here. Yet his wife shivered, filled with a quiet but heavy fear.

The atmosphere in the city was so dark, the people so vengeful and unpredictable, and innocent citizens were executed so often on mere suspicion and hatred, that she couldn't forget that many others just as innocent as her husband were executed every day. Her heart was still loaded with worry. As the winter afternoon grew dark, she knew the terrible carts were rumbled through the streets. She imagined Charles in them, and she clung tighter to his arm and shivered even more.

Her father, cheered her up, showing a kind confidence that looked past her fears. There was no shoemaking or memory of his old cell in the North Tower now! He had done what he promised: he had saved Charles. He wanted them all to rely on him.

They lived very simply, not only because it was safer to avoid showing off, but because they were short on money. During his imprisonment, Charles had been forced to pay large amounts for his food, his guards, and to help support the poorer prisoners. For this reason, and to avoid hiring a servant who might spy on them, they did their own housework. The gatekeepers of the building helped them occasionally, and Jerry Cruncher (lent to them by Mr. Lorry) acted as their daily helper and slept in the apartment every night.

The revolutionary government required by law that the names of all residents be clearly painted on the doorpost of every house at a specific height. Jerry Cruncher's name was already written on the doorpost downstairs. As it grew dark, Jerry returned after watching a painter whom Doctor Manette had hired to add the name of Charles Evremonde, called Darnay, to the list.

Because of the fear and distrust during this time, everyday life was completely different. Like many others, Doctor Manette's household bought their supplies every evening in small amounts from different shops. Everyone wanted to avoid attracting attention, gossip, or jealousy.

For several months, Miss Pross and Jerry Cruncher had been the shoppers, Miss Pross carrying the money and Jerry carrying the basket. Every afternoon when the street lamps were lit, they went out to buy what they needed. Although Miss Pross had lived with a French family for years, she refused to learn a single word of the language. She knew no more of that “nonsense” (as she called it) than Jerry did. Her way of shopping was to shout the English name of the item at the shopkeeper. If the shopkeeper didn't understand, she would look around, grab the item, and hold onto it until they agreed on a price. She always bargained by holding up one less finger than the shopkeeper, no matter what price they asked.

“Now, Mr. Cruncher,” said Miss Pross, her eyes red from crying tears of joy. “If you are ready, I am.”

Jerry hoarsely replied that he was ready. He had lost his rusty attitude long ago, but his spiky hair was still as wild as ever.

“We need all sorts of things,” said Miss Pross, “and it will be a task. We need wine, too. I'm sure those Redheads in the streets will be drinking toasts with it.”

“It will be much the same to your knowledge, miss, I should think,” Jerry replied, “whether they drink to your health or to the Old One's.”

“Who is he?” Miss Pross asked.

Jerry sheepishly explained that he meant the Devil.

“Ha!” said Miss Pross. “We don't need anyone to explain these French rebels. They only care about murder and mischief.”

“Hush, dear! Please, please be careful!” Lucie cried.

“Yes, yes, I'll be careful,” Miss Pross said. “But I hope those smelly men in the streets won't try to hug us. Now, Ladybird, don't move from the fire until I get back! Take care of the husband you just got back, and keep your head on his shoulder until you see me again! Doctor Manette, may I ask a question before I leave?”

“I think you may take that liberty,” the Doctor smiled.

“For heaven's sake, don't talk about liberty! We have more than enough of that here,” said Miss Pross.

“Hush, dear! Again?” Lucie gently chided.

“Well, my sweet,” Miss Pross said, nodding her head firmly, “the simple truth is that I am a subject of King George the Third.” She curtsied at the king's name. “And because of that, my motto is: Confound their politics, frustrate their tricks, and God save the King!”

Jerry, feeling suddenly patriotic, repeated the words after Miss Pross like a response in church.

“I'm glad you are so patriotic, though I wish you didn't have that rough voice,” Miss Pross said. “But here is my question, Doctor Manette. Is there—” she asked casually, which was her way of bringing up their biggest worry, “any chance of us leaving this place soon?”

“I'm afraid not yet. It would still be too dangerous for Charles.”

“Oh well!” Miss Pross said, hiding a sigh as she looked at Lucie's golden hair by the fire. “Then we must be patient and wait. We must keep our heads high, as my brother Solomon used to say. Let's go, Mr. Cruncher! Don't move, Ladybird!”

They went out, leaving Lucie, Charles, the Doctor, and the child by the warm fire. Mr. Lorry was expected to return soon from the bank. Miss Pross had lit the lamp but placed it in the corner so they could enjoy the firelight. Little Lucie sat next to her grandfather, holding his arm, while he whispered a story about a powerful fairy who broke open a prison wall to free a captive. Everything was quiet and peaceful, and Lucie felt more relaxed than she had in months.

“What is that?” she suddenly cried out.

“My dear!” her father said, stopping the story and placing his hand on hers. “Calm down. You are so nervous! The smallest noise startles you! You are my daughter!”

“I thought, Father,” Lucie said, looking pale and speaking in a shaking voice, “that I heard footsteps on the stairs.”

“My love, the stairs are completely silent.”

Just as he said those words, a loud knock shook the door.

“Oh Father, Father! What is it? Hide Charles! Save him!”

“My child,” the Doctor said, standing up and placing his hand on her shoulder. “I _have_ saved him. Do not be weak, my dear. Let me answer the door.”

He took the lamp, walked through the two outer rooms, and opened the door. Heavy footsteps clattered across the floor as four rough men wearing red caps and armed with swords and pistols entered the room.

“Citizen Evremonde, called Darnay,” said the leader.

“Who wants him?” Darnay answered.

“I want him. We want him. I know you, Evremonde; I saw you at the court today. You are a prisoner of the Republic once again.”

The four men surrounded Charles, who stood with his wife and child holding onto him.

“Tell me why I am a prisoner again.”

“You only need to know that you are going back to the Conciergerie prison, and you will find out the reasons tomorrow. You are summoned for trial tomorrow.”

Doctor Manette was frozen in shock, standing with the lamp like a statue. Finally, he put the lamp down, confronted the leader, and grabbed the front of the man's red wool shirt, asking gently:

“You said you know him. Do you know me?”

“Yes, I know you, Citizen Doctor.”

“We all know you, Citizen Doctor,” the other three added.

He looked blankly from one man to another and asked in a quiet voice after a moment:

“Will you answer his question for me then? How did this happen?”

“Citizen Doctor,” the leader said reluctantly, “he has been accused in the Saint Antoine district. This citizen,” he pointed to the second man who had entered, “is from Saint Antoine.”

The second man nodded his head and added:

“He is accused by Saint Antoine.”

“Of what?” the Doctor asked.

“Citizen Doctor,” the leader said with reluctance, “ask no more questions. If the Republic demands sacrifices from you, surely as a good patriot you will be happy to make them. The Republic comes before everything. The People are supreme. Evremonde, we must hurry.”

“Just one word,” the Doctor begged. “Can you tell me who accused him?”

“It is against the rules,” the leader replied, “but you can ask the man from Saint Antoine here.”

The Doctor looked at the second man, who shifted his weight, rubbed his beard, and finally spoke:

“Well, it really is against the rules. But he is accused—very seriously—by Citizen and Citizeness Defarge. And by one other person.”

“Who else?”

“Do _you_ have to ask, Citizen Doctor?”

“Yes.”

“Then,” the man from Saint Antoine said with a strange look, “you will get your answer tomorrow. I will say no more!”

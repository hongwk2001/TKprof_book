CHAPTER V.

The Wine-shop

A large barrel of wine had been dropped and broken in the street. The accident happened while unloading it from a cart; the barrel had rolled out quickly, the metal bands burst, and it lay on the cobblestones right outside the door of the wine shop, shattered like a walnut shell.

Everyone nearby stopped whatever they were doing—or not doing—to run to the spot and drink the wine. The rough, uneven stones of the street, pointing in every direction and seeming designed specifically to cripple anyone walking on them, had dammed the wine into little pools. Each pool was surrounded by a crowding group, depending on its size. Some men knelt down, scooped the wine with their hands, and drank, or helped women leaning over their shoulders to drink before the wine could leak through their fingers. Others dipped into the puddles with chipped clay mugs, or even with scarves taken from women's heads, which they squeezed dry into babies' mouths. Some made small mud walls to block the wine as it flowed, while others, directed by neighbors looking out from high windows, ran around to block small streams of wine branching off in new directions. A few focused on the wine-soaked wood from the broken barrel, licking and even chewing the wet, wine-stained fragments with enjoyment. Since there was no drainage system, not only was all the wine scooped up, but so much dirt was taken up with it that the street actually looked clean—though nobody who knew the neighborhood would ever expect to see a street sweeper there.

Loud laughter and cheerful voices of men, women, and children filled the street while this wine hunt lasted. There was very little roughness in the play, and plenty of fun. There was a strong sense of community, as everyone wanted to share in the excitement together. This led, especially among the luckier ones, to happy hugs, toasts, handshakes, and even groups of a dozen people joining hands to dance. When the wine was gone, and the places where it had pooled were scraped clean down to the dirt by eager fingers, the celebration stopped just as suddenly as it had started. The man who had left his saw in the firewood went back to work. The woman who had left a small pot of hot ashes on a doorstep—trying to warm her own frozen fingers and toes or those of her child—returned to it. Men with bare arms, messy hair, and pale, ghost-like faces, who had come up into the winter light from dark cellars, walked back down to their rooms. A dark gloom settled over the street once more, looking far more natural to the street than sunshine ever did.

The wine was red, and it stained the ground of the narrow street in the Paris suburb of Saint Antoine where it had spilled. It also stained many hands, faces, bare feet, and wooden shoes. The hands of the woodcutter left red marks on the logs he cut, and the forehead of the woman who nursed her baby was stained by the dirty red rag she wrapped back around her head. Those who had licked the barrel wood had a wild, tiger-like red smear around their mouths. One tall, messy man with a long, dirty nightcap on his head dipped his finger in the muddy wine dregs and wrote on a wall—BLOOD.

The time was coming when a different kind of red wine would be spilled on these street stones, and its stain would be red on many of the people there.

Now that the dark cloud settled back over Saint Antoine, the darkness felt heavy. Cold, dirt, sickness, ignorance, and poverty ruled the neighborhood—all of them powerful forces, but poverty most of all. People who had been ground down by life shivered at every corner, walked in and out of doorways, looked from windows, and wore tattered clothes that fluttered in the wind. The system that ground them down was one that made young people look old. The children had aged faces and serious voices. On every face, carved into every wrinkle of age, was the sign of Hunger. It was everywhere. Hunger hung out of the tall houses in the tattered clothes drying on poles; Hunger was patched into the windows with straw, rags, wood, and paper; Hunger was seen in the tiny scraps of firewood the man sawed; Hunger stared down from the smokeless chimneys and rose from the filthy streets, where there wasn't even a scrap of discarded food to eat. Hunger was written on the baker's shelves, in every small, bad loaf of bread left in stock; and at the sausage shop, in the cheap, mystery-meat sausages offered for sale. Hunger rattled like dry bones among the roasting chestnuts in the metal cylinders, and Hunger was chopped into tiny pieces in the cheap bowls of potato scraps fried with a few drops of oil.

Its home was suitable in every way. The narrow, winding street was full of trash and bad smells, with other narrow streets branching off, all filled with people wearing rags and nightcaps, smelling of poverty, and looking grim. Yet in the desperate look of the people, there was still a wild, animal-like thought of fighting back. Though they looked beaten down, there were still fiery eyes among them, tight lips pressed white from anger, and foreheads wrinkled like the hangman's rope they thought about either escaping or using on their enemies. The shop signs were all grim pictures of need. The butcher painted only the leanest, poorest cuts of meat on his sign, and the baker showed only the coarsest, smallest loaves. The crude paintings of people drinking in the wine shops showed them huddling over tiny glasses of sour wine and cheap beer, whispering darkly to one another. Nothing was in good condition except for tools and weapons: the knives and axes in the shop windows were sharp and bright, the blacksmith's hammers were heavy, and the gunmaker's weapons looked deadly. The uneven cobblestone streets, full of mud and puddles, had no sidewalks, ending abruptly at the doors of the houses. The open sewer gutter ran down the middle of the street—when it ran at all, which was only after heavy rains, and then it flooded into the houses. Across the streets, far apart, single streetlamps were hung by a rope and pulley. At night, after the lamplighter lowered, lit, and raised them again, a weak line of dim wicks swung overhead, looking as if they were on a ship at sea. Indeed, they were at sea, and the ship and crew were in danger of a massive storm.

For the time was coming when the starving people of this area, having watched the lamplighter for so long in their hunger, would decide to improve on his method and hang men from those very ropes and pulleys to shine a light on their miserable lives. But that time had not come yet; and the winds blowing over France shook the rags of the poor in vain, for the wealthy, beautiful aristocrats took no warning.

The wine shop was on a corner, looking better than most other shops in the area. The owner stood outside in a yellow vest and green pants, watching the struggle for the spilled wine. "It's not my problem," he said with a shrug. "The market workers dropped it. Let them bring another one."

Just then, his eyes caught the tall joker writing his graffiti, and he called out to him across the street:

"Hey, Gaspard, what are you doing there?"

The man pointed to his writing with great seriousness, as jokers often do. But his joke fell flat, which is also typical.

"What is this? Are you crazy?" said the wine shop owner, crossing the street and wiping out the writing with a handful of mud scooped up from the ground. "Why are you writing on the public street? Isn't there—tell me—any other place to write such words?"

As he argued, he placed his clean hand (perhaps by accident, perhaps on purpose) over the joker’s heart. The joker tapped it with his own hand, jumped high in the air, and landed in a dramatic dance pose, holding one of his dirty shoes which he had pulled off his foot. He looked like an extremely wild, almost wolfish character.

"Put it back on, put it back on," said the owner. "Call wine, wine, and leave it at that." With that advice, he wiped his muddy hand on the joker’s tattered clothes—quite deliberately, as if it was the joker's fault his hand got dirty—then crossed the street again and entered his shop.

The wine shop owner was a thick-necked, tough-looking man of thirty. He must have had a hot temper, because even though it was a freezing cold day, he wore no coat, carrying it slung over his shoulder instead. His sleeves were rolled up, leaving his brown arms bare to the elbows. He wore nothing on his head, showing only his short, curly dark hair. He was a dark-complexioned man, with good, wide-set eyes. He looked friendly enough on the whole, but also stubborn and unyielding; clearly a man of strong determination and purpose. He was not someone you would want to meet in a tight spot, because nothing could make him back down.

His wife, Madame Defarge, sat behind the counter as he walked in. She was a stout woman about his age, with watchful eyes that rarely seemed to look directly at anything, large hands covered in heavy rings, a calm face, strong features, and great composure. There was a strength about her that suggested she rarely made mistakes in any of her business calculations. Being sensitive to the cold, she was wrapped in fur and had a bright shawl wrapped around her head, though her large earrings were still visible. Her knitting sat in front of her, but she had laid it down to pick her teeth with a toothpick. In this pose, holding her elbow in her hand, she said nothing when her husband came in, but gave a tiny cough. This, along with a slight raise of her dark eyebrows, signaled to her husband that he should look around at the clients to see if anyone new had arrived while he was outside.

The owner looked around the room until his eyes rested on an older gentleman and a young lady sitting in a corner. Other customers were there: two playing cards, two playing dominoes, and three standing by the counter nursing their small drinks. As he walked behind the counter, he noticed the older gentleman look at the young lady as if to say, "This is our man."

"What on earth are *you* doing here?" Monsieur Defarge thought to himself. "I don't know you."

But he pretended not to notice the two strangers, and started talking to the three customers drinking at the counter.

"How's it going, Jacques?" one of the three asked Monsieur Defarge. "Is all the spilled wine gone?" "Every drop, Jacques," Defarge replied.

When this exchange of the name "Jacques" was made, Madame Defarge, still picking her teeth, gave another tiny cough and raised her eyebrows slightly higher.

"It is not often," the second customer said to Defarge, "that many of these poor beasts taste wine, or anything other than hard black bread and death. Isn't that right, Jacques?" "That's right, Jacques," Defarge answered.

At this second exchange of the name, Madame Defarge, still picking her teeth calmly, coughed again and raised her eyebrows a bit more.

The last of the three spoke up, putting down his empty glass and smacking his lips. "Ah! So much the worse! It's a bitter taste these poor people always have in their mouths, and hard lives they live, Jacques. Am I right, Jacques?" "You are right, Jacques," Defarge responded.

This third exchange of the name was completed just as Madame Defarge put away her toothpick, kept her eyebrows raised, and shifted slightly in her seat.

"Ah, yes! True!" muttered her husband. "Gentlemen—my wife!"

The three customers tipped their hats to Madame Defarge with polite bows. She acknowledged their greeting with a nod and a quick look. Then she glanced casually around the shop, picked up her knitting with calm focus, and became absorbed in it.

"Gentlemen," said her husband, who had been watching her closely, "good day. The simple room you wanted to see, and were asking about when I went out, is on the fifth floor. The entrance to the stairs is in the small courtyard to the left," he pointed. "Right near the window of my shop. But now that I think of it, one of you has already been up there and can show the way. Goodbye, gentlemen!"

They paid for their wine and left. Defarge was watching his wife knit when the older gentleman stepped forward from his corner and asked for a quick word.

"Of course, sir," Defarge said, walking with him quietly to the door.

Their conversation was very short but intense. At the very first word, Defarge startled and listened closely. It didn't take a minute before he nodded and walked out. The gentleman then signaled to the young lady, and they went out too. Madame Defarge knitted with fast fingers and calm eyebrows, pretending to see nothing.

Leaving the wine shop, Mr. Jarvis Lorry and Miss Manette met Defarge in the doorway he had pointed out to the other men earlier. It opened into a dirty, smelly little courtyard and served as the public entrance to a large tenement building home to many people. In the dark entryway, Defarge knelt before his old master's daughter and kissed her hand. It was a polite action but done with a tense, serious energy; he had completely changed in just a few seconds. The friendly look was gone from his face, replaced by a secret, angry, and dangerous expression.

"It is a long way up, and a bit difficult. We should start slowly," Defarge said sternly to Mr. Lorry as they began walking up the stairs.

"Is he alone?" Lorry whispered.

"Alone! God help him, who else would be with him?" Defarge replied in the same quiet tone.

"Is he always alone, then?"

"Yes."

"By his own choice?"

"Out of necessity. He is exactly as he was when I first saw him, after they found me and asked if I would take him in and keep his secret at my own risk. As he was then, he is now."

"Is he greatly changed?"

"Changed!"

The wine shop owner stopped, punched the wall with his hand, and swore quietly. No verbal answer could have been half as powerful. Mr. Lorry's heart grew heavier as the three of them climbed higher and higher.

Such a staircase in the old, crowded parts of Paris would be bad enough today, but back then, it was absolutely disgusting to anyone not used to it. Every small apartment in the building left its garbage right outside its door on the landing, besides throwing other trash out of the windows. The resulting pile of rotting waste polluted the air, and combined with the smell of extreme poverty, it made the building almost unlivable. They had to climb up through this dark, dirty chimney of filth. Sensing his own growing anxiety and seeing how upset Miss Manette was becoming, Mr. Lorry stopped twice to rest. Each time, they stopped near a dirty barred window where any fresh air seemed to escape and bad smells crawled in. Looking through the rusty bars, they got a sense of the crowded, dirty neighborhood; nothing they could see, except the distant towers of Notre-Dame Cathedral, held any promise of a healthy or happy life.

Finally, they reached the top landing and stopped for a third time. There was still a final, steeper, and narrower flight of stairs to climb to reach the attic. Defarge, who had walked slightly ahead on Lorry's side—as if avoiding any questions from the young lady—turned around, reached into the pockets of the coat he carried, and pulled out a key.

"Is the door locked, then?" Mr. Lorry asked, surprised.

"Yes, it is," Defarge replied grimly.

"Do you think it necessary to keep the poor man locked away like this?"

"I think it necessary to keep him locked in." Defarge whispered this close to Lorry's ear with a heavy frown.

"Why?"

"Why! Because he has lived locked up for so long that he would panic, lose his mind, hurt himself, or even die of fright if the door was left open."

"Is that really possible!" Mr. Lorry exclaimed.

"Is it possible?" Defarge repeated bitterly. "Yes. And it's a beautiful world we live in, where such things are not only possible, but actually done to people under the sun every single day. Long live the Devil. Let's keep moving."

This conversation was held in such low whispers that the young lady didn't hear a word. But by now, she was trembling with emotion, her face showing such deep worry and fear that Mr. Lorry felt he had to offer some comfort.

"Courage, dear miss! Courage! Focus on the task! The worst will be over in a moment; once we pass the door, the hardest part is done. Then, all the help, comfort, and happiness you are bringing to him will begin. Let our friend here support you on that side. Thank you, Defarge. Come along now. Let's do this!"

They went up slowly and quietly. The stairs were short, and they quickly reached the top. Turning a sharp corner, they suddenly saw three men huddled together near the door, peering intently into the room through cracks and holes in the wall. Hearing footsteps, they turned around, showing themselves to be the three "Jacques" who had been drinking in the shop downstairs.

"I forgot about them in the excitement of your visit," Defarge explained. "Leave us, guys; we have business here."

The three men slipped past them and went quietly downstairs.

Since there was no other door on the floor, and the wine shop owner went straight to this one, Mr. Lorry asked him in a sharp whisper:

"Are you turning Monsieur Manette into an exhibit?"

"I show him to a chosen few, in the way you just saw."

"Is that right?"

"I think it is."

"Who are these few? How do you choose them?"

"I choose men who share my goal—under the name Jacques—for whom this sight will do some good. Anyway, you are English, which is different. Please wait here for a moment."

Signaling them to stay back, he leaned down and looked through the crack in the wall. Looking up, he knocked loudly on the door a few times, clearly just to make noise. He then scraped the key across the door three or four times before inserting it loudly into the lock and turning it with as much noise as possible.

The door slowly opened inward, and he looked in and spoke. A weak voice answered back. They only exchanged a syllable or two.

He looked back over his shoulder and gestured for them to come in. Mr. Lorry put his arm securely around the daughter’s waist to support her, feeling that she was about to faint.

"Remember, focus on the task!" he urged, with a tear running down his cheek that had nothing to do with business. "Come in, come in!"

"I am afraid of it," she whispered, shivering.

"Of what?"

"I mean of him. Of my father."

Feeling desperate because of her state and Defarge's gestures, Mr. Lorry draped her shaking arm over his shoulder, lifted her slightly, and hurried her into the room. He sat her down just inside the door and held her close as she clung to him.

Defarge broke out the key, closed the door, locked it from the inside, removed the key again, and kept it in his hand. He did all of this methodically, making as much noise as possible. Finally, he walked slowly across the room to the window, stopped, and turned around to face them.

The attic, built as a storage room for firewood, was dim and dark. The window was actually a door in the roof, with a small crane over it to hoist goods from the street. It had no glass and opened in the middle like a double door. To keep out the cold, one half of this door was shut tight, and the other was opened only a tiny crack. Very little light came through, making it hard to see anything at first; only long habit could give someone the ability to do fine work in such darkness. Yet, that kind of work was being done: with his back to the door and his face toward the window where Defarge stood watching, a white-haired man sat on a low bench, leaning forward, busy making shoes.

CHAPTER IV.
Calm in Storm


Doctor Manette did not return until the morning of the fourth day. They hid most of the terrible events from Lucie so well that only long afterward, when she was far away from France, did she learn that eleven hundred defenseless prisoners of all ages and genders had been killed by the mob. She didn't know that four days and nights had been filled with this horror, or that the very air was smelly from the dead. She only knew that the prisons had been attacked, that political prisoners were in danger, and that some had been dragged out and killed.

The Doctor swore Mr. Lorry to secrecy and explained that the mob had led him through a bloodbath to La Force prison. Inside, he found a self-appointed court judging the prisoners one by one, quickly ordering them to be killed by the mob outside, released, or sent back to their cells. When he was presented to this court, he announced his name and profession, stating that he had been held in the Bastille for eighteen years without a trial. One of the judges stood up and recognized him—and that man was Defarge.

The Doctor checked the record books on the table and saw that his son-in-law was still alive. He begged the court—whose members were half-asleep, dirty with blood, sober, or drunk—for Charles's life. Because the court respected the Doctor as a famous victim of the old government, they agreed to bring Charles Darnay in for questioning. It seemed like Charles was about to be released, but then the mood changed for some reason the Doctor didn't understand, and the judges whispered together. The president of the court then told Doctor Manette that the prisoner had to stay in jail, but for the Doctor's sake, he would be kept safe. Charles was sent back inside, but the Doctor begged so hard to stay and make sure Charles wasn't sent to the murderous mob outside, that the judges let him remain in that bloody room until the danger had passed.

The horrors the Doctor saw there, while getting almost no food or sleep, will not be described. The crazy joy the mob showed for the prisoners who were saved surprised him as much as their anger toward those they killed. He said one prisoner was set free, but a confused rioter stabbed him with a spear as he walked out. Asked to help, the Doctor went outside and found the wounded man being held by a group of killers who were sitting on the bodies of their victims. With a hypocrisy as crazy as a nightmare, these killers helped the Doctor and treated the wounded man gently, carrying him away on a stretcher. But then they immediately grabbed their weapons and went back to murdering people so brutally that the Doctor covered his eyes and fainted.

As Mr. Lorry listened to these secrets and looked at his sixty-two-year-old friend, he worried that these terrible experiences would bring back the Doctor's old mental illness.

But Mr. Lorry had never seen the Doctor look like this before. For the first time, the Doctor felt that his past suffering gave him power. He felt that his eighteen years in prison had forged the strength he needed to break his daughter's husband out of jail. “It all had a purpose, my friend; it wasn't just wasted suffering. Since my beloved daughter helped save my mind, I will help save the most important person in her life. With God's help, I will do it!” said Doctor Manette. And when Mr. Lorry saw his bright eyes, resolute face, and strong bearing—after years of seeming like a stopped clock that was now running with new energy—he believed him.

Even greater obstacles would have crumbled before the Doctor's determination. Working as a doctor who treated everyone—rich and poor, good and bad—he used his influence so well that he was appointed the medical inspector of three prisons, including La Force. He could now tell Lucie that Charles was no longer in solitary confinement, but was with the other prisoners. The Doctor saw Charles every week and brought her messages directly from him. Sometimes Charles even sent letters (though never carried by the Doctor), but Lucie was not allowed to write back, because the authorities suspected that prisoners were plotting with friends abroad.

This new life was stressful for the Doctor, but Mr. Lorry saw that it gave him a new sense of pride. It was a natural and worthy pride, but Mr. Lorry found it interesting to observe. Until now, the Doctor's imprisonment had been remembered as a time of weakness and suffering. Now that those years in the Bastille gave him the power to protect Charles, he felt proud to take charge, asking Lucie and Mr. Lorry to rely on him as the strong one. His role with Lucie was reversed, but only out of love and gratitude, since he wanted nothing more than to help the daughter who had saved him. “It is interesting to watch,” Mr. Lorry thought, “but completely natural and right. Take the lead, my friend, and keep it; it couldn't be in better hands.”

But even though the Doctor tried constantly to set Charles free or at least get him a trial, the revolution was moving too fast. The new era began: the king was put on trial, condemned, and beheaded. The Republic declared war against the world. A black flag waved day and night from the towers of Notre Dame Cathedral. Three hundred thousand men were drafted to fight, rising up from every corner of France as if soldiers grew directly out of the soil. How could the worries of one family stand against the massive flood of the Year One of Liberty—a flood rising from the anger of the people below?

There was no pause, no pity, no peace, and no concept of time. Although days and nights passed normally, no one kept track of dates. Time was lost in the nation's madness, just like in a patient's fever. The executioner showed the king's head to the crowd, and soon after, the head of the queen, whose hair had turned gray from eight months of misery in prison.

And yet, while time flew by, it also felt incredibly long. There was a revolutionary court in Paris and thousands of local committees all over the country. A new law concerning "suspected" people destroyed all safety, making it easy for any bad person to accuse an innocent one. Prisons were packed with people who had done nothing wrong and could get no trial. These horrors became the daily routine. Above all, one terrifying machine became as familiar as if it had existed since the beginning of the world: the Guillotine.

It was the subject of popular jokes: people called it the best cure for a headache, a way to keep hair from turning gray, and the "National Razor" that shaved close. They joked that anyone who kissed it looked through the little window and sneezed into the sack. It was seen as the symbol of a new era, replacing the Christian cross. People wore small models of the Guillotine on their chests instead of crosses, bowing down to it instead of God.

It cut off so many heads that the machine and the ground around it were stained dark red. It could be taken apart and put back together easily when needed. It silenced speakers, destroyed the powerful, and killed the beautiful and the good. In one morning, it executed twenty-one famous politicians in twenty-one minutes. The chief executioner was nicknamed Samson after the strong man in the Bible; but this executioner was stronger than the biblical Samson, and blinder, destroying lives every day.

Amid these terrors, the Doctor kept his head clear. He remained confident in his power, working quietly to save Lucie's husband. Yet the time went by so fast that Charles had been in prison for a year and three months while the Doctor remained patient and hopeful. By December, the Revolution had grown even more violent; rivers in the south were filled with drowned bodies, and prisoners were executed by firing squads. Still, the Doctor walked through this danger calmly. No one was better known in Paris, and no one was in a stranger position. He was quiet, kind, and indispensable in hospitals and prisons, treating killers and victims alike. His past as a Bastille prisoner kept him safe from suspicion, as if he really were a ghost walking among the living.

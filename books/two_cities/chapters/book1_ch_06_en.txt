CHAPTER VI.

The Shoemaker

"Good day!" said Monsieur Defarge, looking down at the white head that was bent low over his shoemaking.

The head was raised for a moment, and a very faint voice responded to the greeting, sounding as if it came from far away:

"Good day!"

"You are still working hard, I see?"

After a long silence, the head was lifted for another moment, and the voice replied, "Yes—I am working." This time, a pair of exhausted, hollow eyes looked at the questioner before the face dropped down again.

The weakness of the voice was pitiful and sad. It was not the weakness of physical illness, though being locked away and eating bad food had certainly played a part. Its most tragic feature was that it was the weakness of long loneliness and lack of use. It sounded like the last faint echo of a sound made a very long time ago. It had so completely lost the life and fullness of a normal human voice that listening to it felt like looking at a once beautiful color that had faded into a dull, weak stain. It was so low and muffled that it sounded like a voice coming from deep underground. It expressed such a sense of a lost and hopeless soul that a starving traveler, exhausted from wandering alone in a desert, would have remembered home and friends upon hearing such a tone before lying down to die.

A few minutes of silent work passed, and the exhausted eyes looked up again. They showed no interest or curiosity, but only a dull, automatic awareness that the spot where his visitor stood was not yet empty.

"I want," said Defarge, who had not stopped looking at the shoemaker, "to let a little more light in here. Can you handle a little more?"

The shoemaker stopped his work. He looked blankly at the floor on one side, as if listening; then he looked the same way at the floor on the other side; and finally, he looked up at the speaker.

"What did you say?"

"Can you handle a little more light?"

"I must handle it, if you let it in." (He put the faintest hint of emphasis on the second word.)

The wooden half-door was opened a little wider and held in place. A wide beam of light shone into the attic room, showing the worker pausing with an unfinished shoe on his lap. His few simple tools and scraps of leather lay at his feet and on his workbench. He had a messy white beard that was not very long, a hollow face, and extremely bright eyes. The thinness of his face made his eyes look huge under his dark eyebrows and messy white hair, even though they were already naturally large, making them look unnaturally big now. His yellow, ragged shirt was open at the neck, showing his thin, worn-out body. He, his old canvas jacket, his loose stockings, and all his ragged clothes had faded from being locked away from light and fresh air for so long. Everything had turned the same dull, paper-yellow color, so that it was hard to tell his clothes and his skin apart.

He held up a hand to shield his eyes from the light, and the bones inside it looked almost transparent. He sat there, staring blankly, pausing in his work. He never looked at the person standing in front of him without looking down to one side first, and then to the other, as if he had forgotten how to connect sounds with the direction they came from. He never spoke without first looking around in this confused way, sometimes forgetting to speak at all.

"Are you going to finish that pair of shoes today?" asked Defarge, signaling for Mr. Lorry to step forward.

"What did you say?"

"Do you plan to finish that pair of shoes today?"

"I can't say that I plan to. I suppose so. I don't know."

But the question reminded him of his work, and he bent over it again.

Mr. Lorry walked forward quietly, leaving Lucie by the door. After he stood next to Defarge for a minute or two, the shoemaker looked up. He did not seem surprised to see another person, but his shaking fingers touched his lips as he looked at him. (His lips and fingernails were the same pale, lead-gray color.) Then his hand dropped back to his work, and he bent over the shoe again. The look and the movement took only a second.

"You have a visitor, you see," said Monsieur Defarge.

"What did you say?"

"Here is a visitor."

The shoemaker looked up as he had before, but without taking his hands off his work.

"Come!" said Defarge. "Here is a gentleman who knows a good shoe when he sees one. Show him the shoe you are working on. Take it, sir."

Mr. Lorry took it in his hand.

"Tell the gentleman what kind of shoe it is, and the maker's name."

There was a longer pause than usual before the shoemaker answered:

"I forget what you asked me. What did you say?"

"I asked if you could describe the kind of shoe for this gentleman."

"It is a lady's shoe. It is a young lady's walking shoe. It is in the latest fashion. I have never seen the fashion, but I have held the pattern in my hand." He glanced at the shoe with a small, brief hint of pride.

"And the maker's name?" said Defarge.

Now that he had no work to hold, he put the knuckles of his right hand in the palm of his left, and then the knuckles of his left hand in the palm of his right, and then rubbed his hand over his bearded chin. He kept repeating this movement without stopping for a second. Trying to bring his mind back from the blank state it slid into after speaking was like trying to wake up a very weak person from a deep faint, or trying to keep a dying man alive in the hope that he might say one last important word.

"Did you ask me for my name?"

"Yes, I did."

"One Hundred and Five, North Tower."

"Is that all?"

"One Hundred and Five, North Tower."

With a tired sound that was not quite a sigh or a groan, he bent back to his work, until the silence was broken again.

"You are not a shoemaker by trade, are you?" asked Mr. Lorry, looking at him closely.

His tired eyes turned to Defarge, as if he wanted Defarge to answer for him. But when no help came from that side, his eyes looked down at the floor, and then back up at Mr. Lorry.

"I am not a shoemaker by trade? No, I was not a shoemaker by trade. I—I learned it here. I taught myself. I asked permission to—"

He drifted off for several minutes, repeating the same pattern with his hands the entire time. His eyes slowly came back to Mr. Lorry's face. When they focused on him, he started, and continued speaking like a sleeper who had just woken up and gone back to a topic from the night before.

"I asked permission to teach myself, and I got it with great difficulty after a long time. I have made shoes ever since."

As he reached out his hand for the shoe, Mr. Lorry said, still looking directly at him:

"Monsieur Manette, do you remember anything about me?"

The shoe fell to the ground, and he sat staring intensely at the questioner.

"Monsieur Manette," Mr. Lorry said, putting his hand on Defarge's arm, "do you remember nothing of this man? Look at him. Look at me. Is there no memory of your old banker, your old business, your old servant, or your past life coming back to your mind, Monsieur Manette?"

As the long-time prisoner sat looking intensely, first at Mr. Lorry and then at Defarge, some long-lost signs of intelligence and deep thought began to show on his forehead, breaking through the dark cloud that hung over his mind. The signs faded again, grew weaker, and then disappeared—but they had definitely been there. And that exact same expression was matched on the young, beautiful face of his daughter, who had crept along the wall to where she could see him. She stood there looking at him. At first, her hands had been raised in frightened pity, as if to shield herself from the sight of him, but now she was reaching out to him, trembling with eagerness to hold his ghost-like face to her warm chest and love him back to life and hope. The expression was repeated so perfectly on her face (though much stronger and clearer) that it looked as if a moving light had passed directly from him to her.

But the darkness returned and took over his mind again. He looked at the two men with less and less interest, and his eyes wandered down to the floor in a sad, blank stare, looking around in his usual confused way. Finally, with a long, deep sigh, he picked up the shoe and went back to work.

"Did you recognize him, sir?" Defarge asked in a whisper.

"Yes, for a moment. At first I thought it was completely hopeless, but I definitely saw the face I used to know so well for a single second. Shh! Let's step back a little. Shh!"

She had moved away from the attic wall and was now standing very close to the bench where he sat. It was deeply sad and unsettling that he was completely unaware of her standing right there, close enough to reach out and touch him as he bent over his work.

Not a word was spoken; not a sound was made. She stood beside him like a quiet spirit, while he bent over his work.

Eventually, he needed to switch the tool in his hand for his shoemaker's knife. The knife lay on the side away from where she was standing. He picked it up and was leaning down to work again when his eyes caught the edge of her dress. He looked up and saw her face. The two men rushed forward, but she stopped them with a wave of her hand. She was not afraid that he would strike her with the knife, even though the men were.

He stared at her with a frightened look, and after a moment, his lips began to form words, though no sound came out. Gradually, during the pauses in his fast, heavy breathing, they heard him say:

"What is this?"

With tears streaming down her face, she blew him a kiss with both hands, then pressed her hands to her chest, as if holding his broken head against her heart.

"You are not the jailer's daughter?"

She sighed, "No."

"Who are you?"

Not yet trusting herself to speak clearly, she sat down on the bench next to him. He pulled back, but she gently laid her hand on his arm. A strange shiver ran through his entire body when she touched him. He set the knife down gently, continuing to stare at her.

Her golden hair, which she wore in long curls, had been pushed back in her excitement and fell over her neck. Moving his hand very slowly, he reached out, picked up a lock of her hair, and looked at it. But while doing this, his mind drifted away again, and with another deep sigh, he went back to making shoes.

But not for long. Freeing her arm, she laid her hand on his shoulder. He looked at her hand doubtfully two or three times, as if to make sure it was really there. Then he laid down his work, reached for his neck, and pulled off a blackened string with a small piece of folded rag tied to it. He carefully opened the rag on his knee. Inside was a tiny bit of hair—only one or two long golden strands that he had wound around his finger a long time ago.

He took her hair in his hand again and looked closely at it. "It is the same. How can it be! When was this? How did this happen?"

As the deep, focused look returned to his forehead, he seemed to notice that she had the exact same expression. He turned her face directly toward the light and looked at her.

"She laid her head on my shoulder the night I was called away. She was afraid of my leaving, though I was not. And when I was brought to the North Tower, they found these strands of hair on my sleeve. 'Will you let me keep them? They can never help my body escape, but they might help my soul.' Those were the words I said. I remember them very well."

He shaped these words with his lips many times before he could say them out loud. But when he finally spoke, his words made sense, even though they came slowly.

"How could this be? — _Was it you_?"

Once again, the two men jumped in surprise as he turned to her with terrifying suddenness. But she sat completely still in his grip and only said in a quiet voice, "I beg you, gentlemen, do not come near us, do not speak, and do not move!"

"Listen!" he cried. "Whose voice was that?"

His hands let go of her as he shouted, reaching up to his white hair and pulling at it wildly. But the outburst soon faded, just as everything except his shoemaking always faded from his mind. He folded his little rag packet back up and tried to hide it in his shirt, but he kept looking at her, shaking his head sadly.

"No, no, no. You are too young, too full of life. It cannot be. Look at what a prisoner I am. These are not the hands she knew, this is not the face she knew, and this is not a voice she ever heard. No, no. She and I existed before the long, slow years in the North Tower—ages ago. What is your name, my gentle angel?"

Grateful for his softer tone and gentle manner, his daughter knelt in front of him, placing her pleading hands on his chest.

"Oh, sir, at another time you will know my name, and who my mother and father were, and why I never knew their sad history. But I cannot tell you now, and I cannot tell you here. All I can tell you right here and now is that I beg you to touch me and bless me. Kiss me, kiss me! Oh, my dear, my dear father!"

His cold white head rested against her bright, golden hair, which seemed to warm and light it up as if the light of freedom itself were shining on him.

"If you hear in my voice—I do not know if it is true, but I hope it is—any sound that reminds you of a voice that was once sweet music to your ears, cry for it, cry for it! If, in touching my hair, you feel anything that reminds you of a beloved head that lay on your chest when you were young and free, cry for it, cry for it! If, when I talk about the home waiting for us, where I will care for you with all my duty and faithful love, I bring back the memory of a home that was empty for so long while your poor heart suffered, cry for it, cry for it!"

She held him tighter around the neck, rocking him against her chest like a child.

"If, when I tell you, my dearest, that your suffering is over, and that I have come to take you away from this place to England where we can live in peace and rest, I make you think of your wasted life and how cruel our native France has been to you, cry for it, cry for it! And if, when I tell you my name and about my father who is alive and my mother who is dead, you learn that I must kneel to my honored father and beg his forgiveness for never working all day, staying awake, and crying all night for his sake—because my mother's love kept his suffering a secret from me—cry for it, cry for it! Cry for her, then, and for me! Gentlemen, thank God! I feel his tears on my face, and his sobs touch my heart. Oh, look! Thank God for us, thank God!"

He sank into her arms, and his face fell against her chest. It was a sight so touching, yet so terrible because of the great pain and injustice that had caused it, that the two men watching covered their faces.

When the attic room had been quiet for a long time, and his heavy breathing and trembling body had finally calmed down—showing how all human storms must eventually end in quiet peace—the two men came forward to help the father and daughter up. He had slowly slipped to the floor and lay there in a deep, exhausted sleep. She had huddled down with him so that his head rested on her arm, and her long hair fell over him like a curtain to block out the light.

"If we can do it without waking him," she whispered, raising her hand to Mr. Lorry as he stood over them, blowing his nose repeatedly, "could we arrange to leave Paris immediately? That way, we can take him directly from this door to the carriage."

"But think about it. Is he strong enough to travel?" asked Mr. Lorry.

"He is much readier for that, I think, than to stay in this city that has been so terrible to him."

"That is true," said Defarge, who had knelt down to watch and listen. "In fact, Monsieur Manette is much better off leaving France entirely. Should I go hire a carriage and fresh horses?"

"That is a matter of business," said Mr. Lorry, quickly returning to his organized, professional way of speaking. "And if business needs to be done, I had better take care of it."

"Then please," Miss Manette urged, "leave us here for now. You see how calm he is, and you don't need to worry about leaving him with me. Why would you be? If you lock the door to make sure no one interrupts us, I am sure you will find him just as quiet when you return. I will watch over him until you get back, and then we can move him immediately."

Both Mr. Lorry and Defarge were reluctant to do this and preferred to have one of them stay behind. However, since they had to arrange for the carriage, horses, and travel papers, and because time was running out as the day ended, they quickly split up the tasks and hurried off to complete them.

Then, as darkness filled the room, the daughter laid her head on the hard floor right next to her father and watched him. The darkness grew deeper and deeper, and they both lay quietly until a light shone through the cracks in the wall.

Mr. Lorry and Monsieur Defarge had prepared everything for the trip. They brought traveling coats, blankets, bread, meat, wine, and hot coffee. Monsieur Defarge placed the food and the lamp he was carrying on the shoemaker's workbench (the only other thing in the room was a simple straw bed). Then he and Mr. Lorry woke the prisoner and helped him stand up.

No one could have understood what was going on in his mind by looking at the scared, blank look on his face. Whether he understood what had happened, remembered what they had said, or even knew he was free were questions that no one could answer. They tried talking to him, but he was so confused and slow to respond that they became worried by his state and decided to leave him alone for the time being. He had a wild, lost way of holding his head in his hands sometimes, which they had not seen before. Yet, he seemed to find some comfort in the sound of his daughter's voice, and always turned toward her whenever she spoke.

With the obedient attitude of someone long forced to follow orders, he ate and drank whatever they offered, and put on the coat and blankets they gave him. He willingly let his daughter put her arm through his, and took—and held—her hand in both of his.

They began to walk down the stairs, with Monsieur Defarge leading the way with the lamp and Mr. Lorry walking at the end. They had only gone down a few steps of the long main staircase when the doctor stopped, staring up at the ceiling and around at the walls.

"Do you remember this place, father? Do you remember coming up here?"

"What did you say?"

But before she could repeat the question, he muttered an answer as if she had already done so.

"Remember? No, I don't remember. It was so very long ago."

It was clear that he had no memory of being brought from the prison to this house. They heard him mutter, "One Hundred and Five, North Tower," and when he looked around, he was clearly looking for the thick fortress walls that had surrounded him for so long. When they reached the courtyard, he automatically changed his step, expecting to cross a drawbridge. When there was no drawbridge and he saw the carriage waiting in the open street, he let go of his daughter's hand and held his head in his hands again.

There was no crowd outside the door, no one looking out from the many windows, and not even a single person walking down the street. An unnatural silence and emptiness filled the air. Only one person was visible, and that was Madame Defarge, who leaned against the doorframe, knitting, and appearing to see nothing.

The prisoner climbed into the carriage and his daughter followed him. But just as Mr. Lorry was stepping in, he stopped on the step because the doctor began pleading sadly for his shoemaking tools and the unfinished shoes. Madame Defarge immediately called out to her husband that she would get them, and walked out of the lamplight, knitting, across the courtyard. She returned quickly, handed them into the carriage, and immediately went back to leaning against the doorframe, knitting, and appearing to see nothing.

Defarge climbed onto the driver's seat and shouted, "To the city gate!" The driver cracked his whip, and the carriage clattered away under the dim, hanging streetlamps.

They rode under the hanging streetlamps—which swung brighter in the wealthier streets and dimmer in the poorer ones—passing by lit-up shops, happy crowds, bright cafes, and theater entrances on their way to one of the city gates. Soldiers with lanterns stood at the guardhouse there. "Your papers, travelers!" one called out. "Look here, officer," said Defarge, getting down and pulling him aside. "These are the papers of the gentleman inside with the white hair. He was placed in my care at the—" He lowered his voice, and there was a stir among the soldiers' lanterns. A uniformed arm reached into the carriage with a light, and the eyes behind the arm stared with a look of deep surprise and curiosity at the white-haired man. "Everything is in order. Move along!" said the soldier. "Goodbye!" replied Defarge. And so, they drove under a few last, dim hanging lamps, and out under the vast sky of stars.

Beneath that canopy of quiet, eternal stars—some so far away from our small Earth that scientists say it is doubtful their light has even reached us to show a place where people suffer and live—the shadows of the night were wide and dark. All through the cold, restless hours of the night until dawn, the same question whispered in the mind of Mr. Jarvis Lorry. He sat opposite the man who had been dug out of his living grave, wondering what parts of his mind were lost forever and what could still be healed:

"I hope you care to be recalled to life?"

And the old answer:

"I can't say."

The end of the first book.

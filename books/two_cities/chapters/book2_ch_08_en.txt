CHAPTER VIII.
Monseigneur in the Country

It was a beautiful landscape, with wheat fields that looked bright but did not produce much. There were patches of poor rye where wheat should have been, patches of poor peas and beans, and patches of the coarsest vegetables used as substitutes for wheat. All of nature, just like the men and women who farmed it, seemed to be growing unwillingly, ready to give up and wither away.

Monsieur the Marquis, in his heavy traveling carriage pulled by four horses and two riders, slowly climbed a steep hill. A red glow on the Marquis's face was not a sign of embarrassment, which would be unlike a nobleman; it was caused by something outside his control—the setting sun.

The sunset shone so brightly into the carriage when it reached the top of the hill that the Marquis was bathed in a deep red light. "It will die out soon," said the Marquis, looking at his hands, "directly."

Indeed, the sun was so low that it dipped below the horizon at that very moment. When the heavy brake was adjusted to the wheel and the carriage slid down the hill in a cloud of dust with a burnt smell, the red glow vanished quickly. The sun and the Marquis went down together, and no light was left when the brake was removed.

What remained was a rugged, open landscape: a small village at the bottom of the hill, a broad rising slope beyond it, a church tower, a windmill, a forest for hunting, and a cliff with a castle on it used as a prison. The Marquis looked at all these darkening objects as night fell, with the air of a man approaching his home.

The village had a single poor street, containing a poor brewery, a poor tannery, a poor tavern, a poor stable yard for changing horses, and a poor fountain. Its people were just as poor. All the villagers were destitute; many sat at their doors chopping up onions for their simple suppers, while others gathered at the fountain to wash leaves and grass that could be eaten. The reasons for their poverty were clear: the state tax, the church tax, the lord's tax, local taxes, and general taxes had to be paid everywhere, until it was a miracle that any village was left at all.

Few children were to be seen, and no dogs. The men and women faced a simple choice: live on the bare minimum down in the village under the mill, or face imprisonment and death in the castle on the cliff.

Announced by a messenger riding ahead and the cracking of the drivers' whips, which coiled like snakes in the evening air, the Marquis arrived at the post house gate. It was next to the fountain, and the peasants stopped what they were doing to watch him. He looked at them and saw the slow, inevitable wearing down of their faces and bodies, which would make the thinness of Frenchmen a stereotype in England for a hundred years.

The Marquis looked over the submissive faces bowing before him, just as his own class bowed before Monseigneur at court. The only difference was that these peasants bowed out of suffering, not to gain favor. Just then, a gray-haired road mender joined the group.

"Bring that man here!" the Marquis ordered his messenger.

The man was brought forward, holding his cap, and the other villagers crowded around to watch and listen, just like the crowd in Paris.

"Did I pass you on the road?" the Marquis asked.

"Monseigneur, it is true. I had the honor of being passed by you."

"Coming up the hill, and at the top?"

"Yes, Monseigneur."

"What were you staring at so closely?"

"Monseigneur, I was looking at the man."

He bent down and pointed his torn blue cap under the carriage. All the villagers leaned down to look as well.

"What man, you pig? And why look there?"

"Pardon, Monseigneur; he was hanging onto the chain of the carriage brake."

"Who?" the Marquis demanded.

"Monseigneur, the man."

"May the Devil take these idiots! What is his name? You know everyone in this area. Who was he?"

"Please, Monseigneur! He was not from around here. I have never seen him in my life."

"Hanging by the chain? Wasn't he suffocated?"

"With your permission, that was the amazing thing, Monseigneur. His head was hanging down—like this!"

He turned sideways to the carriage and leaned back, throwing his face up to the sky and letting his head hang down. Then he straightened up, held his cap, and bowed.

"What did he look like?"

"Monseigneur, he was whiter than a miller. Covered in dust, white as a ghost, and tall as a ghost!"

This description caused a stir in the small crowd, but everyone kept their eyes on the Marquis, perhaps wondering if he had any ghosts on his conscience.

"Well," said the Marquis, feeling that such low people were not worth getting angry at, "you did a fine job, seeing a thief riding on my carriage and keeping your mouth shut. Bah! Put him aside, Monsieur Gabelle!"

Monsieur Gabelle was the postmaster and tax collector. He had come out politely to assist in the questioning, holding the road mender by his sleeve in an official manner.

"Bah! Step aside!" said Monsieur Gabelle.

"Arrest this stranger if he tries to stay in your village tonight, Gabelle, and make sure his business is honest."

"Monseigneur, I am honored to obey your commands."

"Did the man run away, road mender? Where is the rascal?"

The road mender was already under the carriage with a few friends, pointing at the chain. They pulled him out and presented him, breathless, to the Marquis.

"Did the man run away, fool, when we stopped for the brake?"

"Monseigneur, he threw himself over the side of the hill, headfirst, like a man diving into a river."

"See to it, Gabelle. Go on!"

The villagers looking at the chain were still close to the wheels. The carriage started so suddenly that they had to jump back to save their bones. They had very little else to save.

The carriage started quickly but was soon slowed by the steep hill. It climbed at a walking pace, rocking through the sweet-smelling summer night. The drivers quietly fixed their whip lashes, the servant walked beside the horses, and the messenger could be heard riding ahead in the distance.

At the steepest part of the hill stood a small cemetery, with a wooden cross and a thin, worn statue of Christ carved by a local craftsman. It looked terribly thin and spare, perhaps copied from the sculptor's own life.

A woman was kneeling before this sad monument. She turned her head as the carriage approached, stood up quickly, and walked to the carriage door.

"It is you, Monseigneur! Monseigneur, a petition."

With an impatient sigh, but keeping his cold, unchanging face, the Marquis looked out.

"What is it now? Always petitions!"

"Monseigneur, for the love of God! My husband, the forester."

"What about your husband? It is always the same with you people. He cannot pay his taxes?"

"He has paid everything, Monseigneur. He is dead."

"Well, he is at peace. Can I bring him back to life?"

"Alas, no, Monseigneur! But he lies over there under a small mound of poor grass."

"Well?"

"Monseigneur, there are so many of those small mounds of poor grass."

"And?"

She looked like an old woman, but she was actually young. She was crying passionately, clutching her worn hands together and touching the carriage door gently, as if it could feel her appeal.

"Monseigneur, hear me! Hear my petition! My husband died of starvation. So many die of hunger, and many more will die."

"And what can I do? Can I feed them all?"

"God knows, Monseigneur, but I do not ask for that. My petition is that a small marker of stone or wood, with my husband's name, be placed over his grave so we know where he lies. Otherwise, the spot will be forgotten. When I die of the same hunger, I will be buried under another unmarked mound. Monseigneur, there are so many of us, and the hunger is so great! Monseigneur! Monseigneur!"

The servant pushed her away from the door, the carriage sped up, and she was left far behind. The Marquis was driven quickly toward his castle.

The sweet scents of the summer night rose around him, falling impartially like rain on the poor peasants gathered by the fountain, where the road mender was still talking about the ghost-like man. Gradually, the villagers went home, and lights appeared in their windows. As the lights went out and the stars appeared, it looked as if the lights had flown up into the sky.

The Marquis soon entered the shadow of a large house and overhanging trees. The carriage stopped under the light of a torch, and the great door of his castle was opened.

"Has my nephew, Monsieur Charles, whom I expect, arrived from England?"

"Monseigneur, not yet."

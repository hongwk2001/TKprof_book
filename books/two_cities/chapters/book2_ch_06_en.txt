CHAPTER VI.
Hundreds of People

The quiet lodgings of Doctor Manette were in a quiet street corner not far from Soho Square. On a fine Sunday afternoon, four months after the treason trial had passed and been mostly forgotten by the public, Mr. Jarvis Lorry walked from his home in Clerkenwell to have dinner with the Doctor. After initially focusing only on business, Mr. Lorry had become a true friend of the family, and this quiet street corner had become the happiest part of his life.

On this pleasant Sunday, Mr. Lorry walked toward Soho early in the afternoon for three habitual reasons. First, because on nice Sundays he often walked before dinner with the Doctor and Lucie. Second, because on bad Sundays he spent the day with them as a family friend. They would talk, read, look out the window, and simply pass the time together. Third, because he had some small doubts he wanted to resolve, and he knew this was a good time to observe the Doctor's household.

A more charming corner than the one where the Doctor lived could not be found in London. It was a dead-end street, and the front windows of the lodgings looked out onto a pleasant, quiet street. There were few buildings north of Oxford Road back then, and forest trees and wild flowers still grew in fields that have now vanished. As a result, fresh country air circulated freely in Soho, and nearby walls were lined with peaches ripening in the sun.

The summer sun shone brightly into the corner early in the day; but when the streets grew hot, the corner was in the shade. It was a cool, peaceful but cheerful spot, famous for its echoes, and a quiet refuge from the busy streets.

The Doctor rented two floors of a large, quiet house. A few businesses operated there during the day, but they made very little noise and were empty at night. In a building at the back, reached through a courtyard with a green plane tree, organs were made, silver was engraved, and gold was beaten. The craftsman who did this had hung a sign of a golden arm extending from the front hall wall. It looked as if he had turned his own arm to gold and was threatening to do the same to visitors. Very little was ever seen or heard of these trades, or of a quiet lodger upstairs, or of a coachmaker downstairs. Occasionally a workman walked through the hall, a stranger looked around, or a distant clinking sound was heard from the courtyard. Aside from these rare noises, the sparrows in the tree and the echoes in the street had the place to themselves all week.

Doctor Manette saw patients here, drawn by his old reputation and the rumors of his dramatic story. His scientific knowledge and skill in conducting experiments also kept him in demand, earning him as much money as he needed.

Mr. Lorry was thinking of these things when he rang the doorbell on that fine Sunday afternoon.

"Is Doctor Manette at home?"

He was expected back soon.

"Is Miss Lucie at home?"

She was also expected back soon.

"Is Miss Pross at home?"

She might be, but it was impossible for the servant girl to guess whether Miss Pross wanted to see visitors or not.

"Since I feel at home here myself," said Mr. Lorry, "I’ll go upstairs."

Although Lucie had no memory of France, the country of her birth, she seemed to have inherited the French ability to make a place look beautiful with very little money. Simple as the furniture was, it was decorated with so many tasteful little ornaments that the effect was delightful. The arrangement of everything in the rooms, the colors, and the simple decorations showed such care and good sense that as Mr. Lorry looked around, the chairs and tables seemed to ask if he approved.

There were three rooms on the floor, and the doors between them were kept open to let the breeze pass through. Mr. Lorry walked from one room to another, smiling at the pleasant atmosphere. The first was the best room. It contained Lucie's birds, flowers, books, desk, and watercolors. The second was the Doctor’s consulting room, which was also used for dining. The third, shaded by the leaves of the plane tree outside, was the Doctor’s bedroom. In the corner stood the unused shoemaker’s bench and tools, looking just as they had in the dark attic room in Paris.

"I wonder," said Mr. Lorry, looking around, "why he keeps that painful reminder of his suffering!"

"And why shouldn't he?" came a sharp voice that made him jump.

It was Miss Pross, the eccentric, red-haired woman whom Mr. Lorry had first met at the Dover hotel and had since come to know well.

"I would have thought—" Mr. Lorry began.

"Bah! What you would have thought!" said Miss Pross, cutting him off.

"How do you do?" she asked sharply, though not unkindly.

"I am well, thank you," answered Mr. Lorry meekly. "How are you?"

"Nothing to brag about," said Miss Pross.

"Indeed?"

"Yes, indeed!" said Miss Pross. "I am very annoyed about my Ladybird."

"Indeed?"

"For heaven's sake, say something other than 'indeed,' or you will drive me crazy," said Miss Pross. She was known for her bluntness.

"Really, then?" Mr. Lorry corrected himself.

"Really is bad enough," returned Miss Pross, "but better. Yes, I am very upset."

"May I ask why?"

"I don't want dozens of people who are not good enough for Ladybird coming here to see her," said Miss Pross.

"Do dozens of people actually come for that?"

"Hundreds," said Miss Pross.

It was typical of Miss Pross to exaggerate whenever anyone questioned her statements.

"Dear me!" said Mr. Lorry, choosing the safest response.

"I have lived with the darling since she was ten years old," said Miss Pross. "And it’s very hard now."

Not understanding what was so hard, Mr. Lorry simply nodded.

"All sorts of unworthy people are always showing up," said Miss Pross. "Ever since you started it."

"_I_ started it, Miss Pross?"

"Didn't you? Who brought her father back to life?"

"Oh! If _that_ was starting it—" said Mr. Lorry.

"It wasn't ending it, was it? When you started it, it was hard enough. I have no complaint about Doctor Manette, except that he is not worthy of such a daughter—though no one could be. But it is doubly hard to have crowds of people showing up after her to take her affection away from me."

Mr. Lorry knew Miss Pross was very jealous, but he also knew that beneath her eccentric behavior, she was completely devoted to Lucie. She was one of those unselfish women who gladly dedicate their lives to serving the youth, beauty, and success they never had themselves. Mr. Lorry respected her loyal devotion deeply, placing her in his estimation far above many wealthy ladies who kept accounts at Tellson's Bank.

"There never was, and never will be, but one man worthy of Ladybird," said Miss Pross. "And that was my brother Solomon, if he hadn't made a mistake in life."

Mr. Lorry’s past inquiries had revealed that Solomon was actually a heartless thief who had stolen everything Miss Pross owned and abandoned her in poverty. Yet Miss Pross's blind faith in her brother (excusing his behavior as a "slight mistake") only increased Mr. Lorry's respect for her loyalty.

"Since we are alone and are both practical people," he said as they sat down in the living room, "let me ask you: does the Doctor ever mention his shoemaking days to Lucie?"

"Never."

"Yet he keeps the bench and tools next to his bed?"

"Yes," said Miss Pross, shaking her head. "But that doesn't mean he doesn't think about it."

"Do you believe he thinks of it often?"

"I do," said Miss Pross.

"Do you imagine—" Mr. Lorry began.

"Never imagine anything," Miss Pross interrupted. "Don't use your imagination."

"I stand corrected. Do you suppose, then?"

"Now and then," she admitted.

"Do you suppose," Mr. Lorry asked with a twinkle in his eye, "that Doctor Manette has his own theory about why he was imprisoned, or knows the name of the person who put him there?"

"I only know what Ladybird tells me."

"And what is that?"

"She thinks he does."

"Please don't be angry with my questions. I am just a dull businessman, and you are a practical woman."

"Dull?" Miss Pross asked calmly.

Wishing he hadn't used that word, Mr. Lorry quickly said, "No, no. But to return to the subject: isn't it strange that the Doctor, who is clearly innocent, never speaks of his imprisonment? Not with me, and not even with his beloved daughter? I ask this out of genuine care, not curiosity."

"Well," said Miss Pross, softened by his apology, "I think he is simply terrified of the whole subject."

"Terrified?"

"It makes sense. It is a horrible memory. Besides, he lost his mind because of it. Since he doesn't know exactly how he lost his mind or how he recovered, he might fear losing it again. That would make anyone avoid the subject."

This was a deeper observation than Mr. Lorry had expected. "True," he said, "and very frightening to think about. Yet I worry if keeping it bottled up inside is good for him."

"It can't be helped," said Miss Pross. "If we bring it up, he immediately gets worse. It is best to leave it alone. Sometimes he gets up in the middle of the night and walks up and down his room for hours. Ladybird knows this means his mind is pacing in his old prison cell. She goes to him, and they walk up and down together in silence until he calms down. He never explains why he is restless, and she never asks. They just walk together until her presence brings him back to himself."

Despite her claim of having no imagination, Miss Pross's description of this quiet pacing showed she understood his pain well.

The street corner, known for its echoes, now began to echo with the sound of approaching footsteps.

"Here they are!" said Miss Pross, standing up. "And now we will have hundreds of people!"

Due to the strange acoustics of the corner, Mr. Lorry felt as he looked out the window that the footsteps would never arrive. Echoes would fade and new ones would start, sounding close but vanishing. Finally, the father and daughter appeared, and Miss Pross went to open the door.

Miss Pross was a pleasant sight in her devotion, taking off Lucie's bonnet, dusting it, and smoothing her hair with great pride. Lucie was also a pleasant sight, hugging Miss Pross and thanking her for her trouble—which she had to do playfully, or Miss Pross would get upset and cry in her room. The Doctor watched them happily, joking that Miss Pross was spoiling Lucie. Mr. Lorry watched them all, feeling grateful in his later years to be part of such a warm home. But no "hundreds of people" arrived.

By dinner time, there was still no crowd. Miss Pross had prepared a wonderful dinner. Though simple, it was perfectly cooked and served, combining English and French styles. In her practical way, she had searched Soho for poor French immigrants who, for a few shillings, taught her their cooking secrets. The servant girls viewed her as a magician who could turn a simple chicken or vegetable into a feast.

On Sundays, Miss Pross ate with the family, though on other days she ate alone in the kitchen or in her private blue room upstairs. Today, she was in a very good mood, and the dinner was very pleasant.

It was a hot, humid day, and after dinner, Lucie suggested they drink their wine outside under the plane tree. They went out, and Lucie poured Mr. Lorry's wine. They sat talking while the houses behind them looked down and the tree rustled above.

Still, the expected crowd did not show up. Charles Darnay arrived, but he was only one person.

Doctor Manette and Lucie received him warmly. However, Miss Pross suddenly developed a nervous twitch and went inside, calling it a "fit of the jerks."

The Doctor was in high spirits and looked remarkably young. Sitting next to Lucie, their strong resemblance was very clear.

They talked about the old buildings of London, and Darnay asked the Doctor, "Have you visited the Tower of London much?"

"Lucie and I have been there, but only briefly. We know it is very interesting, but not much more."

"I was kept there, as you remember," Darnay said with a slight smile, though he colored slightly. "They told me a strange story when I was there."

"What was that?" Lucie asked.

"While making repairs, workmen found an old, forgotten dungeon. The walls were covered with inscriptions carved by prisoners. In a corner stone, one prisoner who was executed had hurriedly carved three letters. At first they looked like D. I. C., but on closer inspection, the last letter was a G. There was no record of a prisoner with those initials, and people guessed what it meant. Finally, someone suggested the letters spelled the word 'DIG'. They dug beneath the stone floor and found the ashes of a paper inside a small leather bag. What the prisoner wrote will never be known, but he had hidden it from his guards."

"Father!" Lucie cried. "You look ill!"

The Doctor had suddenly stood up, putting his hand to his head with a terrified look.

"No, my dear, not ill. A few large rain drops fell on me and startled me. We should go inside."

He recovered his composure almost immediately. Rain was indeed starting to fall, and he showed them the drops on his hand. But he said nothing about the story Darnay had told. As they went inside, Mr. Lorry noticed the same strange look on the Doctor's face as he looked at Darnay—the same expression he had seen at the courthouse.

He recovered so quickly, however, that Mr. Lorry doubted what he had seen. The Doctor was calm and steady as they walked inside, repeating that the sudden rain had startled him.

At tea time, Miss Pross served tea, still twitching, but there was still no crowd. Sydney Carton arrived, making the guests only two.

The night was very hot, and they sat by the open windows to catch a breeze. The curtains blew in the wind like white wings as thunder rolled.

"The rain drops are still few and heavy," said Doctor Manette. "The storm is coming slowly."

"It is coming surely," said Carton.

They spoke quietly, the way people do when waiting for lightning in a dark room.

The streets below were busy with people running for shelter. The quiet corner echoed with the sound of footsteps, though no one was there.

"A crowd of footsteps, yet the street is empty!" Darnay remarked.

"Isn't it strange, Mr. Darnay?" Lucie asked. "Sometimes I sit here in the evening and imagine—though the fancy frightens me tonight in the dark—"

"Tell us. We want to know."

"It sounds silly. I have sat here alone, listening to the echoes, until I imagined they were the footsteps of all the people who will enter our lives in the future."

"There is a huge crowd coming into our lives, then," Sydney Carton remarked gloomily.

The echoes of footsteps grew faster and louder as the storm approached. It sounded as if people were walking right under the windows and in the room, though the street remained empty.

"Are all these people coming to all of us, Miss Manette, or will we divide them?"

"I don't know, Mr. Darnay. It was just a foolish fancy. I imagined them as the people who will affect my life and my father's."

"I will take them into mine!" said Carton. "I make no conditions. A huge crowd is coming toward us, Miss Manette, and I see them—by the lightning." He spoke as a bright flash illuminated his figure by the window.

"And I hear them!" he added after a clap of thunder. "Here they come, fast and fierce!"

He was referring to the sudden downpour of rain, which drowned out his voice. A powerful storm of thunder, lightning, and rain raged until midnight when the moon finally came out.

The great bell of St. Paul's Cathedral was striking one in the clear night air when Mr. Lorry, escorted by Jerry carrying a lantern, set out for home. Because some stretches of the road were dark and dangerous, Mr. Lorry always had Jerry walk with him.

"What a storm! It was enough to wake the dead, Jerry," Mr. Lorry remarked.

"I've never seen a night that could do that, master," Jerry replied.

"Good night, Mr. Carton. Good night, Mr. Darnay," Mr. Lorry called out. "Will we ever see such a night together again?"

Perhaps. And perhaps they would see that great crowd of people, with its rush and roar, coming down upon them as well.

CHAPTER XIII.
The Fellow of No Delicacy

If Sydney Carton ever showed any spark of his former self, he certainly never did so at the home of Doctor Manette. He had visited frequently over the past year, but had always been the same gloomy and depressed lounger. When he chose to talk, he spoke well; but his habit of caring about nothing, which cast a dark shadow over him, was rarely broken by his inner intelligence.

Yet he did care for the streets around that house, and for the very stones of the pavement. Many a night he wandered there unhappily, when wine failed to bring even temporary cheer. Many a gloomy dawn found his solitary figure lingering there, even when the first rays of the sun lit up the church spires and tall buildings. Perhaps these quiet hours brought back memories of better things, now forgotten and out of reach. Lately, he rarely slept in his bed in the Temple, often getting up after only a few minutes to wander around Soho.

On a day in August, after Mr. Stryver had informed his assistant that "he had changed his mind about marrying," taking his blunt manners to Devonshire, Sydney's feet still walked those streets. The sight and smell of flowers in the city streets brought a touch of joy to the poorest, health to the sick, and a feeling of youth to the old. Carton's aimless steps eventually led him to the Doctor’s door.

He was shown upstairs and found Lucie working alone. She had never felt quite comfortable around him, and received him with some embarrassment as he sat near her table. But looking at his face as they exchanged greetings, she noticed a change in him.

"I fear you are not well, Mr. Carton!"

"No. But the life I lead, Miss Manette, is not healthy. What else can be expected of a man like me?"

"Is it not—forgive me for asking—a pity to live such a life?"

"God knows it is a shame!"

"Then why not change it?"

Looking at him gently, she was surprised and saddened to see tears in his eyes. His voice trembled as he answered:

"It is too late for that. I will never be better than I am. I will only sink lower and get worse."

He rested his elbow on the table and covered his eyes with his hand. The table shook in the silence that followed.

She had never seen him show such feeling and was deeply distressed. Sensing her concern without looking up, he said:

"Please forgive me, Miss Manette. I break down when I try to say what I want to tell you. Will you listen to me?"

"If it will help you, Mr. Carton, or make you happier, I would gladly listen!"

"God bless you for your sweet compassion!"

He uncovered his face after a moment and spoke steadily.

"Don't be afraid to listen. Don't shrink from what I say. I am like a man who died young. My life might have been so much more."

"No, Mr. Carton. I am sure the best part of your life is still ahead of you. You could be much more worthy of yourself."

"You mean worthy of you, Miss Manette. Although I know that is impossible, I will never forget that you said so!"

She was pale and trembling. He spoke with a quiet despair that made the conversation unlike any other.

"If it had been possible, Miss Manette, for you to return the love of the man before you—wasted, drunk, and ruined as you know him to be—he would have known, even in his happiness, that he would bring you only misery, sorrow, and disgrace. I know you cannot love me. I ask for no love, and I am even glad that it is impossible."

"Without love, can I not help you, Mr. Carton? Can I not guide you to a better path? Can I repay your trust in no way? I know you would tell this to no one else," she said tearfully. "Can I do nothing for you?"

He shook his head.

"Nothing. No, Miss Manette, nothing. If you will listen to me for just a moment more, you will have done all you can do. I want you to know that you have been the last dream of my soul. In my ruined life, the sight of you with your father in this beautiful home has stirred old hopes I thought were dead. Since I met you, I have felt a remorse I thought I would never feel again, and have heard old voices telling me to improve. I have had ideas of trying again, shaking off my laziness, and fighting the battle I had given up. It was a dream that ends in nothing, leaving me where I fell, but I want you to know that you inspired it."

"Will nothing of it remain? Oh, Mr. Carton, think again! Try again!"

"No, Miss Manette. I have always known I am unworthy. Yet I was weak enough to want you to know that you turned this heap of ashes into a fire—a fire that does no good and simply burns itself out."

"Since it is my misfortune, Mr. Carton, to have made you more unhappy than before—"

"Don't say that, Miss Manette. You would have saved me if anything could. You will not make me worse."

"Since your feelings are due to my influence, can I not use that influence to help you? Do I have no power for good with you at all?"

"The only good I am capable of now, Miss Manette, I have come to do today. Let me carry through the rest of my life the memory of opening my heart to you, and knowing there was something in me you could pity."

"Which I begged you to believe was capable of better things, Mr. Carton!"

"Do not ask me to believe that, Miss Manette. I know myself too well. I am upsetting you, and I must finish. Will you allow me to believe, when I remember this day, that the last confidence of my life was placed in your pure keeping, and will be shared with no one else?"

"If that brings you comfort, yes."

"Not even by the person you will one day love most?"

"Mr. Carton," she answered after a pause, "the secret is yours, not mine, and I promise to keep it."

"Thank you. And again, God bless you."

He kissed her hand and moved toward the door.

"Do not worry, Miss Manette, that I will ever bring this up again. I will never refer to it. It is as secure as if I were dead. In my last hour, I will cherish the memory that my last honest confession was made to you, and that my name and faults were carried gently in your heart. I hope your life is bright and happy!"

He was so different from his usual self, and it was so sad to think of his wasted talents, that Lucie wept for him as he looked back.

"Be comforted!" he said. "I am not worth your tears, Miss Manette. In an hour, the low companions and bad habits I hate but yield to will make me less worthy of your tears than any beggar on the street. Be comforted! But in my heart, I will always be toward you what I am now. My last request but one is that you believe this."

"I will, Mr. Carton."

"My last request of all is this: I will now leave you, a visitor with whom you have nothing in common. It is useless to say, but it comes from my soul: for you, and for anyone dear to you, I would do anything. If my life had any value or opportunity for sacrifice, I would gladly sacrifice it for you and those you love. Remember me as sincere in this. The time will come when new ties will bind you tenderly to your home—the dearest ties you will ever know. Oh, Miss Manette, when you look at a happy child's face, or see your own beauty reflected in your children, remember that there is a man who would gladly give his life to keep the life you love beside you!"

He said, "Farewell!" and left her.

Book the First--Recalled to Life

CHAPTER I.

The Period

It was the best of times, and it was the worst of times. It was an age of wisdom, and it was an age of foolishness. It was an epoch of belief, and it was an epoch of skepticism. It was a season of Light, and it was a season of Darkness. It was a spring of hope, and it was a winter of despair. We had everything ahead of us, and we had nothing ahead of us. We were all going straight to Heaven, and we were all going straight the other way. In short, the time was so much like our own that some of its loudest voices insisted that it could only be described in extremes, whether for better or for worse.

There was a king with a large jaw and a queen with a plain face on the throne of England; there was a king with a large jaw and a queen with a beautiful face on the throne of France. In both countries, it was crystal clear to the wealthy lords who controlled the government's wealth and power that things in general were settled forever.

It was the year of Our Lord 1775. Spiritual revelations were granted to England during that favored time, just as they are today. Mrs. Southcott had recently celebrated her twenty-fifth birthday, and a prophetic soldier in the Life Guards had announced her grand arrival by claiming that plans were in place for London and Westminster to be swallowed up. Even the famous Cock Lane ghost had been put to rest only twelve years earlier, after tapping out its messages, just like the spirits of this past year (which were surprisingly unoriginal) tapped out theirs. Meanwhile, much more down-to-earth messages had recently arrived for the English King and his people from a congress of British subjects in America. Strange as it seems, these letters turned out to be far more important to the human race than any messages ever received from the spirits of the Cock Lane family.

France, which was generally less interested in spiritual matters than England, was rolling smoothly downhill, printing paper money and spending it quickly. Guided by her priests, France also amused herself with such "merciful" deeds as sentencing a young man to have his hands cut off, his tongue torn out with pliers, and his body burned alive, simply because he did not kneel down in the rain to show respect to a dirty group of monks passing by fifty or sixty yards away. It is highly likely that in the forests of France and Norway, trees were already growing, marked by the Woodman, Fate, to be cut down and sawed into planks to build a certain movable wooden frame with a sack and a heavy knife, which would become terrifying in history. It is also highly likely that in the simple barns of farmers near Paris, there were rough carts sitting that very day—spattered with country mud, sniffed at by pigs, and used by chickens to roost—which the Farmer, Death, had already chosen to carry prisoners to their executions during the coming Revolution. But that Woodman and that Farmer, though they worked without stopping, worked in absolute silence. No one heard them walking around with quiet steps, especially because even suggesting they were active was considered unpatriotic and against God.

In England, there was hardly enough law and order to brag about. Bold burglaries by armed men and highway robberies happened in the capital city itself every single night. Families were publicly warned not to leave town without storing their furniture in secure warehouses. The robber in the dark was often a respectable shopkeeper by day; if recognized and challenged by a fellow merchant whom he stopped while acting as the bandit "Captain," he would calmly shoot him in the head and ride off. The mail coach was ambushed by seven thieves; the guard shot three of them dead before being shot dead himself by the other four because he ran out of ammunition, after which the mail was robbed without any trouble. Even the grand and powerful Lord Mayor of London was forced to halt and hand over his valuables on Turnham Green by a single robber, who stripped the famous man of his wealth in front of all his servants. Prisoners in London jails fought battles with their guards, and the authorities fired heavy guns loaded with bullets right into the crowd. Thieves cut diamond crosses from the necks of noble lords at royal parties. Soldiers marched into St. Giles's to search for illegal goods, the angry crowd shot at the soldiers, the soldiers shot back at the crowd, and nobody thought any of this was out of the ordinary. In the middle of all this, the executioner, always busy and completely useless, was constantly called upon: sometimes hanging long rows of various criminals, sometimes hanging a burglar on Saturday who had just been caught on Tuesday, sometimes branding dozens of people on the hand at Newgate Prison, and sometimes burning rebellious pamphlets at the door of Westminster Hall. Today he would take the life of a terrible murderer, and tomorrow he would hang a poor thief who had stolen only sixpence from a farm boy.

All these things, and a thousand more like them, happened during and around the year 1775. Surrounded by these events, while the Woodman and the Farmer worked unnoticed, the two rulers with the large jaws and the other two with the plain and beautiful faces made a great fuss and ruled with absolute authority. In this way, the year 1775 led these powerful rulers, along with millions of ordinary people—including the characters in this story—along the paths that lay ahead of them.

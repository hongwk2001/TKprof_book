Book the Second--the Golden Thread

CHAPTER I.
Five Years Later

Tellson’s Bank near Temple Bar was an old-fashioned place, even in the year 1780. It was very small, very dark, very ugly, and very uncomfortable. Moreover, it was old-fashioned in a very particular way. The bank owners were actually proud of its smallness, its darkness, its ugliness, and its total lack of comfort. They even bragged about how extreme these qualities were, believing that if the bank were any cleaner or more convenient, it would somehow be less respectable. This was not just a quiet belief, but an active argument they used against more modern and convenient businesses. They would say, "Tellson's doesn’t need extra space, Tellson's doesn’t need bright light, Tellson's doesn’t need fancy decorations. Other banks might need those things, but Tellson’s, thank Heaven, does not!"

Any of these owners would have disowned his own son over the question of rebuilding or modernizing Tellson's Bank. In this regard, the bank was very much like the country itself. England often punished its citizens for suggesting improvements to old laws and customs. Those laws had long been deeply problematic. But they were kept simply because they were old.

Thus, Tellson’s had become the ultimate example of inconvenience. After pushing open a stubborn door that shook with a weak rattle, you fell down two steps into the bank. There you found yourself in a miserable little shop with two small counters. The oldest clerks working there would take your check with hands that shook so much the paper rustled like leaves, while they examined your signature by the dirtiest windows. These windows were constantly sprayed with mud from Fleet Street and made even darker by heavy iron bars and the shadow of the nearby Temple Bar gate. If your business required meeting with "the owners," you were escorted to a dark, prison-like room at the back. There you could meditate on your sins. You would wait there until they finally appeared — hands in their pockets — as if they had all the time in the world. Your money came out of or went into rotting old wooden drawers, sending clouds of dust up your nose and down your throat whenever they were opened. The banknotes had a musty smell, as if they were turning back into moldy rags. Silverware and valuables were stored near the sewer pipes, and the damp air quickly ruined their polish. Important legal documents were kept in improvised vaults made from old kitchens and pantries, absorbing the greasy air. Lighter boxes of family papers went upstairs into a useless dining room that had a massive table but never served any meals. Even in 1780, the old love letters and children's notes stored there had only recently been spared a grim fate. The heads of executed criminals had long been displayed on Temple Bar just outside the windows. Their dead eyes seemed to stare right into the bank. It was a cruelty worthy of the most uncivilized lands.

Indeed, at that time, execution was the standard solution for almost every crime, and Tellson’s Bank fully supported it. Death was nature’s remedy for all problems, so why not the law's? Consequently, the punishments were brutal and universal. Anyone who forged a signature was put to death. Anyone who passed a bad note was put to death. Anyone who opened a letter illegally was put to death. Anyone who stole forty shillings and sixpence was put to death. Anyone who took a horse from outside Tellson's and rode off with it was put to death. Anyone who made a counterfeit coin was put to death. It did not prevent crime at all—in fact, the opposite was true—but it certainly cleared the case off the books and left nothing else to worry about. Thus, Tellson's Bank had caused so many executions that something grim could be said. If all the heads of those it had prosecuted were lined up on Temple Bar, they would have blocked what little daylight reached the ground floor. Other major businesses of the era were no different.

Cramped in dim cupboards and tiny offices, the oldest clerks carried on the bank's business. When they hired a young man, they hid him away in a dark place like a cheese until he grew old and developed the full, moldy Tellson flavor. Only then was he allowed to be seen by customers, looking solemn as he bent over large ledger books in his old-fashioned breeches and leggings.

Outside Tellson’s Bank—and never allowed inside unless specifically summoned—was an odd-job man who worked as a messenger and porter, serving as the bank's living sign. He was always there during business hours unless he was out running an errand. When he was away, his twelve-year-old son, who looked exactly like him, took his place. The bank tolerated this messenger in a dignified way. The business had always had someone in that role, and time had carried this particular man to the job. His last name was Cruncher, and when he was baptized as a child in Hounsditch, he had been given the first name Jerry.

The scene opens at Mr. Cruncher’s private lodging in Hanging-sword Alley, Whitefriars, at half-past seven on a windy March morning in the year 1780. Mr. Cruncher always referred to the year of our Lord as "Anna Dominoes," apparently believing the era was named after a popular game invented by a lady of that name.

Mr. Cruncher’s rooms were not in a pleasant neighborhood. There were only two rooms, even if you counted a tiny closet with a single pane of glass. However, they were kept very clean. Early as it was on this windy morning, the bedroom had already been scrubbed. A clean white cloth was spread on the heavy wooden table, set with cups and saucers for breakfast.

Mr. Cruncher lay asleep under a patchwork blanket, looking like a colorful clown at rest. At first he slept heavily, but gradually he began to roll and toss in bed until his head emerged, his spiky hair looking sharp enough to tear the sheets to pieces. At that moment, he cried out in great frustration:

"Damn me, if she isn't at it again!"

A neat and hardworking woman rose quickly and nervously from her knees in a corner of the room, showing she was the one he was talking to.

"What!" said Mr. Cruncher, looking out from under the covers for a boot. "You're at it again, are you?"

After greeting the morning with this second shout, he threw a boot at the woman as a third greeting. It was a very muddy boot. This introduces a strange detail about Mr. Cruncher’s household: although he often came home after bank hours with clean boots, he frequently woke up the next morning to find them covered in clay.

The boot missed. Mr. Cruncher changed his tone. "What," he said, "what are you up to, Aggerawayter?"

"I was only saying my prayers," his wife replied gently.

"Saying your prayers! You're a fine woman! What do you mean by dropping to your knees and praying against me?"

"I was not praying against you, Jerry. I was praying for you."

"You weren't! And even if you were, I won't have you taking such liberties. Look here, young Jerry! Your mother is a fine woman, praying against your father's business success. You have a very dutiful mother, my son. You have a very religious mother, my boy—going and dropping to her knees to pray that the bread and butter be snatched right out of her own child's mouth!"

Young Jerry (who was standing in his shirt) took this very seriously. Turning to his mother, he strongly protested against any prayers that might take away his food.

"And what do you think, you conceited woman," Mr. Cruncher said — with unconscious contradiction — "that your prayers are actually worth? Name the price you put on your prayers!"

"They only come from the heart, Jerry. They are worth no more than that."

"Worth no more than that," Mr. Cruncher repeated. "Then they aren't worth much. Either way, I won't be prayed against, I tell you. I can't afford it. I'm not going to have your sneaking prayers bring me bad luck. If you must drop to your knees, pray for your husband and child, not against them. If I had a normal wife, and this poor boy had a normal mother, things would be different. I might have made some money last week. Instead, I was prayed against, undermined, and religiously tricked into the worst luck imaginable. Damn me!" said Mr. Cruncher, who was putting on his clothes. "Between your piety and one cursed thing after another, I've been cheated out of my luck this week as badly as any honest tradesman ever was! Young Jerry, get dressed, my boy. While I clean my boots, keep an eye on your mother. If you see her starting to pray again, call me. I tell you," he said, addressing his wife again, "I won't have this. I feel as shaky as an old carriage, as sleepy as if I'd taken opium, and my muscles are so sore that I wouldn't know who I was if it weren't for the pain. Yet I haven't made a single penny from it, and I suspect you've been praying day and night to keep me poor. I won't put up with it, Aggerawayter! What do you have to say to that?"

Growling other remarks like, "Oh yes! You're very religious, aren't you? You wouldn't do anything to hurt your husband and child's interests, would you? Not you!" and muttering in his anger, Mr. Cruncher went to clean his boots and get ready for the day. Meanwhile, his son, whose hair had smaller spikes and whose eyes were set close together like his father's, watched his mother as ordered. He repeatedly startled the poor woman by jumping out of the closet where he was getting dressed, whispering, "You're about to pray, mother! Hey, father!" before disappearing back inside with a mischievous grin.

Mr. Cruncher’s temper did not improve by breakfast time. He was particularly angry when Mrs. Cruncher tried to say grace before the meal.

"Now, Aggerawayter! What are you doing? At it again?"

His wife explained that she was only asking for a blessing on the food.

"Don't do it!" said Mr. Cruncher, looking around as if he expected the loaf of bread to vanish because of her prayers. "I'm not going to be blessed out of my house and home. I won't have my food blessed off my table. Keep quiet!"

With red eyes and a grim face, looking as if he had been up all night at a very unpleasant gathering, Jerry Cruncher grumbled through his breakfast like a wild animal in a zoo. Around nine o'clock, he smoothed down his hair, put on the most respectable and business-like look he could manage, and headed out for the day's work.

It could hardly be called a business, despite his favorite description of himself as an "honest tradesman." His equipment consisted of a wooden stool made from an old chair. Young Jerry carried this stool at his father’s side every morning to a spot beneath the bank window closest to Temple Bar. There, with a handful of straw gathered from passing carriages to keep the cold and wet off his feet, Jerry set up his spot for the day. At this post, Mr. Cruncher was as well known to the people of Fleet Street and the Temple as the Temple Bar gate itself, and he looked almost as dark and gloomy.

Set up by a quarter to nine, in time to tip his hat to the senior clerks as they entered Tellson’s, Jerry took his post on this windy March morning. Young Jerry stood by him when he wasn't running off to tease and annoy smaller boys on the street. Father and son, looking remarkably alike, stood silently watching the morning traffic on Fleet Street. With their heads close together, they looked a bit like a pair of monkeys. The comparison was made even stronger by the fact that the older Jerry chewed and spat out straw, while the younger Jerry watched him and the street with restless, twinkling eyes.

Just then, the head of one of the bank's indoor messengers appeared at the door, and he called out:

"Messenger needed!"

"Hooray, father! Here's an early job to start the day!"

Wishing his father luck, young Jerry sat down on the stool, took over the straw his father had been chewing, and began to think.

"Always rusty! His fingers are always rusty!" muttered young Jerry. "Where does my father get all that iron rust from? He certainly doesn't get it around here!"

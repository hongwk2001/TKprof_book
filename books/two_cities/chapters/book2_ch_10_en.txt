CHAPTER X.
Two Promises

Twelve more months had passed, and Charles Darnay was established in England as a teacher of the French language and literature. Today he would be called a professor, but back then he was a tutor. He taught young men who wanted to learn a living language spoken all over the world. He was also a skilled translator who could write elegant English. Since good teachers were rare in those days, and ruined noblemen had not yet begun working as cooks and carpenters, young Mr. Darnay soon became successful. He was also well-informed about the political situation in France, which was of growing interest to the English. Through hard work and perseverance, he prospered.

He had not expected to find an easy life in London. If he had, he would not have succeeded. He expected hard work, found it, and did it. His success came from his efforts.

He spent part of his time at Cambridge University, teaching students European languages, while others taught Greek and Latin. The rest of his time he spent in London.

Ever since the world began, a man's life has always revolved around one thing—the love of a woman. And so it was for Charles Darnay.

He had loved Lucie Manette from the moment of his trial. He had never heard anything as sweet as her compassionate voice, or seen anyone as beautiful as she was when she stood before him in court. But he had not yet spoken to her about his feelings. A year had passed since the assassination at the castle in France—which now felt like a distant dream—and he had not yet told her that he loved her.

He had good reasons for waiting. It was another summer day when, returning to London from his college work, he went to the quiet corner in Soho to speak to Doctor Manette. It was late afternoon, and he knew Lucie was out with Miss Pross.

He found the Doctor reading in his armchair by the window. The energy that had once supported him in prison had slowly returned. He was now a very active man with strong determination. He was still a bit sudden and fitful at times, but this was becoming rare.

He studied a lot, slept little, handled fatigue easily, and was generally cheerful. When Darnay entered, the Doctor laid aside his book and held out his hand.

"Charles Darnay! I am glad to see you. We have been expecting you for a few days. Mr. Stryver and Sydney Carton were here yesterday, and both said you were overdue."

"I am grateful for their interest," Darnay replied, speaking coldly of them but warmly to the Doctor. "Miss Manette—"

"Is well," the Doctor said, interrupting. "Your return will please us all. She is out buying household goods but will return soon."

"Doctor Manette, I knew she was out. I took this opportunity to speak with you alone."

A long silence followed.

"Yes?" the Doctor said with clear discomfort. "Bring your chair here and speak."

Darnay sat down but found it hard to start.

"I have been close with your family for a year and a half, Doctor Manette, and I hope the subject I want to discuss won't—"

The Doctor put up his hand to stop him. After a moment, he drew his hand back and said:

"Is Lucie the subject?"

"She is."

"It is hard for me to speak of her, Charles Darnay. It is very hard for me to hear her spoken of in that way."

"It is a tone of deep admiration and love, Doctor Manette!" Darnay said respectfully.

Another silence followed before the father replied:

"I believe you. I know you mean it."

The Doctor's discomfort and his reluctance to discuss the subject were so clear that Darnay hesitated.

"Shall I continue, sir?"

Another pause.

"Yes, go on."

"You know what I want to say, though you cannot know how deeply I feel it. Dear Doctor Manette, I love your daughter dearly and devotedly. If there is true love in this world, I feel it for her. You have loved in your life; let that memory speak for me!"

The Doctor sat with his face turned away, looking at the floor. At these words, he stretched out his hand quickly and cried:

"Not that, sir! Leave that alone! I beg you, do not bring that up!"

His cry sounded like real pain, ringing in Darnay's ears. He gestured for Darnay to stop, and Darnay remained silent.

"Forgive me," the Doctor said quietly after a few moments. "I do not doubt your love for Lucie. You may be sure of that."

He turned toward Darnay but did not look him in the eye. He rested his chin on his hand, his white hair covering his face.

"Have you spoken to Lucie?"

"No."

"Or written to her?"

"Never."

"I know your silence is out of respect for me as her father. I thank you for that."

He offered his hand, but did not look up.

Darnay said respectfully, "I know, Doctor Manette, because I have seen you together every day, that the love between you and Miss Manette is extraordinary. It has few equals, even between father and child. I know that Lucie loves and relies on you completely. Because she had no parent in her childhood, she is now devoted to you with all her heart. I know that in her eyes, you are a sacred figure restored to her from the dead. When she hugs you, she holds you with the love of a baby, a girl, and a woman all in one. I know that in loving you, she sees her mother, and loves you through all your suffering. I have seen this every day."

Her father sat silent, his face down. His breathing was faster, but he controlled his emotions.

"Dear Doctor Manette, knowing this, I have kept silent as long as possible. I felt that bringing my love between you would interfere with your special bond. But I love her. God is my witness that I love her!"

"I believe you," her father said sadly. "I have thought so before. I believe you."

Darnay heard the sadness in the Doctor's voice. "But please do not think that if I were lucky enough to marry her, I would ever separate her from you," he said. "I would never suggest such a thing. That would be selfish and wrong. If I had any intention of separating you, I could not face you today."

He laid his hand on the Doctor's hand.

"No, dear Doctor Manette. Like you, I am a voluntary exile from France, driven away by its misery and oppression. Like you, I am trying to build a new life here through my own work. I only want to share your lives, and be faithful to you both to the end. I do not want to take Lucie away, but to help support you both."

His hand remained on the Doctor's. Responding to the touch, the Doctor rested his hands on his chair and looked up for the first time. His face showed a struggle with his old look of fear and doubt.

"You speak very sincerely, Charles Darnay. I thank you, and I will open my heart to you. Do you have any reason to believe Lucie loves you?"

"None. Not yet."

"Do you want to find out right away, with my permission?"

"No. I might not have the courage to ask her for weeks; or I might ask her tomorrow."

"Do you want my advice?"

"No, sir. But I thought you might give me some guidance if you thought it right."

"Do you want a promise from me?"

"Yes, I do."

"What is it?"

"I know that without your approval, I have no hope. Even if Lucie loved me, she would never choose me if it hurt her father."

"If that is true, do you see what else it means?"

"I know that a word from you in favor of any suitor would decide the matter for her. For that reason, Doctor Manette," Darnay said firmly, "I would never ask you to speak for me."

"I am sure of that. Charles Darnay, love creates its own mysteries. My daughter Lucie is a mystery to me in this regard; I do not know if she loves you."

"May I ask, sir, if anyone else is seeking her love?"

"You have seen Mr. Carton here. Mr. Stryver is here too. If anyone is, it is one of them."

"Or both," said Darnay.

"I had not thought of both, and I think neither is likely. What is the promise you want from me?"

"It is that if Lucie ever tells you she loves me, you will tell her what I have said today, and that you believe me. I hope you think well enough of me not to speak against me. That is all I ask, and I will keep my part of the agreement."

"I give you the promise without any conditions," said the Doctor. "I believe your love is genuine, and that you want to bring us closer together, not separate us. If she ever tells me that you are necessary for her happiness, I will give her to you. If there were—Charles Darnay, if there were—"

The young man took his hand gratefully as the Doctor continued:

"—any reasons or fears against the man she loved—as long as he was not responsible for them—they would be forgotten for her sake. She is everything to me. She is worth more than all my past suffering. But this is idle talk."

The Doctor suddenly fell silent, his face turning blank. Darnay felt the Doctor's hand turn cold as it released his.

"You said something to me," Doctor Manette said, smiling suddenly. "What was it?"

Darnay was confused, but remembered his offer to share his own secret.

"I want to be completely honest with you, as you are with me. My name is not my real one, though it is similar to my mother's. I want to tell you my real name, and why I am in England."

"Stop!" said the Doctor.

"I want to tell you so there are no secrets between us."

"Stop!"

The Doctor put his hands over his ears, then placed them on Darnay's lips.

"Tell me only if Lucie accepts you. Tell me on your marriage morning. Do you promise?"

"I promise."

"Give me your hand. She will be home soon, and it is better she does not see us together tonight. Go, and God bless you!"

It was dark when Charles Darnay left, and an hour later when Lucie came home. She entered the room alone—Miss Pross having gone upstairs—and was surprised to find her father's chair empty.

"Father!" she called. "Father dear!"

There was no answer, but she heard a low hammering sound from his bedroom. She walked quietly to his door. She looked in. She ran back terrified, crying, "What shall I do! What shall I do!"

She hesitated only a moment. She went back, knocked on his door, and called him softly. The hammering stopped, and he came out. They walked up and down the room together for a long time.

She went to check on him in his sleep that night. He was sleeping heavily, and his shoemaking tools and unfinished work were on the table as usual.

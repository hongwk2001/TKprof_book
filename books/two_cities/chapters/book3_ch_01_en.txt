Book the Third--the Track of a Storm

CHAPTER I.
In Secret


The traveler made slow progress as he journeyed from England toward Paris in the autumn of 1792. Even if the fallen King of France were still on his throne, the bad roads, poor carriages, and weak horses would have delayed him. But the revolutionary times brought far worse obstacles. Every town gate and village tollhouse was guarded by a band of citizen-patriots holding loaded muskets, ready to shoot at any moment. They stopped everyone, cross-examined them, checked their papers, searched for their names on custom lists, and turned them back, sent them forward, or arrested them, depending on whatever their random judgment decided was best for the new Republic of Liberty, Equality, Fraternity, or Death.

After only a few miles in France, Charles Darnay realized that there was no turning back for him along these country roads until he was officially declared a good citizen in Paris. Whatever happened, he had to keep moving forward. Every small village he passed through and every gate that closed behind him felt like another iron door locking him away from England. The constant surveillance surrounded him so completely that he felt as if he were being carried to his destination in a cage.

This constant watchfulness stopped him dozens of times during each leg of the trip. Soldiers would ride after him to bring him back, ride ahead to wait for him, or ride alongside him to keep him under guard. He had been traveling through France for days when he finally went to bed exhausted in a small town on the highway, still a long way from Paris.

Only showing Gabelle's letter from the Abbaye Prison had allowed him to get this far. The trouble he faced at the guardhouse in this small town made him realize that his journey had reached a turning point. Therefore, he was not surprised when he was awakened in the middle of the night at the small inn where he was being kept until morning.

He was woken up by a nervous local official and three armed patriots wearing rough red caps, who sat right on his bed with pipes in their mouths.

“Emigrant,” the official said, “I am sending you to Paris under guard.”

“Citizen, I want nothing more than to get to Paris, though I could do without the guard.”

“Silence!” growled one of the men in a red cap, hitting the bed cover with the butt of his gun. “Quiet, aristocrat!”

“It is just as the patriot says,” the nervous official remarked. “You are an aristocrat, so you must have a guard—and you have to pay for it.”

“I have no choice,” said Charles Darnay.

“Choice! Listen to him!” cried the scowling patriot. “As if it isn't a favor to be protected from being hanged from a street lamp!”

“It is always as the patriot says,” the official observed. “Get up and get dressed, emigrant.”

Darnay did as he was told and was taken back to the guardhouse, where other patriots in red caps were smoking, drinking, and sleeping by a campfire. He paid a high price for his guard, and they set out together on the muddy, wet roads at three o'clock in the morning.

His guard consisted of two mounted patriots wearing red caps and ribbons showing the revolutionary colors, armed with muskets and swords, riding on either side of him.

Darnay controlled his own horse, but a loose rope was tied to his bridle, with the other end wrapped around the wrist of one of the guards. In this manner, they set out in the pouring rain, trotting over the bumpy town pavement and out onto the muddy roads. They traveled this way all the miles to Paris, changing only their horses and their speed.

They traveled by night, stopping an hour or two after dawn and hiding out until twilight fell. The guards were so poorly dressed that they wrapped straw around their bare legs and placed bundles of straw on their shoulders to keep the rain off. Despite the discomfort of traveling this way, and the danger of one guard being constantly drunk and carrying his gun carelessly, Darnay did not let himself panic. He reasoned that his specific case had not been heard yet, and the facts, which Gabelle could confirm, had not yet been presented.

But when they reached the town of Beauvais in the evening, when the streets were crowded with people, he had to admit that things looked very bad. An angry crowd gathered to watch him get off his horse at the station, and many voices shouted, “Down with the emigrant!”

He paused as he was about to get off his horse and, staying in the saddle as the safest place, said:

“An emigrant, my friends? Don't you see that I came back to France of my own free will?”

“You are a cursed emigrant,” cried a blacksmith, pushing through the crowd toward him with a hammer in his hand, “and you are a cursed aristocrat!”

The postmaster stepped between the blacksmith and Darnay's horse, saying calmly, “Leave him alone, leave him alone! He will be judged in Paris.”

“Judged!” the blacksmith repeated, swinging his hammer. “Yes! And condemned as a traitor!” The crowd roared in agreement.

Stopping the postmaster, who was trying to lead his horse into the yard (while the drunken guard watched calmly from his saddle, holding the rope wrapped around his wrist), Darnay spoke as loudly as he could:

“Friends, you are mistaken. I am not a traitor.”

“He lies!” cried the blacksmith. “He is a traitor under the new law. His life belongs to the people. His cursed life is not his own!”

Just as Darnay saw the crowd prepare to rush him, the postmaster pulled his horse into the yard, the guards followed close behind, and the postmaster shut and locked the creaky double gates. The blacksmith hit the gates with his hammer, and the crowd groaned in disappointment, but they did not try to break in.

“What is this new law the blacksmith mentioned?” Darnay asked the postmaster, after thanking him.

“It is a law to sell the property of all exiles.”

“When was it passed?”

“On the fourteenth.”

“The very day I left England!”

“Everyone says it is just the first of many laws. They say there will be others—if they aren't passed already—banishing all emigrants, and sentencing anyone who returns to death. That is why he said your life is not your own.”

“But those laws haven't actually been passed yet, have they?”

“How should I know!” the postmaster said, shrugging. “Maybe they have, or maybe they will. It is all the same. What can you do?”

They rested on some straw in a loft until the middle of the night, then rode on while the town was asleep. Among the strange changes that made this ride feel like a dream, the rarest thing seemed to be sleep. After riding for miles over lonely, dark roads, they would come across small villages that were completely lit up. In the middle of the night, they would see groups of villagers holding hands and dancing around a dry "Liberty Tree" or gathering to sing revolutionary songs. Fortunately, the people of Beauvais were sleeping, allowing them to pass through quietly into the cold, wet night. They rode past barren fields that had grown no food that year, ruined houses blackened by fire, and patrols of patriots who suddenly jumped out to stop them on the road.

At dawn, they finally arrived at the gates of Paris. The barrier was closed and heavily guarded when they approached.

“Where are this prisoner's papers?” demanded a stern official who came out of the guardhouse.

Stunned by the word "prisoner," Charles Darnay spoke up, explaining that he was a free traveler and a French citizen traveling with a guard that he had paid for because of the chaotic state of the country.

“Where,” the official repeated, completely ignoring him, “are this prisoner's papers?”

The drunken guard pulled the papers out of his cap and handed them over. Reading Gabelle's letter, the official looked surprised and stared closely at Darnay.

Without another word, the official went inside the guardroom, leaving them waiting on their horses outside the gate. During this tense wait, Darnay noticed that the gate was guarded by a mix of soldiers and patriots, with the patriots far outnumbering the soldiers. He saw that while it was easy for carts and peasants to enter the city, it was very difficult for anyone to leave. A large crowd of people, animals, and carriages waited to exit, but the security check was so strict that they could only trickle through. Some people knew they would have to wait so long that they lay down on the ground to sleep or smoke, while others chatted or walked around. Everyone, both men and women, wore red caps and revolutionary ribbons.

After Darnay had waited in his saddle for about half an hour, the official returned and ordered the guards to open the gate. He gave the two guards a receipt for Darnay and told Darnay to get off his horse. He did so, and the two guards took his tired horse, turned around, and rode back without entering Paris.

He followed his guide into a guardroom that smelled of cheap wine and tobacco, where soldiers and patriots in various states of sleep and drunkenness were lounging around. The light in the room, coming partly from dying oil lamps and partly from the cloudy morning, was dim and gloomy. Several record books lay open on a desk, where a rough, dark-looking officer sat in charge.

“Citizen Defarge,” the officer said to Darnay's guide, picking up a slip of paper. “Is this the emigrant Evremonde?”

“This is the man.”

“Your age, Evremonde?”

“Thirty-seven.”

“Married, Evremonde?”

“Yes.”

“Where were you married?”

“In England.”

“Of course. Where is your wife, Evremonde?”

“In England.”

“Of course. You are sent, Evremonde, to the prison of La Force.”

“Good heavens!” Darnay exclaimed. “Under what law, and for what crime?”

The officer looked up from his paper for a moment.

“We have new laws, Evremonde, and new crimes, since you were last here,” he said with a cold smile, and continued writing.

“Please understand that I came here voluntarily, responding to the letter of a fellow countryman which you have there. All I want is the chance to explain my case. Is that not my right?”

“Emigrants have no rights, Evremonde,” was the flat reply. The officer finished writing, read it over, dried the ink with sand, and handed the paper to Defarge, saying, “In secret.”

Defarge gestured with the paper that the prisoner had to follow him. Darnay obeyed, escorted by two armed patriots.

“Are you the one,” Defarge asked quietly as they walked down the steps into Paris, “who married the daughter of Doctor Manette, who was once a prisoner in the Bastille?”

“Yes,” Darnay replied, looking at him in surprise.

“My name is Defarge. I own a wine shop in the Saint Antoine district. Perhaps you have heard of me.”

“My wife came to your shop to find her father? Yes!”

The word “wife” seemed to upset Defarge, who said with sudden anger, “In the name of that sharp new machine called the Guillotine, why on earth did you come back to France?”

“You heard me explain why just a minute ago. Don't you believe it is the truth?”

“It is a bad truth for you,” Defarge said, frowning and looking straight ahead.

“I am completely lost here. Everything is so different, so sudden, and so unfair. Will you help me a little?”

“No.” Defarge spoke without looking at him.

“Will you answer just one question?”

“Maybe, depending on what it is. Ask it.”

“In this prison where I am being sent so unfairly, will I be allowed to communicate with the outside world?”

“You will see.”

“I won't be buried there forever without any chance to explain my case, will I?”

“You will see. But what of it? Many others have been buried in worse prisons before now.”

“But never by me, Citizen Defarge.”

Defarge gave him a dark look and kept walking in silence. As Defarge remained silent, Darnay lost hope of changing his mind. So, he quickly added:

“It is extremely important—and you must know how important it is—that I let Mr. Lorry of Tellson's Bank, who is currently in Paris, know that I have been put in La Force prison. Will you get that message to him?”

“I will do nothing for you,” Defarge replied stubbornly. “My duty is to my country and the People. I am sworn to serve them against you. I will do nothing for you.”

Darnay knew it was useless to beg, and his pride was hurt as well. As they walked in silence, he noticed that the citizens were completely used to seeing prisoners led through the streets. Even children barely looked at him. A few people turned to look, and some shook their fists at him because he was an aristocrat, but otherwise, seeing a well-dressed man going to prison was as ordinary as seeing a worker going to a job. In a narrow, dirty street, an angry speaker on a stool was shouting to a crowd about the crimes of the king and the royal family. The few words Darnay caught made him realize for the first time that the king had been imprisoned and that all foreign ambassadors had left Paris. He had heard nothing on his journey except at Beauvais, because his guards and the security checks had kept him completely isolated.

He knew now that he was in far greater danger than he had realized when leaving England, and that the threats around him were growing quickly. He admitted to himself that he would not have made this trip if he had known what would happen. Yet, his fears were not as dark as they might seem to us looking back. The future was scary, but it was still unknown, and its mystery left room for hope. He had no idea that a horrible massacre, lasting days and nights, would soon stain the city with blood. The name of the Guillotine was barely known to him or to most people. The terrible acts that were about to happen were probably not even imagined yet by the people who would commit them. How could such horrors exist in the mind of a gentle man?

He expected unfair treatment, hardships, and the painful separation from his wife and child, but he did not fear anything worse than that. With these heavy thoughts, he arrived at the gates of La Force prison.

A man with a red, swollen face opened the small gate, and Defarge announced, “The Emigrant Evremonde.”

“What the devil! How many more of them are coming!” exclaimed the bloated man.

Defarge took his receipt without answering the jailer, and left with the two patriots.

“What the devil, I say again!” the jailer said to his wife. “How many more!”

Having no answer, his wife just said, “We must be patient, my dear!” Three assistant jailers who came in when she rang a bell agreed with her, and one added, “For the love of Liberty!”—which sounded very out of place in that grim prison.

La Force was a dark, filthy, and gloomy prison that smelled terribly of sweaty, locked-up bodies. It is amazing how quickly the bad smell of crowded prisoners develops in places that are not kept clean.

“In secret too,” grumbled the jailer, looking at the paper. “As if my prison wasn't already packed to bursting!”

He angrily filed the paper, and Darnay was left waiting for half an hour. Darnay walked back and forth in the arched stone room or rested on a stone bench, while the chief jailer and his assistants stared at him to memorize his face.

“Come!” the chief jailer said finally, taking his keys. “Come with me, emigrant.”

Darnay followed him through the dark prison corridors and up staircases, with many heavy doors slamming and locking behind them. Finally, they entered a large, low room with arched ceilings that was crowded with male and female prisoners. The women sat at a long table reading, writing, knitting, sewing, and embroidering, while most of the men stood behind their chairs or walked around the room.

Thinking that prisoners must be criminals, Darnay shrank back at first. But the strangest part of his journey was that all of them stood up to welcome him with the most polite, elegant manners of high society.

These refined manners looked so strange in the dark, dirty prison that Darnay felt as if he were standing among the dead. They seemed like ghosts—the ghost of beauty, the ghost of elegance, the ghost of pride, the ghost of humor, the ghost of youth, and the ghost of old age—all waiting to be sent away from this miserable shore, looking at him with eyes changed by their imprisonment.

He stood frozen. The rough jailers looked incredibly crude next to the sad mothers, young girls, and elegant women in the room. This complete reversal of normal life made the scene feel even more unreal. Surely they were all ghosts, and his long, strange journey was just a fever dream that had brought him to the underworld.

“In the name of your fellow prisoners,” said an elegant gentleman, stepping forward, “I have the honor of welcoming you to La Force, and of offering our sympathy for the misfortune that brought you here. We hope you will be released soon. It would be rude to ask elsewhere, but here it is normal: what is your name and background?”

Charles Darnay shook off his shock and gave them his name and details as politely as he could.

“I hope,” the gentleman said, watching the chief jailer walk across the room, “that you are not placed 'in secret'?”

“I don't know what that means, but I heard them use those words.”

“Ah, what a pity! We are so sorry to hear that. But take courage; several of us were kept in solitary confinement at first, and it didn't last long.” Then he announced to the room, raising his voice, “I regret to inform everyone—he is in secret.”

A murmur of sympathy spread through the room as Darnay walked toward a barred door where the jailer was waiting. Many voices, especially the gentle voices of the women, offered him encouragement. He turned at the door to thank them, but the jailer shut the gate, and the ghostly crowd vanished from his sight forever.

The door opened to a stone staircase going up. After climbing forty steps (which Darnay had already counted), the jailer opened a low black door, and they entered a solitary cell. It was cold and damp, but not completely dark.

“This is yours,” said the jailer.

“Why am I locked up alone?”

“How should I know!”

“Can I buy a pen, ink, and paper?”

“Those are not my orders. Someone will visit you later, and you can ask then. For now, you can buy food and nothing else.”

The cell contained a chair, a table, and a straw mattress. As the jailer inspected the room and the walls before leaving, Darnay leaned against the wall and had a strange thought: the jailer's swollen face and body made him look like a drowned corpse filled with water. Once the jailer left, Darnay thought, “Now I am left here as if I were dead.” Looking down at the mattress, he felt sick and thought, “And the bugs crawling here are like the worms that eat a body after death.”

“Five paces by four and a half, five paces by four and a half, five paces by four and a half.” The prisoner paced back and forth in his cell, measuring the room over and over, while the distant roar of the city sounded like muffled drums mixed with shouting voices. “He made shoes, he made shoes, he made shoes.” Darnay measured the cell again, walking faster to try to block out that repetitive thought. “The ghosts who vanished when the gate shut. There was one woman dressed in black, leaning near a window, with light shining on her golden hair, and she looked like... Let's ride on again, for God's sake, through the lit-up villages where everyone is awake! He made shoes, he made shoes, he made shoes. Five paces by four and a half.” With these random thoughts spinning in his mind, the prisoner walked faster and faster, stubbornly counting his steps. The roar of the city outside still sounded like muffled drums, but now he imagined hearing the cries of voices he knew in the noise.

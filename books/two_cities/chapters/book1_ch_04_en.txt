CHAPTER IV.

The Preparation

When the mail coach finally arrived in Dover later that morning, the chief waiter at the Royal George Hotel opened the coach door as usual. He did so with quite a bit of ceremony, for traveling by mail coach from London in the winter was a major accomplishment that called for congratulating any adventurous traveler.

By then, there was only one adventurous traveler left to congratulate: the other two had already been dropped off at their destinations along the way. The musty interior of the coach—with its damp, dirty straw, unpleasant smell, and dark atmosphere—looked a lot like a large dog kennel. Mr. Lorry shook himself free of the straw. He was wrapped in a messy coat, with a floppy hat and muddy legs. He looked rather like a large dog stepping out of his kennel.

"Will there be a ferry to Calais tomorrow, waiter?"

"Yes, sir, if the weather stays good and the wind is fair. The tide will be perfect around two in the afternoon, sir. Would you like a room, sir?"

"I won't go to bed until tonight; but I need a bedroom and a barber."

"And then breakfast, sir? Yes, sir. Right this way, please. Show the gentleman to the Concord room! Carry his bag and hot water to Concord. Take off his boots in Concord. (You'll find a nice coal fire there, sir.) Bring the barber to Concord. Hurry up now, get moving on Concord!"

The Concord bedroom was always given to mail coach passengers. Those passengers were always bundled up from head to toe when they arrived. So the room had a funny fascination for the staff of the Royal George Hotel. Only one type of traveler ever went in. But all sorts of different men came back out. As a result, another waiter, two porters, several maids, and the landlady all happened to be lingering in the hallway when a sixty-year-old gentleman walked past. He was formally dressed in a well-worn but very neat brown suit, with large square cuffs and big pocket flaps.

The dining room had no other guests that morning except for the gentleman in brown. His breakfast table was set in front of the fireplace. As he sat there with the firelight shining on him, waiting for his food, he sat so still that he could have been posing for a portrait.

He looked very neat and systematic. He sat with a hand on each knee. Under his vest, a loud watch ticked solemnly, as if competing with the crackling fire. He had good-looking legs and was quietly proud of them. His brown stockings fit smoothly and were made of fine material. His shoes and buckles were simple but neat. He wore an odd, small, tight, light-colored wig that sat very close to his head. It looked less like real hair and more like something spun from silk or glass thread. His shirt was as white as the foam on the breaking waves outside. His face was lit up under that old-fashioned wig by a pair of bright, lively eyes. It must have taken him years of discipline to train his face into the cold, serious expression required at Tellson's Bank. Still, he had a healthy color in his cheeks, and his face, though wrinkled, showed very few signs of worry.

Looking more than ever like someone posing for a painting, Mr. Lorry drifted off to sleep. The arrival of his breakfast woke him up, and he told the waiter as he pulled up his chair:

"I want a room prepared for a young lady who might arrive at any time today. She may ask for Mr. Jarvis Lorry, or she might just ask for a gentleman from Tellson's Bank. Please let me know when she arrives."

"Yes, sir. Tellson's Bank in London, sir?"

"Yes."

"Yes, sir. We often have the honor of hosting your gentlemen as they travel back and forth between London and Paris, sir. There's a huge amount of travel done by the staff of Tellson and Company."

"Yes. We are very much a French firm, as well as an English one."

"Yes, sir. You don't travel back and forth like that much yourself, I take it, sir?"

"Not in recent years. It has been fifteen years since we—since I—last returned from France."

"Really, sir? That was before my time here, sir. Before any of our staff worked here. The George Hotel was under different management back then, sir."

"I believe so."

"But I would bet a good amount, sir, that a company like Tellson and Company was doing very well fifty years ago, let alone fifteen?"

"You could triple that and say a hundred and fifty, and still be close to the truth."

"Is that so, sir!"

The waiter stepped back from the table in wide-eyed surprise. He moved his napkin from his right arm to his left, stood in a relaxed posture, and watched the guest eat and drink. It was the ancient custom of waiters everywhere.

When Mr. Lorry finished breakfast, he went out for a walk on the beach. The tiny, narrow, crooked town of Dover seemed to hide from the shore, burying its head into the white chalk cliffs like a sea ostrich. The beach was a barren waste of stones and rocks. The sea did whatever it wanted, which was mostly destruction. It crashed against the town and thundered against the cliffs. The air in the streets smelled so strongly of fish that you might have thought the sick fish came up to town to breathe it, the same way sick humans went down to bathe in the sea. There was a little fishing in the harbor, and a lot of walking around at night. Small shopkeepers who never seemed to do any actual business somehow made huge fortunes.

As the afternoon faded, the air—which had been clear enough at times to spot the coast of France—grew thick with mist and fog again, and Mr. Lorry’s thoughts seemed to cloud over as well. When it got dark and he sat in front of the fireplace waiting for dinner, just as he had waited for breakfast, his mind was busy digging, digging, digging in the glowing red coals.

Mr. Lorry had been relaxing for a long time. He had just poured the last glass from his bottle with a look of complete satisfaction. He was about to drink it when the clatter of wheels came up the narrow street and rumbled into the inn yard.

He set his glass down without drinking. "This is the young lady!" he said.

A few minutes later, the waiter came in to announce that Miss Manette had arrived from London and would be happy to see the gentleman from Tellson’s.

"So soon?"

Miss Manette had eaten on the road and didn't need anything now, and she was very eager to see the gentleman from Tellson’s right away, if it was convenient for him.

The gentleman from Tellson's finished his glass with quiet desperation, adjusted his odd little wig over his ears, and followed the waiter to Miss Manette's room. It was a large, dark room. The furniture was black horsehair. The tables were heavy and dark. They had been polished so many times that the two tall candles in the middle of the room were reflected back darkly on every surface. The candles looked as if they were buried in deep graves of black wood, waiting to be dug out.

The room was so dark that Mr. Lorry, walking carefully over the worn carpet, assumed at first that Miss Manette was in another room. But as he passed the two tall candles, he saw a young lady of about seventeen standing to greet him. She wore a riding cloak and held her straw sunhat by its ribbon. He looked at her: short, slender, and pretty. Golden hair. Blue eyes looking at him searchingly. A smooth young forehead that had a unique way of wrinkling into a frown—not quite confusion, not quite wonder, not quite alarm, not quite deep focus, but all four at once. The moment he saw it, he remembered a child he had once carried in his arms. It was years ago, crossing this very Channel, on a cold day when hail fell heavily and the sea was rough. The memory faded quickly. He made his formal bow to Miss Manette.

"Please take a seat, sir," she said in a clear, pleasant young voice. It had a slight foreign accent, but only a very little.

"I kiss your hand, miss," said Mr. Lorry, using the old-fashioned manners of his younger days, making another formal bow before sitting down.

"I received a letter from the bank yesterday, sir, informing me that some information—or discovery—"

"The exact word doesn't matter, miss; either one works."

"—regarding the small property of my poor father, whom I never saw because he died so long ago—"

Mr. Lorry shifted in his chair and looked uncomfortably at the mirror's strange carvings. As if *they* had any help to offer anyone in their ridiculous baskets!

"—makes it necessary for me to go to Paris, to speak with a representative of the bank who was kind enough to be sent there for this purpose."

"That would be me."

"So I was told, sir."

She curtsied to him (which is what young ladies did in those days), politely showing that she respected him as someone older and wiser. He bowed back to her again.

"I replied to the bank, sir, that since those who advise me think it necessary for me to go to France, and since I am an orphan and have no friends to go with me, I would be very grateful to travel under that gentleman's protection. The gentleman had already left London, but I believe a messenger was sent after him to ask him to wait for me here."

"I was happy," said Mr. Lorry, "to be given this responsibility. I will be even happier to carry it out."

"Sir, thank you. I am very grateful. The bank told me that the gentleman would explain the details of this business to me, and that I should prepare myself for some surprising news. I have done my best to prepare, and naturally, I am very anxious to know what it is."

"Naturally," said Mr. Lorry. "Yes—I—"

After a pause, he adjusted his tight light wig over his ears again and said, "It is very difficult to begin."

He didn't start right away, but hesitated and looked at her. Her young forehead wrinkled in that unique way—which was pretty and characteristic of her—and she raised her hand, as if automatically trying to catch or stop a passing shadow.

"Are you a complete stranger to me, sir?"

"Aren't I?" Mr. Lorry opened his hands and spread them with a polite smile.

The frown between her eyebrows and just above her delicate nose deepened as she sat down thoughtfully in the chair she had been standing next to. He watched her as she sat thinking, and the moment she looked up at him again, he continued:

"Since England is your adopted home, I assume I should address you as an English lady, Miss Manette?"

"If you please, sir."

"Miss Manette, I am a businessman. I have a business task to complete. As you listen to it, don't think of me as anything more than a talking machine—really, I'm not much else. With your permission, I will tell you the story of one of our clients."

"A story?"

He pretended to misunderstand why she repeated the word, adding quickly, "Yes, a client. In banking, we usually call our contacts our clients. He was a French gentleman, a man of science, highly educated—a Doctor."

"Not from Beauvais?"

"Why, yes, from Beauvais. Just like Monsieur Manette, your father, the gentleman was from Beauvais. Like your father, he was well-known in Paris. I had the honor of knowing him there. Our relationship was professional, but very confidential. At that time, I was working in our French branch, and had been there for—oh, twenty years."

"At that time—may I ask, what time was that, sir?"

"I am speaking of twenty years ago, miss. He married an English lady, and I was one of the trustees of his estate. His financial affairs, like those of many other French gentlemen, were kept entirely in Tellson's hands. In the same way, I have acted as a trustee in some form for dozens of our clients. These are purely business relationships, miss; there is no personal friendship in them, no special interest, nothing emotional. I have moved from one client to another throughout my career just as I move from one account to another during my workday. In short, I have no feelings; I am just a machine. To continue—"

"But this is my father's story, sir." She paused. Her forehead wrinkled in that intense, searching way. "And I am starting to think that when I was left an orphan—because my mother only outlived my father by two years—it was you who brought me to England. I am almost sure it was you."

Mr. Lorry took her small, hesitating hand as she reached out to him trustingly, and kissed it with polite ceremony. He then led the young lady back to her chair. He stood behind the chair, holding the back of it with his left hand. With his right hand he rubbed his chin, adjusted his wig, or gestured as he spoke. He stood looking down at her face. She sat looking up at him.

"Miss Manette, it *was* me. And you will see how true it is that I have no feelings—and that all my relationships are strictly professional—when you realize that I haven't seen you since that day. No; you have been a ward of Tellson's Bank ever since, and I have been busy with other bank business. Feelings! I have no time for them, no opportunity for them. I spend my whole life, miss, turning a giant money-grinding machine."

After this strange description of his daily work, Mr. Lorry flattened his light wig onto his head with both hands. It was completely unnecessary—nothing could possibly be flatter than it already was. Then he returned to his previous posture.

"So far, miss, as you noted, this is the story of your late father. Now comes the difference. If your father had not died when he did—don't be frightened! Why do you jump like that?"

She did jump, startled. And she grabbed his wrist with both hands.

"Please," said Mr. Lorry in a calming voice, placing his left hand over her trembling fingers that held him so tightly. "Please calm yourself—remember, this is just a matter of business. As I was saying—"

The look on her face shook him so much that he stopped, hesitated, and started over:

"As I was saying: suppose Monsieur Manette did not die. Suppose he simply vanished one day, silently and without a trace. Suppose he was taken away. It would be easy enough to guess which terrible place he had been sent to, even if no one could prove it. Suppose he had an enemy—someone with the power to sign blank arrest warrants and lock anyone away in a silent prison forever. That power was so feared in France that even the bravest people refused to whisper about it. Suppose his wife went to the king, the queen, the court, and the church, begging for any news of her husband. All of that begging would come to nothing. If all of this were true, then the story of your father would be the story of this unfortunate gentleman, the Doctor of Beauvais."

"I beg you to tell me more, sir."

"I will. I am about to. Can you handle it?"

"I can bear anything except the uncertainty you are keeping me in right now."

"You speak calmly, and you *are* calm. That’s good!" (Though he looked less confident than his words suggested.) "A matter of business. Look at it as a matter of business—work that must be done. Now, if this doctor's wife, though she was a woman of great courage and strength, had suffered so intensely from this before her baby was born—"

"The baby was a daughter, sir."

"A daughter. A—a matter of business—don't be upset. Miss, if the poor lady had suffered so intensely before her baby was born that she decided to save her child from inheriting any of the agony she had felt, by raising her to believe that her father was dead—No, don't kneel! In Heaven's name, why are you kneeling to me?"

"Listen, this is just work stuff. You're freaking me out, and I can't do my job if I'm stressed. Let's just keep it together. If you could tell me basic math, like nine times nine or how many shillings in twenty pounds, that'd be great. It would make me feel better about your head being in the game."

Without directly answering his odd request, she sat very still after he gently helped her up, and the hands holding his wrists were much steadier than before. This helped calm Mr. Jarvis Lorry down.

"That's right, that's right. Courage! Business! You have important, useful work ahead of you. Miss Manette, your mother decided to raise you this way. When she died—brokenhearted, I believe, having never stopped searching for your father—she left you, at two years old, to grow up healthy, beautiful, and happy. She spared you the dark cloud of wondering whether your father died quickly in prison or wasted away there over many long years."

As he spoke, he looked down with pity and admiration at her flowing golden hair, as if imagining that it could have already been turned gray by worry.

"You know that your parents had very little money, and that what they did have was secured for your mother and you. There has been no new discovery of money or property; but—"

He felt her grip tighten on his wrist, and he stopped. The expression on her forehead, which had caught his attention before and was now frozen, had deepened into a look of pain and horror.

"But he has been—been found. He is alive. Highly changed, most likely; perhaps almost a complete wreck, though we will hope for the best. Still, he is alive. Your father has been taken to the home of an old servant in Paris, and we are going there: I, to identify him if I can; you, to restore him to life, love, work, rest, and comfort."

A shiver ran through her body, and from her into him. She said in a low, clear, awestruck voice, as if speaking in a dream:

"I am going to see his ghost! It will be his ghost—not him!"

Mr. Lorry gently rubbed the hands holding his arm. "There, there, there! Look, now! You know the best and the worst of it now. You are on your way to see the poor, wronged gentleman, and after a smooth sea voyage and a land journey, you will soon be by his side."

She repeated in the same tone, sinking to a whisper, "I have been free, I have been happy, yet his ghost has never haunted me!"

"Only one more thing," said Mr. Lorry, emphasizing the point to keep her attention. "He was found under another name. His true name had been forgotten or hidden long ago. It would be pointless to ask which. It would be even more pointless to find out whether he was deliberately kept prisoner or simply forgotten. It would be dangerous to ask either question now. The safest thing is to say nothing about this anywhere, to anyone, and to get him out of France. Even I, safe as an Englishman, and even Tellson's Bank, as important as we are to French finance, avoid all talk of it. I carry not a single scrap of paper mentioning this mission. My credentials, notes, and records are summed up in one single phrase: 'Recalled to Life.' But what's wrong? She isn't hearing a word! Miss Manette!"

Completely still and silent, not even leaning back in her chair, she sat under his touch, totally unconscious. Her eyes were open and fixed on him, and that worried expression looked as if it were carved or branded into her forehead. She held his arm so tightly that he was afraid to pull away in case he hurt her, so he called out loudly for help without moving.

A wild-looking woman came running into the room ahead of the hotel servants. Even in his panic, Mr. Lorry noticed her: very red complexion, red hair, a strangely tight dress, and an enormous hat that looked like a wooden bucket or a giant block of Stilton cheese. She quickly solved the problem of freeing his arm by planting a strong, muscular hand flat on his chest and shoving him backwards into the wall.

("I really think this must be a man!" was Mr. Lorry's breathless thought as he hit the wall.)

"Look at all of you!" this woman yelled at the hotel servants. "Why don't you go and fetch help, instead of standing there staring at me? I'm not much to look at, am I? Why don't you go get things? I'll show you what's what if you don't bring smelling salts, cold water, and vinegar right now!"

The servants quickly scattered to get these items, and she gently laid the unconscious girl on a sofa, tending to her with great skill and care. She called her "my precious!" and "my sweet bird!" and spread her golden hair over her shoulders with pride and tenderness.

"And you in brown!" she said, turning angrily to Mr. Lorry. "Couldn't you tell her what you had to say without scaring her to death? Look at her, with her pretty pale face and cold hands. Do you call *that* being a banker?"

Mr. Lorry was so embarrassed by this hard-to-answer question that he could only watch quietly from a distance. Meanwhile, the strong woman chased away the hotel servants. She threatened to "let them know" what would happen if they stayed and kept staring. Then she gently helped the young lady recover, encouraging her to lay her head on her shoulder.

"I hope she will be okay now," said Mr. Lorry.

"No thanks to you in brown, if she is. My darling girl!"

"I hope," said Mr. Lorry, after another quiet pause, "that you will be going with Miss Manette to France?"

"As if!" replied the strong woman. "If I was ever meant to cross the ocean, do you think God would have had me born on an island?"

This being another question that was hard to answer, Mr. Jarvis Lorry stepped away to think about it.

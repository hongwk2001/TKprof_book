CHAPTER XXI.
Echoing Footsteps

The corner where the Doctor lived was famous for its echoes. Always busy keeping her husband, father, and family happy, Lucie sat in the quiet house listening to the echoes of the passing years.

At first, though she was a perfectly happy young wife, she would occasionally drop her work as her eyes filled with tears. There was a faint sound coming in the echoes—a light, distant step—that stirred her heart. She felt a mix of hopes and fears: hopes for a child she did not yet know, and fears that she might not survive to enjoy that happiness. In those moments, she imagined the sound of footsteps at her own early grave, and wept for the husband who would be left behind.

But that time passed, and her daughter, little Lucie, lay on her bosom. Soon, the echoes brought the sound of tiny feet and baby talk. No matter how loud other echoes grew, the young mother could always hear these. The quiet house was soon filled with a child’s laughter, bringing her sacred joy.

Lucie heard only friendly and peaceful sounds in the echoes. Her husband’s step was strong and successful; her father’s step was firm. Even Miss Pross joined in the noise, running around the garden under the plane tree.

Even when there were sounds of sadness, they were not harsh. When a little golden-haired boy—who looked just like her—lay dying on his pillow, he said with a smile: "Dear papa and mama, I am sorry to leave you both and my sister, but I must go!" The tears on his mother's cheek were not purely of agony as his spirit left. Children are at peace in heaven.

Thus, the memory of an angel was blended with the other echoes. The wind blowing over the little garden grave was audible to Lucie like the gentle sound of a summer sea, while little Lucie played at her feet, speaking both French and English.

The echoes rarely brought the sound of Sydney Carton’s steps. Only about six times a year, he used his privilege to visit uninvited, sitting with them through the evening as he used to do. He never came after drinking. And the echoes whispered something about him that true echoes have whispered for ages.

No man ever loved a woman, lost her, and watched her become a wife and mother with a pure mind, without her children feeling a strange sympathy and pity for him. Little Lucie was always quick to hug Carton, and the dying boy had said at the end: "Poor Carton! Kiss him for me!"

Mr. Stryver pushed his way through the legal world like a powerful boat, dragging Carton behind him like a towed barge. Carton’s life was swamped and difficult. But habit made it the life he led, and he never thought of trying to become a leader himself. Stryver was now rich, having married a wealthy widow with three dull sons.

Stryver had brought these three boys to Soho like sheep, offering them as pupils to Darnay, saying proudly: "Here are three pupils to help pay for your marriage, Darnay!" Darnay's polite refusal had made Stryver furious. He warned the boys to beware of the pride of beggars like that tutor. He also bragged to his wife that Lucie had once tried to "trap" him into marriage, but that he had been too clever to be caught. His colleagues excused his lies, saying he had told them so often he believed them himself.

These were the echoes Lucie listened to, sometimes sad and sometimes amused, until her daughter was six years old. The echoes of her child's steps, her active father's steps, and her husband's steps were music to her. Her father often told her he found her even more devoted now that she was married, and her husband asked: "What is your secret, my darling, for taking care of all of us without ever seeming hurried?"

But there were other echoes, coming from a distance, that rumbled threateningly. Around little Lucie’s sixth birthday, they began to sound like a great storm rising in France.

On a night in mid-July 1789, Mr. Lorry came in late from the bank and sat with Lucie and Darnay by the window. It was a hot, wild night, reminding them of the storm years before.

"I thought I would have to sleep at the bank," Mr. Lorry said, pushing back his wig. "We have been so busy all day. There is such worry in Paris that customers are sending their property to us in England as fast as they can."

"That looks bad," Darnay said.

"Yes, but people are unreasonable. Some of us at Tellson’s are getting old and don't want to be disturbed without good reason."

"Still," Darnay said, "you know how dangerous the situation in France is."

"I know," Mr. Lorry agreed, grumbling. "Where is Manette?"

"Here I am," the Doctor said, entering.

"I am glad you are home; the worries at the bank have made me nervous. You aren't going out, I hope?"

"No, I will play backgammon with you if you like," the Doctor said.

"I don't think I can play tonight; I am too tired. Is there still tea, Lucie?"

"Yes, we kept it for you."

"Thank you. The child is in bed?"

"And sleeping soundly."

"That’s right. Everything is safe and well here, thank God. But I am not as young as I was! Let us sit quietly and listen to the echoes."

"It was just a fancy, not a theory," Lucie said.

"A fancy, then," Mr. Lorry said, patting her hand. "But they are very loud tonight. Listen!"

They were dangerous, angry footsteps that were about to force their way into their lives—footsteps of a mob raging in Saint Antoine far away.

That morning, Saint Antoine was a massive crowd of poor people, armed with steel blades and bayonets shining in the sun. A loud roar rose from the crowd, and a forest of naked arms reached for weapons.

Muskets, cartridges, powder, iron bars, knives, and axes were distributed. People with no weapons used their bare hands to tear stones and bricks from the walls. Every heart in Saint Antoine was at a fever pitch. Everyone held their life as worthless, ready to sacrifice it.

All this anger centered around Defarge’s wine shop. Defarge, covered in gunpowder and sweat, was directing the crowd, handing out weapons and leading the attack.

"Stay near me, Jacques Three!" Defarge shouted. "Jacques One and Two, lead the patriots. Where is my wife?"

"Here I am!" madame said, calm as ever, but not knitting today. She held an axe in her hand, with a pistol and a knife in her belt.

"Where are you going, wife?"

"With you for now. I will lead the women soon."

"Come then!" Defarge shouted. "We are ready! To the Bastille!"

With a roar from the crowd, the living sea rose and rushed toward the great prison. Bells rang and drums beat as the attack began.

Deep ditches, double drawbridges, massive stone walls, eight great towers, cannons, muskets, fire, and smoke. Defarge worked like a soldier at a cannon for two hours.

One drawbridge fell! "Work, comrades, work! In the name of God or the Devil, work!" Defarge shouted, his cannon hot from firing.

"To me, women!" his wife cried. "We can kill as well as the men!" A crowd of armed, starving women followed her.

Cannons, muskets, fire, and smoke, but the walls and towers still stood. The wounded fell, weapons flashed, and torches burned. The mob roared, but the fortress held, and Defarge kept firing for four hours.

A white flag appeared from the fortress, signaling a surrender. The crowd rushed over the lowered drawbridge, past the stone walls, into the eight surrendered towers.

The force of the crowd carried Defarge into the outer courtyard of the Bastille. He stood against a wall, looking around. Jacques Three was beside him, and Madame Defarge was leading her women, knife in hand. The noise and confusion were deafening.

"The prisoners!"

"The records!"

"The secret cells!"

"The torture instruments!"

The crowd shouted for the prisoners. Defarge grabbed a gray-haired guard carrying a torch and pulled him against the wall.

"Show me the North Tower! Quick!" Defarge ordered.

"I will," the guard said, "but no one is there."

"What does 'One Hundred and Five, North Tower' mean? Quick!"

"It is a cell, monsieur."

"Show it to me!"

"This way."

Jacques Three followed them, disappointed that there was no bloodshed yet. They walked through dark vaults, past prison cages, down stone steps, and up into a tower. The thick walls muffled the noise of the crowd outside.

The guard stopped at a low door, unlocked it, and they entered.

"One hundred and five, North Tower!"

There was a small, grated window high in the wall, a barred chimney, wood ashes on the hearth, a stool, a table, and a straw bed. An iron ring was fixed in the blackened wall.

"Pass the torch along the walls so I can see," Defarge said.

The guard obeyed, and Defarge followed the light.

"Stop! Look here, Jacques!"

"A. M.!" Jacques Three croaked.

"Alexandre Manette," Defarge whispered, tracing the letters with his dusty finger. "And here he wrote 'a poor physician.' He must have carved this calendar on the stone. Give me that crowbar!"

Defarge took the crowbar and smashed the old stool and table to pieces.

"Hold the light higher!" he ordered the guard. "Search the fragments, Jacques. Here is my knife; rip open the straw mattress. Hold the light higher!"

Defarge crawled onto the hearth, looking up the chimney and prying at the iron grate with the crowbar. Mortar and dust fell down. He reached into a crevice in the chimney, searching carefully.

"Nothing in the wood or the straw, Jacques?"

"Nothing."

"Gather them in the middle of the cell and burn them!"

The guard lit the pile, which burned hot. They left it burning and returned to the courtyard, where the noise of the crowd hit them again.

The crowd was searching for Defarge. Saint Antoine wanted the wine shop keeper to guard the prison governor who had shot at the people. They wanted to take him to the City Hall for judgment, ensuring he did not escape.

In the middle of the shouting crowd around the gray-coated governor, Madame Defarge stood immovable. "There is my husband!" she shouted, pointing to Defarge. She stayed close to the governor as they dragged him through the streets. When the crowd began hitting him, she was right beside him. When he fell dead, she put her foot on his neck and cut off his head with her knife.

Saint Antoine began hanging its enemies from the street lamps. The governor's body lay on the steps of the City Hall, and his blood stained Madame Defarge's shoe. The crowd rushed on.

It was a dangerous, angry sea of people, hardened by suffering and seeking vengeance.

In that ocean of faces, two groups of seven faces stood out. Seven released prisoners were carried on the shoulders of the crowd, looking confused and amazed. Higher up, seven dead heads on pikes were carried, their eyelids closed. They looked like witnesses waiting to say to their captors, "You did this!"

Seven prisoners released, seven heads on pikes, the keys of the Bastille, and the records of dead prisoners—these were the sights in the streets of Paris in mid-July 1789. May these dangerous, bloody feet stay far from Lucie Darnay's life! For once stained in blood, they are not easily made clean.

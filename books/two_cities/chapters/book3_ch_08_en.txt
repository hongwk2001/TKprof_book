CHAPTER VIII.
A Hand at Cards


Happily unaware of the new disaster at home, Miss Pross made her way through the narrow streets and crossed the river by the Pont-Neuf bridge, counting in her head the essential things she had to buy. Jerry Cruncher walked at her side carrying the basket. They both looked left and right into the shops they passed, kept a careful eye out for any crowds, and took side paths to avoid excited groups of talkers. It was a cold evening. The misty river was lit up with bright lights and filled with the harsh noise of metalworking barges, where blacksmiths were making guns for the Republic's army. Woe to anyone who crossed _that_ army or got a promotion they didn't deserve! It would have been better for him if his beard had never grown, for the Guillotine would cut his head off.

After buying a few groceries and some lamp oil, Miss Pross remembered they needed wine. After looking into several wine shops, she stopped at one called "The Good Republican Brutus of Antiquity" near the National Palace (which used to be the Tuileries Palace). The place looked cleaner and quieter than the other shops they had passed. Although it had some people in red caps inside, it didn't look as wild as the others. Asking Jerry, and finding that he agreed, Miss Pross went inside, accompanied by her guard.

The room was thick with smoky lamplight. Customers sat smoking pipes and fiddling with worn cards and yellow dominoes. A shirtless, soot-covered worker read a newspaper aloud to a small crowd. Weapons lay scattered around the tables. Two or three customers were slumped asleep, looking like sleeping bears in their heavy black coats. Taking all of this in, the two English customers walked up to the counter and showed what they wanted.

As their wine was being poured, a man in the corner said goodbye to his friend and got up to leave. To walk out, he had to face Miss Pross. The moment she saw his face, she screamed and clapped her hands.

Instantly, everyone in the shop stood up. They assumed someone had been murdered over a political argument, as that was a common occurrence. Everyone looked around for a body, but they only saw a man and a woman staring at each other. The man looked like a typical French Republican, while the woman was clearly English.

The customers in the shop started shouting and talking rapidly, which sounded like a foreign language to Miss Pross and Jerry. But the two were too surprised to pay attention. Not only was Miss Pross shocked and upset, but Jerry Cruncher—for his own secret reasons—was staring in absolute disbelief.

“What is the matter?” said the man who had made Miss Pross scream. He spoke in a quiet, annoyed voice in English.

“Oh, Solomon, dear Solomon!” cried Miss Pross, clapping her hands again. “After not seeing you or hearing from you for so long, to find you here!”

“Don't call me Solomon. Do you want to get me killed?” the man asked in a quiet, frightened way.

“Brother, brother!” cried Miss Pross, bursting into tears. “Have I ever been so mean to you that you ask me such a cruel question?”

“Then hold your tongue,” Solomon said, “and come outside if you want to talk to me. Pay for your wine and come out. Who is this man?”

Miss Pross, shaking her head sadly at her cold brother, said through her tears, “Mr. Cruncher.”

“Let him come out too,” Solomon said. “Does he think I am a ghost?”

Judging by Jerry's face, he definitely did. He said nothing. Miss Pross searched through her purse, tears streaming, and paid for the wine. As she did so, Solomon turned to the other customers and said a few words in French to explain, which made everyone go back to their cards and drinks.

“Now,” Solomon said, stopping at a dark corner, “what do you want?”

“How unkind of a brother whom I have always loved!” cried Miss Pross. “To greet me like this and show no affection at all.”

“Fine, damn it! There,” Solomon said, giving her a quick kiss on the lips. “Are you happy now?”

Miss Pross only shook her head and cried quietly.

“If you expect me to be surprised, I am not,” her brother Solomon said. “I knew you were here; I know about most people who are in Paris. If you really don't want to put my life in danger—which I think you might—leave as soon as possible and let me go my way. I am busy. I am a government official.”

“My English brother Solomon,” Miss Pross wept, looking up with tearful eyes. “He had the potential to be a great man in his own country, and now he is an official among foreigners—and such terrible foreigners! I would almost rather have seen him lying in his—”

“I knew it!” her brother interrupted. “You want to get me killed. You will make me a 'suspected' person, my own sister, just as I am getting ahead!”

“Heaven forbid!” cried Miss Pross. “I would rather never see you again, dear Solomon, even though I have always loved you. Just say one kind word to me, tell me you aren't angry with me, and I will let you go.”

Good Miss Pross! As if their estrangement were her fault, when Mr. Lorry knew for a fact years ago in London that this worthless brother had stolen all her savings and abandoned her!

He was saying a cold, patronizing word to her—looking down on her even though he was the one in the wrong—when Jerry Cruncher touched him on the shoulder and hoarsely asked a very strange question:

“Excuse me, but could I ask a favor? Is your name John Solomon, or Solomon John?”

The official turned to him with sudden suspicion. Jerry had not said a single word until now.

“Come on,” Jerry said. “Speak up, you know.” (Which was hard for Jerry to do with his rough throat.) “John Solomon, or Solomon John? She calls you Solomon, so she must know since she is your sister. But _I_ know you are John. Which name goes first? And what about that name Pross? That wasn't your name back in England.”

“What do you mean?”

“Well, I don't know exactly, because I can't quite remember what your name was back in England.”

“No?”

“No. But I swear it was a two-syllable name.”

“Indeed?”

“Yes. The other spy's name was one syllable. I know you. You were a spy witness at the Old Bailey court. What on earth did you call yourself back then?”

“Barsad,” said another voice, interrupting them.

“That's the name! I'd bet a thousand pounds on it!” Jerry cried.

The speaker was Sydney Carton. He stood with his hands behind him under his coat, leaning next to Jerry as casually as if he were back in the London courtroom.

“Don't be scared, my dear Miss Pross. I arrived at Mr. Lorry's yesterday evening, surprising him. We agreed I would stay hidden until everything was resolved or I could be useful. I am here now to ask for a quiet chat with your brother. I wish your brother had a more honest job, Mr. Barsad. I wish for your sake he wasn't a spy in the prisons.”

"Sheep" was slang at the time for a prison spy. The spy, who was already pale, turned even whiter and asked Carton how he dared—

“I will tell you,” Sydney said. “I saw you, Mr. Barsad, coming out of the Conciergerie prison about an hour ago while I was looking at the walls. You have a memorable face, and I remember faces well. Curious to see you there, and knowing you were involved in the trial of a friend who is now in great trouble, I followed you. I came into this wine shop right after you and sat nearby. Listening to you talk openly and hearing the rumors from the people around you, it was easy to figure out your job. Gradually, my curiosity turned into a plan, Mr. Barsad.”

“What plan?” the spy asked.

“It would be difficult and dangerous to explain in the street. Could you join me for a few minutes in private—at Tellson's Bank, for example?”

“Is that a threat?”

“Oh! Did I say that?”

“Then why should I go?”

“Really, Mr. Barsad, if you don't know, I can't tell you.”

“Do you mean you won't tell me, sir?” the spy asked hesitantly.

“You understand me perfectly, Mr. Barsad. I won't.”

Carton's casual, careless attitude made him seem very formidable. His sharp eyes saw the spy's fear and took advantage of it.

“See, I told you so,” the spy said, looking at his sister angrily. “If I get into trouble, it is your fault.”

“Come, come, Mr. Barsad!” Sydney said. “Don't be ungrateful. Out of respect for your sister, I have been polite about the proposal I want to make. Are you coming to the bank with me?”

“I'll hear what you have to say. Yes, I'll go with you.”

“First, let's walk your sister safely to the corner of her street. May I take your arm, Miss Pross? Paris is not a safe city for you to walk around alone right now. Since your guard knows Mr. Barsad, I will invite him to Mr. Lorry's with us. Ready? Let's go!”

Miss Pross remembered this moment for the rest of her life. When she held Sydney's arm and begged him not to hurt Solomon, his arm felt strong and steady. His eyes looked inspired—completely different from his usual lazy, careless look. She was too worried about her worthless brother to dwell on it much then. And Sydney's kind words comforted her too much to let her think clearly.

They left her at the corner, and Carton led the way to Mr. Lorry's apartment nearby. John Barsad, also known as Solomon Pross, walked next to him.

Mr. Lorry had just finished dinner and sat before a cozy fire, perhaps remembering himself as a younger man at the inn in Dover many years ago. He turned around as they entered, looking surprised to see a stranger.

“This is Miss Pross's brother, sir,” Sydney said. “Mr. Barsad.”

“Barsad?” the old gentleman repeated. “Barsad? I remember that name—and that face.”

“I told you that you had a memorable face, Mr. Barsad,” Carton remarked calmly. “Please sit down.”

As Carton sat down, he reminded Mr. Lorry by saying with a frown, “He was a witness at Charles's trial in London.” Mr. Lorry remembered immediately and looked at the visitor with clear disgust.

“Mr. Barsad has been recognized by Miss Pross as her brother, and he has admitted it,” Sydney said. “Now, here is the bad news. Darnay has been arrested again.”

Shocked, Mr. Lorry cried out, “What are you saying! I left him safe and free just two hours ago, and I was about to go back to see him!”

“He was arrested anyway. When did it happen, Mr. Barsad?”

“Just now, if it's true.”

“Mr. Barsad is the best authority,” Sydney said. “I heard him tell a fellow spy over a bottle of wine that the arrest was happening. He left the guards at the gate and saw them let in. There is no doubt Charles has been captured again.”

Mr. Lorry saw from Carton's face that there was no use arguing. Though confused, he knew he had to keep his head clear, so he calmed himself and listened quietly.

“Now, I hope,” Sydney said to Mr. Lorry, “that Doctor Manette's influence will help him tomorrow. You said he would be tried again tomorrow, Mr. Barsad?”

“Yes, I believe so.”

“Let's hope it helps him tomorrow as much as it did today. But it might not. I admit I am worried, Mr. Lorry, because Doctor Manette was unable to stop this arrest.”

“He might not have known about it in advance,” Mr. Lorry suggested.

“But that fact itself is alarming, considering how close he is to his son-in-law.”

“That's true,” Mr. Lorry admitted, rubbing his chin anxiously and staring at Carton.

“In short,” Sydney said, “this is a desperate time, and we must play a desperate game. Let the Doctor try to win. I will prepare for a loss. No one's life is safe here. Anyone cheered by the mob today might be executed tomorrow. The prize I want to win, if the worst happens, is to have a friend inside the Conciergerie prison. And the friend I intend to win over is Mr. Barsad.”

“You had better have good cards to play, sir,” said the spy.

“Let's look at them. I will see what I have. Mr. Lorry, you know my bad habits; could you give me some brandy?”

The bottle was placed before him, and he drank two glasses quickly before pushing the bottle away.

"He continued like a man laying down his cards one by one. "Mr. Barsad, you are a prison spy, a government agent, sometimes a guard, sometimes a prisoner, always an informer. You are extra valuable because you are English, meaning you look less suspicious. But you are using a fake name. That is a very good card. You currently work for the French Republic, but you used to work for the English government, France's enemy. That is an excellent card. In this suspicious city, everyone will assume that you are still in the pay of England—a spy for Pitt, a traitor hiding in the heart of France. That card cannot be beaten. Do you see my cards, Mr. Barsad?”

“I don't understand what you are playing at,” the spy said nervously.

“I am playing my Ace: reporting you to the nearest revolutionary committee. Look at your own cards, Mr. Barsad, and see what you have. Take your time.”

He poured another glass of brandy and drank it. He saw the spy was worried Carton would drink enough to get angry and report him immediately, so Carton deliberately drank another glass.

“Look at your hand carefully, Mr. Barsad. Take your time.”

The spy's hand was even worse than Carton realized, containing secrets Carton didn't even know. Having lost his job in England because his lies in court were exposed, he had come to France to work as a spy—first spying on English exiles, then on the French citizens. Under the old king, he had spied on Saint Antoine and Defarge's wine shop, using police records about Doctor Manette to try to trick the Defarges into talking. But he had failed completely. He remembered with terror how Madame Defarge had knitted while talking to him, looking at him coldly. Since then, he had seen her in the neighborhood meetings pointing to her knitting to accuse people who were then executed. He knew he was never safe, flight was impossible, and one word could send him to the Guillotine. If he were reported on these charges, Madame Defarge would surely produce her knitted records against him. The spy turned pale as he realized how helpless he was.

“You don't seem to like your cards,” Sydney said calmly. “Are you going to play?”

"The spy turned to Mr. Lorry with a groveling tone. "I think, sir, I can ask a kind gentleman of your age to ask this younger man if he really thinks it is right to report me. I admit I am a spy, and that it's a dirty job—though someone has to do it. But this gentleman is not a spy, so why should he lower himself to act like one?”

"Carton looked at his watch. "I am playing my Ace, Mr. Barsad," he answered, "without any hesitation, in just a few minutes."”

"The spy tried once more, this time turning his eyes to Mr. Lorry to pull him into the conversation. "I hoped, gentlemen, that out of respect for my sister—"”

“I couldn't show more respect for your sister than by freeing her of her brother once and for all,” said Sydney Carton.

“You think so, sir?”

The spy's polite manners, which didn't match his rough clothes, were useless against Carton's blank, mysterious expression. Not knowing what to do, the spy remained quiet while Carton spoke again, looking at his imaginary cards:

“And indeed, now I think again, I have a strong impression that I have another good card here, not yet enumerated. That friend and fellow-Sheep, who spoke of himself as pasturing in the country prisons; who was he?”

“He is French. You don't know him,” the spy said quickly.

“French, is he?” Carton repeated thoughtfully, looking away. “Well, maybe he is.”

“He is, I assure you,” said the spy, “though it doesn't matter.”

“Though it doesn't matter,” Carton repeated slowly. “No, it doesn't matter. But I know his face.”

“I think not. I am sure you don't. It's impossible,” said the spy.

“It's impossible,” Sydney Carton muttered, spinning his glass. “Impossible. He spoke good French, but he sounded like a foreigner, didn't he?”

“He just has an accent from the countryside,” said the spy.

“No, he is a foreigner!” Carton cried, hitting the table as he suddenly realized the truth. “It is Roger Cly! He is in disguise, but it's him. We saw him at the trial in London.”

"Barsad smiled. "Now, you are being hasty, sir," he said. “You are making a mistake. Cly—who was my partner, I admit now—has been dead for years. I was with him when he died. He was buried in London at Saint Pancras Church. The crowd hated him so much that I couldn't attend the funeral service, but I helped put him in his coffin.”

Here, Mr. Lorry noticed a strange, spiky shadow on the wall. Looking over, he saw it was caused by all the hair on Jerry Cruncher's head standing straight up in excitement.

“Let's be reasonable,” the spy said. “To show you that you are wrong, I have Cly's burial certificate right here in my wallet.” He quickly pulled it out. “Here it is. Look at it. You can hold it; it is real.”

Mr. Lorry saw the shadow move, and Jerry Cruncher stepped forward. His spiky hair was standing on end as if he had been shocked by electricity.

Without the spy seeing him, Jerry stood at his side and tapped his shoulder like a ghost.

“That Roger Cly,” Jerry said with a grim face. “So _you_ put him in his coffin?”

“I did.”

“Then who took him out of it?”

Barsad leaned back and stammered, “What do you mean?”

“I mean,” Jerry said, “that he was never in it! No, not him! I will lose my head if he was ever in that coffin.”

The spy looked at Carton and Lorry, who were both staring at Jerry in absolute surprise.

“I tell you,” Jerry said, “that you buried paving stones and dirt in that coffin. Don't tell me you buried Cly. It was a trick. I and two other men know it.”

“How do you know that?”

“What is that to you? Damn!” Jerry growled. “So it's you I have an old grudge against, with your trickery! I'd choke you right now for half a coin.”

Sydney Carton, who was just as surprised as Mr. Lorry, told Jerry to calm down and explain.

“Some other time, sir,” Jerry replied, avoiding the question. “Right now is not a good time to explain. All I say is that he knows very well Cly was never in that coffin. Let him deny it, and I will choke him, or I will go and report him to the police.”

“Well, I see one thing,” Carton said. “I have another card, Mr. Barsad. It is impossible for you to survive in Paris if I report you for working with another English spy who faked his death. They will call it a foreign plot in the prisons against the Republic. That is a winning card—a Guillotine card! Do you play?”

“No!” the spy replied. “I give up. I admit we were so hated in England that faking Cly's death was the only way he could escape. How this man knows it was a trick is a mystery to me.”

“Don't worry about me,” Jerry replied. “You have enough trouble dealing with this gentleman. And listen, I'd still choke you for half a coin!”

The spy turned back to Carton and said, “Let's get to the point. I have to go on duty soon. What is your proposal? Don't ask too much of me. If you ask me to help someone escape, which puts my head on the line, I'd rather refuse. We are all desperate. Remember, I can report you too, and I can lie in court to protect myself. What do you want?”

“Not much. You are a guard at the Conciergerie?”

“I tell you now, an escape is completely impossible,” the spy said firmly.

“Why are you telling me what I didn't ask? You are a guard at the Conciergerie?”

“Sometimes.”

“You can get inside when you choose?”

“I can go in and out when I choose.”

Sydney Carton filled another glass with brandy, poured it slowly onto the hearth, and watched it drip away. Once the glass was empty, he stood up and said:

“We spoke in front of these two so they could witness the cards I hold. Now, come into this back room, and let's have one final word alone.”

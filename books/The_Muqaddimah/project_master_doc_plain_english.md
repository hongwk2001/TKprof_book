# The Muqaddimah: Plain-English Edition — Project Master Document

## 1. What This Book Is

**A curated, plain-English rendering of Ibn Khaldun's actual words.**

Not a synthesis (that is *The Collapse Code*). Not an academic translation (that is Rosenthal). This is the book that lets a general reader sit down and *read the Muqaddimah* — what Ibn Khaldun actually argued, in his own logical sequence, in language that doesn't require a PhD to follow.

The closest analogy: Robert Fagles's *Iliad*. Fagles didn't retell Homer's story with modern examples. He translated it faithfully but in living English that makes you feel why it has lasted 3,000 years. That is what this edition does for the Muqaddimah.

---

## 2. The Problem With What Exists

| Edition | Problem |
|---|---|
| Rosenthal (1958, 3 vols.) | 1,600 pages. Dense academic footnotes. Written for Islamic Studies scholars. |
| Dawood abridgment (1969) | Still scholarly in tone. Cuts 80% but keeps the academic register. |
| Robert Irwin biography | About Ibn Khaldun, not *by* him. |
| *The Collapse Code* (our Book 1) | Applies his framework — doesn't render his text. |

**The gap:** There is no edition that lets a curious, intelligent non-specialist read Ibn Khaldun's actual thinking in one sitting. This edition fills that gap.

## 4. Book Concept

**Working title:** *The Muqaddimah: Ibn Khaldun in Plain English*

**Subtitle options:**
- *The Complete Ideas of the World's First Social Scientist — Finally Readable*
- *A New Plain-English Edition of the 14th Century's Greatest Book*

**Format:** ~35,000–45,000 words / ~140–170 pages / ~2.5hr read

**What it is NOT:**
- Not a word-for-word complete translation (the original is ~520,000 words — unreadable in one volume)
- Not a retelling with modern examples added
- Not a summary or simplification that strips out Ibn Khaldun's reasoning

**What it IS:**
- A curated selection of the Muqaddimah's most important passages and arguments
- Rendered in fluent, clear modern English that preserves Ibn Khaldun's voice — systematic, confident, curious
- Brief editorial introductions (1–2 paragraphs) before each section to orient the reader
- Faithful to his logic and sequence, not reorganized around modern concepts

## 6. Voice and Style Guidelines

Ibn Khaldun writes like a scientist who is also a storyteller. His tone is:
- **Systematic** — he builds arguments step by step, defines terms before using them
- **Confident** — he states conclusions directly, without academic hedging
- **Concrete** — he always grounds abstractions in historical examples
- **Curious** — he follows ideas wherever they lead, including into uncomfortable territory

The plain-English rendering should preserve all four qualities. Avoid:
- Academic hedging ("it could be argued that...")
- Modernizing his examples (his examples are his evidence — don't replace them)
- Over-simplifying (he is rigorous — the rendering should be rigorous too)
- Over-explaining (the editorial intros handle context; the text should speak)

Target reading level: **educated general reader** — someone who reads longform journalism, popular history, and serious non-fiction. Think *The Atlantic* reader, not a textbook reader.

## 7. Differentiation from *The Collapse Code*

| | *The Collapse Code* | *Plain-English Edition* |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | Synthesis — applies Ibn Khaldun to Rome, Apple, Samsung, etc. | Translation — renders what Ibn Khaldun actually wrote |
| **Reader** | Wants modern relevance and case studies | Wants to read the original source |
| **Voice** | Author's voice explaining Ibn Khaldun | Ibn Khaldun's own voice |
| **Structure** | Thematic (one big idea per chapter) | Follows the Muqaddimah's own sequence |
| **Use case** | "I want to understand why civilizations fall" | "I want to read the actual Muqaddimah" |

**They are companion volumes.** A reader who finishes *The Collapse Code* and wants to go deeper buys this. A reader who picks up this edition and wants modern context buys *The Collapse Code*. Each drives sales of the other.

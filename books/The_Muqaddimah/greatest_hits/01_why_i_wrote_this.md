# Why I Wrote This Book

*Ibn Khaldun opens the Muqaddimah by explaining why history, as it has been written, is full of errors — and what kind of knowledge is actually required to get it right.*

---

History is a noble science. On its surface, it offers something for everyone: a record of past nations, their customs and ways of life, the rise of prophets and kings, the growth of states, the accumulation of wealth — all of it preserved so that later generations can learn what came before. This much every curious person understands.

But history has a deeper dimension that only the most penetrating minds can reach. This deeper history is an inquiry into the nature of civilization itself. It is an examination of why things happen the way they do — why societies form, why they grow strong, and why they inevitably collapse.

Most historians never reach this depth. They collect reports and pass them on. They repeat what they have been told without asking whether it is plausible, without checking it against what they know about the nature of human society, without asking: *is this the kind of thing that could actually have happened?*

The result is that the historical record is full of errors — not errors of transmission, but errors of judgment. Historians have accepted absurd figures, implausible events, and impossible stories because they lacked the tools to evaluate them. They treated history as a collection of reports rather than as a body of knowledge that must be tested against observable reality.

The man who wishes to practice history properly needs something more than a good memory and a reliable chain of transmission. He needs to understand the laws that govern the natural world of human civilization. He needs to know how nations differ in their characters, their customs, their values, and their ways of organizing themselves. He needs to understand the nature of political power — how it arises, how it consolidates, and how it decays. He needs to grasp the forces that cause one era to differ fundamentally from another, and one region to develop differently from its neighbors.

Armed with this knowledge, the historian can do what the mere collector of reports cannot: he can look at any historical claim and ask whether it is consistent with the known laws of human society. If a story agrees with those laws, it is plausible and worth preserving. If it contradicts them, it must be rejected — however venerable its source, however ancient its transmission.

Consider how dramatically conditions change across time. The nations that ruled the ancient world — the Persians, the Syrians, the Nabataeans, the Israelites, the Egyptians — lived in ways that are now entirely gone. Their cities are buried. Their customs have vanished. The very languages they spoke have been forgotten. Then came new nations — the later Persians, the Romans, the Arabs, the Franks — who built their civilizations on different principles, developed different customs, and eventually gave way to others in turn.

This constant change is not random. It follows patterns. The conditions that produce powerful, cohesive nations are not mysterious. The forces that cause once-great civilizations to lose their strength and fall to newcomers can be understood and described. History, properly practiced, is the study of these patterns — not the accumulation of colorful stories about kings and battles.

Most of those who have written history did not understand this. They were captivated by the surface — the dramatic events, the famous individuals, the great battles — and they failed to look beneath it for the laws that determined why those events unfolded as they did. The result is that the tradition of historical writing has become corrupted. What was once a serious discipline that attracted the greatest scholars of the Islamic world has become a subject that anyone feels entitled to practice, regardless of whether they understand anything about the forces they are describing.

I have written this book to restore history to its proper foundations.

What I have set out to discover are the laws that govern the rise and fall of civilizations — laws that apply regardless of which civilization we are discussing, regardless of which era, regardless of the particular characters of the rulers involved. These are laws as reliable as the laws that govern the natural world. They operate on human societies with something close to the regularity of nature itself.

Once these laws are understood, history becomes a different kind of knowledge. It becomes a tool for understanding the present, not merely a record of the past. It becomes a lens through which any attentive person can look at the world around them and recognize, in what they see today, the same patterns that have repeated themselves across centuries.

This is what I have tried to provide.

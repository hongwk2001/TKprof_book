# Editor's Note

Ibn Khaldun was born in Tunis in 1332 and died in Cairo in 1406. In his lifetime he served as a court official, a diplomat, a judge, a professor, and a prisoner. He negotiated with the Castilian king Pedro the Cruel on behalf of the Sultan of Granada. He met Tamerlane — then at the height of his conquests — outside the gates of Damascus, and interviewed him through a translator. He watched the dynasties he served rise, fracture, and fall.

In 1375, exhausted by court politics and fearing for his life, he retreated with his family to a remote castle in the mountains of Algeria. He spent the next three years writing the *Muqaddimah* — the introduction to his seven-volume history of the world. It is the only part anyone reads today, because it turned out to be one of the most original works of social thought ever produced.

His central question was simple: why do civilizations rise and fall? Not which ones, not when — the chroniclers had that covered. But *why*. What is the mechanism?

His answer, worked out in thousands of pages of careful observation, rests on a single concept he called *Asabiyyah*: the fierce group solidarity, the unconditional mutual loyalty, that binds people together and makes collective action possible. A group with strong Asabiyyah can conquer. A civilization that has lost it cannot defend itself. And the tragedy — which Ibn Khaldun traces with clinical precision — is that success always, eventually, dissolves the very quality that produced it.

This book selects fourteen passages from Books Two and Three of the *Muqaddimah* — the sections where his most famous ideas appear — and renders them in plain modern English. The goal is to let the argument speak for itself, without the academic apparatus that surrounds most translations. No footnotes, no hedging, no scholarly dispute about what he *really* meant. Just the ideas, as clearly as they can be stated.

The source text is Ibn Khaldun's original Arabic. All observations, arguments, and examples are his. The editor's contribution is the English.

A note on the examples Ibn Khaldun uses: he draws on Arab tribes, Berber dynasties, early caliphs, and medieval North African kingdoms — the world he knew. These examples have been preserved rather than replaced with modern equivalents, because the point is not to prove that his ideas apply to our world (you will find that yourself) but to show how he built the argument from the evidence available to him. The specific names matter less than the pattern they illustrate.

The *Muqaddimah* is a long book. Most of it — particularly the final sixth, which runs to nearly 340,000 words on Islamic jurisprudence, Quranic recitation, the occult sciences, and the classification of knowledge — is of specialist interest only. What follows is what remains when you remove everything that time has made peripheral, and keep only the ideas that are, six hundred and fifty years later, still true.

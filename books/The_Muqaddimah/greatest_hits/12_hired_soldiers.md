# The Hired Soldiers Problem

*When a dynasty can no longer rely on loyal fighters, it hires mercenaries. Ibn Khaldun explains why this solution always fails.*

---

Every declining dynasty reaches the same moment: the original fighting force — the men who carried real Asabiyyah, who fought because the dynasty was theirs — is gone. They have been sidelined, absorbed into court life, or simply died out. Their grandsons wear silk and negotiate precedence in palace hallways.

The dynasty still needs soldiers. So it hires them.

This appears to solve the problem. Mercenary soldiers can be skilled fighters. They can be recruited in large numbers. They can be disciplined, equipped, and deployed against enemies. Dynasties that have made this transition often continue for significant periods of time — in some cases for generations — on the strength of hired military force.

But hired soldiers are not the same as loyal ones, and the difference is not merely sentimental.

A man who fights because the dynasty is his — because his kin built it, because his identity is bound up with its survival, because he cannot imagine its defeat without imagining his own — will fight in ways that a hired soldier will not. He will hold his position when holding it seems futile. He will refuse to break when breaking would save his individual life. He will, if necessary, die rather than let the group down, because the group is the thing that makes his life meaningful.

A hired soldier performs a calculation. He assesses the odds. He weighs his pay against the risk to his life. When the odds are good, he fights effectively. When the odds turn against him — when the battle is clearly lost, when retreat would save his life, when a better employer is visibly available — he calculates again. The outcome of that calculation is not loyalty. Loyalty is not available for hire.

This is not a criticism of hired soldiers as individuals. Many of them are brave, skilled, and professional. The problem is structural. They are fighting for wages in someone else's cause, and no wage is high enough to make that cause their own. The Asabiyyah that makes a fighting force genuinely formidable — the absolute mutual commitment, the refusal to leave each other behind, the willingness to die for the group — cannot be manufactured through a salary arrangement.

The dynasty discovers this gradually, through a series of military failures that would not have happened to the founding generation. Battles are lost that should have been won. Rebellions that should have been quickly suppressed drag on. Enemies who once feared the dynasty's military reputation begin to probe its defenses with increasing confidence. Each failure weakens the dynasty's ability to hire and retain good soldiers, which leads to further failures.

There is a second problem with mercenaries that Ibn Khaldun identifies, and it is more dangerous than their unreliability in battle: their ambition.

A hired soldier who is competent, who commands the respect of his men, who wins battles that the dynasty's own officials cannot win — this man accumulates power. He becomes indispensable. The dynasty, which cannot fight without him, must give him more resources, more authority, more latitude. He is no longer simply executing orders. He is, increasingly, the actual military power on which the dynasty depends.

This is how mercenary commanders become kingmakers. And from kingmaker to king is a short step, taken whenever the ruling dynasty is sufficiently weak and the commander sufficiently ambitious. The histories of the dynasties Ibn Khaldun studied are full of this pattern: the hired military commander who gradually absorbs the power of the ruler he was employed to protect.

The dynasty that has replaced loyal Asabiyyah with hired force has introduced a structural instability into its own foundations. It is dependent on men whose loyalty is conditional and whose ambition is unconstrained by any genuine bond to the ruling house. The more it relies on them, the more leverage they accumulate. And the more leverage they accumulate, the more likely the dynasty is to end not with a foreign conquest but with a quiet internal transfer of power to the men who were supposedly serving it.

The founders would have recognized this danger immediately. They built their power precisely by being the group that could not be bought. Their successors, having lost that capacity, have hired people who can be bought — and must now live with what that means.

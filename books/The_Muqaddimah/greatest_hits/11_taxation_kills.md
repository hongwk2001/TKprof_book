# How Taxation Kills the Dynasty

*Ibn Khaldun on the fiscal logic of collapse: why the more a dynasty needs money, the less it collects — and why this spiral is almost impossible to escape.*

---

There is a financial logic to the decline of dynasties that is as reliable as the social logic of Asabiyyah's erosion. The two operate together, reinforcing each other, driving the dynasty toward collapse from two directions at once.

A new dynasty, in its early years, taxes lightly. This is partly a matter of principle — the founders often come from groups that resented the heavy taxation of the regime they overthrew — and partly a matter of arithmetic. Light taxes, applied to a large number of people in a territory that is recovering from the disruptions of conquest, produce adequate revenue because they do not suppress the activity that generates it. People farm, trade, build, and produce, and a modest share of that activity flows to the state. The economy is alive, and the state lives off its vitality.

As the dynasty matures, its expenses grow. The court expands. The number of officials, soldiers, and dependents that the dynasty must support increases with every generation. The lifestyle of the ruling class becomes more expensive as it becomes more luxurious. The great building projects that powerful dynasties commission — the palaces, the mosques, the city walls — consume enormous resources. And the hired military force that has replaced the original Asabiyyah-driven army costs a great deal more than fighters who were essentially fighting for free, out of loyalty and shared purpose.

To cover these growing expenses, the dynasty raises taxes.

At first, the increase is manageable. People grumble, but they continue to produce. The economy absorbs the additional burden. Revenue rises.

Then the dynasty raises taxes again. And again. Each increase is justified by genuine need — the expenses are real, the shortfalls are real. But each increase also reduces the incentive to produce. Why expand your farm if the additional harvest will be taken? Why invest in a new enterprise if the profits will be extracted before you can reinvest them? The productive population begins to do less, not because they are lazy, but because the rational calculation has changed. Working harder produces more wealth for the dynasty, not for themselves.

Revenue begins to fall. The dynasty, facing a shortfall, raises taxes higher still to compensate. The economy contracts further. A spiral has begun.

Ibn Khaldun's observation here is one of the most remarkable in the Muqaddimah, and one that modern economists have repeatedly rediscovered without acknowledging its source: there is a point beyond which raising tax rates reduces tax revenue, because it reduces the economic activity being taxed. The dynasty, chasing revenue, destroys the base that generates it.

The spiral has a human face. The peasant who can no longer afford to farm abandons his land. The merchant who cannot make a profit stops trading. The artisan who cannot keep enough of his earnings to live on takes up a different life. Each of these withdrawals from productive activity means less for the dynasty to tax. The dynasty responds with more pressure, more extraction, more creative new levies on whatever activity remains. And the cycle continues.

Meanwhile, the deterioration of the tax base accelerates the deterioration of the military. The dynasty can no longer pay its hired soldiers reliably. Soldiers who are not paid regularly are soldiers who are looking for another employer, or who supplement their income through predation on the populations they are supposed to protect. The state's capacity to enforce order declines. Without order, economic activity declines further.

The dynasty that once projected power effortlessly — that once seemed, to those who lived under it, like a permanent feature of the landscape — has become a machine that consumes more than it produces. It is taxing populations that have been reduced to subsistence. It is paying soldiers who are unreliable. It is maintaining a court whose expenses are far beyond what the depleted economy can support.

At this stage, any external shock — a drought, a plague, a coordinated rebellion, an invasion — can bring the whole structure down. The shock does not need to be catastrophic in itself. A dynasty in full health would absorb it. A dynasty in this condition cannot.

The tax spiral is, in the end, the dynasty's attempt to solve through extraction a problem that can only be solved through Asabiyyah. The original dynasty collected its revenue easily because the people producing it were also the people defending it — they had a stake in the dynasty's survival. By the time taxation has become predatory, that stake is gone. The people being taxed no longer see themselves as part of the dynasty's project. They see themselves as its victims. And victims, when they can, stop producing.

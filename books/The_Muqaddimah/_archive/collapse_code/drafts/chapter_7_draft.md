# Chapter 7: Who's Next? 

If you look at the world today through the lens of Ibn Khaldun, it is hard not to feel a chill run down your spine. 

We are living in an era of unprecedented luxury. We have entirely outsourced the production of our food, the manufacturing of our goods, and the defense of our borders. We are currently experiencing a massive wave of "Elite Overproduction," where universities are pumping out millions of highly educated, ambitious young people into an economy that simply doesn't have enough high-status jobs or wealth to satisfy them. 

And as these frustrated elites fight over a shrinking pie, the government prints money and raises taxes, squeezing the middle class until they lose all hope. 

Sound familiar?

### Where Are We in the Cycle?

If Ibn Khaldun's 120-year cycle holds true, we are currently watching the Third Generation of the modern post-World War II order. 

The First Generation (the "Greatest Generation") survived the Great Depression and fought a brutal World War. Their *Asabiyyah* was incredibly high. They built a massive, booming global economy. 

The Second Generation (the "Baby Boomers") grew up in the prosperity created by their parents. They maintained the system, grew the wealth, and enjoyed the comfort, but the deep social cohesion of the World War II era began to crack. 

The Third Generation is in charge now. They have never known a true existential threat. They are deeply divided, highly partisan, and heavily reliant on massive bureaucratic systems that are starting to fail. The *Asabiyyah* of the modern West is arguably lower than it has been in a century. 

### Who Has the Strongest Asabiyyah Today?

Ibn Khaldun's framework forces a question that is uncomfortable but important: if the existing order is in the third-generation phase, who are the hungry outsiders at the gates?

This is not a question about who is "good" or "bad." Ibn Khaldun had no interest in moral judgments. He was an engineer of history, studying forces, not values. And from that engineering perspective, the question is simple: which groups today have the strongest Asabiyyah? Who is hungry, unified, and willing to sacrifice for a shared goal?

**China** is the most obvious candidate. The Chinese Communist Party has spent 75 years building national Asabiyyah around a specific historical narrative: the "Century of Humiliation" — the period from the First Opium War in 1839 to the Communist victory in 1949, when China was carved up by European powers and Japan, its territory occupied, its ports opened to foreign commerce by force. This narrative is taught in every Chinese school. The humiliation was real; what the Party does is channel real historical grievance into present-day Asabiyyah: *we are the people who were brought low, and we are determined to restore our rightful position.*

The Belt and Road Initiative — China's trillion-dollar project to build infrastructure across Asia, Africa, and Europe — has the exact structure of what Ibn Khaldun described as the first act of a rising Asabiyyah: the acquisition of resources and the building of networks that will sustain future power. The semiconductor war — China's determination to develop domestic chip manufacturing despite American export controls — is the desert man refusing to be dependent on the city for his weapons. Whether one agrees with China's politics or not, the directional clarity of its national purpose is unmistakable. A society pulling in one direction with that kind of historical urgency generates Asabiyyah that fragmented democratic systems, by design, cannot easily match.

**The Global South** more broadly is asserting itself in ways that would have been unimaginable twenty years ago. The demographic arithmetic alone is striking. India's 1.4 billion people have a median age of 28. Nigeria, projected to become the world's third-largest country by 2050, has a median age of 18. The United States has a median age of 38. Germany's is 46. Japan's is 49. The West is old; the Global South is young. And youth, in Ibn Khaldun's framework, is not merely a biological fact — it is a proxy for the energy, risk tolerance, and collective hunger that generates Asabiyyah.

The expansion of BRICS+ in 2024 — adding Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Ethiopia, Iran, Egypt, and Argentina to a bloc that already included Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa — represents something the architects of the postwar order would have found unthinkable: a credible institutional alternative to Western-led structures, assembled by countries that feel they have been peripheral to a game the West designed for its own benefit. The resentment that drives this coalition is Asabiyyah — not the polished Asabiyyah of a rising dynasty at its peak, but the raw, unprocessed hunger of people who have watched the comfortable insiders write the rules for a century and have decided to write their own.

**Religious and ideological movements** have always been among the most powerful Asabiyyah generators in history, and Ibn Khaldun's observation on this point remains operationally true in 2026. The fastest-growing religious communities in the world are not in the wealthy secular West — they are in sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America, and South and Southeast Asia. Evangelical Christianity in Nigeria, Pentecostalism in Brazil, Islam in the Sahel region of Africa: these are not stagnant traditions maintaining institutional inertia. They are movements with the ferocious energy of genuine growth — new converts who have chosen their faith actively, communities bound by shared practice and shared identity, organizations that provide real belonging in contexts where the state provides very little.

The communities likely to build the next civilization are probably not the ones whose members treat religion as a cultural inheritance or a Sunday activity. They are the ones whose members would, and sometimes do, walk into a desert for it.

**Technology tribes** are the most unexpected source of new Asabiyyah, and the most instructive because their history is now long enough to observe the full arc. The Bitcoin community, from 2009 onward, exhibited classic desert-tribe mechanics: the early adopters were dismissed as cranks, libertarian extremists, or criminals. They were excluded from the financial system they were trying to replace. They were mocked in newspapers. The early community had no comfort, no institutional support, a shared identity defined partly by hostility from the mainstream, and a loyalty forged in shared ridicule. By 2024, Bitcoin was held in the portfolios of institutional investors and sovereign wealth funds. The pirates had become, if not the navy, at least a recognized maritime force.

The open-source AI community — researchers and engineers who believe the most powerful AI systems should be publicly accessible rather than controlled by a handful of corporations — exhibit the same structure: shared purpose, shared identity as outsiders against a well-funded mainstream, and the intense unity of people who believe they are fighting for something that genuinely matters. The specific content of these movements is almost irrelevant to the Khaldunian analysis. The question is whether they have enough Asabiyyah to sustain the fight long enough to win. Movements that attract true believers consistently outperform movements that attract mercenaries.

None of these groups is guaranteed to prevail. History is not a simple story with a predetermined winner. But Ibn Khaldun's framework tells us what to look for: the group that is most unified, most willing to sacrifice for a shared purpose, and most convinced that they are the hungry outsiders with everything to prove.

### The Optimist's Case: Collapse Is Fertilizer

Here is the part of Ibn Khaldun's philosophy that tends to get lost in the gloom of civilizational decline analysis: he was not a pessimist. He was a scientist.

A scientist who studies forest fires does not conclude that forests are doomed. They understand that fire is part of the forest's life cycle—that the burning of old, dense undergrowth creates the conditions for new growth. The fire is destructive and painful for the things that are burning. It is generative for what comes after.

Ibn Khaldun saw the cycle of civilizational rise and fall in exactly these terms. The collapse of a decadent, over-extended empire is not the end of human civilization. It is the clearing of space for the next hungry, unified group to build something new.

Consider the historical evidence. The fall of the Western Roman Empire in 476 AD is often described as one of the greatest catastrophes in human history—the destruction of a sophisticated civilization and the beginning of the "Dark Ages." But the fall of Rome also freed the Germanic tribes to develop their own institutions and cultures, eventually producing the complex, innovative European civilization of the medieval period. The universities, the legal traditions, the architecture, the philosophical traditions of medieval Europe—all grew in the space that Rome's collapse had cleared.

The fall of the Mongol Yuan Dynasty in China in 1368 led directly to the founding of the Ming Dynasty—which, despite its own eventual decline, produced an extraordinary flowering of Chinese art, literature, and exploration. The Yongle Emperor sent treasure fleets to Africa. Chinese porcelain reached Europe. The cultural output of the early Ming period was extraordinary precisely because the founding generation had the Asabiyyah of people who had just survived an existential struggle.

The pattern recurs further back still, and at even greater scale. Around 1200 BC, four sophisticated Bronze Age civilizations collapsed simultaneously within roughly fifty years: the Mycenaean Greeks, the Hittite Empire, the Egyptian New Kingdom (severely weakened), and the coastal trading cities of the Levant including Ugarit. The Bronze Age collapse remains one of the least fully explained events in ancient history — its causes involved some combination of climate, migration, internal rebellions, and the disruption of the trade networks on which bronze-based economies depended.

What came after was an Iron Age. Bronze required tin — a relatively rare metal that had to be traded across enormous distances, making bronze weapons and tools accessible mainly to elites and states. Iron ore was everywhere. The Iron Age democratized tools and weapons in a way the Bronze Age never had, enabling a broader range of people and communities to farm, build, and fight effectively. The Greek city-states — Athens, Sparta, Corinth — arose in the centuries following the Bronze Age collapse. So did the Phoenician trading networks. So did the Israelite kingdoms. The catastrophe of 1200 BC cleared the ground for the extraordinary flowering of Mediterranean civilization that followed.

Closer in time, the Black Death of 1347 to 1351 killed between 30 and 50 percent of Europe's population in four years — the greatest demographic catastrophe in Western history. It also destroyed the feudal labor system that had kept European peasants bound to the land for centuries. When half the agricultural labor force is dead, the surviving workers can suddenly demand higher wages and greater freedom of movement. The peasants who survived the plague were, in purely economic terms, far better off than the peasants who preceded them. The disruption of the old hierarchies created the conditions for the Renaissance, for early capitalism, for the gradual emergence of a middle class. The horror of the plague was the fertilizer for the next five hundred years of European civilization.

Collapses hurt. They hurt the people living through them enormously. Ibn Khaldun did not minimize this. But from the perspective of the longest arc of human history, they are necessary. The old system, with its calcified hierarchies, its bloated bureaucracies, and its exhausted Asabiyyah, has to clear away before something new can grow.

### What You Can Do With This

Ibn Khaldun did not write *The Muqaddimah* as a prediction of doom. He wrote it as a manual for understanding power. And a manual for understanding power is, implicitly, a guide for navigating it.

If you understand the cycle, you can locate yourself within it. The diagnostic is not complicated.

**Signs you are in a first-generation organization:** The founder is still in the building — or the founding energy is still alive in the people who were there at the beginning. People work excessive hours without being asked because they cannot imagine not doing so. There are no org charts, or the org charts are ignored. Decisions happen in conversations, not committees. Everyone can tell you in one sentence why the organization exists and why it matters. The culture is uncomfortable, demanding, and electric. Joining it feels like enlisting, not taking a job.

**Signs you are in a second-generation organization:** The founder has stepped back or is gone. Professional managers have arrived. The product or service works reliably and has established customers. Processes are being documented because they need to scale. The mission is still referenced but with less urgency — it has become an identity claim rather than a daily operating principle. This is the most stable phase. The organization is genuinely functional. The best career safety is here, but not the best energy.

**Signs you are in a third-generation organization:** No one can tell you why the organization was founded without looking it up. The most valuable internal skill is navigating hierarchy, not creating external value. The senior people are primarily managing their own positions rather than the organization's purpose. The best young people are leaving for first-generation organizations. The word "legacy" appears frequently. Innovation happens despite the organization, not because of it.

If you are building something — a company, a community, a movement — Ibn Khaldun's framework tells you what the essential ingredient is. Not capital. Not technology. Not a clever strategy. Asabiyyah: the shared identity, the shared sacrifice, the "us against the world" conviction of people who have nothing to lose and everything to prove. Protect it deliberately. The moment your organization is comfortable enough that its people are primarily protecting what they have rather than fighting for what they want, the countdown has begun. You will not be able to stop it by announcing that the startup spirit must be preserved. The desert does not come back through a memo.

For your own life: Ibn Khaldun's framework suggests that the Asabiyyah that makes individuals genuinely effective — the clarity of purpose, the tolerance for hardship, the deep loyalty to a small group of people — is generated the same way it is generated in tribes. Through shared struggle. Through doing difficult things together. Through choosing, voluntarily, not to outsource the uncomfortable.

This is not an argument for artificial deprivation. It is an observation that the things most worth doing are almost always things that are difficult, that most people avoid, and that bind you to the people who do them alongside you. The research group that publishes the important paper. The team that ships the product no one thought was possible. The community that builds something real in a neighborhood no one else wanted. These are, in Ibn Khaldun's terms, the desert people. And they are, in his framework, the ones who eventually inherit the earth.

If you are an individual trying to make sense of the world — trying to understand why institutions that seemed permanent are cracking, why the social contract of the 20th century seems to be dissolving, why the future feels less stable than it once did — Ibn Khaldun offers something more valuable than a prediction. He offers a framework. A map.

The map doesn't tell you where to go. But it tells you where you are. And knowing where you are is the beginning of wisdom.

### The Closing Question

Ibn Khaldun died in Cairo in 1406, at the age of 74, having outlived most of his enemies, survived his imprisonment, escaped the burning of Damascus, and watched several of the dynasties he had served rise and fall.

He left behind a book that his own contemporaries largely ignored.

Six hundred and fifty years later, the most powerful people in the world are reading it—not because it is a historical curiosity, but because it is a living guide. Because the cycle he described is still operating. Because the Asabiyyah he identified as the engine of all human power is still the engine of all human power. Because the desert and the city are still in conflict, the hungry outsiders are still eyeing the comfortable insiders, and the third generation is still, somewhere, living in luxury and wondering why everything feels so fragile.

The question he asks, across seven centuries of silence, is the same question he asked in his desert castle in 1377:

Who has the Asabiyyah to build the next civilization?

And the more urgent version of that question, the one that is relevant to every person reading this book:

Will it be you?

# How Dynasties Are Born

*The first generation. Ibn Khaldun describes the founding moment of every great dynasty — and the qualities that make it possible.*

---

Every dynasty begins the same way.

A group of people — a tribe, a clan, a religious movement, a band of soldiers from the margins — comes together around a shared identity and a shared purpose. They have strong Asabiyyah. They are unified in a way that the existing power, fat and settled and comfortable, is not. And at some moment, through a combination of their own ferocity and the existing power's weakness, they break through. They conquer. They establish themselves.

The men of this founding generation have specific qualities, and it is worth understanding them precisely — because the story of every dynasty is, in large part, the story of those qualities disappearing.

The founding generation has known hardship. Not the performance of hardship, not the nostalgic story of hardship told by people who have never actually experienced it — but real, unmanaged hardship. Scarcity. Danger without institutional protection. The absolute dependence of each person on the people around them for survival. This is the forge in which strong Asabiyyah is created, and there is no substitute for it.

From this hardship comes a particular relationship to glory. The founders share it. When they conquer, when they achieve something great, the credit and the spoils are distributed among the group — not because the founders are especially virtuous or egalitarian, but because the Asabiyyah that made the conquest possible was genuinely collective. No single person did it. The group did it. Everyone knows this, and the distribution of rewards reflects the reality of how the victory was achieved.

This shared glory is not merely a matter of fairness. It is the active maintenance of Asabiyyah. As long as the group's achievements are experienced as collective achievements — as long as each member feels that the success of the group is his success — the bonds that hold the group together remain strong. The shared pride is the shared loyalty made visible.

The founding generation is also, typically, simple in its habits. It has not yet had time to develop expensive tastes. The men who seized power lived, until recently, lives of material deprivation. They are not yet attached to luxury, because they have not yet experienced enough of it to become dependent on it. This simplicity keeps them close to the conditions that produced their Asabiyyah in the first place. They have not yet forgotten what it cost to get here.

And they are brave — genuinely, functionally brave — in the way that only people who have faced real danger repeatedly can be brave. Not the theatrical courage of someone performing for an audience, but the quiet, reliable courage of people who have been in difficult situations and have learned that they can endure them. This courage makes them effective in the crises that any new dynasty inevitably faces. There will be rebellions, rival claimants, external enemies who see the new power as vulnerable. The founding generation handles these threats because it has the temperament, the cohesion, and the fighting capacity to handle them.

In the politics of a new dynasty, the founding generation governs through presence and through Asabiyyah rather than through the elaborate bureaucratic machinery that later dynasties require. The ruler is first among equals — respected, obeyed, but not separated from his people by a vast court apparatus. Decisions are made in consultation. Power is exercised through relationships rather than through institutions.

This is, in many ways, an effective way to govern. It is responsive, it is flexible, and it maintains the Asabiyyah of the ruling group by keeping them all engaged in the actual work of governance rather than reducing most of them to ceremonial roles while real power concentrates in a single figure.

But it cannot last.

The dynasty that the founding generation creates will, simply by succeeding, begin to generate the forces that destroy it. Wealth accumulates. Cities are built. Administration becomes more complex and requires more specialization. The ruler, pressed on all sides by competing demands, begins to consolidate power — to reduce the role of the founding group, to bring in advisors and administrators who are personally loyal to him rather than to the group, to build the apparatus of a real court. This is not evil or corrupt. It is, in many ways, a rational response to the demands of governing an increasingly complex polity.

But it is the beginning of the end for the Asabiyyah that built the dynasty.

The founding generation, if they live long enough, can see this happening. Some of them resist it. It does not matter. The process is driven by the logic of power itself, not by the intentions of any individual. And the founding generation, having lived through the hard years, having built something real, having earned their place — dies. And leaves behind a second generation that has inherited everything they built, but not the experience that built it.

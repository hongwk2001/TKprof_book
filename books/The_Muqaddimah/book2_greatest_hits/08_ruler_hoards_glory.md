# The Ruler Who Hoards All the Glory

*The second generation. The dynasty's success begins to corrupt the very things that made it possible.*

---

Once a dynasty is established, a transformation begins — not in a single dramatic moment, but gradually, almost imperceptibly, driven by the inner logic of power itself.

The ruler looks at the group that put him in power and begins to see them differently. While the Asabiyyah of the founding generation was the engine that drove the conquest, that same Asabiyyah is now a complication. The men who fought beside him, who shared in the glory, who have their own followings and their own claims on loyalty — these men are not simply subordinates. They are partners. And partners limit what a ruler can do.

The ruler wants more than partnership. He wants what successful rulers, in Ibn Khaldun's observation, always eventually want: to be the sole source of glory, the sole reservoir of loyalty, the single point to which all power flows. Not because he is unusually greedy or corrupt — though greed and corruption will certainly appear — but because this is what power, once achieved, naturally tends toward. The logic of autocracy is the logic of simplification. It is easier to rule when there is one center of authority than when authority is distributed.

And so, by degrees, the ruler begins to push the founding group aside.

He promotes men who are personally dependent on him — administrators, secretaries, freed slaves, foreigners who have no independent power base and whose elevation comes entirely from his favor and would disappear the moment he withdrew it. These men are loyal in a way that the founding group is not: they are loyal because they have no alternative. They cannot survive without the ruler's protection. The members of the founding group, by contrast, have their own Asabiyyah, their own followers, their own claims to respect that exist independently of the ruler's recognition.

The founding group resists this, of course. Some do so openly, and are suppressed. Others accommodate themselves to the new reality, accept reduced roles, and console themselves with their remaining honors. The younger members of the founding generation — those who have grown up in the court rather than in the desert — often find the transition easier. They have grown accustomed to the privileges of power without the Asabiyyah that originally justified them. They are becoming, in practice, court nobles rather than warriors.

Meanwhile, the dynasty is growing more elaborate. More courtiers. More ceremony. More titles. More administrative machinery. The ruler's household expands. His personal bodyguard grows. The distance between the ruler and the people he governs — including the people who elevated him to power — increases.

All of this is expensive. The dynasty that was born in simplicity must now support a court, a bureaucracy, a professional military force, and the elaborate physical infrastructure of settled power. Revenue requirements grow. Taxation increases. The populations under the dynasty's rule feel the extraction before they feel any benefit from the new administration.

But the most important cost is not financial. It is the cost to Asabiyyah.

When the glory of the dynasty was shared, each member of the founding group felt that the dynasty's success was his success. He had a personal stake in its survival. He would fight for it not because he was ordered to, but because it was his. When the glory is concentrated in the ruler alone, that personal stake disappears. The members of the founding group have become employees, at best — and, at worst, potential threats to be managed, marginalized, and eventually replaced.

The dynasty has traded Asabiyyah for autocracy. In the short term, this trade appears to work. The ruler is more powerful than before. Decision-making is faster. The court is more magnificent. Rivals within the founding group have been neutralized.

But the Asabiyyah was the dynasty's immune system. It was the thing that would have mobilized the best fighters, the most loyal supporters, the most capable administrators, in a genuine crisis. Having weakened it, the dynasty is now dependent on hired replacements — men who serve for pay rather than loyalty — and on the increasingly elaborate institutional machinery of a court that has grown wealthy enough to believe its own permanence.

The desert men who built this dynasty were replaceable by no one. The courtiers who have taken their place are replaceable by anyone.

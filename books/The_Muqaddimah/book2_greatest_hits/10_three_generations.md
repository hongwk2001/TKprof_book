# The Three Generations

*The complete arc. Ibn Khaldun states his most famous observation: that dynasties do not last beyond three generations. He explains precisely why, and precisely what happens in each generation.*

---

In the natural lifespan of human beings, a generation is approximately forty years — the point at which a person reaches full maturity and the peak of their powers. The lifespan of a dynasty follows a similar arithmetic: in the general rule, a dynasty does not survive beyond three generations.

Three generations. One hundred and twenty years. This is the typical arc from founding to collapse.

It is not a rigid law. Some dynasties last longer. Some collapse faster. The number is not a prophecy about any specific dynasty but an observation about the pattern that governs almost all of them. Ibn Khaldun, having spent decades inside the courts and councils of dynasties in various stages of this cycle, arrived at this number not as an abstraction but as an empirical finding — the average result of watching the same process unfold, again and again, across different civilizations and different eras.

Here is what happens in each generation.

**The First Generation**

The founders. They came from hardship — the desert, the steppe, the mountains, the margins of settled civilization. They are still living, in memory and in habit, by the code that shaped them: toughness, simplicity, absolute loyalty to the group, the willingness to endure anything in pursuit of the shared goal.

Their Asabiyyah is intact and fierce. The glory of their achievement is shared among the group. No single figure has yet accumulated enough personal power to push the others aside. Decisions are made collectively. The fighting capacity of the group is real, tested, and available.

The first generation built something from nothing. They know what it cost. They have not forgotten.

**The Second Generation**

The sons. They grew up watching their fathers build an empire. They inherited it. They did not, however, inherit the experience that built it. They know the desert only from their fathers' stories. They grew up in the courts and cities that the founders created, surrounded by the wealth and comfort that conquest makes possible.

They are not weak men. They are often capable administrators, clever politicians, effective managers of the complex apparatus they have inherited. They maintain what their fathers built. The dynasty under their stewardship is often at the height of its wealth and institutional sophistication.

But something has quietly shifted. The second generation has absorbed the habits of sedentary life. They dress more elaborately than their fathers. They eat better. They live in finer buildings. They have come to regard comfort as normal — as the natural condition of life — rather than as the extraordinary achievement that their fathers knew it to be.

They still remember, just barely, that the dynasty was built by fierce men through fierce effort. The memory holds. They are not yet wholly lost. But they are transitioning. They are becoming city people.

**The Third Generation**

The grandsons. They have no living memory of the desert, no memory of the struggle, no personal knowledge of what it cost to build what they have inherited. The Asabiyyah that their grandfathers forged through shared hardship and their fathers maintained through living memory is simply gone. They have grown up inside the luxury their grandfather seized and their father preserved, and they have no frame of reference that exists outside of it.

They are, in practical terms, dependent. They cannot fight for themselves. They rely on hired soldiers for protection. They rely on professional administrators to run the government. They rely on the accumulated wealth of previous generations to maintain the lifestyle they regard as their birthright. They contribute to none of this — they consume it.

More than this: they have lost the capacity to understand that they have lost something. The third generation genuinely believes that what they have is simply what ruling classes have — that the wealth, the luxury, the deference of those around them, the protection of hired armies — is simply the natural condition of rulers. They have no basis for comparison. They have never known anything else.

When a crisis comes — and it will come; it always comes — the third generation faces it without the tools to meet it. They cannot rally the group, because there is no group left to rally; the Asabiyyah dissolved a generation ago. They cannot lead the army, because they have not developed the qualities that make a man capable of commanding genuine loyalty under fire. They cannot endure the privations that a serious crisis demands, because their entire lives have been structured to prevent privation.

At this point, the dynasty is waiting to be replaced. It may still appear powerful. It may still have enormous wealth, vast territory, elaborate institutions. But the living substance that built it and maintained it — the collective will, the shared courage, the unconditional mutual loyalty of people who depend on each other — is gone.

The fourth generation, if there is one, is irrelevant. By the third generation, the dynasty has already ceased to be, in any meaningful sense, the thing it was.

Somewhere beyond the horizon, the next hungry group is watching.

# Why City Life Destroys Fighting Spirit

*The luxury trap. Ibn Khaldun explains how the very success of a civilization becomes the mechanism of its undoing.*

---

Luxury is not a reward that powerful civilizations enjoy while remaining powerful. It is a poison that works slowly, invisibly, and without a known antidote.

The symptoms are so gradual, and the early stages so pleasant, that civilizations rarely recognize what is happening to them until the process is irreversible. By the time the effects become visible — in the softness of the ruling class, in the unreliability of the army, in the collapse of the civic willingness to endure hardship — the damage has been accumulating for generations.

Ibn Khaldun's analysis of this process begins with a simple observation: the qualities required to build a civilization and the qualities produced by living in a successful civilization are opposite.

Building a civilization requires hardship, discipline, courage, and mutual dependence. The men who build great things — who conquer territory, establish order, create institutions — are shaped by conditions that demand these qualities. They are not comfortable. They are not safe. They are not separated from the consequences of failure by layers of wealth and institutional protection. They live with the reality of what failure means, and this reality makes them capable.

The civilization they build, if it succeeds, immediately begins creating conditions that erode those qualities. Wealth accumulates. Comfort becomes available. Safety becomes institutionalized. The children of the founders grow up in a world that the founders fought to create — a world specifically designed to remove the hardships that shaped the founders. This is not a failure of civilization. It is civilization achieving its purpose. The tragedy is that the purpose contains the seed of its own undoing.

The wealthy city dweller sleeps on soft beds. He eats well. He dresses elaborately. He has servants for tasks that desert men perform themselves. He has access to pleasures — music, wine, beautiful things — that the ascetic simplicity of a desert tribe would never have produced. None of this is bad in itself. Much of it is genuinely good. It is the accumulated fruit of human ingenuity and cooperative effort across generations.

But it produces a man who is, in the most literal sense, less capable of fighting.

Not less brave in the abstract — the city dweller may have a perfectly robust concept of courage and may genuinely admire it. What he lacks is the habituated, bodily capacity for hardship that the desert man develops through the unavoidable conditions of his life. The city dweller's body has not been trained by necessity. His mind has not developed the quiet tolerance for danger that comes from living with it daily. His social relationships do not depend on the absolute mutual trust that Asabiyyah requires, because his social relationships exist within a framework of laws and institutions that are supposed to manage conflicts and provide security.

He is, in the language of Ibn Khaldun's world, *civilized* — which means, precisely, that he has been separated from the conditions that produce fighters.

This matters not because fighting is the highest human activity, but because the ability to fight — or rather, the underlying qualities that make fighting possible: hardness, courage, mutual loyalty, willingness to sacrifice individual comfort for collective survival — are the same qualities that produce political will. A civilization that has lost these qualities has lost the will to defend itself. Not the desire to be defended, which remains strong. But the actual will to do what defending requires: to endure hardship, to act collectively despite individual risk, to sacrifice.

When that will is gone, the civilization becomes dependent on hired force. It pays others to defend it. The mercenaries are often capable individually. But they fight for pay, not for loyalty. When the pay is uncertain or the battle is going badly, the calculation changes. They are not willing to die for a city that is not their own, for a ruler who is not their leader, for a cause that is not their cause.

The city walls are still standing. The administrative machinery is still running. The wealth is still there — perhaps more concentrated than ever, since the wealthy always manage to preserve more of their assets than the poor do. But the living substance that once made all of it possible — the Asabiyyah, the shared willingness to stand together — has been consumed by the very prosperity it created.

What remains is a civilization that looks, from the outside, like it is still functioning. It is, in fact, waiting.

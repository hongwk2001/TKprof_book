# About the Source

*The Muqaddimah* is one of the longest and most ambitious works of social thought ever written. The passages in this book come from three of its seven parts. Here is the complete structure, so you know what you have read — and how much remains.

## How These Passages Were Rendered

To create this edition, we focused specifically on the chapters where Ibn Khaldun defines and explores his core theories of *Asabiyyah* (social cohesion) and the life cycle of empires. Rather than trying to awkwardly abridge the entire 711,000-word volume, we extracted the 14 most critical, continuous arguments that form the backbone of his political philosophy. 

These selected passages were then carefully rendered into plain, modern English. The goal was to remove the academic hedging and complex phrasing typical of historical translations, allowing Ibn Khaldun to speak directly to a modern audience. We preserved his original examples—such as Bedouin tribes and early Islamic caliphates—but presented them in a clear, accessible style that highlights the timeless, predictive nature of his observations.

## What This Book Covers

The fourteen passages collected here are drawn from three parts:

**The Author's Introduction** — where Ibn Khaldun explains why conventional history is unreliable and what a proper science of civilization would require. This is Passage 1.

**Book Two: Bedouin Civilization** — the heart of the Asabiyyah theory. Ibn Khaldun develops the concept from first principles, traces how desert solidarity enables conquest, and explains how religion amplifies it. Passages 2 through 6.

**Book Three: Dynasties, Royal Authority, the Caliphate** — the theory applied to the life cycle of states. How dynasties are born, how they mature and corrupt, how they collapse fiscally and militarily, and how the cycle restarts. Passages 7 through 14.

---

Books Four, Five, and Six are not represented here. Book Four deals with the physical geography of cities and territories. Book Five covers the economics of specific crafts and trades. Book Six — the largest section by far, at 28 hours of reading — is an encyclopedic treatment of the Islamic sciences: jurisprudence, Quranic recitation, Arabic grammar, logic, mathematics, astronomy, and the occult sciences.

These sections are of specialist interest. They belong to the 14th century in ways that Books Two and Three do not. The ideas in this collection are the ones that have outlasted their era. The rest of the *Muqaddimah* is evidence that Ibn Khaldun was a man of his time as well as ahead of it.

---

The standard English translation is Franz Rosenthal's three-volume edition, published by Princeton University Press (1958, revised 1967). It remains definitive.

Beowulf

Modern English Edition

Introduction to Beowulf

Welcome to one of the most important and foundational works of English literature. *Beowulf* is an epic poem composed in Old English, consisting of 3,182 alliterative lines. While the exact date of its composition is fiercely debated by scholars—estimates range from the 8th to the early 11th century—it stands as one of the oldest surviving long poems in Old English.

The story was originally passed down orally by "scops" (bards or poets) who would recite the epic in the mead-halls of Anglo-Saxon England. It was eventually written down by Christian monks, resulting in a fascinating blend of pagan Germanic heroic values—such as the pursuit of glory, vengeance, and the inescapable nature of fate ("wyrd")—intertwined with Christian moral commentary and references to the Almighty.

**The Miracle of Survival** The survival of *Beowulf* into the modern era is nothing short of a miracle. The poem exists in a single, fragile manuscript known as the Nowell Codex (officially Cotton MS Vitellius A XV), which dates to around the year 1000 AD. For centuries, the manuscript lay largely forgotten in various private libraries. In 1731, disaster struck when a fire broke out at Ashburnham House in London, where the manuscript was being housed. The edges of the *Beowulf* manuscript were scorched, and the brittle pages slowly began to crumble away over the years. Thankfully, two transcriptions were made in the late 18th century before the damage worsened, preserving the text for future generations.

**The Tale** The epic is set in Scandinavia in the 6th century and follows the heroic exploits of Beowulf, a champion of the Geatish people (from modern-day Sweden). The story unfolds in three major acts. In the first, the young and boastful Beowulf travels to Denmark to aid King Hrothgar, whose magnificent mead-hall, Heorot, is being terrorized by a demonic monster named Grendel. In the second act, Beowulf must dive into a terrifying, monster-infested mere to face an even deadlier foe: Grendel's vengeful mother. Finally, the story leaps forward fifty years. Beowulf, now the wise and aging King of the Geats, must face his ultimate test when a fiery dragon awakens to ravage his kingdom.

**About This Edition** Traditionally, *Beowulf* is translated and presented in verse form, maintaining the original's complex alliteration and rhythmic structure. While poetic translations capture the musicality of the original Old English, they can sometimes be challenging for modern readers to navigate.

This edition takes a different approach. We have adapted the epic poem into a modern, fast-paced prose narrative. By presenting *Beowulf* as a novel-like story—complete with standard paragraphs and modernized vocabulary—we aim to make this incredible adventure accessible to a wider audience, including middle-school readers, ESL learners, and audiobook listeners.

We invite you to step back into the Dark Ages, a time of legendary heroes, bloody feuds, and monsters lurking in the shadows. Enter the mead-hall, take your seat at the bench, and listen to the tale of Beowulf.

Chapter 1

Prologue: The First King, Scyld Scefing

Listen! We have all heard stories about the glory of the ancient Danish kings. In the old days, these noble rulers performed many brave deeds to protect their people and expand their power.

A famous king named Scyld Scefing was one of the greatest. He often captured the feast-halls of enemy tribes and terrified their warriors. Originally, he was just a helpless, abandoned child found floating on the sea. However, he grew up to overcome his early sorrow. He grew strong under the heavens and earned great honor. Eventually, every neighboring tribe across the sea had to obey him and pay him tribute in gold. He was a truly great king!

Later, Scyld had a son, a young boy sent by God to comfort the Danish people. The Lord saw how much the people had suffered during the long time they lived without a leader. Therefore, the Lord of Life granted the boy great honor on earth. This son was named Beow, and his fame spread far and wide across the Northern lands. In those days, a young prince was expected to be generous with gifts and gold while his father was still alive. This generosity earned him loyal companions who would stand by him and fight when war came. Generous deeds are the only way to earn the respect and loyalty of your warriors.

When his time came, Scyld died while he was still a strong and beloved ruler. He went to rest in the Lord’s protection. His close friends carried his body down to the seashore, just as he had ordered when he was still alive. A ship with a curved prow stood waiting at the harbor, covered in ice and ready to sail. It was a hero's vessel. The warriors laid their beloved king in the middle of the boat, placing the generous ruler right next to the mast. They surrounded his body with treasures, beautiful armor, and sharp weapons brought from far-off lands.

No one has ever seen a more beautiful ship than that funeral vessel. It was filled with swords, armor, and battle-gear. A wealth of treasure lay on the king's chest, destined to travel with him into the deep sea. The people gave him just as many treasures as the ones who had sent him away alone over the waves when he was a helpless child.

They also hung a golden banner high above his head to shine in the wind. Then, they let the tides carry the ship out into the dark ocean. Their hearts were heavy and full of grief as they watched it float away. No one can say for certain, not even the wisest counselors or the bravest warriors, where that ship finally landed.

Chapter One: The Great Hall, Heorot and the Monster, Grendel

For many years, Beow the Dane ruled the Danes as their beloved king, taking the throne after his father Scyld passed away. In time, Beow had a son named Half-Dane, who grew to be a fierce warrior and a wise ruler. Half-Dane governed the happy kingdom for the rest of his life. He had four children: Heorogar, Hrothgar, Halga the Good, and a daughter who married King Onela of Sweden.

Hrothgar became a very successful and glorious war leader. His loyal followers obeyed him eagerly, and his band of young warriors grew into a mighty army. Eventually, Hrothgar decided to build a magnificent feast-hall. He wanted it to be grander and more beautiful than any building ever built by man. In this hall, he planned to share all the gifts God had given him with his people, both young and old, keeping only the public land and the lives of his men for himself. He sent orders to tribes all over the world to help decorate this great building. Soon, the massive hall was finished, and Hrothgar named it Heorot. True to his word, the king handed out rings and treasures to his men at the feasts. The hall was high and beautiful, though it was destined to be destroyed by fire years later when a deadly family feud broke out.

However, a powerful demon living in the dark nearby suffered great pain. Every day, the monster had to listen to the loud sounds of joy and laughter coming from the hall. He heard the music of the harp and the clear songs of the poets. The poets sang about how the Almighty created the earth, making the bright plains and surrounding them with water. They sang about how God set the sun and moon to light the land for all living things. The king's men lived in happiness until a fiend from hell began to attack them. This grim monster was named Grendel. He was a hateful creature who wandered the borders of the kingdom, ruling the lonely moors, swamps, and wilderness. Grendel lived among monsters because God had cursed him as a descendant of Cain. When Cain murdered Abel, God banished him from mankind. From Cain came all the monsters and evil spirits of the world, including giants, dark spirits, and demons who fought against God until he finally destroyed them.

Chapter Two: The Terror of Grendel and the Sorrow of King Hrothgar

One night, Grendel went to the great hall to see what the Danish warriors did after a long evening of drinking. He found the noble warriors fast asleep, completely unaware of any danger or human misery. The fierce and hungry demon attacked immediately. He was savage and cruel, dragging thirty warriors out of their beds. He rushed back to his dark lair in the swamp, celebrating his loot and carrying the slaughtered bodies with him.

At dawn, the remaining warriors woke up and realized what Grendel had done. The joy of the feast was replaced by loud crying and deep grief. Hrothgar, their famous king, sat in deep misery. The strong leader suffered for his lost men when he saw the tracks left by the accursed demon. The struggle was too heavy, too disgusting, and went on for too long.

Only one night passed before Grendel returned to commit more murders. He felt no remorse for his crimes because he was too set in his evil ways. Soon, it was easy to find men who preferred to sleep elsewhere, far from the hall. Once the monster's hatred was made clear to everyone, anyone who survived kept themselves as far away from Heorot as possible to stay alive.

So Grendel ruled, fighting unjustly against everyone, until the beautiful hall stood empty. The Danish king suffered twelve long years of grief and sorrow. Soon, the sad story spread across the world. People sang songs about how Grendel fought Hrothgar, waging a hateful war season after season. The monster refused to make peace with the Danes, stop his attacks, or accept payments to end the feud. No one could expect a fair settlement from his hands. The terrible monster was like a dark shadow of death, hunting both young and old. Night after night, he ruled the misty moors, and no one knew where such demons wandered in the dark.

The enemy of mankind committed many terrible crimes and humiliations. During the dark nights, he lived inside the beautiful hall of Heorot. However, he was never allowed to touch the king's sacred throne, nor could he feel God's love, because he was completely cursed. This was a time of deep misery and heartbreak for Hrothgar. His nobles gathered in meetings, trying to figure out how to protect themselves. In their desperation, they even prayed at pagan shrines, begging their old gods to save them. That was their custom. They turned their thoughts to hell because they did not know the true Creator, the Judge of all deeds, or how to praise the Lord of Heaven.

Chapter Three: The Hero of Geatland, Beowulf

Hrothgar, the son of Half-Dane, constantly suffered from this deep sorrow. The wise king could not find a way to end the misery. The hardship on his people was too great, too disgusting, and had lasted far too long. It was a cruel persecution, and the worst nightmare the Danish people had ever faced.

Far away in Geatland, a brave warrior named Beowulf heard about Grendel’s terrible attacks. Beowulf was a loyal follower of King Hygelac, and he was the strongest, most powerful man alive in the world at that time. He ordered his men to prepare a strong ship for a sea journey. He announced that he would sail across the ocean to help Hrothgar, since the famous Danish king needed warriors to defend his land. Even though the wise men of his tribe loved Beowulf and wanted him to stay, they did not try to stop him. Instead, they encouraged the hero and looked for good omens to guide his journey.

Beowulf chose fourteen of the bravest Geatish warriors to go with him. Led by a skilled sailor, they marched down to the shore where their ship was waiting under the high cliffs. The warriors climbed aboard while the waves crashed against the sandy shore. They loaded their bright armor, shields, and weapons into the middle of the ship. Then, they pushed the strong vessel out into the open sea to begin their adventure.

Driven by the wind, the ship flew over the waves like a bird with foam on its neck. By the second day, they had sailed far enough to see the opposite shore. They saw shining sea-cliffs, steep hills, and broad headlands. The sea was crossed, and their journey was over. The Geats quickly stepped onto the beach and tied up their wooden ship to the shore. Their chain-mail armor rattled as they moved. They thanked God for giving them a safe and easy journey across the ocean.

The Danish coast guard, whose duty was to watch the sea-cliffs, saw them from the hills. He watched the strangers carry their bright shields and weapons down the gangway, and he wanted to know who they were. Riding his horse down to the shore, Hrothgar's guard shook his massive spear and challenged them. "Who are you, armed men in chain-mail, who have sailed a tall ship across the ocean? I am the coast guard, and I watch the sea so that no enemy ships can raid the Danish land. No one has ever landed here so openly, and you do not have our passwords or permission. I have never seen a greater warrior on earth than your leader. He is no ordinary guard; his face and bearing show he is a true noble. Before you go any further into Denmark, I must know who your families are so I know you are not spies. Tell me quickly where you came from."

Chapter Four: The Danish Coast-Guard and the Arrival of Beowulf

The leader of the band spoke first. "We are Geats, and we are loyal followers of King Hygelac. My father was a famous warrior named Ecgtheow. He lived a long life before dying at home, and wise men everywhere still remember him. We have come with friendly intentions to seek your lord, Hrothgar, the king of the Danes. Please guide us. We have an important message for your king, and we have nothing to hide. You know if the stories are true. We heard that a mysterious monster is terrorizing the Danes, bringing slaughter and humiliation in the dark nights. I want to offer Hrothgar good advice on how to defeat this demon. I want to help him find relief so his mind can finally rest. Otherwise, he will continue to suffer as long as his beautiful hall stands."

The guard spoke from his horse. "A good warrior must know the difference between what men say and what they actually do. I believe that you are friendly to the Danish king. Go forward with your weapons, and I will guide you to his court. I will also order my young men to guard your ship on the sand. They will keep it safe until you are ready to sail back to Geatland. A warrior who acts with such bravery will surely survive the coming battle."

They set out toward the hall. Their ship remained anchored, resting on its cable near the shore. The warriors wore helmets adorned with golden figures of wild boars, which were meant to protect their lives in battle. The men marched quickly until they saw the beautiful, gold-decorated hall. It was the most famous building in the world, and its light shone over many lands. The guard pointed out the bright hall so the men could march straight to it. Then, the guard turned his horse and said, "It is time for me to return to the shore. May the Almighty Father keep you safe on your journey. I must go back to watch for enemy raiders."

Chapter Five: The King's Herald, Wulfgar

A stone-paved street guided the Geatish warriors toward the royal hall of Heorot. Their hand-linked chain-mail armor clinked softly as they marched. Tired from their long journey across the sea, they placed their heavy shields against the wall of the hall. The men sat down on the wooden benches, their armor ringing as they rested. Their spears, made of strong ash wood with gray iron tips, were stacked together in a corner. These warriors were exceptionally well equipped for war.

A proud Danish warrior stepped forward to meet them. He asked about their families and their purpose: "Where did you get these decorated shields, gray mail-shirts, helmets, and spears? I am Hrothgar's herald and officer. I have never seen a group of strangers who looked so brave. I assume you have come to seek Hrothgar out of pride and courage, not because you were exiled from your home."

Beowulf, the proud leader of the Geats, answered him calmly from beneath his helmet: "We are followers of King Hygelac, and my name is Beowulf. I want to explain our mission to Hrothgar, your famous king, if he will allow us to meet with him."

Wulfgar, a chief of the Wendels who was famous for his bravery and wisdom, spoke next: "I will carry your message to the Danish king. I will ask about your request and return quickly with whatever answer the king decides to give you."

Wulfgar turned and walked to where Hrothgar sat, old and gray-haired, surrounded by his trusted nobles. Wulfgar walked up and stood directly before the king, following the proper customs of the court. He said: "Some warriors from Geatland have just arrived. They have sailed across the ocean, and their leader is named Beowulf. They are asking to speak with you, gracious Hrothgar. Please do not refuse them. Judging by their armor, they are worthy of great respect, and the chief who led them here is clearly a powerful leader."

Chapter Six: The Meeting with King Hrothgar

Hrothgar, the king of the Danes, answered Wulfgar's message: "I knew Beowulf when he was just a young boy! His father was Ecgtheow, and his mother was the daughter of King Hrethel of the Geats. Now, Ecgtheow's brave son has come here, seeking a loyal friend. The sailors who brought our gifts to Geatland told me that this brave warrior has the strength of thirty men in his grip. I believe holy God has sent this hero to protect us Danes from the terror of Grendel. I will offer him rich treasures for his outstanding courage. Hurry! Tell the Geats to come in and meet our nobles. Let them know they are warmly welcomed by the Danish people."

Wulfgar went to the door of the hall and delivered the message to the guests: "My lord, the king of the Danes, knows of your noble family. He says that you are welcome here after your long journey across the sea. You may go inside wearing your armor and helmets to meet Hrothgar, but you must leave your shields and spears outside until your meeting is finished."

Beowulf stood up, surrounded by his impressive band of warriors. As he ordered, a few men stayed behind to guard their weapons. The rest hurried forward, led by Wulfgar into the great hall of Heorot. Beowulf marched proudly beneath his helmet until he stood directly before the king.

Chapter Seven: Beowulf's Pledge to Fight Grendel

Beowulf spoke, his chain-mail armor gleaming like a net hand-woven by a skilled blacksmith: "Hail to you, Hrothgar! I am King Hygelac's nephew and warrior. I have already performed many glorious deeds in my youth. News of Grendel reached me in my homeland. Seafarers say that this great hall stands empty and useless as soon as the evening light fades beneath the sky.

My wise and noble people advised me to seek you out, King Hrothgar, because they knew the strength of my Might. They watched me return from battles stained with the blood of my enemies. They saw me capture five giants, destroy their clan, and hunt sea-monsters in the ocean at night. I endured terrible hardships to avenge my people, crushing their enemies. Now, I will face the monster Grendel alone.

Therefore, I ask you one favor, king of the Danes. Do not refuse me now that I have traveled so far. Allow me and my men alone to clear out your hall of Heorot. I hear that the monster is so reckless that he does not even use weapons. To make my uncle, King Hygelac, proud, I refuse to use a sword or shield. Instead, I will fight the fiend with my bare hands, struggling for life face-to-face. Whoever dies must accept the judgment of the Lord.

If the monster wins, I expect he will fearlessly eat my Geatish warriors in this hall, just as he has devoured your own brave men. If I die, you will not need to bury me. The monster will carry my blood-stained body away to the moors. The lonely beast will eat it ruthlessly and stain his lair in the swamp. You will not have to worry about my funeral.

But if I fall in battle, send my chain-mail armor back to King Hygelac. It is a family treasure from King Hrethel, crafted by the legendary blacksmith Weland. Fate will always go as it must!"

Chapter Eight: Hrothgar's Past and the Feast at Heorot

King Hrothgar replied: "You have come to us, my friend Beowulf, to fight in our defense and out of kindness. Your family has a long history with mine. Long ago, your father Ecgtheow started a massive feud when he killed Heatholaf of the Wylfing tribe. Fearing a bloody war, the Geats could not shelter your father, so he fled across the ocean waves to seek help from the Danes. At that time, I was a young king newly ruling the Danish people. My older brother, Heorogar, had just died, and he was a better man than I. To end your father's feud and save him, I sent rich treasures across the sea to the Wylfings. In return, your father swore an oath of loyalty to me.

It brings me great pain to tell anyone about the humiliation and ruin Grendel has caused us in Heorot. My warriors and guards are vanishing. Fate has swept them away into the monster's horror. Yet, God can easily stop this mad ravager! Many times, my warriors have gotten drunk on beer and sworn to wait in the feast-hall with their swords to fight Grendel. But when morning came, this beautiful hall was always stained with gore, and the benches were drenched in blood. Death has taken my trusted men away, leaving me with fewer veterans every day.

But sit down now to our feast! Relax and tell us stories of your victories, as your heart prompts you."

A bench was quickly cleared for the Geats in the feast-hall. The brave warriors sat down, proud of their strength. A servant walked around, carrying an ornate cup and pouring sweet mead. Occasionally, a poet sang with a clear voice in Heorot. There was great joy and laughter among the gathered Danes and Geats.

Chapter Nine: The Jealous Courtier, Unferth

Unferth, the son of Ecglaf, sat at the feet of King Hrothgar. He was deeply jealous of Beowulf's fame and decided to start a quarrel to humiliate him. Beowulf's arrival and his brave journey across the sea annoyed the courtier. Unferth simply could not stand the thought of anyone else in the world achieving more glory than himself.

"Are you the same Beowulf who raced against Breca in a swimming match on the open sea?" Unferth challenged. "The two of you risked your lives in the deep water out of pride, making a foolish boast! No one could convince you to stop your reckless journey. You swam through the ocean streams, fighting the cold, wintry waves as the sea boiled with surges. You both struggled in the freezing water for seven nights, but Breca beat you. He was stronger! By morning, the current washed him ashore in the land of the Heathoreams. From there, he returned to his home, where he ruled the Brondings and their wealthy city. Breca fully accomplished his boast against you!

Therefore, I expect you to fail here, even though you have survived many battles. You will not survive one whole night waiting for Grendel in Heorot!"

Chapter Ten: Beowulf's Swimming Contest with Breca

Beowulf, the son of Ecgtheow, replied: "Well, my friend Unferth, you have talked a lot about Breca and our race, mostly because you are drunk on beer! But I tell you the truth: I was much stronger in the water and endured far more hardships in the waves than any other man.

When we were boys, we made a boast to risk our lives out on the open ocean. And we did exactly that. As we swam, we held drawn swords in our hands to defend ourselves against dangerous sea beasts. Breca could not swim away from me, nor was he faster in the water, and I stayed right beside him. We swam side-by-side for five nights until a terrible storm drove us apart. Churning waves, freezing cold weather, and a dark night crashed against us. A fierce north wind turned against us, and the waves grew dangerously rough.

The sea monsters grew angry. Fortunately, my hand-linked chain-mail armor protected me from their attacks. A deadly beast pulled me to the bottom of the ocean, holding me tight in his grip. But I managed to stab the monster with the point of my sword. I killed the mighty sea beast by my own hand, freeing myself.

Other monsters constantly attacked me in the dark, but I fought back with my sword as best I could. They did not get to enjoy a feast at the bottom of the sea with my body. Instead, by morning, they lay dead along the shore, killed by my blade. They would never again block the paths of sailors across the deep ocean.

Then, the sun rose in the east, God's bright light. The waves calmed down, and I could see the windy cliffs of the coast. Fate often saves a man who is not yet fated to die, if his courage is strong! In that fight, I killed nine sea monsters with my sword. I have never heard of a harder night battle under the sky, or of a man in worse danger in the ocean. Yet, I escaped with my life, although I was completely exhausted. The currents eventually washed me ashore in the land of the Finns.

I have never heard of you fighting in such terrifying sword battles, Unferth! Neither you nor Breca have ever done anything so daring in combat, though I do not mean to boast. But the truth is, you murdered your own brothers, your close relatives! Because of that crime, you will suffer in hell, no matter how clever you think you are.

I tell you the truth, Unferth: Grendel would never have caused so much horror and humiliation to your king if you were as fierce in battle as you claim to be. But Grendel has learned that he does not need to fear the Danes or their weapons. He does whatever he wants, slaughtering and feasting on the Danish people, expecting no fight from them. But very soon, I will show him the strength and courage of the Geats! After that, when the bright morning sun shines again, anyone who wishes will be able to enter this feast-hall in peace!"

Chapter Eleven: Queen Wealhtheow and the Night Watch

Laughter and joyful voices filled the hall, and the conversations were cheerful. Hrothgar's queen, Wealhtheow, came forward. She was dressed in gold and followed the courtly customs, greeting the men in the hall. First, the noble queen offered the drinking cup to Hrothgar, wishing him joy and reminding him how much his people loved him. The victorious king happily drank from the cup and enjoyed the feast.

Then, Queen Wealhtheow walked through the hall, offering the jeweled cup to both the young warriors and the veterans. Finally, the wise queen carried the cup to Beowulf. She greeted the leader of the Geats, thanking God in her wisdom that her prayers had been answered, and that she had found a hero she could trust to protect her people from these terrible crimes.

Beowulf, the fierce warrior, accepted the cup from Queen Wealhtheow. Eager for battle, he declared: "When I set sail on the ocean with my men, I resolved to fulfill the wishes of your people or die trying, crushed in the monster's grip. I will prove my courage as a hero, or I will meet my death tonight in this feast-hall!"

These brave words pleased the queen. Adorned in gold, she walked back and went to sit beside her husband, the king.

Once again, the hall was filled with brave words, and the people were happy. But soon, Hrothgar decided it was time to retire for the night. He knew that Grendel had been planning to attack the great hall as soon as the sun went down and dark, shadowy figures came stalking under the clouds.

The company stood up. Hrothgar greeted Beowulf, wishing him luck and putting him in charge of the feast-hall. The king said: "Never before, since I first lifted a shield, have I entrusted the great hall of the Danes to any other man. But tonight, I entrust it to you! Protect this beautiful hall! Remember your glory, show your courage, and watch out for the enemy. You will want for nothing if you survive this fight!"

Chapter Twelve: The Attack of Grendel

Then, King Hrothgar departed from the feast-hall with his band of heroes. The leader went to join Queen Wealhtheow in her bedchamber, leaving Heorot behind. But God had set a guard in the hall to watch for Grendel. Beowulf took up this special duty for the king of the Danes, keeping watch in the dark for the monster.

The leader of the Geats trusted firmly in his own great strength and in God's favor. He took off his iron mail-shirt and his helmet, stripping himself of all protection. He handed his decorated sword, the finest of iron blades, to his servant and ordered him to guard the gear. Before going to bed, Beowulf made a final boast to his men: "I do not consider myself any weaker in battle than Grendel! Therefore, I will not kill him with a sword, even though I easily could. Grendel knows nothing of swords or shields. He does not know how to strike back or block a blow, despite his infamous cruelty. Tonight, we will not use swords if he dares to fight without weapons. Let wise God, the Holy Lord, award victory to whichever side he chooses."

Beowulf lay down to rest, placing his cheek against the pillow. Around him, his brave sea warriors fell asleep on their beds. None of them expected to ever see their beloved homeland again, or the towns where they were raised. They knew too well how many Danish warriors bloody death had already swept away in this hall. But God wove a destiny of victory for the Geats, granting them comfort and help. They would completely overcome their enemy through the strength of one man, through Beowulf's own Might. The truth is clear: Almighty God has always ruled over mankind.

In the dark night, Grendel came gliding down from the misty hills. The warriors who were supposed to guard the great hall fell asleep, all except one. The men knew that the demon could not drag them down into the shadows unless God allowed it. But Beowulf stayed awake, watching in anger. With a beating heart, he waited for the battle to begin.

Chapter Thirteen: The Battle in the Hall

Grendel marched from the moors beneath the misty cliffs, carrying God's anger. The wicked monster planned to capture and kill someone in the great hall. He walked under the clouds until he saw the feast-hall, the gold-decorated home of men, shining in the dark. This was not the first time he had visited Hrothgar's home, but never in his life, before or since, did he encounter worse luck or a tougher guard!

The joyless creature reached the building. The door, reinforced with forged iron bands, burst open the moment he touched it. Driven by evil rage, he tore open the entrance of the hall. Instantly, the demon stepped onto the decorated floor, moving angrily. A horrible light, looking like a flame, gleamed from his eyes. He saw many heroes in the hall, a sleeping band of relatives and warriors gathered together. Grendel laughed in his heart, planning to take the lives of every single one of them before daybreak, expecting a massive feast.

But fate would not allow Grendel to eat anyone else after that night. Beowulf, the nephew of Hygelac, watched carefully to see how the monster would attack. Grendel did not wait. For his first move, he quickly seized a sleeping warrior, tore him apart, bit through his bones, drank the blood from his veins, and swallowed huge bites. Soon, he had completely devoured the dead man, even his hands and feet, leaving nothing behind.

Stepping closer, Grendel reached out his hand to seize Beowulf on his bed. But as the demon reached for him, Beowulf grabbed Grendel's hand and propped himself up on his arm, locking him in a tight grip. As soon as the monster felt Beowulf's grip, he realized he had never met a man with a stronger grip in all the world! Terrified, Grendel wanted to escape, but he could not break free. He only wanted to flee into the darkness and hide with other devils. His experience in Heorot was unlike anything he had ever faced before.

Chapter Fourteen: The Victory and Grendel's Torn Arm

Then, Beowulf remembered his boast from the evening. He stood upright and grabbed the monster tightly. Grendel's fingers cracked under the pressure. The giant tried to pull away, but the hero stepped closer, keeping his grip tight. The monster wanted to escape and flee to his swamp home, knowing his fingers were caught in a terrifying grasp. It was a disastrous journey the destroyer had made to Heorot!

The hall echoed with the noise of the struggle. Complete panic fell upon all the Danes and their brave warriors. Both of the fierce combatants were furious, and the building shook. It was a miracle that the beautiful feast-hall withstood the fight and did not collapse to the ground. But it was firmly reinforced inside and out with iron bands, forged with incredible skill. Many gold-decorated benches were torn from the floor during the struggle. The wise Danes had never thought that anyone could break the building apart or destroy it, unless it was swallowed by fire.

A strange and terrifying sound rose up again and again. Panic struck the Danes, hitting everyone who heard the wailing through the wall. God's enemy sang a terrifying song of defeat, a joyless cry of wailing pain from the captive of hell. Beowulf, the strongest man alive, held him fast.

Beowulf had no intention of letting the murderous monster escape alive, as his life was useless to anyone on earth. Many of Beowulf's warriors drew their ancestral swords, wanting to protect the life of their lord. They tried to strike from every side to kill Grendel, but they did not know the truth. No sword on earth, not even the finest iron weapon, could harm the sinful monster! The beast had cast a spell on all weapons and blades, making them useless against him.

Chapter Fifteen: The Defeat of Grendel

Grendel's death was destined to be miserable, and his evil spirit was bound for hell. Chapter Thirty: Hrothgar's Sermon on Pride

The wise Hrothgar spoke, and everyone in the feast-hall fell silent to listen: "Indeed, any leader who rules with truth and justice, guarding his homeland and remembering the past, can say that this champion was born a noble man. I have told you this story as an old king wise with many winters.

Sometimes He allows a noble ruler to enjoy earthly delights, giving him peace in his home and a strong fortress to govern. Guard yourself against such pride, dear Beowulf, best of men, and choose the path of eternal rewards. Do not let pride rule you, famous champion! Now your Might is glorious for a little while, but soon sickness or the sword will rob you of your strength. Fire, flood, a sharp blade, a flying spear, or bitter old age will eventually overtake you. The brightness of your eyes will fade, and death will soon conquer you, as it conquers all men."

Chapter Thirty-One: The Farewell Feast

Hrothgar continued: "Thus I ruled the Danes under the heavens for fifty years, protecting them in war with spears and swords against many tribes across the world. Beowulf, weary from his journey and his underwater battle, also felt a great desire to rest. Chapter Thirty-Two: The Departure and Hrothgar's Tears

Beowulf, the son of Ecgtheow, spoke: "Now we Seafarers, having come from afar, wish to say that we are eager to return to our lord, King Hygelac. The old king kissed the great hero and clasped him around the neck, weeping as he did so. The wise king had a strong feeling that they would never see each other again. Beowulf was so dear to him that Hrothgar could not restrain his sorrow, and a deep, secret longing for the hero filled his heart as the Geat walked away.

Beowulf departed, a proud warrior adorned with gold and rejoicing in his treasure. The ship rode at anchor, waiting for its owner. Queen Modthryth, on the other hand, was notorious in the old days for her pride and terrible crimes. No warrior in the court, except her husband, dared to look directly into her eyes during the day. Chapter Thirty-Four: Beowulf's Report to King Hygelac

The brave hero walked across the wide sandy shore with his chosen warriors. The sun, the world's candle, shone brightly as it hurried from the south. Hygelac questioned his companion, eager to hear of the Geats' adventures: "How did your journey go, beloved Beowulf, when you decided to seek battle far across the salt water at Heorot? Beowulf, the son of Ecgtheow, spoke: "It is no secret to anyone, Lord Hygelac, how Grendel and I fought in that hall. Grendel had brought endless sorrow and lifelong misery to the Danes, but I avenged all of it. Chapter Thirty-Five: The Geatish Feud and the Gifts of Treasure

"An old warrior, seeing the ancient sword and remembering the slaughter of his people, will speak over the honey-wine. Therefore, I do not believe the Heathobards' alliance is sincere, or their friendship with the Danes secure.

Beowulf then told Hygelac the entire story of his struggle with Grendel, and his dangerous journey to the bottom of the lake to slay Grendel's mother.

Chapter Thirty-Six: The Fifty-Year Reign and the Dragon's Fire

A dragon ruled in the dark nights, guarding a treasure hoard in a steep stone mound on a high heath. A secret path lay beneath it, unknown to men. The neighboring people soon felt the beast's terrible rage as it sought revenge.

The thief had not broken into the dragon's hoard intentionally. He was a slave fleeing from his master's harsh blows, desperate for shelter. Once inside, the guilt-ridden man was gripped by terror. Yet in his panic, the miserable slave grabbed the precious gold cup and fled back into the night.

There were many ancient treasures hidden in that stone chamber. A noble survivor of an ancient family had buried the immense legacy of his people there in the days of old. Death had taken all of his relatives, and he was the last warrior left to mourn his friends. Realizing he would only enjoy the wealth for a short time, he built a strong mound near the sea waves, secured by difficult paths. The treasure keeper carried the massive wealth of gold and jewels inside, speaking these words:

"Hold now, O earth, since heroes cannot, the wealth of nobles! Brave men first won it from you. Death in battle has taken every warrior of my people. They have seen their last joy in the feast-hall. I have no one left to carry the sword or polish the gold cup. The warriors have departed. The hard helmet decorated with gold must lose its ornaments, for the men who polished the war masks now sleep in death. The battle armor that withstood the bite of iron on the shield field decays with the warrior. The chain mail cannot travel with the leader. There is no joy in the harp, no song, no hawk sweeping through the hall, and no swift horse galloping in the courtyard. Baleful death has swept our people away."

Sad in mind, he mourned his sorrow alone. He wept day and night until death finally claimed him.

The dragon, a beast of the twilight, found the hoard standing open. He is the one who flies through the night wrapped in fire, searching for burial mounds. Earth-dwellers fear him greatly. It is his nature to seek out gold in the earth and guard it, even though he gains nothing from it.

The terror of the people guarded the huge underground treasure house for three hundred years. But then the thief angered him. The slave carried the gold cup to his master, begging for a treaty of peace. His lord examined the ancient cup, and the slave was pardoned.

When the dragon awoke, his fury was renewed. He sniffed along the stone and found the footprints of the thief, who had slipped past the beast's head in secret. A fortunate man whom God protects can easily survive exile and woe. The dragon searched the ground eagerly, wanting to find the man who had robbed him while he slept. Hot and fierce-tempered, he circled the outside of the mound, but the wasteland was empty. He turned back into the mound, only to confirm that someone had stolen the golden cup.

The dragon waited impatiently for evening to fall. Bursting with anger, the beast resolved to repay the theft of the cup with fire. When the sun set, the dragon flew forth, breathing flames. This was a terrifying beginning for the people of the land, and it would soon bring sorrow to their king.

He hurried back to his hoard and his dark chamber before dawn. He had engulfed the land in fire and flame. He trusted in his mound, his power, and his walls, but that trust deceived him in the end.

The news of the disaster quickly reached Beowulf. His own home, the best of buildings and the royal throne of the Geats, had melted in the waves of fire. It was a heavy heart-sorrow for the king, the greatest grief he had ever known. The wise king feared he had bitterly angered the Ruler, the Eternal Lord, by breaking some ancient law. His breast boiled with dark thoughts, which was not his usual custom. The fire-dragon had destroyed the people's fortress and their coastal fortress. For this, the king planned his revenge.

The king ordered a magnificent battle-shield to be made entirely of iron. He knew that wooden shields could not help him against the dragon's flames. The noble prince was destined to reach the end of his days in this world, and the dragon was also fated to die with him. The proud king scorned to seek the dragon with a large army. He did not dread the battle, nor did he count the dragon's warfare, its Might, and its courage as any threat. In the past, Beowulf had survived many hostile clashes and battle-crashes, ever since he had cleansed Hrothgar's feast-hall and crushed Grendel's monstrous kin.

Not the least of his battles was the one in which Hygelac was slain. The king of the Geats died in Frisia from deadly sword blows on the battlefield. Beowulf escaped by his own strength, performing a legendary feat of swimming. He carried thirty sets of armor on his arm when he plunged into the sea. The enemy warriors who fought him had no reason to boast, for very few returned home after facing that fierce war-wolf.

Beowulf swam across the wide sea, lonely and grieving, back to his people. There, Queen Hygd offered him the treasure and the kingdom, the rings and the throne. She did not believe her young son, Heardred, could defend the land against foreign enemies now that Hygelac was dead. But the people could not persuade the noble Beowulf to become king over Heardred. Chapter Thirty-Eight: The March to the Barrow

When King Onela heard that his rebellious nephews had found refuge with the Geats, he became furious. He launched a massive invasion, marching his army into Geatland to punish the king. In the fierce battle that followed, King Heardred was struck down and killed by a Swedish sword. With his revenge complete, King Onela marched his army back to Sweden. This left the throne of the Geats empty, and Beowulf was chosen to rule. Beowulf took the crown and became a great and wise king, guiding his people in peace for fifty years.

In the years that followed, Beowulf never forgot the murder of King Heardred, and he quietly planned to avenge his death. Beowulf supported Eadgils' claim to the throne by sending a large Geatish army and powerful weapons across the sea. King Hrethel sang a sorrowful mourning song, retreating to his bed. After losing his eldest son, Herebeald, his home and fields felt empty. The old king carried a deep sorrow. He could not avenge the death upon the killer, nor could he show his anger, even though he no longer loved the boy's brother, Haethcyn. The champion arose, holding his shield and wearing his helmet and chain mail beneath the stone cliffs. He trusted in his own strength, as a coward never would. He saw a stone arch and a boiling stream rushing out from the mound. The water was hot with battle fire, and no one could approach the hoard without being burned by the dragon's flames.

On this day, for the first time in his life, he had to fight without Fate granting him victory. The king raised his hand and struck the beast with his ancient sword, but the blade slipped on the bone and failed to bite deep enough.

Furious after the blow, the dragon spewed deadly fire far and wide. The king could not boast of victory, for his sword had failed him, as good iron never should. It was a hard destiny for Beowulf to leave this earth and go to a foreign home, as all men must eventually leave this fleeting life.

It was not long before the combatants met again. The dragon took heart, breathing fire once more, while the king suffered in the heat. None of his noble companions stood by him to help. Instead, they fled into the woods to save their lives. Only one warrior felt sorrow in his heart, remembering the unbreakable family bonds.

Chapter Forty: The Loyal Companion, Wiglaf

His name was Wiglaf, the son of Weohstan, a prized shield-warrior, a nobleman of the Swedish royal family, and a relative of Aelfhere. He saw his king suffering from the intense heat beneath his battle mask. Wiglaf remembered the honors that Beowulf had given him: the rich estate of the Waegmundings and all the rights his father had possessed. He could not hold back. His hand seized his yellow wooden shield, and he drew his ancient sword.

That sword was a legendary family treasure, once owned by Eanmund, the son of Ohthere. King Onela had given Weohstan that armor, which was Eanmund's war gear, even though Eanmund was Onela's own nephew. Onela never spoke of avenging his nephew, because Weohstan had slain him. Weohstan kept the sword and mail-shirt for many years, until his son Wiglaf was old enough to perform heroic deeds. When Weohstan died, full of years, he left his son countless war garments and weapons.

This was the first time the young warrior was to fight beside his noble lord. His courage did not melt, nor did his father's sword fail him in the battle. The dragon soon discovered this when they clashed.

Wiglaf spoke to his companions with a sorrowful heart, urging them to do what was right: "I remember the time we drank honey-wine in the feast-hall, promising our king that we would repay him for our armor, helmets, and swords if he ever needed us. He chose us for this journey from his entire army, judging us worthy of glory. He gave us these treasures because he trusted us as brave warriors, even though our king intended to fight this monster alone. He has accomplished the most glorious deeds and daring acts of any man.

Now the day has come when our leader needs our help. Let us go to him and help our king while the terrible fire lasts! God knows I would rather the fire consume my body alongside my king. It is not honorable for us to carry our shields back home unless we first defeat the foe and defend the life of our king. I know well that his past deeds do not deserve to leave him suffering alone. The sword, the helmet, and the mail-shirt must be shared between us!"

Wiglaf waded through the deadly fumes, carrying his helmet to help his king. He spoke brief words: "Beloved Beowulf, fight well, just as you promised in your youth. You swore that you would never let your glory fade while you lived. Now, famous champion, you must defend your life with all your Might! I will stand by you."

The angry dragon came charging a second time, breathing surging fire to attack his enemies. Wiglaf's wooden shield burned away in the flames, and his mail-shirt offered him no protection. But the young man bravely stepped behind his relative's iron shield once his own was destroyed.

Then the war king remembered his glory once more. He struck a mighty blow with his sword, driving it into the dragon's head. But Naegling snapped! Beowulf's sword failed him in the fight. It was not his destiny that iron blades should help him. His hand was too strong, and he shattered every sword he swung in battle. Carrying a strong weapon to fight did not benefit him.

Chapter Forty-One: The Death of the Dragon

The fierce fire-dragon, the terror of the Geats, renewed the feud for a third time. Rushing at the brave hero when the opportunity arose, the hot, battle-grim monster clamped its sharp teeth around Beowulf's neck. The king was covered in his own life-blood, which welled up in torrents, staining his armor.

Then, the noble Wiglaf, standing by his king in his hour of need, displayed the great courage and skill that were natural to him. He did not strike at the dragon's head, but to help his relative, his hand was badly burned as he plunged his gold-adorned sword into the beast's belly. The blow struck deep, and the dragon's fire immediately began to fade.

The king regained his senses and drew a sharp battle-knife from his mail-shirt. With a swift stroke, the king sliced the serpent in half. They had defeated the enemy, driving the life out of the beast. The two noble relatives had destroyed the dragon together. Such should a warrior be in times of need!

But for Beowulf, this was his final hour of victory. The wound that the dragon had given him began to burn and swell, and he soon felt the deadly poison boiling in his chest. The king walked to a seat by the wall, keeping his wits about him. He gazed upon the giants' ancient architecture, seeing how the stone arches inside the chamber were supported securely by pillars.

The loyal Wiglaf washed the famous prince, bathing his bloody wounds with water and unfastening his helmet to let him breathe.

Beowulf spoke, despite his painful, mortal wound. He knew well that he had lived his portion of earthly joy. His days were done, and death was close at hand.

"Now I would have given my armor to my son, if God had granted me an heir of my own blood to succeed me. I have ruled this people for fifty years. No king of any neighboring nation dared to threaten me with war or terror. I waited for my appointed time on earth, ruled my kingdom justly, sought no dishonest quarrels, and swore no false oaths. Because of this, I can rejoice even as I die of these wounds. The Creator of Men will have no reason to accuse me of killing my own relatives when my soul leaves my body.

Now go quickly, beloved Wiglaf, and examine the hoard under the gray stone, now that the dragon lies dead and deprived of his treasure. Hurry, so that I may look upon the ancient wealth, the gold, and the bright gems. Seeing these treasures will make it easier for me to give up my life and the kingdom I have ruled for so long."

Chapter Forty-Two: The Treasure Hoard Revealed

Wiglaf quickly obeyed his wounded and battle-sick king. Wearing his chain mail and battle-shirt, he entered the stone chamber. The brave warrior, rejoicing in victory, saw many precious jewels as he walked past the pillars. Gold glistened on the ground, and wonderful wall-hangings decorated the dragon's den. He saw ancient pitchers, the vessels of long-forgotten men of old, rusty helmets, and skillfully twisted golden armbands.

Treasure and gold buried in the earth can easily corrupt any man, no matter who hides it, for gold tempts the hearts of the living.

Wiglaf also saw a golden banner hanging high over the hoard, a masterpiece of hand-weaving. A bright light shone from the banner, illuminating the floor so he could examine the treasures. There was no sign of the dragon, for the sword had killed it.

Thus, Wiglaf plundered the hoard inside the mound, filling his arms with cups and dishes of his own choosing. He also took the golden banner, the brightest of beacons. Beowulf's iron blade had already slain the monster that had guarded the treasures for so long, breathing fire at midnight until he met a violent death.

Wiglaf hurried back, eager to return and show the treasures to his king. He wondered if he would find Beowulf still alive on the cliff where he had left him, growing weaker by the minute. He found his beloved lord bloody and near death. Wiglaf splashed him with water once again, until the old king gathered his strength to speak.

Chapter Forty-Three: Beowulf's Death and the Mourning of the Geats

The dying king spoke in sorrow, gazing upon the gold: "I offer my deepest thanks to the Lord of All, the King of Glory, the Eternal Lord, for these treasures. I am grateful that I could win this wealth for my people before my death. Since I have traded my life for this hoard, you must look after the needs of the Geats. I can remain here no longer.

Command the brave warriors to build a magnificent mound on the cliff by the sea, after the funeral fire has cooled. It shall tower high on the Whale's Ness as a monument, so that Seafarers who guide their tall ships through the mists of the ocean will call it Beowulf's Barrow in the days to come."

The brave king took the golden collar from his neck and gave it to Wiglaf, the young warrior, along with his gold-decorated helmet, his ring, and his mail-shirt, commanding him to use them well.

"You are the last of our family, the Waegmundings! Fate has swept all my relatives away to their destiny, those brave warriors in their valor. Now I must follow them."

Those were the last words from the old king's breast before his body was committed to the hot funeral flames. His soul departed to seek the judgment of the righteous.

It was a sorrowful moment for the young Wiglaf, who had to look upon his beloved lord lying lifeless on the earth. The dragon lay dead beside him, destroyed in the ruin. The coiled dragon could no longer guard the treasure hoard, for the sharp iron blades had slain him, leaving the beast lifeless on the sand near his cave. Never again would he fly through the night, showing his fiery form in the sky. Wiglaf had stilled him with his sword. Indeed, few men in the land, no matter how strong or brave, had ever succeeded in facing the poison breath of a dragon or entering his stone chamber if the guardian was awake. The golden treasure was bought with Beowulf's death, and both combatants had reached the end of their lives.

Soon, the ten cowardly warriors who had fled into the woods returned. They had not dared to fight with their spears when their lord needed them most. Carrying their shields and weapons in shame, they walked to where Wiglaf sat beside the body of the king. Wiglaf was exhausted, trying in vain to revive Beowulf with water. He could not keep his leader on this earth or change the will of the Almighty, for God's judgment rules over all men, just as it does today.

Wiglaf turned to the cowards with a bitter heart: "If one wishes to speak the truth, he must say that the king threw away the armor he gave you. He gave you helmets and mail-shirts as you sat on the beer-benches, yet you fled when war came. The king had no reason to boast of his companions. Yet God, the Master of Victories, allowed him to avenge himself alone with his sword. I could offer him little protection in the fight, but I did what I could to help my relative. The monster grew weaker when I struck him, and his fire boiled less fiercely. Too few defenders stood by the king in his hour of need.

Now, all sharing of treasure, gifts of swords, and the joy of home will end for your families. Every man of your kin will be stripped of his land and rights, forced into exile, once the nobles of the world hear of your cowardly flight. Death is better for any warrior than a life of disgrace!"

Wiglaf ordered the news of the battle to be carried to the encampment on the cliff, where the remaining nobles had sat in sorrow all morning, wondering if their king was dead or if he would return. The messenger rode up the headland and told the truth to them all:

"Now the beloved king of the Geats lies dead on his rest-bed, slain by the dragon. Beside him lies his enemy, killed by dagger wounds. Wiglaf sits by Beowulf, one living warrior keeping watch over the dead.

We must now expect war once the Franks and Frisians learn of our king's death. Our ancient feud with the Franks was started when Hygelac raided Frisia with his fleet.

Nor do I expect any peace from the Swedish people. It is widely known that King Ongentheow slew Haethcyn near Ravenswood.

This is the feud and deadly hatred for which I expect the Swedes to attack us, now that they know our protector is dead. Beowulf defended our lands and treasures against all enemies after our heroes fell, always guiding the people with wisdom and performing noble deeds.

Now let us hurry to look upon our king and carry him to the funeral pyre. Let us burn the treasures with him. This immense wealth was bought at a terrible price, paid for with the king's life. The fire must swallow it all. No warrior shall wear these jewels, and no maiden shall wear a gold ring from this hoard. Instead, sad in mind and stripped of gold, our people must wander in exile, now that our leader has laid aside laughter and song. The morning spear will be cold in our hands, and the harp will no longer wake us. Instead, the dark raven will fly over the fallen, telling the eagle and the wolf of his feast."

The messenger spoke the grim truth. The warriors arose and walked with tears to the Eagle's Ness to see the wonder. They found their king lying lifeless on the sand. The prince of the Geats had died a wonderful death.

Beside him lay the hateful dragon, scorched by hot coals. The beast was fifty feet long. He had once ruled the night sky, returning to his cave before dawn, but now he was cold in death. Beside the dragon stood ancient cups, pitchers, dishes, and precious swords, eaten away by rust after resting in the earth for a thousand years.

Wiglaf ordered wood to be gathered for the funeral pyre. The warriors pushed the dragon over the cliff into the sea, allowing the waves to swallow him. They loaded the wagon with gold and treasures, and carried the old king to the Whale's Ness.

The Geats prepared a magnificent funeral pyre, hanging it with helmets, shields, and mail-shirts, just as Beowulf had requested. Weeping, they laid their beloved lord in the center. The warriors lit the fire on the mound. The black wood-smoke rose above the flames, and the sound of roaring fire mingled with their weeping. A Geatish woman sang a sorrowful dirge, weeping for the dark days of war, slaughter, captivity, and disgrace that now awaited her people. The smoke rose until it swallowed the sky.

The Geats built a high monument on the headland, widely visible to Seafarers. In ten days, they built the beacon for their king, surrounding the ashes of the fire with a strong wall. They placed the rings and jewels in the barrow, leaving the gold in the sand where it remains today, as useless to men as it was before.

Twelve noble warriors rode around the mound, chanting their dirges and mourning their king. They praised his heroic deeds and honored his life, just as it is fitting to praise a beloved leader when he leaves this earth.

Thus the Geats mourned the fall of their lord. They said that Beowulf was, among all the kings of the world, the mildest, the most gentle, the kindest to his people, and the most eager for honor.

A Note on This Modernized Edition

This edition of Beowulf was adapted to make the ancient masterpiece accessible to language learners, students, and casual readers. We applied the following modernizing guidelines throughout:

* Audiobook and Listening Optimization: The narrative was translated from complex alliterative verse into a novel-like prose story. Every sentence was structured for natural spoken flow, making the narrative easy to follow when listening.

* Linear Idea Progression: Complex or winding poetic thoughts and archaic kennings were translated into clear, logical prose. This helps the reader follow the action and emotion of Beowulf's epic battles without losing the thread of the sentence.

* Clean Punctuation and Formatting: Visual clutter was removed, and the text was organized into standard paragraphs and numbered chapters to provide a familiar reading experience.

* Historic Illustrations: This edition features classic illustrations by J. R. Skelton, originally published in 1908 in *Stories of Beowulf*. Because they were published before 1929, these historic illustrations are firmly in the public domain and have been included to bring the epic battles of Beowulf to life.

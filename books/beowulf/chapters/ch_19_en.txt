Chapter Nineteen: The Great Feast and the Story of Finn

Then, Hrothgar presented Beowulf with a golden banner to reward his victory, along with an adorned helmet, a mail-shirt, and a precious sword. Many saw these treasures carried before the hero. Beowulf drank from the cup in the hall, having no reason to feel embarrassed by such grand gifts in front of the other warriors. I have rarely heard of anyone giving four gold-decorated treasures to another on the mead-benches in a friendlier manner. Around the crown of the helmet, a metal ridge wrapped with wire protected the wearer's head so that no sharp sword could cut through it in battle.

Next, the king ordered eight horses with gold-plated bridles to be led directly into the hall. One of the horses carried a saddle decorated with artwork and treasures. This was Hrothgar's own battle saddle, which he rode whenever he went out to fight. The courage of the famous leader never failed at the front lines when warriors were falling around him on the battlefield.

Hrothgar, king of the Danes, gave Beowulf possession of both the horses and the weapons, telling him to use them well. Thus, the famous king generously repaid Beowulf's brave deeds in battle with horses and treasures, in a way that no honest man could ever criticize.

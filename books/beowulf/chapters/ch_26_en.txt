Chapter Twenty-Six: The Giant Sword and the Swim to the Cave

Beowulf, the son of Ecgtheow, spoke: "Remember, noble king Hrothgar, now that I am ready for this dangerous journey, what we discussed before. If I should lose my life in your service, I ask that you treat my young followers and close companions as your own. Also, dear Hrothgar, send the treasures you gave me to my lord, King Hygelac. When the ruler of the Geats looks upon this gold, he will know that I found a generous king and enjoyed his favor. And let Unferth, your famous spokesman, have my old family treasure, my hardened, wave-patterned sword. I will win glory with Hrunting, or death will take me!"

After speaking these words, the Geatish hero hurried bravely forward, not waiting for an answer. The dark, boiling water swallowed him, and it took a long time before he reached the bottom of the lake. The swamp hag, who had greedily guarded these waters for fifty years, soon realized that a human was exploring her domain from above. She reached out and grabbed the hero in her terrible grip, but she could not harm his body. The strong links of his chain mail protected him, preventing her hateful fingers from tearing his skin.

When she reached the bottom, the sea-wolf carried the hero into her underwater lair. Although Beowulf was brave, he could not wield his weapons because she held him so tightly. As they moved, many strange water-beasts harassed him, striking his armor with their sharp tusks as they pursued him. Then Beowulf realized he was inside some kind of hall where the water could not reach him. The currents could not drag him down because of the roofed chamber. Inside the cave, he saw a bright fire burning, casting a brilliant light through the darkness.

The brave warrior soon saw the monstrous swamp hag. He swung his sharp sword with all his strength, driving the blade down upon her head. But the flashing sword would not cut through her skin. The edge failed the hero in his hour of need, even though it had survived many close battles, shearing through helmets and armor in the past. This was the first time the legendary blade had ever failed to wound an enemy.

Yet Beowulf, nephew of Hygelac, remained resolute and full of courage. The angry warrior threw down the decorated sword, leaving the steel-edged blade on the ground. He trusted instead in his own strength and his powerful grip. A man must act this way when he wants to win lasting praise in battle, caring nothing for his own life.

Furious and swollen with rage, the Geatish leader seized Grendel's mother by the shoulder. He threw his deadly enemy to the floor with a mighty heave, ready to fight to the end.

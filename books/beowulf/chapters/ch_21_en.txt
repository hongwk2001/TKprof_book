Chapter Twenty-One: Queen Wealhtheow's Plea for Her Sons

Thus, the two sides swore a firm peace treaty. King Finn promised Hengest with solemn oaths that he would rule the surviving Danes fairly. Furthermore, no one was allowed to break the treaty by word or deed. The Danes were not to complain about serving their king's killer, since they were forced to do so by fate. If any Frisian tried to restart the feud with insulting words, the sword would settle it.

The funeral pyre was prepared, and gold was brought from the treasure hoard. Hnaef, the best warrior of the Danes, was laid on the fire. On the pyre, everyone could see blood-stained armor, helmets decorated with golden boars, and the bodies of many nobles killed in battle. Queen Hildeburh ordered her own son to be burned on Hnaef's pyre, placing his body alongside his uncle. She wept and sang sad songs of mourning. The bodies were lifted up, and a massive funeral fire roared, climbing up to the clouds. Their bodies burned, wounds burst open, and blood flowed from the heat. The fire swallowed everything, consuming the fallen of both nations. Their glory was gone.

Grieving for their lost friends, the surviving warriors departed for their homes in the land of the Frisians. But Hengest was forced by a grim fate to stay with Finn through the long winter. He longed for his homeland, but he could not sail his longship on the ocean. The sea boiled with storms, and winter locked the waves in ice. Finally, spring arrived, bringing bright weather and a new season.

As winter passed and the earth grew beautiful again, Hengest was eager to leave Finn's court. Yet, he thought more about revenge than about his journey home. He wondered how he could start a fight to punish the Frisians. So, he accepted the challenge when a warrior laid a famous battle-sword in his lap, a legendary blade well-known to everyone.

Soon, a violent battle broke out at Finn's home. The Danish warriors Guthlaf and Oslaf returned from a sea journey, complaining about the suffering Finn had caused them. They could no longer hold back their anger. The hall was stained with the blood of their enemies. King Finn was slain among his guards, and Queen Hildeburh was captured. The Danes loaded Finn's treasures, jewels, and gems onto their ships. They carried the queen back across the sea to her own people.

The poet's song was finished. Joy returned to the hall, the benches grew noisy, and cup-bearers poured wine from beautiful pitchers. Then, Queen Wealhtheow came forward, wearing a golden crown. She walked to where Hrothgar and his nephew Hrothulf sat. At that time, they were still at peace, loyal to one another. Unferth, the spokesman, sat at the king's feet. Everyone still trusted his courage, even though he had killed his own relatives in a past sword fight.

The queen spoke: "Take this cup, my noble lord and king! Be joyful, and speak to the Geats with kind words, as a leader should. Be generous to them, remembering all the gifts you now have. I hear you want to adopt Beowulf as your son. Heorot, the great hall, has been saved! Enjoy your wealth while you can, and leave your kingdom to your family when it is your time to die. I trust that our nephew Hrothulf will rule our young men honorably if you die before he does. I expect he will treat our sons well, remembering all the kindness we showed him when he was a child."

She then turned to the bench where her sons Hrethric and Hrothmund sat with the other young princes. The brave Beowulf sat beside the two brothers.

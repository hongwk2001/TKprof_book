Chapter Twenty-Five: Beowulf's Promise to Hunt the Hag

Beowulf, the son of Ecgtheow, replied: "Do not grieve, wise king! It is better for a man to avenge his friend than to mourn in vain. Each of us must eventually face the end of our life in this world. Therefore, let him who can win glory before death, for that is the greatest honor left for a warrior after he is gone.

Arise, guardian of the kingdom! Let us go quickly and track the path of Grendel's mother. I promise you that she will not find shelter. She will not escape into the depths of the earth, the mountain forests, or the bottom of the sea, no matter where she turns. Have patience in all your troubles today, as I know you will."

The old king leaped up and thanked God, the mighty Lord, for the hero's encouraging words. Hrothgar ordered a horse with a braided mane to be saddled for their journey. The wise king rode in state, surrounded by a troop of loyal shield-bearers. As they marched, they saw the monster's footprints everywhere along the forest trails. Her tracks led straight across the dark swamp. She had carried away the lifeless body of Hrothgar's dearest advisor, the best of the Danes who guarded the hall.

The warriors climbed steep, rocky slopes, narrow paths, and dangerous cliffs where water-monsters had their lairs. Beowulf rode ahead with a few wise men to scout the path. Suddenly, they found gnarled trees hanging over gray stones, a joyless and eerie forest. Below them lay blood-stained, turbulent water. It was a bitter grief for all the Danes, and a painful sight for their noblemen, when they found Aeschere's head lying on the cliff above the lake.

The water boiled with hot blood, and the warriors watched in horror. The war-horn blew a loud battle song, stirring their spirits. Sitting on the bank, the men saw many serpents in the water and strange sea-dragons swimming in the deep. Water-monsters lay on the ledges of the cliffs. These were the beasts that often go on destructive journeys across the sea in the morning, terrorizing sailors. When they heard the blast of the horn, the monsters rushed away in anger. Beowulf shot an arrow from his bow, piercing one of the swimming monsters to the heart. The creature grew weak as death overtook it in the water. The warriors quickly hooked the beast with barbed boar-spears, dragging it onto the shore. They stared at the terrifying, scaly creature.

Beowulf put on his armor, completely fearless. His broad, hand-woven chain mail was ready to protect his body under the water. The strong links would guard his chest from any hostile grip or the malicious grasp of the enemy. A shining helmet, decorated with gold and shaped with boar figures, would protect his head. A skilled smith had crafted it in the ancient days so that no sword could ever cut through it.

Beowulf also received great help from Unferth, Hrothgar's spokesman. Unferth lent him a famous ancient sword named Hrunting. The iron blade was decorated with poison patterns and hardened in battle blood. It had never failed any warrior who held it in his hand and dared to walk the path of danger. This was not the first time the blade was called upon to perform a deed of courage, and Unferth gave it to Beowulf, forgetting his own boasts.

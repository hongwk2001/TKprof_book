Chapter Thirty-One: The Farewell Feast

Hrothgar continued: "Thus I ruled the Danes under the heavens for fifty years, protecting them in war with spears and swords against many tribes across the world. During all those years, I did not fear any enemy under the sky. But a sudden change came to my homeland, and grief followed joy when Grendel, the old enemy, invaded my feast-hall. Because of that terrible persecution, I carried a heavy heart-sorrow for many years. Therefore, thanks be to the Creator, the Eternal Lord, that I have survived to look upon this blood-stained head with my own eyes.

Go now to your seat, honored champion, and enjoy the feast. When morning comes, we will share a vast treasure."

The Geatish hero was glad at heart and quickly went to his seat as the wise king commanded. Once again, a magnificent feast was prepared for the brave warriors in the feast-hall. The dark cover of night grew thick over the castle. The company arose, and the old Danish king retired to his bedchamber. Beowulf, weary from his journey and his underwater battle, also felt a great desire to rest. A hall officer, who politely looked after all the needs of a noble guest in those days, guided the traveler to his quiet quarters.

The great-hearted hero rested. The gold-adorned building towered high under the sky. The guest slept inside until the black raven cheerfully announced the dawn. Bright light hurried over the shadows of the hills, and the warriors woke, eager to return to their own people. The bold Geat wished to board his ship and set sail immediately.

Beowulf ordered Hrunting to be returned to Unferth, thanking him for the loan of the weapon. He praised the sword as a good companion in battle, strong and reliable, and said he found no fault with its edge. He was indeed a noble-minded warrior. When the men were fully equipped in their armor and ready to travel, Beowulf went to the high seat to bid farewell to the wise king.

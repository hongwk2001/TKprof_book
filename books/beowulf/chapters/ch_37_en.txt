Chapter Thirty-Seven: Beowulf's Grief and the Eleven Companions

The fiend began to vomit flames and burn the splendid houses and buildings of the Geats. The glare of the fire was terrifying to all the people. The hateful dragon did not intend to leave anyone alive. People everywhere saw the dragon's attack and his cruel revenge. It showed how much the monster hated and humiliated the Geats. He hurried back to his hoard and his dark chamber before dawn. He had engulfed the land in fire and flame. He trusted in his mound, his power, and his walls, but that trust deceived him in the end.

The news of the disaster quickly reached Beowulf. His own home, the best of buildings and the royal throne of the Geats, had melted in the waves of fire. It was a heavy heart-sorrow for the king, the greatest grief he had ever known. The wise king feared he had bitterly angered the Ruler, the Eternal Lord, by breaking some ancient law. His breast boiled with dark thoughts, which was not his usual custom. The fire-dragon had destroyed the people's fortress and their coastal fortress. For this, the king planned his revenge.

The king ordered a magnificent battle-shield to be made entirely of iron. He knew that wooden shields could not help him against the dragon's flames. The noble prince was destined to reach the end of his days in this world, and the dragon was also fated to die with him. This was true even though the beast had guarded the saved gold for a long time.

The proud king scorned to seek the dragon with a large army. He did not dread the battle, nor did he count the dragon's warfare, its Might, and its courage as any threat. In the past, Beowulf had survived many hostile clashes and battle-crashes, ever since he had cleansed Hrothgar's feast-hall and crushed Grendel's monstrous kin.

Not the least of his battles was the one in which Hygelac was slain. The king of the Geats died in Frisia from deadly sword blows on the battlefield. Beowulf escaped by his own strength, performing a legendary feat of swimming. He carried thirty sets of armor on his arm when he plunged into the sea. The enemy warriors who fought him had no reason to boast, for very few returned home after facing that fierce war-wolf.

Beowulf swam across the wide sea, lonely and grieving, back to his people. There, Queen Hygd offered him the treasure and the kingdom, the rings and the throne. She did not believe her young son, Heardred, could defend the land against foreign enemies now that Hygelac was dead. But the people could not persuade the noble Beowulf to become king over Heardred. Instead, he supported the young prince with friendly counsel and honor until the boy grew older and ruled the Geats.

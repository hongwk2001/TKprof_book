Chapter Twenty: The Story of Finn, Hengest and Hildeburh

Furthermore, King Hrothgar gave a valuable treasure to every single warrior who had traveled across the sea with Beowulf. He also ordered that gold be paid to compensate for the Geatish warrior Grendel had killed. The monster would have killed even more of them, if wise God and Beowulf's courage had not prevented that fate. God ruled over all mankind then, just as he does now. Therefore, wisdom and foresight are always best. Anyone who lives a long life in this world of conflict must experience both joy and sorrow.

Singing and music filled the hall in the presence of King Hrothgar. The wooden harp was played, and many songs were sung as Hrothgar's poet entertained the guests along the mead-benches.

The poet sang the tragic story of King Finn's sons. The Danish leader, Hnaef, was fated to fall in battle against the Frisians. Hnaef's sister, Queen Hildeburh, who was married to Finn, had no reason to trust the loyalty of the Frisians. Blameless, she lost her loved ones in the battle, as both her son and her brother fell, wounded by spears. She was a truly grieving woman.

Hildeburh, the daughter of Hoc, wept when morning came. Under the open sky, she saw the dead bodies of her relatives, where she once held the world's greatest joy. The war had killed almost all of Finn's warriors. He could no longer fight against Hengest, the new Danish leader, nor could he protect his surviving men from the Danes.

So, King Finn offered peace terms to Hengest. The Frisians promised to clear out another hall and a throne for the Danes, sharing power equally between them. Every day during the distribution of treasure, Finn would honor the Danes. He promised to give just as much gold and treasures to Hengest's men as he gave to his own Frisian warriors in the beer-hall.

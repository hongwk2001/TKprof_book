Chapter Twelve: The Attack of Grendel

Then, King Hrothgar departed from the feast-hall with his band of heroes. The leader went to join Queen Wealhtheow in her bedchamber, leaving Heorot behind. But God had set a guard in the hall to watch for Grendel. Beowulf took up this special duty for the king of the Danes, keeping watch in the dark for the monster.

The leader of the Geats trusted firmly in his own great strength and in God's favor. He took off his iron mail-shirt and his helmet, stripping himself of all protection. He handed his decorated sword, the finest of iron blades, to his servant and ordered him to guard the gear. Before going to bed, Beowulf made a final boast to his men: "I do not consider myself any weaker in battle than Grendel! Therefore, I will not kill him with a sword, even though I easily could. Grendel knows nothing of swords or shields. He does not know how to strike back or block a blow, despite his infamous cruelty. Tonight, we will not use swords if he dares to fight without weapons. Let wise God, the Holy Lord, award victory to whichever side he chooses."

Beowulf lay down to rest, placing his cheek against the pillow. Around him, his brave sea warriors fell asleep on their beds. None of them expected to ever see their beloved homeland again, or the towns where they were raised. They knew too well how many Danish warriors bloody death had already swept away in this hall. But God wove a destiny of victory for the Geats, granting them comfort and help. They would completely overcome their enemy through the strength of one man, through Beowulf's own Might. The truth is clear: Almighty God has always ruled over mankind.

In the dark night, Grendel came gliding down from the misty hills. The warriors who were supposed to guard the great hall fell asleep, all except one. The men knew that the demon could not drag them down into the shadows unless God allowed it. But Beowulf stayed awake, watching in anger. With a beating heart, he waited for the battle to begin.

Chapter Forty-Three: Beowulf's Death and the Mourning of the Geats

The dying king spoke in sorrow, gazing upon the gold: "I offer my deepest thanks to the Lord of All, the King of Glory, the Eternal Lord, for these treasures. I am grateful that I could win this wealth for my people before my death. Since I have traded my life for this hoard, you must look after the needs of the Geats. I can remain here no longer.

Command the brave warriors to build a magnificent mound on the cliff by the sea, after the funeral fire has cooled. It shall tower high on the Whale's Ness as a monument, so that Seafarers who guide their tall ships through the mists of the ocean will call it Beowulf's Barrow in the days to come."

The brave king took the golden collar from his neck and gave it to Wiglaf, the young warrior, along with his gold-decorated helmet, his ring, and his mail-shirt, commanding him to use them well.

"You are the last of our family, the Waegmundings! Fate has swept all my relatives away to their destiny, those brave warriors in their valor. Now I must follow them."

Those were the last words from the old king's breast before his body was committed to the hot funeral flames. His soul departed to seek the judgment of the righteous.

It was a sorrowful moment for the young Wiglaf, who had to look upon his beloved lord lying lifeless on the earth. The dragon lay dead beside him, destroyed in the ruin. The coiled dragon could no longer guard the treasure hoard, for the sharp iron blades had slain him, leaving the beast lifeless on the sand near his cave. Never again would he fly through the night, showing his fiery form in the sky. Wiglaf had stilled him with his sword. Indeed, few men in the land, no matter how strong or brave, had ever succeeded in facing the poison breath of a dragon or entering his stone chamber if the guardian was awake. The golden treasure was bought with Beowulf's death, and both combatants had reached the end of their lives.

Soon, the ten cowardly warriors who had fled into the woods returned. They had not dared to fight with their spears when their lord needed them most. Carrying their shields and weapons in shame, they walked to where Wiglaf sat beside the body of the king. Wiglaf was exhausted, trying in vain to revive Beowulf with water. He could not keep his leader on this earth or change the will of the Almighty, for God's judgment rules over all men, just as it does today.

Wiglaf turned to the cowards with a bitter heart: "If one wishes to speak the truth, he must say that the king threw away the armor he gave you. He gave you helmets and mail-shirts as you sat on the beer-benches, yet you fled when war came. The king had no reason to boast of his companions. Yet God, the Master of Victories, allowed him to avenge himself alone with his sword. I could offer him little protection in the fight, but I did what I could to help my relative. The monster grew weaker when I struck him, and his fire boiled less fiercely. Too few defenders stood by the king in his hour of need.

Now, all sharing of treasure, gifts of swords, and the joy of home will end for your families. Every man of your kin will be stripped of his land and rights, forced into exile, once the nobles of the world hear of your cowardly flight. Death is better for any warrior than a life of disgrace!"

Wiglaf ordered the news of the battle to be carried to the encampment on the cliff, where the remaining nobles had sat in sorrow all morning, wondering if their king was dead or if he would return. The messenger rode up the headland and told the truth to them all:

"Now the beloved king of the Geats lies dead on his rest-bed, slain by the dragon. Beside him lies his enemy, killed by dagger wounds. Wiglaf sits by Beowulf, one living warrior keeping watch over the dead.

We must now expect war once the Franks and Frisians learn of our king's death. Our ancient feud with the Franks was started when Hygelac raided Frisia with his fleet.

Nor do I expect any peace from the Swedish people. It is widely known that King Ongentheow slew Haethcyn near Ravenswood.

This is the feud and deadly hatred for which I expect the Swedes to attack us, now that they know our protector is dead. Beowulf defended our lands and treasures against all enemies after our heroes fell, always guiding the people with wisdom and performing noble deeds.

Now let us hurry to look upon our king and carry him to the funeral pyre. Let us burn the treasures with him. This immense wealth was bought at a terrible price, paid for with the king's life. The fire must swallow it all. No warrior shall wear these jewels, and no maiden shall wear a gold ring from this hoard. Instead, sad in mind and stripped of gold, our people must wander in exile, now that our leader has laid aside laughter and song. The morning spear will be cold in our hands, and the harp will no longer wake us. Instead, the dark raven will fly over the fallen, telling the eagle and the wolf of his feast."

The messenger spoke the grim truth. The warriors arose and walked with tears to the Eagle's Ness to see the wonder. They found their king lying lifeless on the sand. The prince of the Geats had died a wonderful death.

Beside him lay the hateful dragon, scorched by hot coals. The beast was fifty feet long. He had once ruled the night sky, returning to his cave before dawn, but now he was cold in death. Beside the dragon stood ancient cups, pitchers, dishes, and precious swords, eaten away by rust after resting in the earth for a thousand years.

Wiglaf ordered wood to be gathered for the funeral pyre. The warriors pushed the dragon over the cliff into the sea, allowing the waves to swallow him. They loaded the wagon with gold and treasures, and carried the old king to the Whale's Ness.

The Geats prepared a magnificent funeral pyre, hanging it with helmets, shields, and mail-shirts, just as Beowulf had requested. Weeping, they laid their beloved lord in the center. The warriors lit the fire on the mound. The black wood-smoke rose above the flames, and the sound of roaring fire mingled with their weeping. A Geatish woman sang a sorrowful dirge, weeping for the dark days of war, slaughter, captivity, and disgrace that now awaited her people. The smoke rose until it swallowed the sky.

The Geats built a high monument on the headland, widely visible to Seafarers. In ten days, they built the beacon for their king, surrounding the ashes of the fire with a strong wall. They placed the rings and jewels in the barrow, leaving the gold in the sand where it remains today, as useless to men as it was before.

Twelve noble warriors rode around the mound, chanting their dirges and mourning their king. They praised his heroic deeds and honored his life, just as it is fitting to praise a beloved leader when he leaves this earth.

Thus the Geats mourned the fall of their lord. They said that Beowulf was, among all the kings of the world, the mildest, the most gentle, the kindest to his people, and the most eager for honor.

Chapter Thirty-Eight: The March to the Barrow

Two Swedish princes, the sons of Ohthere, rebelled against their uncle, the powerful Swedish king Onela, but their rebellion failed. Fearing for their lives, the young princes fled across the sea to seek safety in the land of the Geats. They arrived at the court of King Heardred, who welcomed them warmly and offered them shelter in his hall. However, this act of kindness proved to be Heardred’s ruin. When King Onela heard that his rebellious nephews had found refuge with the Geats, he became furious. He launched a massive invasion, marching his army into Geatland to punish the king. In the fierce battle that followed, King Heardred was struck down and killed by a Swedish sword. With his revenge complete, King Onela marched his army back to Sweden. This left the throne of the Geats empty, and Beowulf was chosen to rule. Beowulf took the crown and became a great and wise king, guiding his people in peace for fifty years.

In the years that followed, Beowulf never forgot the murder of King Heardred, and he quietly planned to avenge his death. The opportunity came when he befriended the exiled Prince Eadgils, the surviving Swedish prince who had fled from Onela. Beowulf supported Eadgils' claim to the throne by sending a large Geatish army and powerful weapons across the sea. With Beowulf's strong support, Prince Eadgils invaded Sweden and fought a bitter war against his uncle. Eadgils avenged all his past wrongs by defeating the army and killing King Onela in battle. Eadgils then became the new king of Sweden, and the long feud between their kingdoms was finally settled.

Thus Beowulf, the son of Ecgtheow, had survived every war, dangerous battle, and trial, until the fateful day came when he had to fight the dragon. The king of the Geats, filled with anger, went with eleven chosen companions to confront the beast. He had learned how the feud began, for the precious gold cup had been brought to him by the man who stole it. The thirteenth member of the group was the slave who had caused the conflict in the first place. A miserable captive, he was forced to guide the warriors to the spot. He led them against his will to the stone chamber beneath the earth, near the crashing waves of the sea. Inside, the mound was filled with gold wire ornaments and precious jewels. The monstrous dragon had guarded these treasures for centuries, and it was a dangerous place for any man to enter.

The battle-hardened king sat on the cliff, bidding farewell to his close companions and loyal thanes. His heart was heavy, restless, and prepared for death. Fate was close at hand, ready to claim the old king, steal his soul's treasure, and divide his life from his body. The king would not live much longer on this earth.

Beowulf, the son of Ecgtheow, spoke: "I survived many wars in my youth. I remember them all. I was only seven years old when King Hrethel took me from my father to raise me. The king cared for me, giving me treasures and food, always mindful of our family bond. During his life, he loved me no less than his own sons: Herebeald, Haethcyn, and my beloved lord Hygelac.

But tragedy struck the eldest son. Haethcyn accidentally shot his older brother, Herebeald, with an arrow from his bow. He missed his mark and killed his own brother with a bloody shaft. It was a terrible, heartbreaking accident that could not be avenged or compensated. There was no way to pay for the crime, yet the father had to live with the grief, watching his eldest son die unavenged.

It is just as sorrowful for an old man to see his young son hang on the gallows. He can only sing a mourning song while his son hangs as food for the ravens, and his old wisdom cannot help him in his sorrow. Every morning reminds him of his lost child. He has no heart to wait for another heir in his halls. Grieving, he looks upon his son's deserted home, the wind-swept feast-hall, and the empty beds. The riders sleep in the grave. There is no sound of the harp or laughter in his courts, as there once was in the happy days of the past."

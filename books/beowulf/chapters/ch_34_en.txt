Chapter Thirty-Four: Beowulf's Report to King Hygelac

The brave hero walked across the wide sandy shore with his chosen warriors. The sun, the world's candle, shone brightly as it hurried from the south. They marched eagerly to the court where they knew King Hygelac, who had slain the Swedish king Ongentheow, was distributing golden rings to his people. News of Beowulf's return quickly reached the king. He was told that his nephew and shield brother had returned to the court alive and well from the battlefield. The king immediately ordered the feast-hall to be cleared and prepared for his guests.

Beowulf sat directly opposite the king, uncle facing nephew, after the loyal lord welcomed him with warm and earnest words. Queen Hygd moved through the feast-hall pouring sweet drinks, showing her love for the people and offering the cup to the heroes.

Hygelac questioned his companion, eager to hear of the Geats' adventures: "How did your journey go, beloved Beowulf, when you decided to seek battle far across the salt water at Heorot? Did you ease the famous sorrows of Hrothgar, the Danish king? I was deeply anxious, filled with grief, because I did not trust your journey. I begged you not to confront that murderous demon, but to let the Danes settle their own war with Grendel. I thank God that I see you back safe and sound."

Beowulf, the son of Ecgtheow, spoke: "It is no secret to anyone, Lord Hygelac, how Grendel and I fought in that hall. Grendel had brought endless sorrow and lifelong misery to the Danes, but I avenged all of it. No relative of Grendel on earth, no matter how long that wicked race lives, has any reason to boast of that twilight battle.

When I arrived, I first went to the royal hall to greet Hrothgar. As soon as the king knew my purpose, he welcomed me and gave me a seat beside his own sons. The company was full of joy. I have never seen such merriment among men in any feast-hall. At times, the noble queen Wealhtheow walked through the hall to encourage the young men, presenting a golden ring to a warrior before returning to her seat. At times, Hrothgar's daughter carried the cup to the older warriors. I heard the men call her Freawaru as she offered the vessel to the heroes.

She is promised, young and gold-adorned, to Ingeld of the Heathobards. Hrothgar arranged this marriage, believing it to be a wise plan to settle their ancient feuds and quarrels. But a deadly spear seldom rests for long after a prince is slain, even if the bride is noble.

It may deeply displease the lord of the Heathobards and his warriors when Danish noblemen walk the hall with his bride. The Danes wear the ancient weapons of the Heathobards' ancestors, decorated with golden rings. The Heathobards owned those treasures until they lost their companions and their lives in battle."

Chapter Thirty-Three: The Return to Geatland and the Tale of Modthryth

Beowulf departed, a proud warrior adorned with gold and rejoicing in his treasure. The ship rode at anchor, waiting for its owner. As they marched to the shore, the warriors praised Hrothgar's generosity. He was a blameless king in every way, until old age, which spares no one, took away the joy of his strength.

The brave young men reached the shore, wearing their strong chain mail. The coast guard spotted their return, just as he had watched their arrival before. He did not insult them from the cliff, but rode down to greet them, declaring that the bright-armored Geats would be welcomed home by their people.

The warriors loaded the curved prow ship with armor, horses, and Hrothgar's treasures. The mast towered over the saved wealth. Beowulf presented the boat guard with a gold-bound sword. On the benches of the beer-hall, the guard would now be highly respected because of this family treasure. Beowulf boarded the ship, and they sailed through deep waters, leaving the land of the Danes behind.

They hoisted the sail and secured it to the mast. The wooden ship groaned under the pressure, but the wind did not hinder their journey. The vessel sped forward, foaming over the waves and moving across the sea paths. Soon, they saw the familiar cliffs and headlands of Geatland. Driven by the wind, the ship surged onto the sandy shore.

The harbor guard, who had watched the horizon for a long time in eager expectation of the beloved warriors, was waiting on the beach. He secured the broad ship to the sand with anchor ropes to prevent the waves from carrying the wooden vessel away. Beowulf ordered the treasures and plated gold to be carried ashore. The home of King Hygelac was close by, where the ruler lived with his companions near the sea wall.

The hall was magnificent, and the king was majestic. Queen Hygd, although young, was wise and noble. She had lived only a few winters within the castle walls as the daughter of Haereth, yet she was generous and gave many treasures to the Geatish people.

Queen Modthryth, on the other hand, was notorious in the old days for her pride and terrible crimes. No warrior in the court, except her husband, dared to look directly into her eyes during the day. If anyone did, he was quickly bound in strong ropes. Soon after, the sword would settle the matter, its decorated blade cutting through his neck. It is not the duty of a queen, even a beautiful one, to take the life of a loyal subject over an imagined insult.

However, Hemming's relative put an end to her cruelty. The warriors drinking ale told a different story. They said she caused less ruin and committed fewer evils once she was married to the young champion, Offa. She had traveled across the sea to Offa's court at her father's command. There on the throne, she was renowned for her goodness, using her life well and loving the king. Offa was a brave and generous ruler, widely honored for his wisdom and victories in battle.

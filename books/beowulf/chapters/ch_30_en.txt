Chapter Thirty: Hrothgar's Sermon on Pride

The wise Hrothgar spoke, and everyone in the feast-hall fell silent to listen: "Indeed, any leader who rules with truth and justice, guarding his homeland and remembering the past, can say that this champion was born a noble man. Your fame is known far and wide, my friend Beowulf, across every land. You possess both great Might and wisdom. I will keep the friendship we swore to each other, and you will bring long-lasting comfort and help to your people.

King Heremod was not like this to the Danes. He did not bring them joy, but brought only slaughter and destruction. In his fits of rage, he killed his own companions and counselors, until the famous prince was left completely alone. Although God had granted him power and strength above all other men, Heremod's heart grew bloodthirsty and cruel. He gave no treasures to his people to win glory, and he lived a joyless life, suffering the consequences of his own cruelty. Learn from this example, Beowulf, and understand true virtue! I have told you this story as an old king wise with many winters.

It is a wonder to see how God, in His great power, distributes wisdom, land, and authority to mankind. He rules over all things. Sometimes He allows a noble ruler to enjoy earthly delights, giving him peace in his home and a strong fortress to govern. God makes a broad kingdom subject to him, so that the man cannot imagine his own end in his foolishness. He lives in wealth, and neither sickness nor old age troubles him. Sorrow does not darken his mind, and no enemy threatens him with war. The whole world bends to his will, and he knows no hardship.

He suspects no danger until his pride grows and takes root, while his soul's guardian, which is his conscience, falls asleep. That sleep is too deep, and the enemy, the devil, is close at hand, shooting a deadly arrow from his bow. The arrow strikes the king in the heart, and he cannot protect himself from the corrupt desires of the devil. What he has held for a long time now seems too little. Greedy and angry, he hoards his wealth, stops giving out golden rings, and forgets the destiny that awaits him, ignoring that God has granted him all his honors.

In the end, his body inevitably grows weak and dies. Another ruler takes his place, one who distributes the ancient treasures of the nobles generously, without any fear or greed.

Guard yourself against such pride, dear Beowulf, best of men, and choose the path of eternal rewards. Do not let pride rule you, famous champion! Now your Might is glorious for a little while, but soon sickness or the sword will rob you of your strength. Fire, flood, a sharp blade, a flying spear, or bitter old age will eventually overtake you. The brightness of your eyes will fade, and death will soon conquer you, as it conquers all men."

Chapter Thirty-Nine: Beowulf's Last Battle

King Hrethel sang a sorrowful mourning song, retreating to his bed. After losing his eldest son, Herebeald, his home and fields felt empty. The old king carried a deep sorrow. He could not avenge the death upon the killer, nor could he show his anger, even though he no longer loved the boy's brother, Haethcyn. Because of this grief, he withdrew from the joy of men and passed into God's light, leaving his land and fortress to his remaining sons.

After Hrethel died, crime and warfare broke out between the Swedes and the Geats across the wide waters. King Ongentheow's sons were bold and warlike, refusing to keep peace. They launched many bloody, treacherous attacks around Hreosnabeorh. My relatives avenged those crimes, as is well known, though one paid with his life. The battle was fatal to Haethcyn, then lord of the Geats. By morning, his brother Hygelac avenged him, as Eofor struck down Ongentheow. The Swedish king's helmet was split, and the old king fell mortally wounded. Eofor's hand did not hold back the fatal blow.

I repaid Hygelac for the treasures and land he gave me, using my bright sword in battle. He had no need to hire foreign warriors or seek help from the Danes or the Swedes. I was always at the front of his army, fighting alone at the spearpoint. I will continue to fight as long as this sword lasts. It has served me well ever since I slew Daeghrefn, the champion of the Hugas, with my bare hands. Daeghrefn could not carry the golden breast ornament back to the Frisian king. He fell in battle, a prince of great Might. My sword did not kill him, but my powerful grip crushed his body and stopped his heart. Now, my sharp blade and strong hand must fight for this hoard.

Beowulf spoke his final boast: "I survived many wars in my youth. Yet, as the old guardian of my people, I will seek this feud and win glory once more, if the dragon will come out of his stone mound to face me."

He bid farewell to his brave companions for the last time: "I would not use a sword against the dragon if I knew how else to grapple with him and fulfill my boast, as I did against Grendel. But I expect hot battle-fire, venom, and poison breath, so I wear my shield and mail-shirt. I will not retreat a single step from the dragon. We will let Fate, the creator of all men, decide between us at the wall. My heart is bold, and I will make no further boasts. Wait on the mound in your armor, warriors, to see which of us survives his wounds. This is my battle alone, not yours. No other man has the Might to fight this monster and perform such a deed. I will win the gold with courage, or death will take your lord."

The champion arose, holding his shield and wearing his helmet and chain mail beneath the stone cliffs. He trusted in his own strength, as a coward never would. He saw a stone arch and a boiling stream rushing out from the mound. The water was hot with battle fire, and no one could approach the hoard without being burned by the dragon's flames.

The king of the Geats shouted in his anger. His clear voice echoed under the gray stone, alerting the dragon. The beast recognized the voice of a man, and there was no time left for peace. Hot venom and fire blasted out from the stone, and the earth shook.

The hero raised his shield against the monster. The coiled dragon rushed forward to meet him, eager for battle. The king had already drawn his ancient, sharp sword. Both fighters felt terror. Beowulf stood firmly behind his iron shield as the dragon coiled itself, ready to strike.

The blazing dragon glided forward in loops, rushing toward its fate. The iron shield protected the king's body for a shorter time than he had hoped. On this day, for the first time in his life, he had to fight without Fate granting him victory. The king raised his hand and struck the beast with his ancient sword, but the blade slipped on the bone and failed to bite deep enough.

Furious after the blow, the dragon spewed deadly fire far and wide. The king could not boast of victory, for his sword had failed him, as good iron never should. It was a hard destiny for Beowulf to leave this earth and go to a foreign home, as all men must eventually leave this fleeting life.

It was not long before the combatants met again. The dragon took heart, breathing fire once more, while the king suffered in the heat. None of his noble companions stood by him to help. Instead, they fled into the woods to save their lives. Only one warrior felt sorrow in his heart, remembering the unbreakable family bonds.

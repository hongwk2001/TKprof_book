Chapter Twenty-Two: The Golden Collar and the Sleepers

The cup was carried to Beowulf, and the guests offered him friendly words. Hrothgar presented him with twisted gold ornaments, two armbands, a mail-shirt, rings, and the largest golden collar ever seen on earth. It was grander than any treasure since the hero Hama carried the legendary Brosing necklace back to his bright city, escaping the hatred of the wicked king Eormenric.

Years later, King Hygelac of the Geats wore this same golden collar on his final journey. He died defending his battle spoils under his banner. Fate took him when, out of pride, he started a feud with the Frisians. The mighty lord fell beneath his shield after carrying the precious stones across the sea. The king's life, his armor, and the golden collar fell into the hands of the enemy. Weaker warriors looted the dead bodies, and the Geats lay fallen on the battlefield.

The feast-hall grew noisy with applause. Wealhtheow spoke before the crowd: "Wear this ring with luck, beloved Beowulf, and use this mail-shirt from our treasury! Prosper, show your great strength, and be a kind guide to my young sons. I will remember to reward you for this. You have earned the praise of men far and near, as wide as the sea that surrounds the windy cliffs. May you be a prosperous noble for as long as you live! I gladly grant you these treasures. Treat my sons with kindness, for you have brought us great joy. Here in Heorot, every warrior is true to one another, kind of heart, and loyal to the king. The nobles are united, the people are eager, and everyone does as I ask."

She returned to her seat. It was a magnificent feast, and the men drank wine. They did not know the grim destiny that was fated for many of them. When evening came and Hrothgar departed for his bedchamber, a great number of warriors stayed to guard the hall, just as they had done in the past. They cleared away the benches and covered the floor with beds and pillows. One of the warriors, doomed to die, lay down on his bed.

They placed their wooden shields at their heads. On the benches above each noble, their helmets, chain-mail armor, and mighty spears stood ready. It was their custom to always be ready for war, whether at home or in the field, in case their king ever needed them. They were a noble people.

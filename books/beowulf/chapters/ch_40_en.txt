Chapter Forty: The Loyal Companion, Wiglaf

His name was Wiglaf, the son of Weohstan, a prized shield-warrior, a nobleman of the Swedish royal family, and a relative of Aelfhere. He saw his king suffering from the intense heat beneath his battle mask. Wiglaf remembered the honors that Beowulf had given him: the rich estate of the Waegmundings and all the rights his father had possessed. He could not hold back. His hand seized his yellow wooden shield, and he drew his ancient sword.

That sword was a legendary family treasure, once owned by Eanmund, the son of Ohthere. Weohstan had killed Eanmund in battle when the prince was a friendless exile, carrying off his dark helmet, ringed mail-shirt, and ancient sword. King Onela had given Weohstan that armor, which was Eanmund's war gear, even though Eanmund was Onela's own nephew. Onela never spoke of avenging his nephew, because Weohstan had slain him. Weohstan kept the sword and mail-shirt for many years, until his son Wiglaf was old enough to perform heroic deeds. When Weohstan died, full of years, he left his son countless war garments and weapons.

This was the first time the young warrior was to fight beside his noble lord. His courage did not melt, nor did his father's sword fail him in the battle. The dragon soon discovered this when they clashed.

Wiglaf spoke to his companions with a sorrowful heart, urging them to do what was right: "I remember the time we drank honey-wine in the feast-hall, promising our king that we would repay him for our armor, helmets, and swords if he ever needed us. He chose us for this journey from his entire army, judging us worthy of glory. He gave us these treasures because he trusted us as brave warriors, even though our king intended to fight this monster alone. He has accomplished the most glorious deeds and daring acts of any man.

Now the day has come when our leader needs our help. Let us go to him and help our king while the terrible fire lasts! God knows I would rather the fire consume my body alongside my king. It is not honorable for us to carry our shields back home unless we first defeat the foe and defend the life of our king. I know well that his past deeds do not deserve to leave him suffering alone. The sword, the helmet, and the mail-shirt must be shared between us!"

Wiglaf waded through the deadly fumes, carrying his helmet to help his king. He spoke brief words: "Beloved Beowulf, fight well, just as you promised in your youth. You swore that you would never let your glory fade while you lived. Now, famous champion, you must defend your life with all your Might! I will stand by you."

The angry dragon came charging a second time, breathing surging fire to attack his enemies. Wiglaf's wooden shield burned away in the flames, and his mail-shirt offered him no protection. But the young man bravely stepped behind his relative's iron shield once his own was destroyed.

Then the war king remembered his glory once more. He struck a mighty blow with his sword, driving it into the dragon's head. But Naegling snapped! Beowulf's sword failed him in the fight. It was not his destiny that iron blades should help him. His hand was too strong, and he shattered every sword he swung in battle. Carrying a strong weapon to fight did not benefit him.

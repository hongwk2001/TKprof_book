Chapter Eighteen: Hrothgar's Victory Gifts

Beowulf, the son of Ecgtheow, replied: "We fought this battle with great goodwill and boldly faced the unknown monster. I wish you could have seen Grendel himself, dying in his full power! I planned to pin him quickly to his deathbed, holding him tight in my grip so he would struggle for his life under my hands, unless his body slipped away. But I could not stop him from leaving, since God did not will it. I did not hold him firmly enough, and the fiend was too strong in his escape.

However, to save his life, Grendel left his hand, arm, and shoulder behind. Yet, the miserable creature bought no comfort by doing so, and the sinful monster will not live much longer. Pain has caught him in an inescapable grip, binding him in deadly chains. There, the creature must await the final judgment to see how God will sentence him."

After that, Unferth, the son of Ecglaf, was much quieter and stopped boasting of his battle deeds. This happened after the nobles looked up at the high roof and examined Grendel's hand and fingers, placed there through Beowulf's strength. On the end of each finger were hard nails that looked like steel, forming the hateful claw and the horrible spikes of the monster. Everyone agreed that no sword, no matter how sharp or ancient, could have cut through that bloody claw.

Then, orders were given to decorate the inside of Heorot. Many men and women worked together to prepare the feast-hall. Gold-woven tapestries shone along the walls, showing beautiful sights to anyone who looked upon them. The main building was badly damaged, even though it was reinforced with iron bands, and the door hinges were ripped apart. Only the roof survived completely undamaged when the outlawed monster fled, despairing of his life.

Death is not easy to escape, no matter how hard one tries! Every living human on earth must eventually seek out their final resting place, where their body will sleep in the grave after the feast of life is over.

Then, the time came for Hrothgar to enter the hall. The king himself wanted to join the feast. I have never heard of a larger or more noble band of warriors gathered around their king! The famous men sat on the benches and enjoyed the feast. Hrothgar and his nephew Hrothulf courteously drank many cups of mead in the great hall. Heorot was filled with friends, and at that time, there was no treachery among the Danish people.

Chapter Thirty-Five: The Geatish Feud and the Gifts of Treasure

"An old warrior, seeing the ancient sword and remembering the slaughter of his people, will speak over the honey-wine. Sad in heart and filled with grim thoughts, he will test the spirit of a young warrior. He will stir up the old hatred by saying: 'My friend, do you not recognize the sword your father carried in his last battle under his helmet, where the Danes slew him? The brave Danes controlled the battlefield when Withergyld lay dead after our heroes fell. Now, the son of one of those Danish killers walks here in our halls, boasting of the murder and wearing the very treasure you should rightfully inherit!'

He will remind the young man with painful words again and again, until one of the bride's Danish servants is killed by a sharp sword to pay for his father's deeds. The killer will escape, knowing the land well. Then the peace oaths will be broken by both sides, and deadly hatred will burn in Ingeld. His love for his bride will fade because of his grief. Therefore, I do not believe the Heathobards' alliance is sincere, or their friendship with the Danes secure.

But I must tell you more of Grendel, noble king, so that you may know the outcome of our hand-to-hand combat."

Beowulf then told Hygelac the entire story of his struggle with Grendel, and his dangerous journey to the bottom of the lake to slay Grendel's mother.

"To you, noble ruler, I bring these treasures and offer them gladly. All my joy depends on you! I have few close relatives except you, Hygelac."

Beowulf ordered the boar-headed standard, the towering battle helmet, the gray mail-shirt, and the magnificent war sword to be brought in. He said: "Hrothgar, the wise king, gave me this armor, commanding me to explain its origin to you. He said that King Heorogar of the Danes owned it for a long time. Yet Heorogar would not give the breastplate to his own brave son, Heoroward, despite his loyalty. Use it well!"

Four swift horses, golden-dun in color, followed the treasures. Beowulf presented them all to the king. So should a relative behave, never weaving a net of malice for his own family or plotting the death of his companions. Beowulf was completely loyal to his uncle, Hygelac, and each was always mindful of the other's benefit.

Beowulf then gave Queen Hygd the magnificent necklace that Wealhtheow, the Danish queen, had presented to him, along with three elegant horses with bright saddles. From that day on, her breast was adorned with the precious jewel.

Thus Beowulf, the son of Ecgtheow, showed himself brave in honorable deeds. He did not slay his companions in drunken anger. He had no cruel heart, but guarded the great strength God had given him. For a long time, the Geats had looked down on him, considering him slow and lazy, and their king would not honor him at the beer-bench. But a glorious reversal came to the hero for all his troubles.

The king ordered Hrethel's gold-adorned sword to be brought in. There was no better blade in all of Geatland. Hygelac laid the sword on Beowulf's lap, granting him a vast estate of seven thousand hides of land, a hall, and a princely throne. Both of them inherited land and rights in the nation, but the crown itself belonged to Hygelac as the higher in rank.

In later years, after Hygelac fell in battle and swords brought death to Heardred under his shield, the broad kingdom passed into Beowulf's hands. He ruled it well for fifty years, a wise and aging king, until a dragon began to terrorize the land in the dark nights, guarding a treasure hoard in a steep stone mound on the high heath.

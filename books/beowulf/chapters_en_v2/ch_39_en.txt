Chapter Thirty-Nine: Beowulf's Last Battle

> *The final blood-boiling charge, the old king begins a bloody duel with the hellfire-breathing dragon.*

King Hrethel took to his bed, humming a death dirge. After losing his eldest son, Herebeald, his grand halls and sprawling fields felt hollow. A crushing sorrow weighed on the old king. He couldn't avenge the murder, nor could he unleash his fury—even though he despised the killer, his own son Haethcyn. Eaten alive by grief, Hrethel turned his back on the joys of men. He slipped away into God's light, leaving his kingdom and fortresses to the sons who remained.

When Hrethel breathed his last, the oceans boiled with war. Blood and chaos erupted between the Swedes and the Geats. King Ongentheow’s sons were ruthless warmongers who spat on peace treaties, launching savage, treacherous raids around Hreosnabeorh.

My kin answered their crimes with steel. We avenged the slaughtered, though the price was heavy. That vicious war claimed Haethcyn, the Geatish lord. But by dawn, his brother Hygelac had his revenge. Eofor, our champion, cornered Ongentheow. His blade shattered the Swedish king's helmet and split his skull. Eofor’s arm didn't hesitate—he delivered the killing blow without an ounce of mercy.

I paid Hygelac back for every ounce of gold and strip of land he gave me, carving out his empire with my bright sword. He never had to hire mercenaries or beg the Danes or Swedes for backup. I was his vanguard. I fought alone at the absolute bleeding edge of his army.

And I will keep fighting until this sword shatters.

It has served me flawlessly ever since I snapped the neck of Daeghrefn, the Hugas' champion. He tried to carry our golden treasures back to his Frisian king, but he didn't make it. The mighty prince fell on the battlefield. My blade didn't end him—my bare hands did. I crushed his ribcage and squeezed until his heart burst.

Now, my iron grip and sharpened steel are coming for the dragon's hoard.

Beowulf raised his voice in one final, earth-shaking oath: "I waded through oceans of blood in my youth. Now, I am the old guardian of my people. I will rip glory from this beast, if that overgrown lizard has the guts to crawl out of its stone hole and face me!"

He locked eyes with his men, bidding them a grim farewell. "If I could rip this dragon apart bare-handed like I did Grendel, I’d leave my blade behind. But I’m walking into a storm of venom, poison, and hellfire. That’s why I carry this iron shield and wear this heavy mail.

"I will not take a single step backward. Not one. Fate, the master of all men, will decide which of us dies at the wall. My heart is burning for this fight, so I’m done talking.

"Wait up here in your armor, warriors. Watch and see which of us survives the slaughter. This is my war, not yours. No other man alive has the sheer power to butcher this monster. I will tear that gold from his claws, or death will take your king!"

The great champion surged to his feet. Gripping his heavy shield, helmet strapped tight and chainmail gleaming, he marched beneath the towering cliffs. He trusted his own brute strength—something a coward could never comprehend.

Ahead, a massive stone arch loomed. A boiling, steaming river roared out from the barrow. The water churned with the dragon’s infernal heat. No man could step anywhere near the hoard without melting into slag.

Fury boiling in his veins, Beowulf roared his challenge. His deafening battle-cry echoed off the gray stone, plunging deep into the cavern.

The dragon heard it. It recognized the voice of a man. The time for peace was dead.

A monstrous blast of white-hot fire and venom exploded from the rocks. The very earth convulsed.

Beowulf braced his shield. The colossal beast surged forward, a mass of heavily armored coils thrashing with murderous glee. The king ripped his ancient, razor-sharp sword from its scabbard.

Both titans stood on the edge of terror, but neither backed down. Beowulf planted his boots behind his iron wall as the dragon coiled like a massive spring, ready to snap.

The blazing wyrm slithered forward, looping and thrashing toward its destiny. A storm of fire engulfed the king. His iron shield absorbed the hellish blast, but the metal began to warp faster than he anticipated.

For the first time in his long, blood-soaked life, Fate had turned its back on him. Victory was not guaranteed today.

Gritting his teeth against the heat, the king swung his arm and smashed his ancient sword into the beast. The blade sparked against armored scales, slipping on bone. It didn't bite deep enough.

Enraged by the strike, the dragon erupted. It unleashed a hurricane of deadly, scouring fire that torched the cliffside.

Beowulf stumbled back. He could claim no victory—his trusted steel had failed him when he needed it most, as true iron never should. A grim realization hit him. The mighty Beowulf was being forced from this earth, violently pushed toward the afterlife where all men eventually go.

But the fight was far from over.

The dragon rallied, sucking in a massive breath before exhaling a second apocalyptic wave of fire. The king baked alive inside his armor, fighting a losing war against the inferno.

And where were his elite warriors? His noble, hand-picked guard?

They weren't standing by his side. Terrified by the beast, they broke formation and scattered into the woods like cowards, desperate to save their own skins.

Out of all of them, only one man stopped. Only one warrior felt his heart twist with sorrow and rage, remembering the unbreakable bonds of brotherhood and blood.

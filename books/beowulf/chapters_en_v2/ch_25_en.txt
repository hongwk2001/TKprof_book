Chapter Twenty-Five: Beowulf's Blood-Oath

> *Hrothgar in despair, revealing the horrific and bizarre secrets of the cursed swamp.*

Beowulf, son of Ecgtheow, locked eyes with the broken king. 
"Stop your weeping, wise king!" his voice boomed. "It’s better for a man to avenge his brother than to waste time mourning. Every single one of us is walking toward death. The only thing that matters is the blood we spill and the glory we steal before the end. That’s the only true legacy for a warrior!

"On your feet, guardian of the realm! Let’s hunt this hag down. I swear this oath to you: Grendel's mother will find no safe harbor. Not in the bowels of the earth, not in the blackest forest, and not at the bottom of the abyssal sea. She can run, but she cannot hide. Give me your patience today—and I will give you her head."

The old king surged to his feet, thanking the Almighty for the hero's iron will. Hrothgar bellowed for his warhorse. 
The beast was saddled, and the wise king rode out in state, a wall of hardened shield-bearers flanking him. The monster's tracks were everywhere, carved deep into the forest mud. The trail of death led straight into the suffocating gloom of the swamp. She had dragged Hrothgar’s finest champion into the dark.

The war-party scaled jagged rock-faces and treacherous, narrow ridges where monsters made their lairs. Beowulf pushed ahead, leading the scouts. 
Suddenly, the trees twisted into gnarled, lifeless claws hanging over gray stone. Below them, a festering lake of blood-red water boiled. 
The Danes choked on a collective gasp. There, mounted on the cliff overlooking the churning lake, was the severed head of Aeschere. 

The water frothed with gore. The war-horn shattered the silence, screaming a bloody battle anthem. 
The warriors lined the banks. The lake was swarming. Massive serpents and grotesque sea-dragons writhed in the bloody deep. Hideous water-abominations basked on the cliff ledges—the same horrors that ripped sailors apart on the open seas. 
At the blast of the horn, the beasts scrambled in fury. 
Beowulf didn’t hesitate. He drew his bow and unleashed a shaft. The arrow slammed through the scales of a swimming monster, piercing its black heart. The beast thrashed, death seizing it instantly. 
The warriors roared, sinking barbed boar-spears into the dead monster and hauling its massive, scaly carcass onto the shore. They stared at the grisly trophy.

Beowulf began strapping on his war-gear, entirely unbothered by the horror before him. 
His broad, hand-forged chainmail was a fortress of steel, forged to repel any demonic claws waiting in the deep. A blazing gold helmet, crowned with war-boar crests, was strapped to his head. A master smith of the ancient world had hammered it into shape, ensuring no mortal or immortal blade could ever breach it.

Then came a surprise. Unferth, Hrothgar's arrogant loudmouth, stepped forward with an offering. 
He handed Beowulf Hrunting—a legendary, ancient sword. The iron blade was etched with venomous runes, quenched in the blood of endless wars. It had never failed a warrior brave enough to wield it in the crucible of battle. 
Unferth, suddenly forgetting his old boasts, surrendered the weapon to a better man. Hrunting was ready for war.

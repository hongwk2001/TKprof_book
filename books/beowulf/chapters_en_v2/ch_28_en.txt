Chapter Twenty-Eight: The Return to the Surface with Grendel's Head

> *The giant's sword on the wall, the hero squeezes out all his strength and strikes like lightning upon the witch's head.*

Up on the cliff, King Hrothgar and his wise Danes watched the churning lake. Blood welled up, staining the black waves crimson.

The gray-haired elders whispered their worst fears. They were sure the hero wouldn't return. To them, the monster of the deep had clearly won. By afternoon, the Danes abandoned their post. Hrothgar trudged home, his heart heavy with grief.

But Beowulf's Geats stayed. 

Sick with dread, they sat by the water's edge and stared into the bloody depths. They prayed to see their warlord again, though hope slipped through their fingers.

Deep beneath the surface, a miracle unfolded. The giant-forged sword, drenched in toxic blood, began to melt. It dissolved into bloody icicles, vanishing like winter frost under the warm breath of spring. God, the true Creator, had shattered the cold magic.

Surrounded by untold riches, Beowulf ignored the gold. He wanted only two things: the glittering hilt of the melted sword, and Grendel's massive severed head. The steel blade was gone—eaten away by the hag's boiling, venomous blood.

With the monsters dead, the vast depths were finally cleansed. Beowulf kicked upward, swimming through the purified waters with his grisly prize. 

The Geatish warlord broke the surface and dragged himself ashore. He grinned, hefting his massive, bloody burden. His men rushed the shoreline, shouting prayers of thanks. They stripped off his heavy helmet and battle-scarred mail, marveling at his survival. Behind them, the bloody lake lay dead and calm.

They marched for Heorot, roaring with victory. The burden was immense—it took four massive warriors to hoist Grendel's monstrous head on a spear shaft. But they moved with purpose. Soon, the fourteen Geats hit the plains, with Beowulf striding at their front, a god of war returning to the mead-hall.

Chapter Thirty: Hrothgar's Sermon on Pride

> *The Danes leave without hope, but the Geat warriors stand their ground, firmly believing in their hero's return.*

The old king Hrothgar rose. The feast-hall instantly plunged into absolute silence. 

"Any leader who rules with an iron fist of truth and justice, guarding his realm and honoring the blood of the past, can look at you and see a true hero," Hrothgar bellowed. "Your fame burns across the world, Beowulf! You wield immense power, yet your mind is just as sharp. Our pact stands strong. You will be a fortress for your people."

He paused, his voice turning grim. "King Heremod was not like you. He didn't bring victory to the Danes—he brought slaughter. Driven by blind rage, he butchered his own brothers-in-arms. The great prince isolated himself in a fortress of his own madness."

Hrothgar's eyes locked onto Beowulf. "God forged him stronger than any mortal, yet Heremod let his heart turn to poison. He hoarded his gold, denied his warriors their rightful spoils, and died a miserable, cursed wreck. Learn from his ruin, Beowulf! Let this old warrior's scars be your guide."

The king swept his gaze across the hall. "It is a terrifying wonder how God hands out power, land, and wisdom. He commands everything. He lets a king feast on victory, gifting him impenetrable fortresses and endless borders. Drunk on invincibility, the king forgets that death comes for us all."

"He lives untouchable—no sickness, no aging, no war. The world bows to him. But then, pride takes root like a parasite. His conscience falls into a dead sleep. And when the watchman sleeps, the Enemy strikes! A poisoned arrow pierces the king’s black heart."

Hrothgar leaned forward. "Suddenly, his vast empire isn't enough. Greed claws at his soul. He locks away his gold, starving his loyal warriors of their rings, forgetting that his glory was a gift from above."

"But the end always comes," Hrothgar warned. "The king rots and dies. A new ruler steps over his corpse and throws open the treasure vaults, rewarding the bold without an ounce of fear or greed."

"Crush that pride, Beowulf! You are the greatest of men—choose the eternal path. Do not let arrogance master you!" The old king’s voice echoed like thunder. "Right now, your strength is absolute. But mark my words: sickness, or a savage blade, will strip it away. Fire, flood, a flying spear, or the crippling grip of old age will tear you down. The fire in your eyes will burn out. In the end, death conquers everyone. Even you."

Chapter Twenty-One: Queen Wealhtheow's Plea for Her Sons

> *The Queen's desperate prayer, raising the golden cup in hopes of eternal peace.*

A bitter peace was struck. King Finn swore blood-oaths to Hengest: he would rule the surviving Danes with an even hand. No man, by word or blade, could shatter the truce.

The Danes swallowed their fury. Fate had bound them to serve the man who slaughtered their king. But Finn laid down the law: if any Frisian dared mock the Danes for their defeat, the sword would do the talking.

They built the pyre. Gold was dragged from the hoard. Hnaef, the greatest of Danish warlords, was hauled onto the timber. Beside him lay shattered armor, gold-boar helmets, and a mountain of dead nobles.

Queen Hildeburh stood by the wood, shattered by grief. She ordered her own son cast into the flames beside his slaughtered uncle. She wept, screaming her sorrow into the wind as the torches were thrown.

The pyre roared to life. A massive pillar of fire clawed at the clouds. Flesh burned, wounds split open, and boiling blood sizzled against the heat. The inferno devoured the fallen of both nations. Their glory turned to ash.

Winter fell like an iron cage. The surviving warriors scattered, but a grim destiny chained Hengest to Finn's court. He craved the sea and his homeland, but ice locked the waves and brutal storms battered the coast.

Then came spring, shattering the ice and bringing bright skies. But Hengest had no thoughts of home. He wanted blood. He wanted revenge on the Frisians.

The waiting ended when a warrior dropped a legendary battle-sword into Hengest's lap. The steel hummed for violence. Hengest took the blade. He embraced the slaughter.

War exploded inside Finn's stronghold. The Danish warlords, Guthlaf and Oslaf, burst in from the sea. They were done choking on their fury. They unleashed absolute carnage.

Finn's hall was painted with Frisian blood. King Finn was butchered among his own guards. The Danes looted his hoard, stripped his jewels, and dragged Queen Hildeburh to their ships, sailing her back to her people.

The poet's song ended. The hall erupted in cheers. Benches rattled, wine flowed, and the feast roared back to life.

Queen Wealhtheow stepped into the light, wearing a crown of beaten gold. She strode to where King Hrothgar sat beside his nephew, Hrothulf. For now, their loyalty was unbroken. At the king's feet sat Unferth. Everyone still respected his nerve, even though he was known for slaughtering his own kin in a past feud.

The queen raised her cup. "Take this, my king! Drink! Speak to the Geats with the thunder of a true leader! Shower them in gifts, for your kingdom is secure!"

Her eyes flashed. "I hear you wish to take Beowulf as your son. Heorot is purged! Revel in your wealth, but remember your bloodline. When death comes for you, leave this kingdom to your true family."

She stared hard at Hrothulf. "I know our nephew Hrothulf. If you fall before he does, he will lead our warriors with honor. He will remember the love we showed him and shield our sons."

With that, she walked to the princes' bench. There sat her boys, Hrethric and Hrothmund. And seated right beside them was the ultimate weapon himself: Beowulf.

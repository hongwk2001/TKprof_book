Chapter Thirty-Two: The Departure and Hrothgar's Tears

> *Hrothgar's hot tears, the old king weeps as he embraces the hero he will never see again.*

Beowulf locked eyes with the king and spoke. "We've come a long way, Hrothgar, but it's time we returned to our lord, King Hygelac. You've hosted us like royalty. We lack for nothing. 

"But hear this, noble ruler. If I can earn more of your favor in blood and iron, just say the word. If word crosses the sea that rival warlords are marching on your borders, I won't just send aid. I'll sail a thousand battle-hardened warriors to your shores and break them myself.

"King Hygelac is young, but he's a warrior to the bone. He'll back me up. When you need a forest of spears at your back, I'll bring them. And if your son Hrethric ever crosses the water to the Geatish courts, he'll find a shield wall of friends. A brave man always carves out a place in foreign lands."

Hrothgar's face lit up with fierce pride. "The gods themselves put that fire in your chest! Never have I heard a warrior so young speak with such lethal wisdom. You've got a titan's strength, a veteran's mind, and the silver tongue of a king. 

"I'll say it plain. If sickness, a stray spear, or a coward's blade ever strikes down Hygelac, the Geats could find no better man to wear the crown. If you're willing to take the throne, there's no one better to guard their gold and their borders.

"The longer I know you, Beowulf, the sharper my respect for you grows. You've forged a blood-pact of peace between the Geats and the Danes. You've butchered the old grudges and buried our ancient wars. As long as I draw breath and rule this realm, our treasures are yours. 

"Our ships will cut through the ocean waves carrying gold and steel, binding our peoples together. From this day on, we stand shoulder-to-shoulder as loyal brothers, and we will absolutely obliterate any enemy foolish enough to stand against us."

Inside the grand hall, Hrothgar heaped twelve more priceless treasures into Beowulf's arms. He wished the hero safe passage across the violent seas and begged him to return. 

Then, the old king pulled Beowulf in close. He threw his arms around the massive Geat's neck and kissed him, tears spilling down his weathered face. A dark, unshakable premonition gripped Hrothgar's soul—he knew they would never see each other again. Beowulf had fought his way into the old king's heart. Hrothgar couldn't hold back the grief, an aching sorrow tearing at him as the great hero turned and strode out of the hall.

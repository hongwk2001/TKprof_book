Chapter Twenty-Two: The Golden Collar and the Sleepers

> *The glory of the golden necklace, the careless warriors fall asleep unaware of impending death.*

They brought the ceremonial cup to Beowulf, showering the slayer with praise. Hrothgar heaped the spoils of victory upon him—coils of twisted gold, heavy armbands, a flawless mail-shirt, and thick rings. 

But the centerpiece was a massive golden collar, the largest ever forged on earth. 

Nothing matched its glory since the legendary hero Hama tore the Brosing necklace from the hoard, escaping the wicked King Eormenric and hauling the prize back to his shining city.

Years later, King Hygelac of the Geats would wear that exact collar into his final, bloody war. Driven by pride, he sparked a savage feud with the Frisians. Fate caught him. He died under his banner, defending his plunder. The mighty lord fell beneath his splintered shield, his life, his armor, and that legendary collar ripped away by enemy hands. Vultures and lesser men looted the dead, leaving Geatish warriors to rot on the battlefield.

Back in Heorot, the mead-hall roared with applause. Queen Wealhtheow stepped before the battle-hardened crowd. 

"Wear this ring and forge your own luck, Beowulf!" she proclaimed. "Wrap yourself in this mail-shirt from our royal vault. Claim your glory, unleash your brute strength, and guide my young sons with a steady hand. I won't forget to reward you."

The warriors cheered. 

"You've cemented your legacy," Wealhtheow continued, her voice echoing over the noise. "Men will sing your praises from the furthest shores to the storm-battered cliffs! Live long, fight hard, and prosper. I gladly hand over this wealth. Just watch over my boys. You've dragged Heorot back into the light. Look around—every warrior here is loyal to the bone, bound by blood, and sworn to the king. We are a united front!"

With that, she swept back to her throne. The feast hit a fever pitch. 

Wine flowed like rivers. Laughter shook the rafters. The men drank deeply, utterly blind to the grim slaughter waiting in the dark. 

When the sun finally died and Hrothgar retired to his chambers, a massive company of warriors locked down the hall. Just as in the old days, they shoved aside the heavy wooden benches and covered the floor with beds and bolsters. 

One warrior, marked for death, closed his eyes for the last time.

The men stacked their wooden shields at their heads. Above each fighter, on the massive benches, sat their gear—dented helmets, heavy chain-mail, and lethal iron-tipped spears. 

They didn't just sleep; they waited. Whether safe at home or neck-deep in enemy territory, they were always primed for war. If their king called, they answered with steel. They were true warriors to the end.

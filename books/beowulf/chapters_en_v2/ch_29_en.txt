Chapter Twenty-Nine: The Presentation of the Trophies

> *Blood signaling victory, only the hilt of the giant's sword survives the boiling toxic blood.*

Beowulf strode into Heorot, blood-spattered and victorious. The legendary warrior approached Hrothgar's throne as his men dragged their prize across the feast-hall floor. They hauled Grendel’s severed head by its matted hair, dropping it right where the warriors drank. 

The queen gasped. The hardened soldiers stared in pure awe at the grisly, monstrous trophy.

Beowulf stood tall and spoke out. "Look upon this, King Hrothgar! We ripped these spoils from the depths of the lake to prove our victory. It was a bloodbath down in that underwater hell. If the Almighty hadn't shielded me, they would have butchered me on the spot."

He gestured to the weapons. "Unferth’s sword, Hrunting, couldn't pierce her hide—good blade as it is. But the Lord of Men guided my eyes. I saw a massive, ancient sword hanging on the cavern wall. God favors the fearless when all else fails."

"I tore the giant weapon from the stone and slaughtered the beast in her own lair," Beowulf declared, his voice echoing through the silent hall. "When her boiling, toxic blood erupted, it melted the blade into nothing but slag. But I brought the hilt back from the demons. I have avenged the slaughtered Danes. The debt is paid in blood."

He locked eyes with the king. "I swear it. You and your warriors—young and old—can finally sleep in peace. Heorot is safe. The shadow of death is gone."

With a powerful thrust, Beowulf presented the golden hilt, forged by ancient giants, to the weary king. With the monsters eradicated, the relic belonged to Hrothgar, the greatest treasure-giver in the north.

Hrothgar took the hilt, tracing his fingers over the ancient gold. Carved into the metal was a legendary story: the Great Flood that drowned the ancient, rebellious giants. The Creator had unleashed the raging waters to wipe the enemies of the Eternal Lord from the earth. And along the golden cross-guard, glowing rune-letters spelled out the name of the first warrior who had ever wielded the magnificent, serpentine sword.

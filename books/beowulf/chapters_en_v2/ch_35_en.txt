Chapter 35: Blood Debts and Plunder

> *Bestowal of treasures and an oath of loyalty, the hero dedicates all glory to the king who believed in him.*

"Picture this," Beowulf continued. "An old, scarred veteran sits over his mead, eyes locked on a stolen sword. Memories of slaughter burn in his gut. Boiling with grim fury, he corners a young warrior and stokes the ashes of an ancient grudge. 'Look there, boy,' he’ll hiss. 'See that blade? It's the same iron your father swung in his last stand, right before the Danes butchered him! They stole our field, slaughtered our heroes, and left Withergyld to rot. And now? The son of your father's murderer struts through our own halls, flashing the very steel that should be strapped to your hip!'

"He'll twist that knife every single night. Day after day, words will drip like poison until steel flashes and one of the bride’s Danish escorts chokes on his own blood to pay for his father's sins. The killer will vanish into the hills, and just like that—the peace pact shatters. Ingeld’s heart will harden, his blood boiling hotter than his love for his new bride. That’s why I don't buy this Heathobard alliance. Their 'friendship' with the Danes is built on a powder keg.

"But enough of politics, my king. You want to know how I broke the monster."

Beowulf launched into the brutal saga of his bare-handed brawl with Grendel. He held nothing back, recounting every shattered bone, and plunged headfirst into the terrifying dive to the bottom of the blood-lake to butcher Grendel's demon mother.

"And I brought the loot straight to you, my lord!" Beowulf roared. "You're my blood, Hygelac. Everything I do, I do for the glory of our house!" 

He signaled his men. They hauled in a mountain of war-gear: a gilded, boar-crested standard, a towering iron helm, a mesh of steel ring-mail, and a monstrous war-sword. 

"Hrothgar himself laid this armor at my feet," Beowulf said. "It belonged to his brother, King Heorogar. Heorogar wouldn't even give this breastplate to his own son. But now, it’s yours. Wear it and conquer!"

Four magnificent, golden-dun warhorses were paraded into the hall next. Beowulf surrendered it all. That is how true brothers in arms behave—no backstabbing, no weaving treacherous nets in the shadows, no plotting the demise of their own kin. Beowulf’s loyalty to his uncle was unbreakable. They were a unified front of pure Geatish power.

To Queen Hygd, Beowulf gifted the legendary, sun-bright necklace he'd won from Wealhtheow, along with three sleek, brightly saddled steeds. From that day forward, the queen wore the staggering jewel like a badge of Geatish triumph.

This was Beowulf, son of Ecgtheow. A titan who wielded his god-given strength with absolute discipline. He wasn't some drunken brawler who slaughtered his own men in mead-soaked rages. For years, the Geats had mocked him, calling him a sluggish, worthless boy. The king himself used to ignore him at the feast-tables. 
Not anymore. The lazy boy was dead. A legend had returned, silencing every whisper of doubt.

Hygelac answered the tribute in kind. The king commanded Hrethel's gold-hilted heirloom sword to be brought forth—the deadliest blade in all of Geatland. He slammed the heavy weapon down onto Beowulf’s lap. Along with the steel, he granted him a colossal estate: seven thousand hides of land, a sprawling mead-hall, and a princely throne of his own. Hygelac wore the crown, but Beowulf now ruled as a lord of war.

Years bled away. Hygelac was eventually cut down in the fury of war, and his heir Heardred met his end beneath a storm of enemy blades. The crown, and the burden of the broad kingdom, fell to Beowulf. 

He ruled with an iron grip for fifty brutal, prosperous years. He grew ancient and wise. But the peace would not last. 
High on a desolate heath, inside a steep stone mound bursting with ancient gold, something awoke in the dark. A dragon began to stir. And it was hungry for fire.

Chapter Thirty-Eight: The March to the Barrow

> *With the shadow of death at his back, the old king leads eleven warriors to the dragon's lair.*

Two Swedish princes, sons of Ohthere, rebelled against their uncle, the tyrant King Onela. Their rebellion shattered, and they fled for their lives across the freezing sea, seeking sanctuary with the Geats. 

King Heardred threw his doors open. He gave the exiled royals food, fire, and shelter. But that mercy bought him only death.

When Onela heard his hunted nephews had survived, his fury boiled over. He unleashed a massive invasion, driving his war machine into Geatland to slaughter the young king. The clash was brutal. A Swedish blade gutted King Heardred on his own soil. His vengeance bought with blood, Onela marched his army back to Sweden. 

The Geatish throne stood empty. The people turned to the one man who could hold the kingdom together: Beowulf. He claimed the crown, ruling with iron and wisdom. For fifty years, he kept the peace.

But Beowulf never forgot the murder of Heardred. He plotted his vengeance in the dark. 

His chance arrived when he took in Prince Eadgils, the lone surviving Swedish royal who had outrun Onela's wrath. Beowulf armed the exile to the teeth. He sent a massive strike force of Geatish warriors and heavy steel across the sea. Backed by Beowulf’s savage might, Eadgils tore into Sweden. He slaughtered Onela’s army, crushed his uncle's skull in battle, and seized the crown. The bloody feud was finally dead.

Thus Beowulf, son of Ecgtheow, had waded through decades of slaughter, dodging death in every war and desperate clash. Now, the final trial awaited him: the dragon.

Boiling with rage, the Geatish king rounded up eleven hand-picked elite killers to face the beast. He knew exactly how this war started—the priceless gold cup had been dropped at his feet. The thirteenth man in his squad was the pathetic slave who had triggered the nightmare. Dragged by his collar, the terrified captive was forced to show them the way. 

He led the warband down to a buried stone vault, built right against the roaring surf. Inside, the tomb choked with priceless jewels and twisted gold wire. For centuries, a monstrous, fire-breathing horror had slept on that hoard. Anyone stepping inside was asking for a brutal death.

The battle-scarred king sat heavily on a cliffside, turning to his sworn brothers. His blood ran cold, his heart restless but utterly fearless. Death was breathing down his neck, ready to rip the soul from his battered flesh. The old king’s time on this earth was bleeding out.

Beowulf, son of Ecgtheow, spoke: "I’ve waded through a lifetime of blood. I remember every scar. I was just seven years old when King Hrethel took me from my father. He fed me, armored me, and loved me like his own flesh and blood. To him, I was no different than his own sons: Herebeald, Haethcyn, and my lord Hygelac.

"But a nightmare ripped that family apart. Haethcyn pulled his bow and put an arrow through his own older brother's heart. He missed the target and slaughtered Herebeald with a bloody shaft. A catastrophic, gut-wrenching mistake. No gold could pay that debt. No blood could balance the scales. Hrethel had to watch his eldest son rot, unavenged.

"It’s a crushing hell for an old man to watch his boy swing from the gallows. He can only choke out a death song while the ravens pick his son’s bones clean. All his wisdom means nothing. Every sunrise is a brutal reminder of the boy he lost. He doesn't want another heir. He stares at the dead boy’s empty hall, stripped by the wind, the beds cold. The riders are rotting in the dirt. The harp is silent. The laughter is dead."

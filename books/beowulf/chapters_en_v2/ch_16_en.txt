Chapter Sixteen: The Legend of Sigemund

> *The monster's arm hung from the rafters, the Danes praise the hero's overwhelming strength.*

Morning broke, and the feast-hall buzzed with a mob of hardened warriors. Warlords and chieftains rode from all corners of the earth, pushing across wide dirt roads just to lay eyes on the grisly trophy: Grendel's severed, bleeding claw. Nobody shed a single tear for the butchered beast. 

Instead, they tracked the carnage. They eagerly followed the gory trail where the broken monster had dragged its mangled carcass all the way back to the demon-infested lake to die.

The lake was boiling with blood. Vile, crimson waves surged and churned with hot gore. Grendel had plunged into this swampy hell-pit to hide, completely stripped of his savage joy. He bled out in the murky depths, coughing up his heathen soul as the underworld swallowed him whole.

The warriors didn't linger in the gloom. They spurred their white war-steeds and galloped back in triumph. Their voices echoed through the valleys, chanting Beowulf's name. They swore up and down that under the vast stretch of the sky—north or south—no warrior walked the earth who was deadlier, braver, or more fit to wear a crown than Beowulf.

They meant no disrespect to their own king, of course. Hrothgar was a fine ruler, and they loved him. But Beowulf was a living god of war.

As they tore down the open paths, racing their brown horses in the dust, an old poet rode among them. This king's thane was a master of the ancient sagas, a man who carried a thousand blood-soaked legends in his mind. Inspired by the morning's glory, he forged new verses on the spot. He spun a raw, brilliant tale of Beowulf's brutal victory, capturing the sheer savagery of the night before.

Then, the bard shifted his tune. He roared out the epic saga of Sigemund, the mighty son of Waels. 

He sang of Sigemund's ruthless crusades, his brutal blood-feuds, and secrets so dark that only his loyal nephew, Fitela, knew them. The two were a terrifying storm on the battlefield. Side by side, they had carved a bloody path through hordes of giants, butchering them with heavy broadswords.

But Sigemund's greatest kill—the one that cemented his immortal legacy—happened alone.

Deep in a gray stone cavern, he had stalked a colossal dragon hoarding a massive mountain of gold. Without Fitela to back him up, Sigemund faced the winged nightmare solo. He didn't flinch. He lunged in and drove his greatsword straight through the beast's scaly hide, pinning the screeching monster against the cavern wall. 

The dragon died choking on its own fire. 

Sigemund ripped the treasure from the beast's dead claws, loading a ship to the brim with gold and glowing jewels. Behind him, the dragon's ruined carcass literally melted into a puddle of foul slime, consumed by its own infernal heat.

Now, Beowulf stood on that exact same pedestal. A dragon-slayer's equal. A legend forged in blood.

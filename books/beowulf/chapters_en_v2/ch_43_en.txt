Chapter Forty-Three: Beowulf's Death and the Mourning of the Geats

> *The death of a great hero, beside the ashes of the dragon, the Geats pour out tearing wails.*

The dying king stared at the gleaming gold, his voice thick with pain and triumph. "I thank the King of Glory for this haul. I bled for this hoard, but I secured it for my people before the end. My time is up. You must lead the Geats now.

"When the funeral fire dies, command my warriors to raise a mighty mound on the sea-cliffs. Let it pierce the sky at Whale's Ness. Let every sailor steering his longship through the ocean mists look up and know it as Beowulf's Barrow."

With trembling hands, the old king unclasped the golden collar from his neck. He handed it to young Wiglaf, along with his gilded helm, his ring, and his blood-stained mail. 

"Wear them well," Beowulf rasped. "You are the last of the Waegmundings. Fate has scythed down our bloodline, sweeping every brave warrior to his doom. Now, it's my turn to join them."

Those were the last words to leave the old king's chest. His eyes glazed over, his soul departing to seek the judgment of the righteous.

Grief slammed into Wiglaf as he stared down at his fallen lord. 

Nearby lay the monstrous dragon, mangled and lifeless. The beast would guard its hoarded gold no more. Iron blades had shredded its scaled hide, spilling its foul blood across the sand. Never again would it ignite the night sky. Wiglaf's blade had grounded the terror forever. 

Few men in history had ever survived the poison breath of a wyrm, let alone breached its stone vault while it was awake. This treasure was paid for in blood, buying the deaths of both king and monster.

Then, out of the woods, the cowards crawled back. 

The ten oath-breakers, the men who had abandoned their lord when he needed them most, trudged out of the trees. They carried their shields and spears in utter shame, stopping where Wiglaf sat beside the ruined king. The young warrior was exhausted, his hands soaked as he tried in vain to wash the grime and blood from Beowulf's face. He couldn't drag his leader back from the dead.

Wiglaf looked up. His heart turned cold with fury.

"If a man wants the raw truth," Wiglaf snarled, "he'd say our king threw his gold away on cowards. He gave you helmets and mail-shirts while you bragged on the mead-benches. But when the fire came, you ran. The king had no reason to boast of his so-called brothers!"

Wiglaf stood, gripping his bloody sword. "God granted him the strength to carve out his vengeance alone! I could offer little help in the blaze, but I did what I could. I struck the beast, and its fire choked. But too few blades stood beside the king when it counted. 

"Now hear this! All sharing of treasure, all gifts of swords, all the joy of your homes—it is finished. When the lords of the world hear of your cowardice, your families will be stripped of your lands. You will be exiled. Death is better than a life chained to disgrace!"

Wiglaf dispatched a rider to the cliffside encampment. The remaining nobles had sat in miserable silence all morning, waiting to see if their king would return. The messenger spurred his horse up the headland and delivered the crushing truth.

"Our king is dead," he shouted. "The dragon slaughtered him, but the beast lies dead beside him, butchered by our blades. Wiglaf sits alone, a living guard over the dead.

"Brace yourselves. When the Franks and Frisians hear of Beowulf's fall, they will bring war to our shores. We have bled them before, and they have not forgotten.

"And do not expect peace from the Swedes. King Ongentheow butchered Haethcyn at Ravenswood, and that blood feud still burns. Now that they know our protector is gone, they will strike. Beowulf was the only wall standing between our treasures and our enemies!

"So hurry! Let us go to our king and carry him to the pyre. Burn the treasure with him! He bought this gold with his life, and the fire must devour it all. No warrior will wear these jewels. No maiden will wear these rings. Stripped of wealth, we will wander in exile. Our laughter and songs die with him. The morning spears will be cold in our hands, and the harp will fall silent. Only the dark raven will circle the battlefield, calling the eagle and the wolf to feast!"

The grim truth hit them like a physical blow. Weeping, the warriors marched to Eagle's Ness. 

They found their mighty prince lying dead on the sand, a heroic end to a legendary life. Beside him sprawled the colossal ruin of the dragon, fifty feet of scorched scales and charred flesh. Scattered around the beast lay ancient cups, rusty swords, and golden plates, treasures that had rotted in the earth for a thousand years.

Wiglaf gave the order. The warriors hauled timber to build the pyre. They shoved the dragon's massive corpse over the cliff, watching the dark waves swallow the beast forever. Then, they loaded a wagon with the cursed gold and carried their king to Whale's Ness.

The Geats built a towering funeral pyre. They hung it with helmets, shields, and mail-shirts, just as Beowulf had commanded. Weeping, they laid their beloved lord in the center.

They torched the wood. Black smoke billowed into the sky. The roaring flames drowned out their cries. An old Geatish woman sang a sorrowful dirge, wailing of the war, slaughter, and slavery that now stalked her people. The smoke rose until it swallowed the heavens.

For ten days, they built a massive stone beacon on the headland. It was a monument that could be seen by sailors across the sea. Inside the thick walls, they buried the king's ashes. They threw the rings and jewels back into the earth, burying the gold where it remains today—as useless to men as it ever was.

Twelve battle-hardened warriors rode their horses around the towering mound. They chanted dirges, honoring his life and singing of his savage glory, giving a hero the send-off he deserved.

The Geats mourned their fallen lord. They said that of all the kings in the world, Beowulf was the most fiercely protective, the kindest to his people, and the hungriest for glory.

Chapter Thirty-Four: Beowulf's Report to King Hygelac

> *The hero's blood-boiling report to his liege, recounting the tragedy and epic victory in Denmark.*

Beowulf and his elite guard stormed up the wide, sandy beach. The sun beat down on them, blazing like a torch in the southern sky. They marched hard for the royal court. They knew King Hygelac—the very man who butchered the Swedish king Ongentheow—was handing out gold rings to his loyal men.

Word of Beowulf’s arrival hit the court like a thunderclap. 

The king heard the news: his nephew, his finest shield-brother, had walked away from the slaughter alive. Hygelac barked orders, clearing the great feast-hall to make way for the returning heroes. 

Inside, Beowulf sat face-to-face with his king. Uncle and nephew locked eyes. The king embraced him with fierce, booming words of welcome. Queen Hygd glided through the hall, pouring mead and thrusting heavy drinking cups into the hands of the bloodied warriors. 

Hygelac leaned forward, burning with curiosity. "Give it to me straight, Beowulf! How went the campaign? You sailed across the black sea to Heorot to pick a fight with a monster. Did you lift Hrothgar’s curse?" 

The king’s face darkened. "I agonized over this, nephew. I told you not to go. I told you to let the Danes bleed for their own war against Grendel. But by the gods, I’m glad to see you breathing."

Beowulf, son of Ecgtheow, cracked a grim smile. "It’s no secret, my king. Grendel and I tore that hall apart."

He leaned in, his voice ringing with absolute confidence. "That freak brought nothing but death and misery to the Danes. So I made him pay. I slaughtered him. No kin of Grendel will ever boast about that midnight brawl—not while their wretched bloodline still breathes on this earth."

"When I first hit their shores," Beowulf continued, "I marched straight to Hrothgar. Once he heard my plan, he threw a feast and gave me a seat of honor among his own sons. The mead flowed. The warriors roared. I’ve never seen a hall so alive."

"Queen Wealhtheow drifted through the crowd, handing out gold rings and stoking the fires in the young men's bellies. Even Hrothgar's daughter, Freawaru, carried the cup to the grizzled veterans."

Beowulf paused, his eyes narrowing. "She’s betrothed to Ingeld of the Heathobards. Hrothgar thinks marrying her off will bury the old blood feuds. He thinks he can buy peace with a pretty bride." 

He shook his head. "But peace never lasts. When a prince lies dead, the spears don't stay quiet for long. No matter how noble the bride."

Beowulf sneered. "Just wait until the Heathobard lords see Danish nobles strutting around with their bride. The Danes will be flaunting ancient swords and gold-ringed armor that they looted from dead Heathobards. The very weapons those men lost along with their lives."

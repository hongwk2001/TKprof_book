Chapter Twenty: The Story of Finn, Hengest, and Hildeburh

> *A tragedy within the song, an epic of bloody revenge adds fuel to the feast.*

King Hrothgar wasn't done yet. He unloaded a mountain of priceless treasure onto every single warrior who had braved the churning seas with Beowulf. For the Geatish fighter Grendel had torn apart, Hrothgar paid out in blood-gold. 

The hell-beast would have slaughtered them all if God’s iron will and Beowulf’s raw, savage grit hadn't stood in its path. The Almighty commanded the battlefield then, just as He commands it now. Sharp wits and looking ahead keep a man alive, because if you breathe long enough in this war-torn world, you’ll taste both the sweetest glory and the bitterest ash.

Music roared through the great hall. The king’s poet plucked the wooden harp and launched into an epic saga, his booming voice echoing over the mead-benches, thrilling the drunken warriors.

He sang the blood-soaked tragedy of King Finn’s sons. Hnaef, the fierce Danish warlord, was doomed to bleed out in a brutal clash against the Frisians. Queen Hildeburh—Hnaef's own sister, shackled in marriage to Finn—learned the hard way that Frisian loyalty was a hollow joke. Completely blameless, she watched her world shatter. Both her son and her brother took spears to the gut, dropping dead on the chaotic battlefield. Her grief was absolute. 

When the morning sun broke, Hildeburh, daughter of Hoc, collapsed in tears. Under a cold, open sky, the mangled corpses of her kin lay rotting exactly where her greatest joy used to reside. 

The slaughter left Finn's war-band gutted. Most of his elite fighters were dead in the mud. Overwhelmed and outmatched, he couldn't push back against Hengest, the furious new Danish commander. He couldn't even keep his surviving men breathing against the Danish onslaught.

Backed into a corner, King Finn sued for peace. He offered Hengest a desperate truce: the Frisians would clear out a brand new mead-hall and carve out a fresh throne, splitting power right down the middle. Furthermore, whenever Finn cracked open the armory to pass out the spoils, he swore to shower the Danes in gold and jewels, matching every single coin he handed his own Frisian warriors in the beer-hall.

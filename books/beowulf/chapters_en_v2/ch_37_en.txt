Chapter Thirty-Seven: Beowulf's Grief and the Eleven Companions

> *The burning lands of the Geats, the grief-stricken old king resolves for one final battle.*

The fiend unleashed a torrent of flame, turning the great halls of the Geats into blazing pyres. The fiery glare struck terror into the hearts of the people, for the hateful dragon meant to leave no survivors. The monster’s cruel revenge was a brutal statement of its hatred. Before dawn broke, it vanished back to its dark hoard. The beast had drowned the land in fire and now placed its trust in its cavern walls and terrible power. But that trust would soon be shattered.

The news hit Beowulf hard. His own home—the grandest hall and the mighty throne of the Geats—had been reduced to ash by the dragon's fire. A suffocating grief seized the old king. Fear gripped him that he had somehow enraged the Eternal Lord, breaking some ancient law to bring down this hellfire. Dark, chaotic thoughts boiled in his chest, shattering his usual calm. The fire-drake had razed their coastal fortress and scorched their lands. The king demanded vengeance. 

He commanded his smiths to forge a colossal battle-shield, wrought entirely of iron. Wood would only fuel the beast's flames. The noble warlord knew his final days on this earth were approaching. But the dragon, despite hoarding its cursed gold for ages, was destined to die right alongside him.

The proud king scoffed at the idea of marching on the beast with an army. He didn't fear the slaughter, and he dismissed the dragon's might and fury as a mere nuisance. He had survived countless blood-soaked clashes and battle-crashes. He was the man who had cleansed Hrothgar's hall and pulverized Grendel's monstrous bloodline.

One of his most brutal trials was the fall of Hygelac. When the Geatish king was cut down in Frisia by deadly sword blows, Beowulf survived through sheer, superhuman strength. Plunging into the roaring sea, he swam to safety carrying the armor of thirty dead men on his arm. The enemy warriors who clashed with him had no stories to boast about—few lived to see their homes after crossing blades with that fierce war-wolf.

Lonely and grieving, Beowulf swam across the vast ocean back to his people. Queen Hygd offered him the kingdom, its treasures, and the throne, doubting her young son Heardred could defend their borders now that Hygelac was dead. But the Geats couldn't sway the noble Beowulf to steal the crown. Instead, he stood by the young prince, fiercely guarding and guiding him until the boy grew into a ruler.

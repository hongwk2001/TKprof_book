Chapter Nineteen: The Great Feast and the Story of Finn

> *Massive treasures bestowed by the king, golden comfort for the warriors who shed blood.*

Hrothgar didn't hold back. To honor the slaughter of Grendel, the king bestowed Beowulf with a glittering golden banner, a master-crafted helmet, a heavy mail-shirt, and a legendary sword.

The hall erupted in cheers as the spoils were marched before the monster-slayer. Beowulf drained his mead cup, unashamed to claim his due. These weren't just gifts; they were a warlord's tribute. The helmet bore a reinforced iron ridge, coiled with wire—armor forged to shatter enemy blades before they could crack a warrior's skull. 

But Hrothgar wasn't finished. 

The king commanded eight warhorses, champing at the bit in gold-plated bridles, to be marched right onto the mead-hall floor. One beast bore a saddle encrusted with gems and intricate metalwork. It was Hrothgar's personal war-saddle—the very seat from which the legendary king had directed the slaughter of his enemies, never flinching as men were hacked to pieces around him.

"Take them," Hrothgar declared, handing Beowulf the reins and the steel. "Ride them into glory."

With open hands, the Danish king paid the blood-price for Grendel's defeat. He showered Beowulf with stallions and steel, a kingly reward so staggeringly generous that no man could ever speak a word against it.
